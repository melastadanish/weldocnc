## Section 3: What Does Turbine Casing Machining Require?

**H2:** What Does CNC Turbine Casing Machining Require?

Weldo CNC machines turbine casings in 316L stainless steel, 17-4 PH stainless (H1025 condition), Inconel 625, carbon steel A105, and duplex stainless 2205, to a rotor clearance bore tolerance of ±0.025 mm and split-line flange face flatness of ±0.025 mm/300 mm, from its ISO 9001:2015 certified facility in Dongguan, China. The facility holds a general precision capability of ±0.005 mm across CNC milling and turning operations. Every turbine casing order includes a CMM — coordinate measuring machine — dimensional inspection report covering bore diameter, bore concentricity, split-line flatness, and bolt circle position. Prototype and low-volume turbine casings ship in 1–5 business days from drawing approval.

Turbine casing machining has three tolerance relationships that must all hold simultaneously. Rotor clearance bore — the internal bore diameter that determines the gap between the rotor and casing — must stay within ±0.025 mm: too tight and the rotor contacts the casing at operating temperature; too loose and rotor efficiency falls as flow bypasses through the clearance gap. Split-line face flatness — the mating surface between the upper and lower casing halves — must hold ±0.025 mm across a 300 mm span so the flange seals under bolt clamp load. Bolt hole position must hold ±0.05 mm relative to the bore datum so the two halves align on assembly.

Surface finish on the split-line face — Ra 1.6 µm — determines whether a gasket or metal-to-metal seal sits correctly across the full flange width. A face machined rougher than Ra 1.6 µm creates leak paths under pressure. These three requirements — bore diameter, split-line flatness, and surface finish — are all verified by CMM and profilometer before the casing ships.

## Section 6: Turbine Casing Machining Process

**H2:** How Weldo CNC Machines Turbine Casings

Datum selection drives every decision in turbine casing machining. The primary datum is the casing bore centreline — the rotor axis. Setting that datum first means bore diameter, bore concentricity, flange face flatness, bolt circle position, and inlet/outlet port location are all measured from one reference. A casing machined from an outer-face datum will accumulate position error between the bore and the features referenced to it — which shows up as rotor contact or flange seal failure on assembly.

Split-line machining is the process step most often skipped by suppliers quoting on price alone. Both casing halves — upper and lower — must be machined together as an assembly or a matched pair on the split-line face. Machining each half separately cannot guarantee that the two flange faces are co-planar when the halves are bolted together. At Weldo CNC, the halves are fixtured together before the split-line face is machined — one cut across both faces ensures flatness of ±0.025 mm/300 mm holds across the assembled joint, not just on each half in isolation.

Large-bore turbine casings — bore diameter 300 mm to 1000 mm — are machined on a horizontal boring mill or a large CNC machining centre. Confirm bore diameter at the quote stage so the correct machine is assigned. Boring bar overhang is kept below 4× bore diameter to control deflection within the tolerance band. Inconel 625 casings run at 20–40 m/min with high-pressure coolant and planned tool changes — the cycle time is longer than stainless, and that time is quoted honestly before the order is placed.

CMM inspection covers bore diameter, bore concentricity relative to the rotor axis, split-line flatness across both assembled halves, bolt circle position, and inlet/outlet port diameter. The profilometer reading for split-line surface finish is included in the same report. Every measurement is reported as an actual value against the drawing nominal and tolerance — not pass/fail checkboxes.

## Section 9: FAQ

**H2:** Turbine Casing Machining — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: What bore tolerance can Weldo CNC hold on turbine casings?**

Rotor clearance bore tolerance is ±0.025 mm — the critical dimension that controls rotor-to-casing clearance at operating temperature. Bore concentricity relative to the casing centreline holds to ±0.025 mm TIR. Both are verified by CMM and reported as actual measured values in the inspection report. If your drawing calls for tighter tolerances on specific features, confirm at the quote stage — general facility precision reaches ±0.005 mm on appropriate part geometries.

**Q2: What materials do you machine for turbine casings?**

Confirmed turbine casing materials include 316L stainless steel, 17-4 PH stainless in H1025 condition (1000 MPa tensile), Inconel 625, carbon steel A105, and duplex stainless 2205. Each material has a specific machining protocol — Inconel 625 in particular requires slow cutting speed (20–40 m/min), TiAlN-coated carbide tooling, and high-pressure coolant. **Q3: How long does turbine casing machining take?**

**Q4: What is matched-pair split-line machining and why does it matter?**

Matched-pair split-line machining means both casing halves — upper and lower — are fixtured together before the split-line face is cut. One machining pass across both halves guarantees the two flange faces are co-planar. Machining each half separately cannot achieve this: two independent machining operations accumulate separate positional errors, and when the halves are bolted together the faces may be offset or angled. That misalignment breaks the gasket or metal-to-metal seal and allows leakage across the split-line joint under pressure.

**Q5: What file formats do you accept for turbine casing quotes?**

STEP (.stp / .step) is required for 3D geometry. Include a 2D drawing specifying bore diameter and tolerance, bore concentricity, split-line flatness, split-line surface finish Ra, bolt circle diameter and position tolerance, port diameters, material grade, and any heat treatment or surface finish requirements. For large-bore casings, confirm bore diameter in the enquiry so the correct machine is assigned before quoting.

**Q6: What drives the cost of turbine casing machining?**

Material is the largest variable — Inconel 625 raw material costs significantly more than 316L stainless, and machines at one-fifth the cutting speed, so both material and cycle time are higher. Bore size drives machine selection — large-bore casings require horizontal boring mills with longer setups. Split-line flatness verification on assembled halves adds CMM time. And surface finish at Ra 1.6 µm on the split-line face requires a finishing pass separate from the roughing operation. Provide bore diameter, material, split-line specification, and port count in the quote enquiry for an accurate number.

**Q7: How does Weldo CNC compare to a standard machine shop for turbine casing work?**

A standard job shop will machine each casing half independently and measure flatness on each half separately. That approach cannot guarantee split-line co-planarity across the assembled joint — it guarantees flatness on each half in isolation, which is a different measurement. Weldo CNC machines both halves together as a matched pair and measures flatness on the assembled casing. The CMM report shows actual flatness deviation across the assembled split line, against the drawing tolerance. That is the measurement that matters for seal performance.
