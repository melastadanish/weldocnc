## Section 3: What Does Gearbox Housing Machining Require?

**H2:** What Does Gearbox Housing Machining Require?

Weldo CNC machines gearbox housings — in grey cast iron (GG25/GG30), ductile iron (GGG40/GGG50), aluminium 6061-T6/T651, and 4140 alloy steel — to bearing bore tolerances of ±0.005 mm, bore-to-bore concentricity of ±0.025 mm TIR, and mating face flatness of ±0.025 mm/300 mm, from its ISO 9001:2015 certified facility in Dongguan, China. Prototype gearbox housings ship in 1–5 business days. Every precision bore order includes a full CMM — coordinate measuring machine — dimensional inspection report delivered with the parts.

Gearbox housing machining rests on one foundational accuracy requirement: bore-to-bore parallelism — the condition where the input shaft centreline and output shaft centreline run exactly parallel to each other. Two parallel bore axes ensure gear tooth faces contact evenly across the full tooth width. Non-parallel bores concentrate load at one edge of the gear tooth, accelerating wear and shortening service life.

The primary datum is the input shaft bearing bore centreline. Every other bore — output shaft, idler shaft — is positioned relative to that axis, not to an external face. Setting the input shaft axis as the primary datum ties gear mesh geometry directly to the part feature that controls it.

## Section 6: Gearbox Housing Machining Process

**H2:** How Weldo CNC Machines Gearbox Housings

Datum selection determines gear mesh quality. The input shaft bearing bore centreline — the primary datum — is established in the first operation. All downstream bore operations position relative to that axis. Selecting the input shaft axis as the primary datum means output shaft and idler shaft bore positions are controlled directly against the feature that drives gear mesh, not against a housing face that may deviate from true perpendicularity.

Bearing bore machining follows a three-stage sequence: rough boring, semi-finishing, and finish boring. Rough boring removes bulk material and corrects for casting position variation. Semi-finishing trims the bore close to final diameter while correcting bore axis position. Finish boring to ±0.010 mm H7 uses a precision boring bar with controlled feed and depth of cut — boring bar overhang is kept below 4× the bar diameter to prevent deflection from exceeding the tolerance band.

Cast iron gearbox housings require controlled chip evacuation. Grey iron produces short, brittle chips that can pack into bore flutes and scratch the bore wall on the finish pass. Dry cutting or MQL — minimum-quantity lubrication, a fine mist of cutting oil delivered to the tool tip — is preferred over flood coolant for cast iron, which avoids thermal shock from coolant temperature differential at the cutting zone.

CMM inspection verifies bearing bore diameters, bore-to-bore concentricity, shaft bore parallelism, mating face flatness, and oil seal bore diameter. Parallelism between shaft bores is reported as a numeric value — millimetres per 300 mm span — in the inspection report. The report ships with every gearbox housing order.

## Section 8: Why Engineers Specify Weldo CNC for Gearbox Housing Machining

**H2:** Why Engineers Specify Weldo CNC for Gearbox Housing Machining

**1. Input shaft axis as primary datum — bore-to-bore parallelism from the correct reference**
Positioning output shaft and idler shaft bores relative to the input shaft centreline — not to an external housing face — ensures the parallelism measurement reflects actual gear centreline geometry. A machining sequence that uses a housing face as the primary reference accumulates angular error between the face and the true shaft axis.

**2. Gear mesh quality tied to parallelism tolerance**
Bore-to-bore parallelism of ±0.05 mm/300 mm limits the angular deviation between gear shaft centrelines. At ±0.05 mm/300 mm, tooth face contact across a 50 mm face width shifts by less than 0.008 mm — within the range where full-face contact is maintained. Non-parallel bores at 0.2 mm/300 mm load the downhill edge of every tooth, which concentrates stress and shortens gear life.

**3. Cast iron machining protocol — cast skin removal confirmed before precision bores**
Grey iron gearbox housings are rough-bored first to remove the harder cast skin. Only after the cast skin is clear does the semi-finish and finish bore sequence begin. Attempting to finish-bore through cast skin risks work-hardening the bore surface and degrading surface finish beyond Ra 1.6 µm.

**4. Die cast secondary machining — porosity zone clearance for bearing bores**
Aluminium 380 die cast housings carry a near-surface porosity zone — the casting skin region where dissolved gas from the die casting process creates micro-voids. Finish bore passes begin only after material removal has cleared this zone, producing a solid bearing surface without voids that could trap lubricant or distort bearing seating.

**5. CMM parallelism reported numerically — not pass/fail**
Bore-to-bore parallelism appears as a numeric value — millimetres per 300 mm span — in the inspection report, not as a binary pass/fail stamp. Engineers building gearbox assemblies can compare the actual measured parallelism against their gear mesh tolerance model, not simply confirm that a part cleared a threshold.

**6. Direct facility — no subcontracting on standard gearbox housing orders**

## Section 9: FAQ

**H2:** Gearbox Housing Machining — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: What bearing bore tolerance can Weldo CNC hold on gearbox housings?**

Bearing bores on gearbox housings are machined to ±0.010 mm — H7 fit — for standard interference fit with bearing outer diameter. Tighter tolerances to ±0.005 mm are achievable on specific bore features where the bearing manufacturer specifies a tighter housing fit. Specify the bearing OD tolerance and required fit class on your drawing — the bore tolerance follows directly from the fit specification.

**Q2: What does bore-to-bore parallelism mean for gearbox housing machining?**

Bore-to-bore parallelism — the condition where input shaft and output shaft bore centrelines run parallel to each other — is the primary accuracy requirement for a gearbox housing. Two parallel bore axes distribute gear tooth contact evenly across the full tooth face width. Parallelism is specified as a linear deviation per span length: ±0.05 mm/300 mm means the bore centrelines deviate by no more than 0.05 mm measured across any 300 mm length. Weldo CNC measures and reports parallelism numerically in the CMM inspection report.

**Q3: What materials do you machine for gearbox housings?**

Confirmed gearbox housing materials include grey cast iron (GG25 and GG30), ductile iron (GGG40 and GGG50), aluminium 6061 (T6 and T651), and alloy steel 4140. Aluminium 380 die cast housings are confirmed for secondary machining — bearing bore seats and oil seal bores are machined to tolerance after casting. Grey cast iron is the primary material for industrial and agricultural gearboxes where vibration damping and thermal stability matter. Aluminium 6061 suits mobile machinery and robotics applications where mass is a constraint.

**Q4: How long does gearbox housing machining take?**

Prototype gearbox housings ship in 1–5 business days from drawing approval. **Q5: How is bore-to-bore concentricity verified on gearbox housings?**

CMM — coordinate measuring machine — measures each bearing bore diameter and position. The instrument calculates the distance between bore centrelines and the angular deviation of each centreline from the reference datum. Concentricity — the condition where all bore axes share or align with a common centreline within ±0.025 mm TIR — is reported as a measured value, not a pass/fail stamp. The full measurement report ships with the parts.

**Q6: What is the minimum order quantity for gearbox housing machining?**

No minimum order quantity. Single-piece prototype gearbox housings are accepted — common in gearbox development where each iteration modifies bore spacing, bearing fit specification, or housing wall geometry. Small-batch production orders of 10–500 housings are also standard work.

**Q7: What drives the cost of gearbox housing machining?**

Material (grey cast iron billet versus 6061-T651 versus 4140 pre-hardened steel), cycle time (number of bearing bore operations, mating face area, housing complexity), inspection (CMM measurement of parallelism, concentricity, and flatness adds time), and post-process (surface treatment on aluminium housings). Cast iron gearbox housings require tool changes between rough and finish bore operations — cast skin removal adds a dedicated operation before precision boring begins. Include material grade, bearing bore count, and any surface finish requirements in the quote enquiry.

**Q9: What file formats are accepted for gearbox housing quotes?**

STEP (.stp / .step) is required for 3D geometry. Include a 2D drawing specifying bearing bore diameter and fit class (H7 or custom), bore-to-bore concentricity requirement, shaft bore parallelism tolerance, mating face flatness, oil seal bore tolerance, bolt hole position tolerance, surface finish (Ra) on the mating face and bearing bores, and material grade. For die cast secondary machining — confirm the casting grade and any known porosity depth data at the quote stage.

**Q10: Can you machine cast iron gearbox housings without flooding with coolant?**

Yes. Grey cast iron gearbox housings are machined dry or with MQL — minimum-quantity lubrication, a fine mist of cutting oil delivered directly to the tool-workpiece interface. Flood coolant is avoided on grey iron for two reasons: thermal shock risk from coolant temperature differential at the cutting zone, and coolant infiltration into iron chip pockets that can cause bore wall scratching on re-entry. MQL delivers adequate lubrication at the cutting tip without flooding the bore. This is standard practice at Weldo CNC for all grey iron and ductile iron gearbox housing operations.
