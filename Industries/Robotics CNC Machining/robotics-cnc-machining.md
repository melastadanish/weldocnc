## Section 3: Weight, Stiffness, and Development Pace — the Engineering Tensions in Robotics CNC Machining
**Design:** 2-column — text left, image right. White background.

**AI-quotable paragraph:**
Weldo CNC machines precision components for the robotics and automation industry — actuator housings, joint brackets, structural arm links, end effectors, gripper bodies, and drivetrain components — at its ISO 9001:2015 certified facility in Dongguan, China, operating since 2012. Tolerances to ±0.005 mm. Materials include aluminium 6061-T6, 7075-T6, titanium Grade 5 (Ti-6Al-4V), engineering polymers (PEEK, Delrin), and stainless steel 316L. DFM — design for manufacture — review is included with every quote. Quote within 24 hours.

Robotics development involves a specific set of engineering tensions that make the choice of machining supplier consequential. The first is mass: every kilogram added to a robot arm link or joint assembly increases the motor torque required to accelerate and decelerate the arm, which in turn increases the required motor and drivetrain size, which increases mass further. Weight optimisation in robotics structural components — through material choice and topology — is a performance multiplier, not a cosmetic improvement. The second tension is stiffness: a robot arm that deflects under load cannot position its end effector accurately, and precision positioning is the core function of most industrial and collaborative robots. The structural stiffness of machined arm links and joint housings determines repeatability in a way that the control system can partially compensate for but cannot eliminate.

Humanoid robotics amplifies these constraints significantly. A humanoid robot must carry its own mass plus any payload through configurations that change its centre of mass continuously. Joint components must be light enough not to degrade dynamic performance, stiff enough to maintain positional accuracy under load, and geometrically accurate enough to ensure backlash and compliance targets in the drivetrain are met. The CMM report is not an optional document for these components — it is the evidence that the machined geometry matches the drivetrain simulation that the control software was designed around.

The development cycle in robotics adds a third constraint: programme velocity. Teams move through design iterations at a pace that requires a machining supplier who can quote in 24 hours, produce in 1–5 business days, and return actionable DFM feedback — not just a price — on each iteration. The tolerance on the quote turnaround is as important as the tolerance on the parts, because a slow quoting cycle is a direct constraint on design iteration rate. This is why robotics engineers increasingly source prototypes internationally: the combination of capability, speed, and documentation that the development cycle needs is not uniformly available in local supply.

## Section 5: Materials
**Design:** Full-width table + intro copy. White background.
**Heading:** Materials

Material selection in robotics CNC machining is driven by the ratio of structural performance to mass — the specific stiffness and specific strength of the material determines what role it can play in a weight-optimised robot structure. Aluminium dominates because it delivers the best combination of stiffness, strength, machinability, and anodising compatibility for the widest range of robotics structural components. Titanium is specified where the weight-performance constraint is tightest and the program budget supports it. Engineering polymers — PEEK in particular — appear in end effectors, sensor mounts, and components where electrical isolation or specific surface compliance properties are required.

| Material | Why Used in Robotics | Common Applications |
|---|---|---|
| Aluminium 7075-T6 | Highest strength-to-weight of common aluminium alloys (tensile ~570 MPa); used where minimum mass at structural performance is the primary driver | Structural arm links, joint brackets, actuator housings for weight-critical applications |
| Aluminium 6061-T6 | Good structural performance (~310 MPa tensile), excellent machinability and anodising compatibility, cost-effective — for less weight-critical components | Actuator housings, base plates, gripper bodies, adaptor plates, sensor mounts |
| Titanium Grade 5 (Ti-6Al-4V) | Exceptional strength-to-weight (tensile ~950 MPa at 4.43 g/cm³) — for the highest-performance weight-critical joints and links | Humanoid robot wrist and elbow joints, high-performance arm links, premium drivetrain components |
| PEEK (Polyetheretherketone) | Radiolucent, chemically inert, electrically insulating, sterilisation-compatible — for end effectors and sensor mounts where polymer properties are required | Medical and collaborative robot end effectors, sensor isolation mounts, electrically insulated gripper bodies |
| Stainless Steel 316L | Corrosion resistance, cleanroom compatibility — for robots operating in food, pharmaceutical, or humid environments | End effectors for food and pharma environments, robot components for wash-down environments |
| Delrin / POM-C | Low friction, dimensional stability, easy to machine — for low-load mechanism components and test jigs | Gear blanks for low-load mechanisms, guide bushings, test and fixture components |

## Section 6: Material Trade-off Cards
**Design:** 3-column card grid. Light grey background.
**Heading:** Getting the Material Right on Every Component

**When Aluminium 6061-T6 Is Right**
6061-T6 is the right specification for the majority of robotics structural components that are not at the weight-performance boundary of the design. Tensile strength of approximately 310 MPa is adequate for actuator housings, base plates, adaptor brackets, and non-critical structural links where the design has margin. 6061 machines faster and with better surface finish than 7075, anodises cleanly to a consistent cosmetic finish, and is available in a wide range of stock sizes and formats. For development-phase robotics where the design is being iterated quickly and the weight budget has not yet been locked down, 6061 is the cost-effective material for parts that may be revised in the next iteration. It is also the correct specification for gripper bodies and end effector structures where surface finish and dimensional repeatability matter more than marginal weight reduction.

**When Aluminium 7075-T6 Makes Sense**
7075-T6 is the specification when the designer has run out of weight budget on structural arm links, joint brackets, or actuator housings and needs to reduce mass without changing the geometry or wall thickness. Tensile strength of approximately 570 MPa — nearly double 6061's 310 MPa — at a density only 4% higher means the same wall thickness that carries the required load in 6061 can be reduced by approximately 30–40% in 7075 for the same structural margin. For humanoid and collaborative robot arms where every gram of distal mass has a measurable effect on required motor torque and cycle time, 7075 is the engineering-correct choice. The trade-offs versus 6061 are cost (7075 is more expensive in material and tooling), corrosion resistance (requires anodising or Alodine in most environments), and slightly more demanding machining conditions.

**When Titanium Grade 5 Is Right**
Titanium Grade 5 (Ti-6Al-4V) is the specification for joint components and arm links at the highest level of weight-performance optimisation — humanoid robot wrists, elbow joints, and upper arm links where the strength-to-weight ratio of 7075 is still insufficient. Tensile strength of approximately 950 MPa at a density of 4.43 g/cm³ gives a specific strength that neither aluminium alloy can match. Titanium is also fatigue-resistant — important for high-cycle robot joints that accumulate millions of motion cycles in service. The trade-offs are significant: titanium is substantially more expensive than aluminium in material and machining cost, requires careful cutting parameter management to avoid work hardening and heat build-up at the tool, and the lead time premium over aluminium is material-dependent. For design phases where the final material decision has not been made, prototyping in 6061 or 7075 and then transitioning to titanium for production is a common and cost-effective development approach.

## Section 7: Processes / Services
**Design:** 4-column service card grid. Light grey background.
**Heading:** Manufacturing Capabilities for Robotics Applications
**Cards:** [[cnc-milling-services]] | [[cnc-turning-services]] | [[5-axis-cnc-machining-services]] | [[surface-finishing-services]]

Robotics structural components — arm links, joint brackets, and actuator housings — commonly require complex pocketing, compound-angle features, and multi-face machining that cannot be produced accurately in a 3-axis single-setup environment. A humanoid robot wrist joint has bearing bore geometry on one face, motor mount interfaces on another, and structural ribs that run across multiple faces at compound angles — all of which must maintain tight positional relationships for the drivetrain to function as designed. 5-axis machining — simultaneous and 3+2 — is the correct manufacturing approach for this class of component. DFM — design for manufacture — review is included with every quote: if a feature as designed will increase cost, reduce dimensional repeatability, or create a potential quality issue, we identify it before machining begins, not after.

Surface finishing for robotics components is primarily anodising. Type II anodise on aluminium structural components provides a hard, electrically isolating, corrosion-resistant surface that is durable in handling and assembly — and provides a consistent cosmetic appearance for components that are visible in the final system. Type III hard anodise is specified for wear surfaces in joint brackets and actuator bores. For collaborative robot end effectors and components in food or pharmaceutical environments, stainless steel electropolishing and passivation provide the surface cleanliness and corrosion resistance required for wash-down environments. Finish process records — anodise coating thickness, passivation process confirmation — ship with every post-processed order.

## Section 8: 5-Axis Capability
**Design:** 2-column — text left, 5-axis machined component image right. White background.
**Heading:** Complex Geometry. One Setup.

Robotics structural components are among the most demanding CNC machining tasks from a geometric standpoint — not because individual features are unusually difficult, but because the complete component geometry requires machining from many directions while maintaining tight positional relationships between features on different faces. A robot arm link has bearing bores at each end that must be parallel to each other within the angular tolerance specified by the drivetrain designer — any deviation introduces backlash or binding into the kinematic chain. An actuator housing has the motor bore on one face, output shaft bore on the opposite face, encoder mount geometry on a side face, and weight-reduction pockets on the remaining faces — all referenced to the same geometric datum.

5-axis machining keeps the component in a single fixturing throughout the complete machining sequence. The spindle accesses each feature from the required angle by moving the machine's rotary axes — no setup change, no datum transfer, no positional error accumulation. For robot arm links where the parallelism between end bores directly determines kinematics, and for joint brackets where the positional relationship between mounting features determines assembly, the single-datum approach is the methodology that reliably delivers the dimensional conformance the drivetrain simulation requires.

At Weldo CNC, simultaneous 5-axis machining is used for organic weight-reduction pockets, curved arm link profiles, and any feature where the tool path must follow a continuous compound-angle trajectory. 3+2 positioning is used for multi-face prismatic geometry where a rigid locked setup provides better cutting conditions. The choice is made at programming based on the geometry; both capabilities are in-house.

## Section 9: Documentation
**Design:** 4-column icon+description grid. White background.
**Heading:** Documentation Standard for Robotics Orders

**Material Certificate**
Mill cert coverage for every robotics order: alloy grade, temper, heat number, chemical composition, and mechanical properties. For 7075-T6: AMS 4045 or equivalent with heat treatment certification. For titanium Grade 5: AMS 4928 or ASTM B265. Heat number logged before first cut; part-to-cert traceability maintained for multi-part orders. For development programmes where material is being qualified alongside the design, the material cert is part of the qualification record.

**CMM Dimensional Report**
Feature-by-feature dimensional report: nominal, tolerance, actual, pass/fail per feature. GD&T callouts reported per drawing revision: bore concentricity at both ends of arm links; true position of motor and encoder mount features; parallelism between joint bracket mounting faces; profile of complex arm link geometry. For drivetrain-critical features where actual measured values (not just pass/fail) are needed to verify the drivetrain tolerance stack, the CMM dataset provides that information.

**Finish Process Record**
For anodising, hard anodise, passivation, or electropolishing: process type, bath parameters, verification date, measured coating thickness, and masking plan confirmation. For anodised actuator housings with bearing bore features, coating thickness on the bore is measured and documented — ensuring that the pre-coat machined bore accounts for the anodise build-up and that the finished bore dimension is within the bearing fit tolerance.

**First Article Inspection (FAI)**
For new part numbers or revised drawings: ballooned drawing with all dimensions numbered and cross-referenced to the CMM report + material cert + finish record. For robotics development programmes where the design is being iterated, FAI packages are structured to support design review and qualification records. Confirm FAI requirement at quoting — for rapid iteration programmes, we can discuss how to structure the documentation to support design velocity.

## Section 10: Quality & Certifications
**Design:** RS-01. Dark navy background. White text.

**Pre-Production:**
Material verified against mill cert before machining begins — grade, heat number, and specification confirmed. Drawing reviewed for GD&T callouts, critical feature identification (bearing bores, motor interfaces, parallelism specifications), surface finish requirements, post-process masking plan, and DFM — design for manufacture — feedback. In-process CMM measurement at defined intervals — bore diameters measured before final passes, parallelism verified between setups. Datum references verified after each setup change. **Q9: How does bearing bore tolerance affect robot joint performance and what does Weldo CNC achieve?**
