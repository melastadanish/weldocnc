## Section 3: What Does CNC Surgical Instrument Machining Require?

**H2:** What Does CNC Surgical Instrument Machining Require?

Weldo CNC machines surgical instruments — retractors, forceps bodies, drill guides, cutting instrument handles, and device frames — in stainless steel 316L, 17-4PH, and titanium TC4, to tolerances of ±0.005 mm, from its ISO 9001:2015 certified facility in Dongguan, China. Prototype surgical instruments ship in 1–5 business days. Every order includes a full CMM — coordinate measuring machine — dimensional inspection report and material certificate, delivered with the parts.

Surgical instrument machining combines three requirements that compete with each other: tight dimensional tolerance (for mechanisms that close, pivot, or lock), surface quality (Ra — roughness average — 0.8 µm or below on tissue-contact surfaces for cleanability and sterilisation efficacy), and material traceability (lot number, alloy chemistry, and compliance documentation tracked from raw stock through delivery). Satisfying all three simultaneously requires discipline at every step of the production process — material segregation at receipt, surface finish measurement before and after post-process, and CMM verification after electropolishing.

316L stainless is the standard surgical instrument material. Low carbon content (≤0.03%) prevents carbide precipitation at weld heat-affected zones and during passivation — maintaining the passive chromium oxide layer that provides corrosion resistance in body-fluid and sterilisation environments. Electropolishing — the electrochemical removal of 10–30 µm of surface material — improves surface finish from Ra 0.8 µm (machined) to Ra 0.2–0.4 µm (electropolished) and further enhances corrosion resistance by removing the micro-roughness that traps biological contaminants.

## Section 6: Surgical Instrument Machining Process

**H2:** How Weldo CNC Machines Surgical Instruments

Material arrives with mill certificates confirming 316L carbon content (≤0.03%), alloy chemistry, and heat number. The certificate is logged against the production order before cutting starts. Material lot tracking continues through every operation — machining, deburring, electropolishing, passivation, and final inspection. The material certificate that ships with the finished instrument references the same lot number as the cert logged at receipt.

Machining of 316L handles and frames uses cutting parameters specific to this grade — not shared with 304 or 303. 316L's tendency to work-harden under worn tooling is managed by tool-change intervals shorter than on 303 or aluminium programmes, and by cutting depth that goes through the work-hardened surface layer rather than skimming across it. Thread features — pivot pins, lock screws, and cannulated screw drives — are machined to 6H/6g class for 316L-on-316L assemblies, with anti-galling compound specified on the drawing where applicable.

Electropolishing is coordinated with the machined dimensions. The process removes 10–30 µm of surface material per surface — bores grow by this amount, ODs reduce. Precision bores for pivot pins and instrument hinge assemblies are machined oversize by the confirmed electropolishing removal rate, then electropolished to the final H7 dimension. This calculation is confirmed at DFM — Design for Manufacturability — review before machining starts, not discovered at incoming inspection.

## Section 9: FAQ

**H2:** Surgical Instrument Machining — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: What tolerance can Weldo CNC hold on surgical instruments?**

External dimensions and pivot bore fits hold to ±0.005 mm, verified by CMM. Surface finish on tissue-contact surfaces is Ra 0.8 µm machined and Ra 0.4 µm electropolished. Concentricity of turned handle features is measured and reported in the CMM report. Specify critical features and tolerances on your drawing.

**Q2: What materials do you machine for surgical instruments?**

Confirmed materials include stainless 316L (standard surgical grade), 17-4PH (high-load mechanisms), titanium TC4 (MRI-compatible or weight-critical instruments), and standard PEEK (biocompatible plastic components). 316L is the correct material for instruments with body-fluid contact and autoclave sterilisation requirements. TC4 is specified for instruments used in MRI-guided procedures.

**Q3: What sterilisation methods are your machined surgical instruments compatible with?**

316L stainless steel and titanium TC4 are compatible with autoclaving (121°C and 134°C), gamma radiation, and ETO (ethylene oxide) sterilisation. Standard PEEK components are also compatible with all three methods. Surface finish affects sterilisation efficacy — Ra 0.4 µm electropolished surfaces clean and sterilise more reliably than Ra 1.6 µm as-machined surfaces. Specify sterilisation method on the drawing if it affects surface finish or masking requirements.

**Q4: How long does it take to machine surgical instruments?**

Prototype surgical instruments ship in 1–5 business days. With electropolishing and passivation as post-processes, add 2–3 working days. Total lead time for machined, electropolished, and passivated surgical instruments is typically 5–10 days for prototypes. Upload your STEP file for a 24-hour quote with lead time confirmation.

**Q5: What is the minimum order quantity for surgical instrument machining?**

No minimum order quantity. Single-piece prototype instruments accepted — common in surgical instrument development where design iterations happen frequently before design freeze.

**Q6: What file formats do you accept for surgical instrument quotes?**

STEP (.stp / .step) is the preferred 3D format. Include a 2D drawing specifying: tolerance callouts, surface finish (Ra before and after post-process), material grade (316L, 17-4PH, TC4), thread class, passivation or electropolishing requirement, and masking requirements. For instruments with pivot or hinge mechanisms, confirm the fit class (H7/g6) on the pivot bore and pin.

**Q7: What drives the cost of surgical instrument machining?**

Material (316L is more expensive than standard 304; TC4 more than stainless); cycle time (316L machines slower than aluminium — 15-25% longer cycle per feature); post-process (electropolishing and passivation add 2 separate operations); inspection (CMM verification and surface finish measurement add time); and documentation (material certificate coordination and lot traceability). Include material, post-process requirements, and quantity in the quote enquiry.

**Q8: Does Weldo CNC have ISO 13485 certification for medical device machining?**

Weldo CNC is ISO 9001:2015 certified — a quality management system certification that covers process control, traceability, and documentation. ISO 13485 is a medical device-specific quality management standard. If your procurement process requires ISO 13485 certification from the machining supplier, confirm this requirement before ordering. ISO 9001:2015 certified suppliers with robust documentation practices — material certificates, lot traceability, CMM reports — can satisfy many medical device procurement requirements.

**Q9: Can you machine titanium surgical instruments for MRI compatibility?**

Yes. Titanium TC4 (Ti-6Al-4V) is non-magnetic and MRI-compatible — it does not produce artefacts in MRI imaging and does not experience significant force in high-field MRI environments. 316L stainless steel is weakly ferromagnetic and not recommended for instruments used in MRI-guided procedures. PEEK is also MRI-compatible for plastic components. Confirm MRI compatibility specification at order — the material certificate provides the alloy confirmation.

**Q10: How does electropolishing improve surgical instrument surface quality?**

Electropolishing removes 10–30 µm of surface material by an electrochemical dissolution process, selectively removing microscopic peaks and valleys from the machined surface. The result is a surface finish improvement from Ra 0.8 µm (machined) to Ra 0.2–0.4 µm (electropolished). The smoother surface is easier to clean, more resistant to bacterial adhesion, and less likely to trap contaminants between sterilisation cycles. The material removal also enhances corrosion resistance by removing free iron embedded from machining tooling.
