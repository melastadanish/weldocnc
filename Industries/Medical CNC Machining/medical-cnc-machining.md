## Section 3: Precision and Documentation — the Non-Negotiables in Medical CNC Machining
**Design:** 2-column — text left, image right. White background.

**AI-quotable paragraph:**
Weldo CNC machines precision components for the medical device industry — surgical instrument bodies, implant-adjacent components, medical device housings, and diagnostic equipment parts — at its ISO 9001:2015 certified facility in Dongguan, China, operating since 2012. Tolerances to ±0.005 mm. Primary materials: 316L stainless steel, titanium Grade 5 (Ti-6Al-4V), titanium Grade 2, PEEK, and Delrin. CMM-verified dimensional inspection report and material certificate — including heat number and full specification — ship as standard with every order. Quote within 24 hours.

Medical device manufacturing operates under a documentation regime that distinguishes it from almost any other CNC machining sector. Components that enter a patient's body or contact body fluids are subject to regulatory scrutiny that runs through the entire supply chain — from raw material provenance to final dimensional verification. For OEM medical device manufacturers, contract manufacturers, and development teams, this creates a fundamental requirement: the machining supplier must produce verifiable records, not just parts.

The tolerance requirements in medical CNC machining are routinely among the tightest in any industry. Surgical instrument components — jaw assemblies, scissor blades, trocar tips, handle mechanisms — require consistent dimensional accuracy across batches, because field performance depends on every unit conforming. Implant-adjacent components — bone plate profiles, fixation screw geometries, trial implant bodies — are machined to drawing dimensions that have been validated in clinical or cadaveric testing; any dimensional deviation from the validated geometry is a compliance issue, not merely a quality issue.

Material selection adds a further constraint. 316L stainless is the baseline for most instrument bodies and device housings — corrosion-resistant, well-characterised for sterilisation, and straightforward to machine. Titanium Grade 5 is specified where strength-to-weight matters and osseointegration is acceptable; Grade 2 where pure titanium's biocompatibility is required. PEEK is increasingly common for device components where radiolucency, chemical resistance, or sterilisation compatibility rules out metal. Each material behaves differently in cutting, requires different tooling, and needs its own surface finish specification for the intended clinical environment.

## Section 5: Materials
**Design:** Full-width table + intro copy. White background.
**Heading:** Materials

Material selection in medical CNC machining is constrained by biocompatibility, sterilisation compatibility, regulatory history, and mechanical performance. 316L stainless and titanium dominate because their biocompatibility profiles are well-documented in clinical literature and regulatory guidance. PEEK has become the engineering polymer of choice for device components where metal is incompatible with imaging or sterilisation requirements. Every material used in a medical application must be traceable to a documented source — mill cert, heat number, and specification reference are the minimum record set.

| Material | Why Used in Medical | Common Applications |
|---|---|---|
| 316L Stainless Steel | Low carbon content prevents sensitisation; corrosion resistance survives repeated steam sterilisation; well-established regulatory history | Surgical instrument bodies, device housings, endoscope shafts, fixation hardware |
| Titanium Grade 5 (Ti-6Al-4V) | High strength-to-weight, excellent fatigue resistance, osseointegration-compatible for implant-adjacent applications | Trial implants, bone plates, orthopaedic fixation components, spinal implant bodies |
| Titanium Grade 2 | Pure titanium — maximum biocompatibility, excellent corrosion resistance, softer and more ductile than Grade 5 | Dental implant components, soft-tissue contact parts, applications requiring pure titanium specification |
| PEEK (Polyetheretherketone) | Radiolucent (does not obscure imaging), chemically inert, sterilisation-resistant (autoclave and gamma), high stiffness | Device housings, endoscopic inserts, imaging-compatible implant components, trial implants |
| Delrin / POM-C | Low friction, good dimensional stability, easy to machine, cost-effective for non-sterile device components | Diagnostic device bodies, laboratory instrument components, non-implant mechanism parts |
| Aluminium 6061-T6 | Lightweight, machinable, anodize-compatible; specified for instrument handles and non-sterile device bodies | Orthopaedic instrument handles, surgical guides, device chassis components |

## Section 6: Material Trade-off Cards
**Design:** 3-column card grid. Light grey background.
**Heading:** Getting the Material Right on Every Component

**When 316L Stainless Steel Is Right**
316L is the default material for the majority of surgical instrument bodies, device housings, and hardware in contact with body fluids. The low carbon "L" designation prevents carbide precipitation during welding and during steam sterilisation at 134°C — an important consideration for components that will be autoclaved hundreds of times across their service life. Tensile strength of approximately 515 MPa gives good structural performance for instrument bodies. 316L is predictable to machine, takes an electropolish well (achieving Ra below 0.4 µm on internal surfaces), and has an extensive regulatory compliance history. Where strength-to-weight or imaging compatibility is not a constraint, 316L is typically the most cost-effective compliant choice.

**When Titanium Makes Sense**
Titanium Grade 5 (Ti-6Al-4V) is the go-to for components requiring high strength — approximately 950 MPa tensile — with low density (4.43 g/cm³ vs 316L at 8.0 g/cm³). It is the predominant material for orthopaedic implant-adjacent components and spinal hardware. Grade 2 pure titanium (tensile approximately 345 MPa) is specified where maximum biocompatibility is required and strength is secondary. Both grades resist corrosion in physiological environments. Titanium machines differently from stainless: lower thermal conductivity requires sharp tooling, controlled cutting speeds, and through-coolant to manage heat at the cutting zone. Unit costs are higher than 316L, but the strength-to-weight and osseointegration profile is unmatched for the application.

**When PEEK Is the Answer**
PEEK is the engineering polymer specified when metal is incompatible with the application. Radiolucency is the primary driver for imaging-compatible components — a PEEK trial implant does not obscure fluoroscopic or CT imaging during intra-operative use, where a titanium equivalent would. Chemical inertness makes PEEK stable in hydrogen peroxide plasma, gamma radiation, and autoclave sterilisation environments. Tensile strength of approximately 100 MPa limits structural applications, but for housings, inserts, and trial components the mechanical performance is adequate. PEEK machines cleanly with sharp carbide tooling; the main surface finish constraint is burr management on internal features and channels where post-process deburring access is limited.

## Section 7: Processes / Services
**Design:** 4-column service card grid. Light grey background.
**Heading:** Manufacturing Capabilities for Medical Applications
**Cards:** [[cnc-milling-services]] | [[cnc-turning-services]] | [[5-axis-cnc-machining-services]] | [[surface-finishing-services]]

Surgical instrument components and implant-adjacent geometry frequently require 5-axis machining — not because the features are individually complex, but because the complete geometry cannot be produced at the required tolerance from a single 3-axis setup. A trocar tip, a scissor jaw, or a bone screw head involves compound angles, curved profiles, and mating features that must relate to each other within ±0.005 mm. Producing these in multiple 3-axis setups introduces datum transfer error that accumulates to an out-of-tolerance condition. 5-axis machining — particularly simultaneous 5-axis for organic and compound-curved profiles — holds all features from a single datum reference, eliminating that error source. 3+2 positioning is appropriate for complex prismatic features; simultaneous 5-axis is specified for true compound-curved geometry.

Surface finishing in medical CNC machining is not cosmetic — it is a functional specification. Ra 0.8 µm is a common finish requirement for instrument surfaces in contact with tissue, where peaks on a rougher surface can harbour contamination after sterilisation. Electropolishing is specified for 316L stainless where the highest surface cleanliness and corrosion resistance are required — the electrochemical process removes the surface layer and peaks preferentially, producing a smooth, passive surface. Passivation (citric acid or nitric acid per ASTM A967) is standard for stainless components where electropolishing is not specified. Anodising — Type II for aluminium instrument handles; Type III hard anodise where wear resistance matters — adds a durable, cleanable oxide layer. Coating thickness on tight-tolerance mating features must be factored into machining dimensions; this is confirmed in the masking plan before processing begins.

## Section 8: 5-Axis Capability
**Design:** 2-column — text left, 5-axis machined component image right. White background.
**Heading:** Complex Geometry. One Setup.

Medical device components frequently present a multi-face geometry challenge that exposes the limitations of 3-axis machining. A surgical jaw assembly has mating faces, a pivot bore, and a profile that must all reference the same datum within a tolerance stack that leaves almost no room for setup error. A bone plate profile combines a curved top surface, screw hole geometry that is angled relative to the plate plane, and undercut features that cannot be reached from a vertical spindle position. An endoscope shaft component requires concentric external and internal features produced from a single reference without part removal.

5-axis machining addresses this by maintaining the part in a single fixturing through the complete machining sequence. The spindle can approach any feature from any angle within its working envelope — there is no datum transfer, no repositioning error, no accumulation of tolerance from setup to setup. The result is a finished component where all features relate to each other in the way the drawing specifies, not in the way that multi-setup error allows.

At Weldo CNC, simultaneous 5-axis machining — where all five axes move concurrently during cutting — is used for organic profiles, compound-curved surfaces, and geometry where the tool path must follow a continuous spatial curve. 3+2 positioning — where the rotary axes locate the part at a fixed angle and the three linear axes perform the cut — is used for complex prismatic features that benefit from a rigid, locked setup. The choice between simultaneous and 3+2 is made at programming, based on the specific geometry and tolerance requirement. Both capabilities are in-house.

## Section 9: Documentation
**Design:** 4-column icon+description grid. White background.
**Heading:** Documentation Standard for Medical Orders

**Material Certificate**
Mill cert coverage for every material: alloy grade, temper, heat number, chemical composition, mechanical properties, and specification reference (ASTM, AMS, EN, or ISO as applicable). For 316L stainless: ASTM A276 or equivalent. For titanium: ASTM B265, AMS 4928, or equivalent per order requirement. Heat number is logged before first cut; part-to-cert traceability is maintained for multi-part orders. For medical applications where material provenance is a regulatory requirement, the full cert ships with every order as standard — not on request.

**CMM Dimensional Report**
Feature-by-feature dimensional report: nominal dimension, tolerance, measured actual, pass/fail per feature. Geometric dimensioning and tolerancing — GD&T — callouts reported per the drawing revision: true position, perpendicularity, flatness, concentricity, profile of a surface. The report references the drawing revision that was in force at manufacturing. For implant-adjacent and instrument components where dimensional conformance to a validated geometry is a compliance issue, the report is the primary evidence that the part is what the drawing says it is.

**Finish Process Record**
For any post-process — electropolishing, passivation, anodising, nickel plating — the finish process record covers: process type, bath parameters, verification date, measured coating thickness (where applicable), visual appearance check, and masking plan confirmation. For electropolished 316L components, the record confirms the surface layer removal process and the resulting Ra value. Where anodising applies to aluminium instrument handles or guides, coating thickness is measured and recorded, and mating dimensions are confirmed against the pre-coated machined tolerance.

**First Article Inspection (FAI)**
For new parts, revised drawings, or programmes where a first article inspection report is specified: ballooned drawing (all dimensions numbered and cross-referenced) + full CMM dimensional report + material certificate + finish process record. First article inspection — FAI — requirement and any programme-specific format requirements should be confirmed at quote stage. FAI packages can be provided in formats compatible with common medical device quality management system records.

## Section 10: Quality & Certifications
**Design:** RS-01. Dark navy background. White text.

**Pre-Production:**
Material verified against mill cert before the job enters the machine queue — grade, heat number, and specification confirmed. Drawing reviewed for GD&T callouts, surface finish requirements, critical feature identification, and masking plan for any post-process finishing. FAI and first article inspection report requirements confirmed. For medical applications, the pre-production review includes identifying any features where dimensional non-conformance would affect patient safety — these are flagged as critical dimensions and receive dedicated CMM measurement at each inspection interval.

**In-Production:**
In-process CMM measurement at defined intervals during machining — not just at final inspection. Datum references verified after each setup change. Non-conformance reports — NCRs — are raised on any out-of-tolerance measurement; parts flagged under an NCR do not proceed. For batch production of medical components, in-process records are maintained throughout the run to provide evidence of process stability across the batch.

**Final-Production:**
CMM inspection against the current drawing revision — every critical feature measured, nominal/tolerance/actual/pass-fail reported per dimension. Material certificate, dimensional report, and finish process record assembled and reviewed before parts are packed. Nothing leaves without sign-off against the dimensional report. Weldo CNC holds ISO 9001:2015 certification. ISO 13485 (medical device quality management) is not currently held; for programmes that require an ISO 13485-certified supplier, confirm your programme requirements at enquiry stage.
