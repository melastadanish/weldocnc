## Section 3: What Does CNC Implant Machining Require?

**H2:** What Does CNC Implant Machining Require?

Weldo CNC machines implant-adjacent components — trial implants, patient-specific surgical guides, structural implant bodies, and bone fixation plates — in titanium TC4 (Ti-6Al-4V Grade 5), titanium TA2 (Grade 2), and standard unfilled PEEK, to tolerances of ±0.005 mm, from its ISO 9001:2015 certified facility in Dongguan, China. Prototype implant components ship in 1–5 business days. Every order includes a full CMM — coordinate measuring machine — dimensional inspection report and material certificate with lot number.

Implant machining differs from general precision machining in one fundamental way: material grade and traceability are regulatory requirements, not quality preferences. Standard unfilled PEEK must be confirmed as ISO 10993 biocompatible — PEEK GF30 or CF30 are not. Titanium must be confirmed as the correct grade (TC4 or TA2) with UNS designation from the mill certificate, not from a verbal assurance. A grade discrepancy in an implant supply chain is a regulatory non-conformance regardless of how well the part is machined. Weldo CNC tracks lot number from material receipt through delivery for all implant-designated orders.

Titanium TC4 is the standard structural implant material. Its osseointegration behaviour — the ability of bone tissue to grow on and bond to the titanium surface — depends in part on surface finish and surface chemistry. Machined surfaces at Ra 0.8–1.6 µm support osseointegration for bone-contact implants. Bead blasting to Ra 2–4 µm increases surface area and is specified for surfaces where enhanced tissue adhesion is desired. Plasma spray or hydroxyapatite coating — not offered by Weldo CNC — is a further surface modification that accelerates osseointegration.

## Section 6: Implant Machining Process

**H2:** How Weldo CNC Machines Implant Components

Material receipt starts the traceability record. Titanium bar or billet arrives with a mill certificate — alloy designation (Ti-6Al-4V or Grade 5), UNS number (R56400), heat number, chemical composition, and mechanical properties. The certificate is logged against the production order. For PEEK implant orders, the lot certificate includes the PEEK grade designation and ISO 10993 biocompatibility reference. Both certificates travel with the finished parts.

Titanium TC4 implant machining uses sharp, positive-rake carbide tooling with flood coolant. TC4 work-hardens under worn tooling — tool changes happen at conservative intervals for implant-designated runs. First-article inspection is run on the first part off each new tool set before the batch continues. The work-hardened surface condition would reduce fatigue strength of the implant if undetected — surface condition is assessed by Ra measurement after each tool change, not assumed from a fixed cutting parameter set.

Patient-specific implant components — custom-fit bone plates, cutting guides, alignment jigs — require CMM surface scanning against the 3D model rather than point-measurement against a 2D drawing. The 3D model is the specification. Weldo CNC's CMM capability includes surface scanning for patient-specific geometry, with the scan overlay against the CAD nominal provided as part of the inspection report.

## Section 8: Why Engineers Specify Weldo CNC for Implant Machining

**H2:** Why Engineers Specify Weldo CNC for Implant Machining

**1. Material grade verification before machining — not after**
TC4 alloy designation, PEEK biocompatible grade, and 316L carbon content are confirmed from mill certificates before cutting starts. PEEK GF30 and CF30 are stored and tracked separately from biocompatible-grade PEEK — no cross-contamination of grade designations in the production queue.

**2. Lot traceability from receipt to shipment**
Every implant-designated order carries a lot number from material receipt through each production operation to final inspection. The material certificate that ships with the implant references the same lot number checked at receipt. Nothing in the traceability chain is reconstructed after the fact.

**3. TC4 tool life managed to prevent work hardening**
Titanium work-hardens under worn tooling. For implant-designated TC4 runs, insert change intervals are conservative and first-article inspection runs after each new tool set. Ra measurement after each tool change confirms cutting remains in the clean-shear regime — not the work-hardening regime.

**4. CMM surface scanning for patient-specific geometry**
Custom implant profiles are verified by CMM surface scan against the 3D model, not by point measurement against a 2D drawing. The scan overlay and deviation map are included in the inspection report.

**5. ISO 9001:2015 certified — documentation that procurement requires**
Material certificate, CMM dimensional report (or surface scan), and lot traceability record ship together. ISO 9001:2015 certification with a 97.48% annual pass rate covers the production system. ## Section 9: FAQ

**H2:** Implant Machining — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: What tolerance can Weldo CNC hold on implant components?**

External dimensions and bore diameters hold to ±0.005 mm, verified by CMM. Patient-specific profiles are verified by CMM surface scan against the 3D CAD model. Surface finish is Ra 0.8 µm standard on bone-contact surfaces; Ra 0.4 µm on articulation surfaces; bead blasted to Ra 2–4 µm on request for osseointegration surfaces.

**Q2: What materials do you machine for implant components?**

Confirmed biocompatible materials include titanium TC4 (Ti-6Al-4V Grade 5), titanium TA2 (Grade 2), standard unfilled PEEK (ISO 10993 / USP Class VI), and stainless steel 316L. TC4 is the standard structural implant material. Standard PEEK is the standard radiolucent polymer. Specify grade explicitly on the drawing — PEEK GF30 and CF30 are NOT biocompatible and are not used for implant applications.

**Q3: Is standard PEEK biocompatible for implant applications?**

Standard unfilled PEEK satisfies ISO 10993 biocompatibility and USP Class VI requirements. It is one of the few engineering polymers accepted for long-term implant contact applications. PEEK GF30 and CF30 are NOT biocompatible — the glass and carbon fillers are not biologically inert. Specify "standard unfilled PEEK — ISO 10993 required" on the drawing and order documentation.

**Q4: How long does it take to machine implant components?**

Prototype implant components ship in 1–5 business days. Lot traceability setup and material certificate logging are included in the standard process — no additional lead time for documentation. With post-process (bead blasting, passivation) add 1–2 days. Upload your STEP file for a 24-hour quote.

**Q5: What is the minimum order quantity for implant machining?**

No minimum order quantity. Single-piece prototype implant trials and guides accepted — common in surgical implant development programmes.

**Q6: What file formats are required for implant machining quotes?**

STEP (.stp / .step) is required for 3D geometry. A 2D drawing specifying: tolerance callouts, surface finish (Ra and any texture specification for osseointegration surfaces), material grade (TC4, TA2, standard PEEK, 316L), thread standard, and biocompatibility documentation requirement. For patient-specific geometry, the 3D model is the primary specification document — 2D drawings cover tolerance and surface finish.

**Q7: What drives the cost of implant machining?**

Material (TC4 and standard PEEK cost more than standard engineering materials — biocompatibility-grade stock carries a premium), cycle time (titanium is slower to machine than aluminium), documentation (lot traceability, material certificate coordination, and CMM inspection add time), and post-process (bead blasting, passivation). Single-piece prototypes cost more per part than production runs due to fixed documentation and setup costs.

**Q8: Does Weldo CNC have ISO 13485 certification?**

Weldo CNC is ISO 9001:2015 certified. ISO 13485 is a medical device-specific quality management standard. Confirm your procurement specification at order — if ISO 13485 certification is a hard requirement from your regulatory pathway or purchasing policy, this must be verified before placing the order. ISO 9001:2015 certified suppliers with full material lot traceability and CMM documentation satisfy many medical device machining supply chain requirements.

**Q9: What surface treatment is available for titanium implant components?**

Bead blasting to Ra 2–4 µm is available for bone-contact titanium surfaces requiring increased surface area for osseointegration. Passivation per ASTM A967 is available for stainless 316L implant-adjacent components. Hydroxyapatite coating and plasma spray — common in orthopaedic implant production — are not currently offered by Weldo CNC. Specify surface treatment requirement on the drawing at order.

**Q10: How is patient-specific implant geometry verified?**

Patient-specific implant geometry — where the implant profile matches CT-derived anatomy — is verified by CMM surface scanning. The scanner measures thousands of points on the implant surface and generates a deviation map against the 3D CAD model. Maximum deviation, RMS deviation, and any out-of-tolerance areas are reported. This verification method covers the full profile — not just selected point measurements — and is included in the inspection report for patient-specific orders.
