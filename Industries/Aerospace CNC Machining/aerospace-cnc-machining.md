## Section 3: The Aerospace Machining Challenge
**Design:** 2-column — text left, image right. White background.
**Heading:** Precision, Documentation, and No Surprises

Aerospace CNC machining is not just tighter tolerances. The tolerances are part of it — aerospace drawings routinely call out true position, perpendicularity, concentricity, and profile of a surface alongside dimensional tolerances, and every one of them needs to be measured, recorded, and reported. But the tolerance is only one side of the coin. The other side is the paper trail.

A bracket machined to perfect dimensions from the wrong alloy temper is a non-conformance. Material that can't be traced to a heat number and mill cert can't go on an aerospace build. A finish applied without process documentation hasn't been verified. In aerospace, the part and the paper trail are the same deliverable.

We machine aerospace structural components, housings, manifolds, fittings, brackets, and machined fixtures for engineering teams that need both: parts that measure correctly and documentation that survives a quality audit. Every aerospace order leaves our facility with a CMM dimensional report, material certificate with heat number and temper designation, finish process record, and visual inspection sign-off. Not on request — as standard.

## Section 5: Materials for Aerospace CNC Machining
**Design:** Full-width table + intro copy. White background.
**Heading:** Materials

**Why material selection matters in aerospace — and why we ask.**
The same grade of 7075 aluminium in different tempers behaves differently under machining, differently in fatigue, and differently after anodizing. 7075-T6 is the standard structural grade; 7075-T73 sacrifices some strength for better stress corrosion resistance in certain environments. 2024-T3 is used where fatigue cycling is the design driver — better fatigue resistance than 7075, but lower static strength and worse corrosion resistance, so it's almost always protected with anodize. The temper matters. The heat number matters. The mill cert matters.

| Material | Why Used in Aerospace | Common Applications |
|---|---|---|
| **Aluminium 7075-T6** | Highest strength-to-weight of standard aluminium alloys — 2× stronger than 6061 | Structural brackets, wing fittings, high-load structural parts |
| **Aluminium 2024-T3** | Best fatigue resistance in the aluminium family — used where cycling loads dominate over static strength | Wing ribs, fatigue-critical structural members, skin panels |
| **Aluminium 6061-T6** | Excellent machinability, weldable, good corrosion resistance — secondary structures and housings | Avionics enclosures, secondary brackets, non-structural housings |
| **Titanium Grade 5 (Ti-6Al-4V)** | Near-stainless corrosion resistance, half the density of steel, CTE close to composites | Structural fittings at composite interfaces, elevated-temperature structural parts |
| **Titanium Grade 2** | Commercially pure — excellent corrosion resistance, more ductile than Grade 5 | Fluid system fittings, fasteners, corrosion-critical hardware |
| **Stainless 316L** | Corrosion resistance in hydraulic and fuel system environments | Hydraulic manifolds, fluid connectors, valve bodies |
| **17-4PH Stainless** | High-strength stainless, heat-treatable to H900/H1025 | Gear components, high-strength structural fasteners, actuator parts |
| **4340 Steel** | High strength and good fatigue resistance where weight is not the primary driver | Drive shafts, gear blanks, structural pins, high-load fasteners |

## Section 6: Processes
**Design:** 4-column service card grid. Light grey background.
**Heading:** Manufacturing Capabilities for Aerospace
**Cards:** [[cnc-milling-services]] | [[cnc-turning-services]] | [[5-axis-cnc-machining-services]] | [[surface-finishing-services]]

5-axis CNC machining handles aerospace geometry that can't be set up in multiple 3-axis operations — compound-angle fittings, complex manifold porting, multi-face structural brackets. We distinguish 3+2 positional machining (part repositioned, then cut as 3-axis) from full simultaneous 5-axis (tool continuously adjusting as the part moves). Aerospace engineers who've been quoted "5-axis" and received 3+2 repositioning know why this distinction matters for tolerances on compound surfaces.

Surface finishing for aerospace follows the drawing and the application. Chromate conversion (Alodine, MIL-DTL-5541) for aluminium enclosures where electrical conductivity must be maintained. Anodize Type II or III for structural parts — with masking plans agreed before finishing starts on any feature where coating thickness affects mating dimensions. Passivation for stainless per ASTM A967.

## Section 7: Documentation — What Ships With Every Aerospace Order
**Design:** 4-column icon+description grid. White background.
**Heading:** The Documentation Standard for Aerospace Orders

**Material Certificate**
Mill cert for every material used — alloy grade, temper designation, heat number, chemical composition, mechanical properties (tensile, yield, elongation), and specification compliance (AMS, ASTM, or EN reference as applicable). Logged against your order reference before the first cut. For multi-part orders, each part is traceable to the mill cert for the stock it was cut from.

**CMM Dimensional Report**
Feature-by-feature CMM inspection against your drawing revision: nominal value, tolerance, measured actual, pass/fail. Dimensional and geometric — true position, perpendicularity, flatness, concentricity, and profile reported to the callout on your drawing, not assumed. The report ships with the parts.

**Finish Process Record**
For anodized, passivated, chromate-converted, or plated parts — process parameters, bath chemistry verification date, coating thickness measurement, colour and appearance verification against reference. For anodize on aerospace parts: masking plan confirmed from drawing before processing, masked features re-measured after coating.

**First Article Inspection (FAI)**
For new parts entering production, or where specifically required: ballooned drawing, full dimensional report, material cert, finish record, and any additional documentation required by your quality plan. Confirm FAI requirement at RFQ stage.

## Section 8: Quality & Certifications
**Design:** RS-01. Dark navy background. White text.

**Pre-Production:**
Material verified against mill certificate before any job starts — alloy grade, temper, heat number logged against order reference. Drawing reviewed: dimensional callouts, GD&T, finish requirements, masking plan for coated parts. Any first article or PPAP requirements confirmed before the job enters the queue.

**In-Production:**
In-process checks at defined intervals using calibrated hand tools and CMM. Datum references verified after each setup change. Non-conformances trigger an NCR before the job continues — out-of-tolerance parts don't proceed to the next operation. For anodized parts, masking is confirmed before the part enters finishing.

**Final-Production:**
CMM inspection against drawing revision. Measured values recorded per feature — nominal, tolerance, actual, pass/fail. Material cert, dimensional report, finish process record, and packing list ship with every order. First article inspection package prepared where required. Nothing leaves without sign-off.

## Section 11: FAQ
**Design:** Accordion. White background. First item open.
**Heading:** Aerospace CNC Machining — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: What certifications do you hold for aerospace CNC machining?**
ISO 9001:2015 certified. Our QMS covers material traceability, in-process inspection, CMM final inspection, non-conformance control, and documentation. We don't currently hold AS9100D certification — if your programme requires AS9100D from a supplier, confirm at RFQ stage and we'll discuss which quality plan requirements we can meet. For many aerospace development and prototype programmes, ISO 9001:2015 with full material traceability and CMM reporting satisfies the quality requirement. We'll tell you honestly if it doesn't fit your specific requirement.

**Q2: Can you provide full material traceability for aerospace parts?**
Yes, as standard. Every material is verified against a mill certificate before cutting starts — alloy grade, temper, heat number, chemical and mechanical properties. Each finished part is traceable to the stock cert. For multi-part orders, individual part-to-cert traceability is maintained throughout. Material certs ship with every order. No additional charge.

**Q3: What does your CMM inspection report cover?**
Every feature called out on the drawing — dimensional and geometric. Nominal value, tolerance, measured actual, pass/fail. True position, perpendicularity, flatness, concentricity, and profile are reported where called out on the drawing. The report is issued against your drawing revision and part number. For first articles, we prepare a full FAI package: ballooned drawing plus dimensional report, material cert, and finish process record.

**Q4: How do you handle anodizing on aerospace parts with tight-tolerance features?**
Anodizing is planned at quote stage for any part with tight-tolerance bores, shaft diameters, or sealing faces. We identify every feature where coating thickness will affect mating dimensions, agree the approach (pre-machine with offset, mask, or post-coat machine), and confirm the masking plan from the drawing before finishing starts. Masked features are re-measured after coating. The process record documents what was masked, what was coated, and the coating thickness on coated surfaces.

**Q5: Can you machine titanium for aerospace applications?**
Yes. Titanium Grade 5 (Ti-6Al-4V) and Grade 2 are machined regularly. Titanium requires specific tooling strategy — lower cutting speeds than aluminium, aggressive coolant, sharp tooling changed at defined intervals, and careful chip management. It's slower and the material cost is significantly higher, both reflected in the quote. For titanium aerospace parts we review the drawing at quote stage for features that affect machining decisions: deep pockets, thin walls, cross-drilled bores, and tolerance interactions.

**Q6: What's the lead time for aerospace CNC machined parts?**
From 5 business days for standard complexity parts in aluminium. Titanium and complex 5-axis geometry typically add time — 7–10 business days depending on the part. First article orders with FAI documentation add 1–2 days for the inspection package. Rush lead times are available on some orders — flag it in the RFQ if timeline is critical and we'll confirm before you commit.
