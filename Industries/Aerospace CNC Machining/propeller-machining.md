## Section 3: What Does CNC Propeller Machining Require?

**H2:** What Does CNC Propeller Machining Require?

Weldo CNC machines propellers — for UAV, aerospace, and marine applications — in aluminium 6061, aluminium 7075, and titanium TC4 (Ti-6Al-4V), to tolerances of ±0.005 mm on blade profile, pitch, and chord width, using 5-axis CNC machining at its ISO 9001:2015 certified facility in Dongguan, China. Prototype propellers ship in 1–5 business days. Every order includes a full CMM — coordinate measuring machine — dimensional inspection report.

A propeller blade is defined by three surfaces that must all be held simultaneously: the pressure face, the suction face, and the leading and trailing edges. In 3-axis machining, each face requires a separate setup and a separate datum. Each datum re-interpretation accumulates error — the blade section at mid-span may be dimensionally correct on each face individually but twisted relative to the other. Simultaneous 5-axis machining holds all three surfaces in one clamping against one datum, which is the only reliable way to hold blade chord angle — the pitch — to the drawing specification.

Hub concentricity is the second critical requirement. The bore that mounts the propeller to the motor or shaft must be concentric with the blade root. An off-centre bore produces vibration at high RPM — in a UAV application, that vibration propagates through the airframe and into inertial measurement units. Concentricity is confirmed by CMM after machining and reported numerically in the inspection report.

## Section 6: Propeller Machining Process

**H2:** How Weldo CNC Machines Propellers

Propeller machining starts with datum selection. The hub bore axis is the primary datum — all blade geometry is referenced to the bore centreline. This means the bore is rough-machined first, the part is re-fixtured on the bore datum, and all five-axis blade surface machining references that datum. Hub bore concentricity to the blade root is then a direct output of the setup, not a post-machining assembly fit.

Blade surfaces are machined in simultaneous 5-axis mode — the trunnion tilts and the rotary axis positions continuously while the spindle cuts the aerofoil profile. This eliminates the step discontinuities that appear on blade surfaces machined in indexed (3+2) mode, where each indexed position leaves a tool-path boundary visible in the surface finish. The result is a smooth blade surface at Ra 1.6 µm that transitions continuously from root to tip.

Finishing passes use a ball-end mill with a small step-over to achieve the required Ra. For propellers requiring very smooth surfaces — UAV propellers sensitive to acoustic noise or marine propellers requiring anti-fouling compatibility — a polishing pass or manual finishing brings the surface to Ra 0.8 µm. Anodizing is applied after machining for aluminium propellers — the dimensional growth on blade surfaces is small (5–12 µm), but hub bores are machined oversize by the confirmed anodize thickness and re-measured post-anodize.

## Section 8: Why Engineers Specify Weldo CNC for Propeller Machining

**H2:** Why Engineers Specify Weldo CNC for Propeller Machining

**1. 5-axis simultaneous machining holds blade pitch to the drawing**
Blade pitch — the angle of each blade section relative to the rotation plane — is a 3D geometric relationship that cannot be verified or corrected after the fact in 3-axis setups. Simultaneous 5-axis machining holds pitch by referencing all blade surfaces to the hub bore datum in one setup. The CMM report confirms pitch angle numerically against the 3D model.

**2. Hub bore concentricity confirmed by CMM — not assumed**
Bore concentricity to blade root axis is measured and reported as a total indicator runout (TIR) value in the CMM dimensional report. For UAV propellers, a concentricity error of 0.05 mm TIR at a hub diameter of 30 mm introduces a detectable vibration at operational RPM. The inspection report shows the actual measured value — not a pass/no-pass estimate.

**3. DFM review before the 5-axis programme runs**
Leading edge radius definition, blade root fillet geometry, and hub bore datum selection are reviewed at DFM — Design for Manufacturability — before programming. Blade geometry that is ambiguously defined in the STEP file — missing fillets, zero-radius leading edges that cannot be machined — is flagged at quote stage, not mid-production.

**4. Direct facility — no subcontracting on standard propeller orders**
100+ CNC machines in-house in Dongguan, including 5-axis capability for propeller profile machining. Standard propeller orders run in that facility.

**5. CMM report and material certificate in every shipment**
Blade profile, pitch, hub bore diameter, and concentricity measured and recorded. Material certificate confirming alloy and temper. Both documents travel in the shipping box.

## Section 9: FAQ

**H2:** Propeller Machining — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: What tolerance can Weldo CNC hold on machined propeller blades?**

Blade profile (chord and thickness) holds to ±0.005 mm with 5-axis simultaneous machining, verified by CMM surface scanning against the 3D model. Blade pitch angle holds to ±0.1°. Hub bore diameter holds to H7 tolerance. Concentricity of hub bore to blade root axis is measured and reported as TIR — typically below 0.02 mm on precision propeller orders.

**Q2: What file format is required for propeller machining?**

A STEP file with the full 3D blade surface defined — including leading and trailing edge geometry, root fillets, and hub bore. Blade geometry cannot be inferred from 2D section drawings alone. The 5-axis CAM programme is written from the surface model; CMM inspection scans the blade surface against the same CAD nominal. Include a 2D drawing specifying hub bore tolerance, surface finish, and pitch angle where critical.

**Q3: What materials do you machine for propellers?**

Confirmed propeller materials include aluminium 6061-T6, aluminium 7075-T6, titanium TC4 (Ti-6Al-4V), stainless steel 316L, and brass C360. Aluminium 6061-T6 is the standard for UAV and general aviation propellers. Aluminium 7075-T6 for high-load or high-RPM applications. Titanium for aerospace weight-critical propellers. Stainless and brass for marine saltwater applications.

**Q5: What is the minimum order quantity for propeller machining?**

**Q6: Do you machine propellers for UAV applications specifically?**

Yes. UAV propellers — typically Ø100–500 mm diameter, 2 or 3 blades, aluminium 6061-T6 or 7075-T6 — are a confirmed part type. Hub bore concentricity is verified by CMM. Surface finish on the blade faces is Ra 1.6 µm standard; Ra 0.8 µm achievable with a polishing pass for low-noise applications.

**Q7: What drives the cost of CNC propeller machining?**

Blade count (3-blade propeller costs more than 2-blade at the same diameter), diameter (larger propellers have longer 5-axis cycle times), material (titanium costs more than aluminium per cycle), surface finish (polishing pass adds time), and quantity (production runs per-part cost less than prototypes). Upload your STEP file and blade count for a 24-hour quote.

**Q8: How does 5-axis machining improve propeller quality over 3-axis?**

Five-axis simultaneous machining eliminates the datum shift between setup changes that accumulates error across blade faces in 3-axis machining. Blade pitch — the angle of each section — is held in one setup because all faces reference the same datum continuously. 3-axis propeller machining with separate setups per face can hold each face to tolerance individually while the blade is twisted overall, because the pitch angle is not verified between setups. Five-axis holds pitch angle as a direct output of the single-setup machining programme.

**Q9: Can propellers be anodized after CNC machining?**

Yes. Aluminium propellers — 6061-T6 and 7075-T6 — accept Type II anodizing after machining. Anodize thickness of 5–15 µm per surface is small relative to blade chord dimensions but significant for hub bore fits. Hub bores are machined oversize by the confirmed anodize thickness, so the final bore after anodizing is the H7 target. Blade surface anodize does not affect aerodynamic profile at typical 10–15 µm thicknesses.

**Q10: Do you dynamically balance propellers after machining?**

Dynamic balancing is not a standard Weldo CNC service — static balance (mass distribution checked by weighing) is available on request. Dynamic balancing requires a balancing machine and is typically performed by the customer or a specialist service after delivery. Blade profile symmetry and hub concentricity — both confirmed by CMM — are the primary contributors to propeller balance. Contact the quoting team to confirm what balance specification your application requires.
