## Section 3: What Does CNC Turbine Blade Machining Require?

**H2:** What Does CNC Turbine Blade Machining Require?

Weldo CNC machines turbine blades — compressor and fan blade geometries — in titanium TC4 (Ti-6Al-4V), stainless steel 17-4PH, and tool steel, to tolerances of ±0.005 mm on aerofoil profile, root features, and attachment geometry, using 5-axis simultaneous CNC machining at its ISO 9001:2015 certified facility in Dongguan, China. Prototype turbine blades ship in 1–5 business days. Every tight-tolerance order includes a full CMM — coordinate measuring machine — dimensional inspection report, delivered with the parts.

Turbine blade machining is among the most demanding CNC operations in aerospace. The fir-tree root — the serrated attachment feature that locks the blade into the disc — requires tight pitch-to-pitch spacing on the tree teeth, accurate depth, and a surface finish that prevents fretting fatigue at the root. Root geometry is inspected by CMM against the 3D model — caliper measurement is insufficient for verifying the compound curves and serration pitch. Aerofoil profiles are equally demanding: chord width, thickness distribution, and twist angle must all be within tolerance over the full blade span.

Titanium TC4 — the most common turbine compressor blade material — work-hardens rapidly under worn tooling. A single dull insert on a titanium blade programme produces a work-hardened surface layer that concentrates stress and reduces fatigue life below specification. Tool life is actively managed during blade machining: inserts are changed at conservative intervals, and the first part off each new insert is inspected before the full batch runs. Surface roughness (Ra) on blade faces is typically specified at Ra 1.6 µm or better — Weldo CNC measures and reports Ra on all critical blade surfaces.

## Section 6: Turbine Blade Machining Process

**H2:** How Weldo CNC Machines Turbine Blades

Turbine blade programmes begin with fixture design. The blade root — the fir-tree or dovetail attachment — is the primary datum. The fixture holds the blade by a temporary extension at the tip, allowing the 5-axis head to access both aerofoil faces and the root surfaces from a single clamping. This datum setup ensures the root geometry and the aerofoil section reference the same axis — which is the correct engineering relationship, since the blade loads transfer through the root into the disc.

Aerofoil surfaces are machined with a ball-end mill in simultaneous 5-axis mode. The tool tilts continuously relative to the blade surface normal — keeping the cutter at the most efficient contact angle as the blade twists from root to tip. This eliminates the indexed-position step lines that would appear at each re-positioning in 3+2 machining. The result is a continuous surface at Ra 1.6 µm, which is within the aerodynamic boundary layer specification for most compressor blade applications.

Fir-tree root machining uses carbide form tools ground to the specific serration profile. The pitch, depth, and root radius of each serration tooth are ground into the tool, and the tool traces the serration along the root in multiple passes. CMM verification uses a probe stylus to measure each tooth from multiple approach angles — depth, pitch, and root radius all reported against the CAD nominal. This step is the highest-stakes inspection on a turbine blade because a root that fails here cannot be reworked.

**2. Titanium cutting parameters managed to prevent work hardening**

## Section 9: FAQ

**H2:** Turbine Blade Machining — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: What tolerance can Weldo CNC hold on turbine blade aerofoil profiles?**

Five-axis simultaneous machining holds aerofoil chord and thickness to ±0.005 mm, blade twist to ±0.1°, and fir-tree root pitch to ±0.01 mm. These tolerances are verified by CMM scanning the blade surface against the 3D CAD nominal — not by caliper measurement, which cannot verify the compound curvature of an aerofoil profile.

**Q2: What file format is required for turbine blade machining?**

A STEP file with the full 3D blade surface defined — including aerofoil profile at multiple span stations, root geometry, and tip geometry. Aerofoil sections cannot be reliably reconstructed from 2D section drawings at tight tolerance. Include a 2D drawing specifying fir-tree root tolerances, surface finish requirements (Ra), and any material or heat treatment specifications.

**Q3: What materials do you machine for turbine blades?**

Confirmed materials include titanium TC4 (Ti-6Al-4V), titanium TA2 (Grade 2), stainless steel 17-4PH (H900), and stainless 431. TC4 is the standard compressor blade material — highest strength-to-weight ratio below 600°C. 17-4PH for applications where titanium is cost-prohibitive but high strength is required. 431 for industrial turbines and test hardware.

**Q4: How long does it take to machine turbine blades?**

Prototype turbine blades ship in 1–5 business days from drawing approval. Small-batch production (5–20 blades) ships in 5–15 business days. Titanium blade cycle time is longer than aluminium — titanium's lower cutting speed and more frequent insert changes extend cycle time. Upload your STEP file with blade count and material for a 24-hour quote.

**Q5: What is the minimum order quantity for turbine blade machining?**

No minimum order quantity. Single-blade prototypes accepted — common for aerodynamic development programmes where each iteration changes the blade profile.

**Q6: Can you machine cooling holes in turbine blades?**

Small-diameter drilling (Ø1–3 mm) at compound angles into blade surfaces is possible using 5-axis positional drilling. True film-cooling holes — very small diameter (Ø0.3–0.8 mm) at high compound angles through a thin aerofoil section — are typically produced by EDM or laser drilling, not CNC milling. Confirm the cooling hole diameter and wall section at quote stage.

**Q7: What drives the cost of turbine blade machining?**

Material (titanium TC4 costs significantly more than steel per kilogram), 5-axis cycle time (blade span, complexity, and finish specification drive programme length), insert consumption (titanium wears inserts faster than steel or aluminium — insert cost is embedded in the quote), and inspection (CMM surface scanning and root geometry measurement add time). Include material, blade count, span, and surface finish specification in the quote enquiry.

**Q8: How does Weldo CNC compare to local turbine blade machining suppliers?**

The documentation difference is the most cited distinction. Weldo CNC ships a CMM report with numerically measured aerofoil profile, root geometry, and surface finish — not a form gauge certificate. Titanium-specific cutting parameters prevent work hardening that would degrade fatigue life below specification. ISO 9001:2015 with a 97.48% annual pass rate covers the production system. Customers in 100+ countries receive the same inspection documentation standard regardless of order size.

**Q9: Does work hardening in titanium affect blade fatigue life?**

Yes. A work-hardened surface layer in TC4 — produced by worn tooling or incorrect cutting parameters — increases compressive residual stress at the surface but also increases brittleness at the stress-hardened zone. Under cyclic fatigue loading (blade bending), the hardened layer can initiate fatigue cracking earlier than the base material specification predicts. Weldo CNC manages this risk with insert change intervals and first-article surface inspection after each insert set, producing a Ra 1.6 µm surface that indicates clean chip formation without work hardening.
