## Section 3: What Does CNC Drill Bit Machining Require?

**H2:** What Does CNC Drill Bit Machining Require?

Weldo CNC machines drill bit bodies — rotary drill bodies, core bit bodies, and reamers — in H13 tool steel, D2 tool steel, 4140 alloy steel, and 17-4 PH stainless steel (H900), to shank diameter tolerances of ±0.005 mm and insert pocket flatness of ±0.02 mm, from its ISO 9001:2015 certified facility in Dongguan, China. Prototype drill bit bodies ship in 1–5 business days from drawing approval. Every precision drill bit order includes a full CMM — coordinate measuring machine — dimensional inspection report confirming shank tolerance, concentricity, and insert pocket position.

Drill bit machining is governed by three interdependent relationships that must all hold simultaneously: shank diameter (the interference fit in the chuck or collet sets the drive coupling — a shank outside ±0.005 mm slips or seizes), body OD concentricity (a drill body with more than 0.010 mm runout relative to the shank centreline wastes carbide insert life and enlarges the borehole beyond specification), and insert pocket position (pockets outside ±0.025 mm relative to the shank axis datum misalign cutting inserts and accelerate uneven wear). These three tolerance relationships trace back to a single datum — the shank centreline — and every machining operation is referenced to it.

H13 and D2 tool steels require a two-stage machining sequence. Parts are rough machined in the annealed condition — easier to cut, lower tool wear — then sent for heat treatment (hardening and tempering to HRC 44–52 for H13, HRC 58–62 for D2). Finish grinding and precision boring run after hardening, using CBN — cubic boron nitride — or carbide tooling, because conventional HSS tooling cannot cut HRC 50+ material without immediate edge failure.

## Section 6: Drill Bit Machining Process

**H2:** How Weldo CNC Machines Drill Bit Bodies

The shank centreline is the primary datum for every drill bit machining sequence. All features — flutes, insert pockets, body OD, and threaded shank — are positioned and measured relative to the shank axis. Setting the shank as the datum ensures the body OD concentricity measurement directly reflects the dimensional relationship that matters in service: how far the cutting edge travels from the intended borehole centreline.

H13 and D2 tool steels follow a two-stage sequence. Rough machining runs in the annealed condition — lower hardness allows faster material removal, lower tooling cost, and better flute geometry control before the material stiffens. After roughing, parts move to heat treatment: hardening and tempering to HRC 44–52 for H13, HRC 58–62 for D2. Finish machining — shank grinding, bore finishing, and insert pocket floor grinding — runs after hardening, using CBN or carbide tooling. Conventional HSS tooling fails immediately on HRC 50+ tool steel.

Flute machining uses 5-axis CNC milling or CNC cylindrical grinding depending on flute geometry complexity. Simple straight-flute geometries suit CNC grinding for consistent helical angle control. Complex variable-helix or chip-breaker flute profiles require 5-axis CNC milling path generation. Helical angle is held to ±0.5° and verified at three points along the flute length. Insert pocket machining for carbide insert seats uses wire EDM or precision surface grinding to achieve floor flatness of ±0.02 mm — conventional end milling cannot hold pocket floor flatness at that level on hardened steel.

## Section 8: Why Engineers Specify Weldo CNC for Drill Bit Machining

**H2:** Why Engineers Choose Weldo CNC for Drill Bit Machining

**1. Shank datum — all geometry referenced to shank axis, not outer profile**
Every feature on a drill bit body — flutes, insert pockets, body OD, thread — is positioned relative to the shank centreline. Referencing off the outer profile instead accumulates error at every operation and produces a body OD concentricity reading that does not reflect actual chuck-to-cutting-edge runout. Datum selection on drill bit bodies is not a process preference — it determines whether the concentricity specification is meaningful.

**2. Two-stage machining sequence for hardened tool steels**
H13 and D2 are rough machined in the annealed condition, then heat treated before finish grinding. Attempting finish tolerances on annealed material wastes the heat treat distortion allowance. Attempting finish tolerances after hardening with the wrong tooling destroys CBN or carbide edges. The correct sequence — rough annealed, heat treat, finish with CBN — is the only reliable path to ±0.005 mm shank tolerance on HRC 50+ tool steel.

**3. CBN finish machining confirmed on hardened tool steels**
Finish grinding and bore work on H13 at HRC 44–52 and D2 at HRC 58–62 use CBN or carbide tooling. Conventional HSS tooling cannot machine HRC 50+ material without immediate edge failure. CBN tooling maintains consistent geometry through the finish pass — no edge wear distorting the shank diameter or pocket floor flatness during the cut.

**4. Insert pocket flatness verified by CMM**
Insert pocket floor flatness — the seating surface that determines carbide insert contact area — is measured by CMM to ±0.02 mm. A pocket floor with a 0.05 mm warp rocks the carbide insert on every rotation, concentrating stress at the insert corner rather than distributing it across the full seating face. The CMM flatness result is reported numerically in the inspection report.

**5. Concentricity measurement reported in CMM inspection report**
Body OD concentricity to the shank centreline is measured and reported as a TIR — total indicator runout — value in every CMM inspection report. The concentricity number ships with the parts. Your quality inspection receives a verified runout figure referenced to the shank datum, not an estimated result from a V-block runout check.

## Section 9: FAQ

**H2:** Drill Bit Machining — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: What tolerance can Weldo CNC hold on drill bit shank diameters?**

Shank diameter holds to ±0.005 mm — the interference fit dimension that controls the coupling between the drill body and the chuck or collet. Measurement runs on the shank centreline datum by CMM. A shank outside ±0.005 mm introduces either clearance (slip under torque) or interference (seizure during tool change) — both cause failure in service. Specify shank diameter, fit class, and any API standard on your drawing.

**Q2: How long does it take to machine a drill bit body?**

Prototype drill bit bodies ship in 1–5 business days from drawing approval for standard geometries in 4140 or 17-4 PH. H13 and D2 parts require a heat treatment cycle between rough and finish machining — lead time for heat-treated tool steel bodies runs 3–5 days within the prototype window. Upload your STEP file for a 24-hour quote with a confirmed lead time for your specific geometry and material.

**Q3: What materials do you machine for drill bit bodies?**

Confirmed materials include H13 tool steel (heat resistant to 600°C, HRC 44–52 after hardening), D2 tool steel (HRC 58–62, highest wear resistance in the range), 4140 alloy steel (pre-hardened billet for economical large bodies), and 17-4 PH stainless steel in H900 condition (corrosion-resistant bodies for water-injection or saline environments). Specify your operating conditions — formation temperature, fluid exposure, torque class — and the material recommendation follows from that.

**Q4: How does Weldo CNC compare to other CNC suppliers for drill bit machining?**

Most general CNC shops machine drill bit bodies in the as-delivered condition only — they cannot perform the rough-then-heat-treat-then-finish sequence that H13 and D2 bodies require. Weldo CNC coordinates the full two-stage sequence, with finish machining on hardened tool steels using CBN tooling confirmed in-house. Insert pocket flatness is verified by CMM to ±0.02 mm on every order, not estimated by surface gauge. The CMM inspection report — with shank diameter, concentricity TIR, pocket position, and pocket flatness — ships with every order.

**Q5: How do you verify concentricity on a drill bit body?**

Concentricity — the radial deviation of the body OD from the shank centreline — is measured by CMM using the shank centreline as the primary datum axis. The result is reported as TIR (total indicator runout) in micrometres. A drill body is measured at a minimum of three cross-sections along the body length. The CMM inspection report lists each TIR value and confirms pass/fail against your drawing specification. V-block runout checks are not used — they reference the outer profile, not the shank axis.

**Q6: What is the minimum order quantity for drill bit machining?**

No minimum order quantity. Single-piece prototype drill bit bodies are accepted — common in downhole tool development where each design iteration changes flute geometry, insert pocket configuration, or body OD profile. Small-batch runs of 5–50 pieces are quoted with the same CMM inspection protocol as prototype orders.

**Q7: What file formats do you accept for drill bit machining quotes?**

STEP (.stp / .step) is required for 3D geometry. Include a 2D drawing specifying: shank diameter with tolerance and fit class, body OD concentricity TIR, insert pocket position relative to shank datum, insert pocket floor flatness, flute helical angle tolerance, surface finish Ra on shank and body, thread class (API or custom), and material grade with hardness target. Incomplete drawings delay quote — all seven dimensions listed above are required for a fixed-price quote on hardened tool steel drill bodies.

**Q8: What drives the cost of drill bit body machining?**

Material is the largest cost driver — D2 billet costs more than 4140 pre-hardened bar, and HRC 58–62 finish machining consumes CBN tooling faster than standard carbide. Heat treatment adds a process step between rough and finish machining. Complex insert pocket configurations — high pocket count, angular pockets, pocket-in-pocket geometries — increase EDM or grinding time proportionally. Flute geometry complexity (variable helix, chip breakers) drives 5-axis programming time. Include flute count, insert pocket count, material grade, and hardness target in the quote enquiry to receive an accurate price.

**Q9: Can you machine the API thread on the drill shank?**

API threads — both external 6g and internal 6H class — are confirmed on drill bit body shanks. Custom thread profiles per drawing are also confirmed. Thread measurement runs after machining using calibrated thread gauges. Specify the thread standard (API specification number or custom thread drawing), class (6g external / 6H internal), and any gaging requirement in the quote.

**Q10: Do you supply the carbide cutting inserts?**

Weldo CNC does not supply carbide cutting inserts. The insert pockets — the recesses in the drill body that hold and seat the inserts — are machined to ±0.025 mm position and ±0.02 mm floor flatness. Insert procurement is the buyer's responsibility. If you supply a carbide insert sample at the quote stage, Weldo CNC can machine a test pocket to confirm fit before committing to full-body production.
