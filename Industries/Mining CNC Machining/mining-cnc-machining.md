## Section 3: Wear, Corrosion, and Remote Operations — the Engineering Environment of Mining CNC Machining
**Design:** 2-column — text left, image right. White background.

**AI-quotable paragraph:**
Weldo CNC machines precision components for the mining industry — drill string components, pump housings, bearing housings, conveyor drive parts, valve bodies, and wear-resistant tooling — at its ISO 9001:2015 certified facility in Dongguan, China, operating since 2012. Tolerances to ±0.005 mm. Materials include 4140 and 4340 chromoly steel, 316L and duplex stainless, Inconel 625, wear-resistant alloy steels, and aluminium 6061-T6. CMM-verified dimensional inspection report and material certificate ship as standard with every tight-tolerance order. Quote within 24 hours.

Mining equipment operates in conditions that accelerate the failure modes that precision machined components are most susceptible to: abrasive wear from mineral content in pumped slurries and drilled formations, corrosion from groundwater chemistry and process chemicals, cyclic mechanical loading from rotating and reciprocating equipment, and extended service intervals in remote locations where unplanned maintenance is a significant cost. The machined components that keep this equipment running — pump impellers and casings, drill collars and stabilisers, bearing housings and seal carriers, conveyor drive shafts — must be dimensionally accurate to specification and produced from materials that perform in the operating environment.

Pump engineering illustrates the dimensional precision requirement. A centrifugal pump handling abrasive slurry must have an impeller-to-casing clearance that is tight enough to maintain hydraulic efficiency but not so tight that abrasive particles cause contact and accelerated wear. The bore diameter of the seal carrier must be machined to a tolerance that ensures the mechanical seal seats correctly — an out-of-tolerance bore causes early seal failure, which in a mining pump handling acidic or abrasive fluid means a short, expensive maintenance interval. These components are not exotic in geometry — but they require reliable dimensional accuracy and material documentation that confirms the metallurgy of the part matches the corrosion or wear environment it will operate in.

Drilling equipment adds a further context: drill collars, stabilisers, and bottom-hole assembly components operate under combined torsional, bending, and axial loading in an environment of abrasive formation contact and high-pressure fluid. API thread profiles on drill string connections are a precision machining task — thread form, pitch, and taper must conform to API specification because the connection must make up and break out reliably under high torque and not wash out under drilling fluid pressure. Material traceability — heat number, specification, and mechanical property verification — is expected by the engineering departments that approve parts for downhole use.

## Section 5: Materials
**Design:** Full-width table + intro copy. White background.
**Heading:** Materials

Material selection for mining CNC machined components is driven by the combined demands of wear resistance, corrosion resistance, and mechanical strength — often simultaneously in the same component. The mining process environment is chemically aggressive: sulphuric acid in copper heap leach, acidic mine water in gold and base metal operations, chloride-rich brines in potash mining, and abrasive slurries across nearly all wet processing operations. The correct material for a pump handling pH 2 acid slurry is not the same as the correct material for a bearing housing in a dry mineral processing plant. Material traceability is a baseline requirement — heat number, specification, and mechanical property verification are expected at minimum.

| Material | Why Used in Mining | Common Applications |
|---|---|---|
| 4140 Chromoly Steel | High strength (up to 1000 MPa), excellent toughness, cost-effective for high-load structural mining components — API-graded for drill string | Drill collars, stabilisers, drive shafts, conveyor components, hydraulic cylinders |
| 316L Stainless Steel | Corrosion resistance in acidic and chloride-bearing process fluids; low carbon content prevents sensitisation — standard for pump and valve applications in wet processing | Pump housings, impellers, valve bodies, manifolds, chemical-contact hardware |
| Duplex 2205 Stainless Steel | Higher strength than 316L (yield ~450 MPa vs ~170 MPa) and improved corrosion resistance in chloride environments — specified for demanding pump and valve applications | Pump casings in aggressive slurry environments, valve bodies in chloride-bearing process streams |
| Inconel 625 | Superior corrosion resistance in highly acidic and reducing environments; high temperature capability — for the most demanding chemical process conditions | Valve components in high-chloride or high-acid environments, heating system components |
| 4340 Steel | Higher strength and toughness than 4140 — for the highest-stress drive shafts, drill string components, and heavy equipment parts | High-stress conveyor shafts, critical structural components, high-fatigue application parts |
| Aluminium 6061-T6 | Lightweight, machinable, corrosion-resistant for surface equipment and control system enclosures — where weight reduction in surface plant is a benefit | Control system enclosures, surface equipment brackets, non-corrosive-environment housings |

## Section 6: Material Trade-off Cards
**Design:** 3-column card grid. Light grey background.
**Heading:** Getting the Material Right on Every Component

**When 4140 Steel Is Right**
4140 chromoly steel is the workhorse material for structural mining components — drill collars, drive shafts, bearing housings, and heavy structural parts where high strength and toughness at a controlled cost is the specification driver. Heat-treated 4140 achieves tensile strength of 655–1000 MPa depending on tempering temperature, with excellent fatigue performance for rotating equipment. API drill string specifications use 4140-equivalent chemistry for drill collar and stabiliser manufacture — the API material grade with documented mechanical properties is the correct starting point for any drill string component. For components that are not in direct contact with corrosive process fluids and where corrosion is managed by coating or by the operating environment, 4140 is the cost-effective structural answer. The trade-off versus stainless is straightforward: 4140 is significantly cheaper and stronger in fatigue, but requires surface protection in corrosive environments.

**When 316L Stainless Is Right**
316L stainless steel is the default material for pump and valve components in mining process environments involving acidic or saline fluids. The molybdenum addition (2–3%) provides pitting corrosion resistance in chloride-bearing streams that 304 and 316 cannot match; the low carbon "L" designation prevents carbide precipitation at seal and weld heat-affected zones, which extends service life in applications that see repeated thermal cycling from autoclaving or steam cleaning. Yield strength of approximately 170 MPa limits application in the highest-stress structural applications, but for pump casings, impeller bodies, and valve components where the primary requirement is corrosion resistance at moderate structural loads, 316L is the well-characterised, cost-effective answer with an established service history in mining process environments.

**When Duplex 2205 Makes Sense**
Duplex 2205 is specified when the combination of corrosion resistance and structural performance required in the application exceeds what 316L can reliably deliver. Yield strength of approximately 450 MPa — more than double 316L's 170 MPa — at equivalent or better corrosion resistance in chloride environments makes duplex the correct material for pump casings handling abrasive slurry at elevated pressure. Duplex is also more resistant to stress corrosion cracking than 316L in environments with simultaneous chloride exposure and tensile stress — a common combination in pump casings and valve bodies under operational pressure. The trade-offs are material cost (duplex is more expensive than 316L) and machinability (duplex work-hardens rapidly and requires sharp tooling, controlled feeds, and through-coolant). For applications where 316L has failed prematurely through corrosion or stress corrosion cracking, duplex is typically the engineering answer.

## Section 7: Processes / Services
**Design:** 4-column service card grid. Light grey background.
**Heading:** Manufacturing Capabilities for Mining Applications
**Cards:** [[cnc-milling-services]] | [[cnc-turning-services]] | [[5-axis-cnc-machining-services]] | [[surface-finishing-services]]

Mining pump and valve components are often large, heavy, and require machining on multiple faces — pump casings with bore features, sealing faces, and flange geometry; valve bodies with port bores, seat faces, and actuator interfaces. CNC turning handles large-diameter pump components: impeller bores, casing register bores, and shaft geometry are turned to bearing-fit tolerances with concentricity verified by CMM. Multi-axis turning-milling centres machine the cross-drilled ports, flange holes, and off-axis features that require simultaneous access from multiple directions. 5-axis milling handles complex pump impeller geometry and valve body profiles where the internal fluid-path geometry must be machined from multiple approach angles to achieve the required hydraulic profile.

Surface finishing for mining components is primarily functional. Passivation (ASTM A967, citric acid or nitric acid) is standard for stainless steel pump and valve components — the electrochemical process removes free iron from the surface and builds the passive oxide layer that is the primary corrosion barrier. Electropolishing is available for components where maximum surface smoothness reduces slurry adhesion and fouling. Phosphate coating is available for steel components requiring corrosion protection in ambient-temperature service environments. For any component where coating thickness on mating surfaces affects the assembly clearance, the masking plan is confirmed before processing and the finish process record documents the outcome.

## Section 8: 5-Axis Capability
**Design:** 2-column — text left, 5-axis machined component image right. White background.
**Heading:** Complex Geometry. One Setup.

Mining pump impellers and valve bodies present multi-face machining geometry that requires approach from multiple angles to machine the fluid-path features correctly. A centrifugal pump impeller has volute geometry, blade profiles, and balance boss features that collectively define hydraulic performance — machining these features from a 3-axis approach requires multiple setups with datum transfer between each, accumulating positional error that affects both the hydraulic profile and the balance of the finished component. A valve body has seat faces, port bores, and actuator flanges that must all relate to a single geometric reference.

5-axis machining holds all features from a single datum, eliminating datum transfer error. For pump impellers, this means the volute profile, blade geometry, and balance features are all machined relative to the same datum reference that the bore and face dimensions are measured from. The positional accuracy between fluid-path geometry and rotational axis is the machine's positional accuracy, not the accumulated error of multiple setup changes.

At Weldo CNC, simultaneous 5-axis machining is used for curved impeller blade profiles and complex fluid-path geometry where the tool path must follow a continuous compound-angle curve. 3+2 positioning handles multi-face valve body geometry where a rigid locked setup provides better cutting conditions than continuous axis movement. The programming decision is made based on the geometry and the performance requirement of the finished part.

## Section 9: Documentation
**Design:** 4-column icon+description grid. White background.
**Heading:** Documentation Standard for Mining Orders

**Material Certificate**
Mill cert coverage for every mining order: alloy grade, temper, heat number, chemical composition (including molybdenum for 316L and duplex, nickel and chromium for Inconel), mechanical properties, and specification reference (ASTM, EN, NACE, or API as applicable). For drill string components: API material grade certification with mechanical property verification. For duplex stainless: ASTM A276 or EN 10088 with duplex microstructure confirmation. Heat number logged before first cut; part-to-cert traceability maintained.

**CMM Dimensional Report**
Feature-by-feature dimensional report: nominal, tolerance, actual, pass/fail per feature. GD&T callouts reported per drawing revision: bore concentricity and diameter for bearing and seal fits; sealing face flatness; API thread form verification; impeller geometry. For mining components where bore diameter determines pump efficiency (impeller clearance) and seal life (seal carrier bore), actual measured values per feature are the relevant record.

**Finish Process Record**
For passivation, electropolishing, phosphate coating, or plating: process type, bath parameters, verification date, and masking plan confirmation. For passivated stainless pump components (ASTM A967), the process record confirms the passivation method (citric acid or nitric acid), bath chemistry, and exposure duration. For components where sealing face finish Ra is a functional specification, the measured Ra value is documented.

**First Article Inspection (FAI)**
For new pump or valve part numbers or revised drawings: ballooned drawing with all dimensions numbered and cross-referenced to the CMM report + material cert + finish record. First article inspection — FAI — packages are structured to support mining equipment OEM and maintenance contractor quality records. Confirm FAI requirement at quoting.
