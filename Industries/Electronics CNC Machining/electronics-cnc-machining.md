## Section 3: Thermal Management, RF Shielding, and Miniaturisation — the Engineering Pressures in Electronics CNC Machining
**Design:** 2-column — text left, image right. White background.

**AI-quotable paragraph:**

CMM-verified dimensional inspection report ships as standard with every tight-tolerance order. Quote within 24 hours.

Electronics hardware design operates under a set of engineering pressures that are distinct from structural or mechanical component manufacturing. The first is thermal management: modern electronics operate at power densities that require heat removal at the component level, often through machined heat sinks, cold plates, or integrated thermal interfaces. The second is electromagnetic — RF housings, waveguide components, and EMI-shielded enclosures require machined cavity geometry with dimensional accuracy that determines electromagnetic performance, not just structural integrity. The third is miniaturisation: connector bodies, sensor housings, and precision electronics interfaces are getting smaller with each generation, pushing machining tolerance requirements to levels that require 5-axis capability and CMM verification.

The RF and microwave sector illustrates the precision requirement most clearly. A waveguide housing is machined to cavity dimensions that are a direct function of the target frequency — the cavity dimensions determine the cutoff frequency, and dimensional error translates directly to RF performance error. A bandpass filter housing machined 0.05 mm out of tolerance on the cavity depth will not perform to specification regardless of how the signal path is designed. The engineers specifying these parts know the tolerance they need; the machining supplier needs to be able to hold it, verify it, and document it.

Cold plates and liquid cooling systems for high-power electronics add another context: internal channel geometry must be accurate to ensure uniform coolant flow, heat transfer efficiency, and leak-free assembly. Channels that are over-depth undermine the wall thickness needed for pressure integrity; channels that are under-depth reduce cooling efficiency. The geometry is often complex — serpentine or parallel flow paths, inlet and outlet port features, sealing face geometry — and requires 5-axis machining to produce to tolerance from a single datum reference.

## Section 5: Materials
**Design:** Full-width table + intro copy. White background.
**Heading:** Materials

Material selection for electronics CNC machined components is driven by a combination of electrical, thermal, structural, and finishing requirements that are specific to the function of each part. Aluminium dominates because it provides the best combination of thermal conductivity, machinability, and anodising compatibility for enclosures and heat management components. Copper and brass are specified for connector bodies, RF components, and any application where electrical conductivity or plating adhesion is critical. Engineering polymers — PEEK and Delrin — appear in applications where electrical isolation, chemical resistance, or radiolucency is required and structural loads are within polymer capabilities.

| Material | Why Used in Electronics | Common Applications |
|---|---|---|
| Aluminium 6061-T6 | High thermal conductivity (167 W/m·K), excellent machinability, anodize-compatible, lightweight structural performance | RF housings, enclosures, chassis, heat sinks, cold plates |
| Aluminium 6082 | Higher strength than 6061 in some tempers, similar machinability and thermal conductivity — European electronics industry preference | Enclosures, structural electronics chassis, RF housing variants |
| Copper C101 (oxygen-free) | Highest conductivity (400 W/m·K) for thermal and electrical applications — oxygen-free grade for plating and RF applications | High-performance heat sinks, cold plates, RF contact components |
| Brass CW614N (free-machining) | Excellent machinability, good conductivity, gold and tin plating adhesion — connector body standard | Connector bodies, waveguide flanges, RF contacts, terminal hardware |
| Stainless 316L | Corrosion resistance, cleanroom compatibility, electropolish available — for sensor housings in demanding environments | Sensor housings, process-compatible electronics enclosures |
| PEEK / Delrin (POM-C) | Electrical isolation, chemical resistance, dimensional stability — for insulating components and test fixtures | PCB spacers, test jig bodies, insulating bushings, connector inserts |

## Section 6: Material Trade-off Cards
**Design:** 3-column card grid. Light grey background.
**Heading:** Getting the Material Right on Every Component

**When Aluminium Is Right**
Aluminium 6061-T6 is the default for the majority of electronics enclosures, RF housings, and heat sinks. Thermal conductivity of 167 W/m·K provides effective heat spreading from component to ambient. Density of 2.7 g/cm³ keeps assemblies light. Anodising compatibility — Type II for corrosion protection and appearance, Type III hard anodise for wear surfaces — provides a durable, electrically isolating surface finish that is well-matched to electronics assembly environments. 6061-T6 is machinable at high speeds, takes a good surface finish, and is available in a wide range of stock sizes. For cost-sensitive enclosure production and enclosures where anodising is the specified finish, 6061 is the optimum choice. 6082 is an alternative for European programmes or where the slightly different alloy chemistry is preferred by the specifying engineer.

**When Copper Is Right**
Copper is specified when thermal or electrical performance requirements exceed what aluminium can provide. Copper C110 (electrolytic tough pitch) has thermal conductivity of approximately 390 W/m·K — more than twice aluminium's 167 W/m·K. For high-power laser cooling, high-density computing cold plates, and RF components where skin depth and conductivity at microwave frequencies matter, copper is the only material that delivers the required performance. The trade-offs are significant: copper is approximately 3.5× denser than aluminium (8.96 vs 2.7 g/cm³), significantly more expensive per kilogram, and harder to machine at speed without correct tooling and coolant strategy. Oxygen-free copper (C101) is specified for RF applications and plating because standard C110 can outgas during soldering or vacuum processes. For applications where performance is the design driver and weight and cost are secondary, copper is the right answer.

**When Brass Is Right**
Brass is the default connector body material — specifically free-machining brass (CW614N, equivalent to 360 brass). It machines faster and more cleanly than any other engineering metal, producing sharp threads, accurate connector geometry, and excellent surface finish for electroplating. Gold, tin, and nickel plating adhesion on brass is well-characterised and process-controlled, which matters for the multi-decade connector qualification cycles that electronics programmes depend on. Electrical conductivity of approximately 26 MS/m is adequate for connector applications where current density is not extreme. For any connector body, threaded terminal, or small RF interface component that will be plated, brass is almost always the right specification on both engineering and cost grounds.

## Section 7: Processes / Services
**Design:** 4-column service card grid. Light grey background.
**Heading:** Manufacturing Capabilities for Electronics Applications
**Cards:** [[cnc-milling-services]] | [[cnc-turning-services]] | [[5-axis-cnc-machining-services]] | [[surface-finishing-services]]

RF housings and cold plates present multi-face machining requirements that expose the limitations of 3-axis approaches. An RF housing has cavity features on the interior face, connector port geometry on side faces, mounting flanges on the base, and lid mating features on the top — all of which must maintain tight positional relationships. Cavity depth and width are frequency-critical; connector port positions determine waveguide alignment; flange face flatness determines the seal against the lid. In a 3-axis multi-setup environment, positional error accumulates between setups. 5-axis machining holds all features from a single datum, eliminating that error source and providing the dimensional consistency that RF performance requires.

Surface finishing for electronics components is a functional specification. Anodising — Type II for standard electronics enclosures — provides a hard, electrically isolating, corrosion-resistant surface that is the standard finish for aluminium electronics chassis and housings. Type III hard anodise is specified for wear-critical surfaces. Alodine (chromate conversion coating per MIL-DTL-5541) is specified where electrical conductivity at the enclosure surface must be maintained — Type II anodise is an electrical insulator; alodine is conductive. For RF housings where grounding continuity across the seam between body and lid is required, alodine on mating faces and anodise on external surfaces is a common hybrid specification. Bright tin or gold plating on brass connector bodies is handled in-house; the finish process record covers plating thickness measurement and bath verification date.

## Section 8: 5-Axis Capability
**Design:** 2-column — text left, 5-axis machined component image right. White background.
**Heading:** Complex Geometry. One Setup.

Electronics CNC machined components frequently combine interior cavity geometry with exterior mounting features, connector ports on multiple faces, and sealing surfaces that must all meet tight dimensional requirements relative to each other. An RF housing for a microwave assembly has an interior cavity machined to frequency-specific dimensions, SMA or waveguide connector ports on two or three faces, mounting hole patterns on the base, and a lid mating face that must be flat to within ±0.005 mm to ensure EMI seal integrity. Producing all of these features in a 3-axis multi-setup environment introduces positional error between each setup change.

5-axis machining solves this by keeping the component in a single fixturing throughout the complete machining sequence. All features — cavity, connector ports, mounting holes, lid face — are machined from a single datum reference. Positional accuracy between features is the machine's positional accuracy, not the accumulated error of repeated re-fixturing. For RF housings where connector port position relative to cavity is a frequency-sensitive specification, the single-datum approach is the correct engineering solution.

At Weldo CNC, 3+2 positioning — where the part is locked at a fixed angle and three linear axes machine the feature — is the standard approach for multi-face electronics enclosures. The angular positions are set once; all features on each face are machined before repositioning. Simultaneous 5-axis — where all five axes move concurrently — is used for curved external profiles, organic heat sink geometry, and complex cooling channel access. The programming choice is made based on the geometry and tolerance requirement.

## Section 9: Documentation
**Design:** 4-column icon+description grid. White background.
**Heading:** Documentation Standard for Electronics Orders

**Material Certificate**
Mill cert coverage for every material: alloy grade, temper, heat number, chemical composition, and mechanical properties. For aluminium 6061-T6: ASTM B209 or equivalent. For copper C101 (oxygen-free): ASTM B170. For brass CW614N: EN 12164 or equivalent. Heat number logged before first cut; part-to-cert traceability maintained. For RF and microwave applications where material conductivity is part of the RF performance specification, the mill cert confirms the alloy grade and composition that the design was based on.

**CMM Dimensional Report**
Feature-by-feature dimensional report: nominal, tolerance, actual, pass/fail per feature. GD&T — geometric dimensioning and tolerancing — callouts reported per drawing revision: cavity depth and width, connector port position (true position), flange face flatness, sealing surface profile. For RF housings where cavity dimensions are frequency-critical, the actual measured values (not just pass/fail) are the relevant evidence — the measured cavity depth at multiple points across the floor of the cavity confirms dimensional uniformity.

**Finish Process Record**
For anodising, alodine (chromate conversion coating), electropolishing, or plating: process type, bath parameters, verification date, measured coating thickness, and masking plan confirmation. For anodised aluminium enclosures, coating thickness is measured on representative surfaces; for alodine, the process record confirms the MIL-DTL-5541 type and class. For plated brass connectors, plating thickness per IPC-4552 or equivalent is measured and documented.

**First Article Inspection (FAI)**
For new part numbers or revised drawings: ballooned drawing with all dimensions numbered and cross-referenced to the CMM report + material cert + finish record. First article inspection — FAI — packages are structured to support electronics programme qualification records. Confirm FAI requirement and any programme-specific format at quoting.
