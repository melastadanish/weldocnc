## Section 3: What Does Heat Sink Machining Require?

**H2:** What Does CNC Heat Sink Machining Require?

Weldo CNC machines heat sinks — aluminium 6061-T6/T651, aluminium 6063-T5, and copper C110 — to fin pitch tolerances of ±0.05 mm and base flatness of ±0.05 mm/100 mm, from its ISO 9001:2015 certified facility in Dongguan, China. Parts ship in 1–5 business days. Every order includes a CMM — coordinate measuring machine — dimensional inspection report confirming base flatness and mounting hole position, delivered with the parts. Tolerances on critical features hold to ±0.005 mm.

Three relationships define a machined heat sink: fin geometry (pitch, height, thickness — these control the convection area available per unit volume), base flatness (the mounting face must be flat to ±0.05 mm/100 mm to ensure contact with the thermal interface material — TIM — that bridges the gap between the heat sink and the component), and mounting hole position (bolt pattern must align to the PCB or component footprint within ±0.05 mm of the base datum). Miss any one of these three and the heat sink either runs hotter than calculated or fails to mount correctly.

Fin deflection is the core machining challenge. Thin fins — 1–2 mm thick, 20–40 mm tall — behave like cantilever beams under tool load. Climb milling — where the cutter engages the work in the feed direction rather than against it — reduces the lateral cutting force on each fin pass. Sharp carbide tooling lowers cutting resistance further. Together, these two practices hold fin thickness to ±0.05 mm on fins that would otherwise spring away from the tool.

## Section 4: Materials for Heat Sink Machining

**H2:** Materials for CNC Machined Heat Sinks

| Material | Grade | Thermal Conductivity | Why Used | Best For |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Aluminium | 6061-T6 / T651 | 167 W/m·K | High machinability, corrosion resistant, good thermal performance | General-purpose heat sinks, prototype and production quantities |
| Aluminium | 6063-T5 | 201 W/m·K | Higher conductivity than 6061, slightly softer — good fin detail | High-conductivity requirements where fin geometry is complex |
| Copper | C110 | 391 W/m·K | Highest thermal conductivity — 2.3× aluminium 6061 | High-power applications — power modules, RF amplifiers, laser diodes |
| Aluminium | 1100 | 222 W/m·K | Softest aluminium alloy — easy to machine, best conductivity in aluminium range | Applications where thermal performance is the primary driver over strength |

**Material selection note:** Aluminium 6061-T6 is the most common starting point — machinability, conductivity, and corrosion resistance in one grade. Aluminium 6063-T5 adds 20% more conductivity at the cost of slightly lower tensile strength (186 MPa vs 310 MPa for 6061). Copper C110 delivers the highest thermal performance of any confirmed grade but requires slower cutting speeds and heavier lubrication — machine time per part is longer, and tool wear is managed more aggressively than on aluminium. Specify copper when junction temperature calculations leave no margin for aluminium's lower conductivity.

## Section 6: How Weldo CNC Machines Heat Sinks

**H2:** How Weldo CNC Machines Heat Sinks

Base flatness is set first. The component mounting face — the surface that contacts the TIM and, through it, the component — is face-milled to ±0.05 mm/100 mm before any fin work begins. Every subsequent feature references this datum. Setting fins first, then correcting base flatness, degrades the relationship between fin height and TIM contact depth.

Fin machining follows a climb milling sequence with sharp carbide end mills. Climb milling — where the cutter feeds in the same direction as the work movement — reduces the lateral cutting force on each fin pass. Conventional milling pushes the fin away from the tool; climb milling pulls it toward the cutter, keeping deflection within the tolerance band. On 1 mm fins at 30 mm height, deflection under tool load without climb milling can exceed 0.2 mm — four times the ±0.05 mm tolerance. Carbide tool condition is checked after every batch: a worn edge raises cutting force and reintroduces deflection.

Copper C110 heat sinks run at cutting speeds 30–40% lower than aluminium. Higher lubrication flow protects the tool edge and clears chips — copper chips are tough and sticky compared to the broken chips aluminium produces. Cycle time per copper part is longer. Tool life per batch is actively monitored and replaced before wear reaches the point where fin geometry degrades.

CMM surface scanning verifies base flatness after machining. The scanner generates a point cloud across the full base area — maximum deviation, flatness across any 100 mm span, and localised low or high spots all reported numerically. A pass/fail result against the drawing specification goes into the inspection report that ships with every order.

**H2:** Why Engineers Choose Weldo CNC for Heat Sink Machining

**1. Fin deflection controlled by climb milling sequence**
Thin fins deflect under tool load. Climb milling — combined with sharp carbide tools — reduces lateral cutting force on each fin pass. Fin thickness holds to ±0.05 mm on 1–2 mm fins without requiring secondary correction passes.

**2. Base flatness verified by CMM surface scan — not point measurement**
A three-point surface check can pass a warped base. CMM surface scanning covers the full mounting face area and reports every deviation numerically. The TIM contact confirmation goes into the inspection report with every shipment.

**3. Aluminium 6063 vs 6061 selection explained — thermal conductivity trade-off**
Aluminium 6063-T5 offers 201 W/m·K conductivity vs 167 W/m·K for 6061-T6 — a 20% gain. The trade-off is tensile strength: 186 MPa vs 310 MPa. For heat sinks where fin geometry is the primary design constraint and structural load is low, 6063 is the correct grade. For heat sinks with thread engagement, press-fit inserts, or significant mechanical load, 6061 holds tolerance better under assembly stress.

## Section 9: FAQ

**H2:** Heat Sink Machining — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: What tolerance can Weldo CNC hold on heat sink fin pitch and base flatness?**

Fin pitch holds to ±0.05 mm across the full fin array. Base flatness — the TIM contact face — holds to ±0.05 mm/100 mm, verified by CMM surface scan. Mounting hole position holds to ±0.05 mm relative to the base datum. Through-hole diameters for press-fit inserts or heat pipes hold to ±0.025 mm. Specify all critical features and tolerances on your drawing — the CMM inspection report confirms each one numerically against the drawing specification.

**Q2: What materials do you machine for heat sinks?**

Confirmed heat sink materials include aluminium 6061-T6/T651, aluminium 6063-T5, aluminium 1100, and copper C110. Aluminium 6061-T6 is the standard for most heat sinks — machinability, 167 W/m·K conductivity, and corrosion resistance in one grade. Aluminium 6063-T5 adds conductivity (201 W/m·K) where the design requires it. Copper C110 at 391 W/m·K is used for high-power applications — power modules, RF amplifiers, laser diode sub-mounts — where aluminium conductivity leaves insufficient margin.

**Q3: How long does it take to machine heat sinks?**

Heat sink prototypes ship in 1–5 business days from drawing approval. Parts with complex fin arrays, press-fit heat pipe bores, or copper material may require the upper end of that window. Upload your STEP file for a 24-hour quote with lead time confirmation. Small-batch production runs — 101 to 10,000 pieces — ship in 5–15 business days.

**Q4: What is the minimum order quantity for heat sink machining?**

No minimum order quantity. Single-piece prototype heat sinks are accepted — common in thermal design validation where each iteration changes fin pitch, fin height, or base thickness before production geometry is confirmed.

**Q5: What file formats do you accept for heat sink machining quotes?**

STEP (.stp / .step) is required for 3D geometry. Include a 2D drawing specifying fin pitch tolerance, fin height, base flatness, mounting hole position tolerance, surface finish on the base face (Ra value), material grade, and any post-process requirements — anodizing type, colour, or heat pipe bore pre-anodize oversize. 2D drawings upload as PDF or DWG.

**Q6: What drives the cost of heat sink machining?**

Material is the first driver — copper C110 stock costs more per kilogram than aluminium, and copper cycle time is 30–40% longer. Fin count and depth drive cycle time directly — more fins, deeper fins, and tighter fin pitch all increase machine time. CMM surface scanning for base flatness verification adds time on precision orders. Anodizing adds a post-process operation. Include fin count, material, base dimensions, and any post-process requirements in the quote enquiry to get an accurate number.

**Q7: How does Weldo CNC compare to extrusion suppliers for heat sinks?**

Extruded heat sinks are lower cost per piece at high volumes but constrained to uniform cross-sections — fin pitch, fin height, and base thickness are fixed by the die. CNC machined heat sinks carry no tooling cost, accept any fin geometry, and allow features an extruded profile cannot produce: undercuts, blind pockets, counterbores, and non-uniform fin spacing. For prototypes, geometry iterations, and production volumes below the extrusion MOQ break-even point, CNC machining is the correct process. No die cost. No lead time for tooling.

**Q8: How do you verify base flatness on heat sinks?**

Base flatness is verified by CMM surface scanning across the full mounting face. The scanner builds a point cloud that reports maximum deviation, flatness over any 100 mm span, and any localised highs or lows. A pass/fail result against the drawing specification is included in the inspection report that ships with the parts. Single-point or three-point surface checks are not used — they cannot detect centre sag or edge roll on a base face that will carry TIM load.

**Q9: Can Weldo CNC anodize heat sinks after machining?**

Type II anodizing is available as a post-machining operation on aluminium heat sinks. Type II adds 2.5–12.5 µm per surface. Through-holes specified for press-fit heat pipes or inserts are machined oversize before anodizing to hold the required final diameter after coating builds up. Anodizing also increases surface emissivity — useful on natural-convection heat sinks where radiation contributes to cooling. Confirm anodizing colour and whether clear or black is required at the quote stage.

**Q10: What surface finish is required on the heat sink base face?**

The TIM contact face — the base mounting surface — requires Ra 0.8 µm to ensure full contact with the thermal interface material. Rougher surfaces create air pockets under the TIM layer that raise contact thermal resistance. Fin surfaces do not require the same finish: Ra 3.2 µm on fin faces is standard — the convection surface benefits from airflow turbulence, not mirror smoothness. Specify Ra values for both faces on your drawing to confirm which tolerance applies where.
