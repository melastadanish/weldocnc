## Section 5: Materials for Humanoid Robot CNC Machining
**Design:** Full-width table + intro copy. White background.
**Heading:** Materials

**Why material selection matters in robotics — and why the choice changes by joint.**
A humanoid robot is a weight budget problem. Every gram at a distal joint — wrist, ankle, finger — is load the proximal motor has to carry through its full range of motion, every cycle. The material decision for a torso frame (where stiffness and datum accuracy matter most) is different from the decision for a wrist pivot (where minimum cross-section and fatigue life under high-cycle loading matter most). Getting each component's material right isn't a specification exercise — it's an engineering trade-off that affects motor sizing, battery life, and structural margin across the whole system.

| Material | Why Used in Humanoid Robotics | Common Applications |
|---|---|---|
| **Aluminium 7075-T6** | Highest strength-to-weight of standard aluminium alloys — 2× stronger than 6061. Allows wall thickness reduction without structural compromise | Actuator housings, torso frames, structural joint links, spine components |
| **Aluminium 6061-T6** | Excellent machinability, better colour consistency for anodize than 7075, weldable | Motor casings, sensor mounts, secondary brackets, external visible components |
| **Titanium Grade 5 (Ti-6Al-4V)** | Strength-to-weight beats any aluminium alloy at small cross-sections. High fatigue limit for high-cycle joints. Low density for distal component weight saving | Wrist and ankle pivots, dexterous hand links, high-cycle fatigue joints |
| **4340 Steel** | High static strength and good fatigue resistance — correct material for high-contact-stress drive components | Harmonic drive housings, gear blanks, drive shafts, high-load pins |
| **17-4PH Stainless** | High-strength stainless, heat-treatable to H900 (over 1,300 MPa tensile). Corrosion resistance with structural performance | Harmonic drive components, actuator shafts, precision gear bodies |
| **PEEK** | High-strength engineering plastic. Lightweight, chemically resistant, machinable to tight tolerances | Dexterous hand spacers, non-structural pivot components, electrical isolation parts |

## Section 6: Material Trade-off Cards
**Design:** 3-column card grid. Light grey background.
**Heading:** Getting the Material Right on Every Component

**Card 1 — When 7075 Is Right**
7075-T6 is the default for structural links, actuator housings, and torso frames where strength-to-weight is the primary driver. At 572 MPa tensile strength — 2× stronger than 6061 — it lets wall thicknesses come down without compromising structural integrity. It doesn't weld, and it anodizes with less colour consistency than 6061. For internal structural parts, neither limitation matters. For external visible surfaces where colour uniformity matters, 6061 often makes more sense.

**Card 2 — When Titanium Makes the Weight Budget**
Titanium Grade 5 (Ti-6Al-4V) costs more to machine and significantly more in material. The argument for it: near-stainless corrosion resistance, fatigue performance at low cross-sections, and a strength-to-weight ratio that beats any aluminium alloy. In a 70 kg humanoid robot, a 50 g saving at the wrist joint — repeated across 6 joints — is 300 g off the extremities. That's load the motors don't carry. For joints under high-cycle fatigue loading, titanium's fatigue limit is a genuine engineering argument, not a premium specification.

**Card 3 — High-Strength Steel for Drive Components**
Harmonic drives, planetary gearboxes, and precision drive components aren't a place to save weight with aluminium. 4340 steel and 17-4PH stainless are the right materials where surface hardness, contact stress, and fatigue cycling matter. 17-4PH in H900 condition reaches over 1,300 MPa tensile — suitable for high-load drive shafts and gear components. These are machined regularly; the surface finish and dimensional requirements the gear geometry demands are understood.

## Section 7: Processes / Services
**Design:** 4-column service card grid. Light grey background.
**Heading:** Manufacturing Capabilities for Humanoid Robots
**Cards:** [[cnc-milling-services]] | [[cnc-turning-services]] | [[5-axis-cnc-machining-services]] | [[surface-finishing-services]]

5-axis CNC machining handles robot component geometry that can't be set up reliably in multiple 3-axis operations — actuator housings with bearing seats on multiple faces, joint links with compound pocket geometry, torso frames with features referenced to multiple datums. Simultaneous 5-axis motion holds positional accuracy across all faces from a single setup; 3+2 positional machining handles multi-face access where full simultaneous motion isn't required. The tolerance achieved on compound surface features differs between the two approaches — quoted accordingly.

Surface finishing for robot components follows function and visibility. Hardcoat anodize (Type III) for wear interfaces — bearing seat areas and articulation surfaces — where HV 400–600 hardness improves fatigue life and wear resistance. Anodize Type II for external visible components where colour consistency and corrosion protection are the requirement. Electroless nickel for steel drive components — even coverage including internal bores, HV 500–700 after heat treatment. Black oxide for steel hardware where near-zero dimensional impact is needed. For any tight-tolerance feature where coating thickness affects mating dimensions, masking is confirmed from the drawing before finishing starts.

**Prototype-to-production in one facility — how the iteration flow works:**
Humanoid robot development doesn't proceed in a straight line. Joint geometry changes after motion testing. Actuator housing wall thickness gets revised after thermal analysis. Hand component pivot geometry changes after grip force testing. Stage 1 (1–5 prototype parts): quote in 24 hours with DFM review, parts in 1–5 business days. Stage 2 (5–20 validation parts): same DFM review, CMM inspection on the first article. Stage 3 (20–200 pre-production): production fixtures built and approved, in-process inspection at defined intervals, dimensional report with every batch. Stage 4 (200+ production): same facility, same fixture, same tooling plan — no supplier transition, no re-approval.

## Section 8: 5-Axis Capability
**Design:** 2-column — text left, 5-axis machined robot component image right. White background.
**Heading:** Complex Geometry. One Setup.

Actuator housings, joint links, and torso frames for humanoid robots routinely require features on multiple faces — bearing seats, porting, bracket interfaces, cable routing channels, and weight-reduction pockets that all need to be positioned accurately relative to each other. In 3-axis machining, that means multiple setups and accumulated positional error between them. In 5-axis, it means one setup, one datum reference, consistent accuracy across every feature.

5-axis machining handles simultaneous multi-axis motion for compound surface geometry and 3+2 positional machining for multi-face part access. For humanoid robot components — where bearing bore concentricity to external datum faces is a hard requirement, not a suggestion — the 5-axis approach produces results that multiple 3-axis setups can't reliably replicate at the tolerances the geometry demands.

## Section 9: Documentation — What Ships With Every Order
**Design:** 4-column icon+description grid. White background.
**Heading:** The Documentation Standard for Robot Component Orders

**Material Certificate**
Mill cert for every material used — alloy grade, temper designation, heat number, chemical composition, and mechanical properties. Logged against your order reference before the first cut. For pre-production batches, each part is traceable to the mill cert for the stock it was cut from. If a material question arises after delivery, the file answers it.

**CMM Dimensional Report**
Feature-by-feature CMM inspection against your drawing revision: nominal value, tolerance, measured actual, pass/fail. Bearing seat bores, pivot interfaces, datum faces, and positional callouts all reported. The report is issued against your drawing revision and part number, and ships with the parts.

**Finish Process Record**
For anodized, nickel-plated, black-oxidised, or otherwise finished parts — process parameters, coating thickness measurement, and appearance verification. For any tight-tolerance feature where coating thickness affects mating dimensions: masking plan confirmed from drawing before processing, masked features re-measured after coating.

**First Article Inspection (FAI)**
For new parts entering production or where specifically required: ballooned drawing, full dimensional report, material cert, and finish record. For pre-production approval processes (PPAP), confirm requirement at order stage and it's planned in before the job enters the queue.

## Section 13: FAQ
**Design:** Accordion. White background. First item open.
**Heading:** Humanoid Robot CNC Machining — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: What's your lead time for humanoid robot prototype parts?**
1–5 business days for standard aluminium geometry. Complex 5-axis parts or titanium typically run toward the 5–7 day range depending on part complexity. If you're in a sprint before a demo or investor review, flag it in the RFQ — we'll tell you honestly what's achievable rather than quoting a lead time that slips.

**Q2: What tolerances can you hold on robot joint and actuator components?**
Standard precision CNC achieves ±0.005 mm on critical features — bearing seat bores, pivot interfaces, and datum faces. For complex multi-surface geometry machined simultaneously on 5-axis, tolerances to ±0.003 mm are achievable. Bearing seats are typically quoted to H7 bore tolerance. If your drawing carries GD&T callouts — true position, perpendicularity, concentricity — send the file and we'll review capability against each callout at quote stage.

**Q3: Can you machine harmonic drive components to the required tolerances?**
Yes. Harmonic drive geometry requires tight bore tolerances and specific surface finish on the wave generator contact surface. Flex spline housings, circular spline bores, and wave generator shafts are machined regularly. Confirm the specific tolerance callouts and surface finish requirements on the drawing — if anything is at the edge of verified capability, it's flagged at quote stage rather than attempted and missed.

**Q4: How do you handle DFM feedback between design iterations?**
Every quote includes a DFM review — wall thickness flags, bore depth assessments, feature accessibility for tooling. When a revised STEP is uploaded after changing actuator housing geometry, DFM notes come with the quote. An engineer reviews the file. The feedback comes with the number.

**Q5: Should I use 7075 or titanium for joint links?**
Depends on the weight budget and the loading. 7075-T6 at 572 MPa tensile is right for most joint links — significantly stronger than 6061, lighter than titanium, much cheaper to machine. Titanium Grade 5 is warranted where the cross-section needs to be small, the fatigue cycle count is high, or corrosion resistance matters at the joint interface. The cost difference is real — titanium machining takes longer and material cost is 5–8× aluminium. A direct cost comparison is available if the decision is live at quote stage.

**Q6: What order quantities do you accept for robot components?**
No minimum order quantity. Single prototypes, small validation batches of 5–20 parts, and pre-production runs of 200+ are all accepted. Prototypes run on a 1–5 day lead time. Small production batches typically run 5–15 business days. For larger production volumes, lead time is confirmed at quote stage based on part complexity and current capacity.

**Q7: What file formats do you need for a robot component quote?**
STEP (.stp / .step) is preferred for 3D geometry. SolidWorks (.sldprt), Parasolid (.x_t), IGES, and other standard CAD formats are also accepted. For 2D drawings: PDF is preferred. Send both the 3D model and 2D drawing — the drawing carries tolerances, GD&T callouts, and surface finish requirements that drive the machining plan. If you only have a STEP file, send it and we'll return DFM notes with the quote, but tolerances will be assumed as ISO 2768-m unless specified.

**Q8: Can you match the surface finish on external visible robot components?**
Yes. External visible surfaces — covers, links, sensor mounts — get bead blast plus anodize as standard for a consistent matte or satin result. If a specific colour, gloss level, or texture is required for a product launch or investor demo, specify it in the RFQ. For primary A-surfaces, cosmetic acceptance criteria are agreed before the batch runs.

**Q9: What factors affect the cost of machined robot components?**
Material grade (titanium costs significantly more to machine than aluminium), part complexity and number of setups, tolerance callouts (tighter tolerances require slower feeds and additional inspection time), surface finish and post-processing, and batch size (setup cost amortised over more parts). Upload a STEP file and drawing for a quote within 24 hours — for multi-component assemblies, send the full set and we'll quote by part.

**Q10: How does sourcing robot components from Weldo CNC compare to local suppliers?**
The practical advantages are proximity to Shenzhen's robotics ecosystem, 5-axis in-house capability for complex multi-face geometry, and a clear prototype-to-production path in one facility. For robotics engineers running rapid iteration cycles, the combination of 24-hour quoting, DFM at every stage, and 1–5 day prototype lead times compresses the design loop. Weldo CNC has operated since 2012, is ISO 9001:2015 certified, and machines 100+ materials across 100+ CNC machines.
