## Section 5: Weight, Strength & Material Trade-offs
**Design:** 3-column card grid. White background.
**Heading:** Getting the Material Right on Every Component

**Card 1 — When 7075 Is Right**
7075-T6 is the default for structural links, actuator housings, and torso frames where strength-to-weight is the primary driver. At 572 MPa tensile strength — 2× stronger than 6061 — it lets wall thicknesses come down without compromising structural integrity. It doesn't weld, and it anodizes with less colour consistency than 6061. For internal structural parts, neither limitation matters. For external visible surfaces where colour uniformity matters, 6061 often makes more sense.

**Card 2 — When Titanium Makes the Weight Budget**
Titanium Grade 5 (Ti-6Al-4V) costs more to machine and significantly more in material. The argument for it: near-stainless corrosion resistance, fatigue performance at low cross-sections, and a strength-to-weight ratio that beats any aluminium alloy. In a 70kg humanoid robot, a 50g saving at the wrist joint — repeated across 6 joints — is 300g off the extremities. That's load the motors don't carry. For joints under high-cycle fatigue loading, titanium's fatigue limit is a genuine engineering argument, not a premium specification.

**Card 3 — High-Strength Steel for Drive Components**
Harmonic drives, planetary gearboxes, and precision drive components aren't a place to save weight with aluminium. 4340 steel and 17-4PH stainless are the right materials where surface hardness, contact stress, and fatigue cycling matter. 17-4PH in H900 condition reaches over 1,300 MPa tensile — suitable for high-load drive shafts and gear components. We machine these regularly and understand the surface finish and dimensional requirements the gear geometry demands.

## Section 6: Built for Iteration
**Design:** Horizontal timeline / 4-stage process flow. Light grey background. Icon per stage.
**Heading:** From First Prototype to Pre-Production

Humanoid robot development doesn't proceed in a straight line. Joint geometry changes after motion testing. Actuator housing wall thickness gets revised after thermal analysis. Hand component pivot geometry changes after grip force testing. The manufacturing partner that handles this well isn't just fast — they flag DFM issues at every quote stage so the iteration cycle doesn't include a machining failure as a data point.

**Stage 1 — Prototype (1–5 parts)**
Upload STEP, specify material and tolerance class. Quote in 24 hours with DFM review — wall thickness, bore depth, feature accessibility for tooling. Parts in 3–5 days. If geometry needs to change, we tell you before cutting, not after.

**Stage 2 — Design Validation (5–20 parts)**
Revised geometry from prototype testing. Same DFM review process. Parts to the same drawing revision across the batch — consistent datum setup, consistent tooling. CMM inspection on the first article in the batch.

**Stage 3 — Pre-Production (20–200 parts)**
Geometry is confirmed. Production fixtures are built and approved. In-process inspection runs at defined intervals — not just first and last. Dimensional report with every batch. Material certs standard. If your programme needs a first article inspection package or PPAP documentation, confirm at order stage and it's planned in, not added at the end.

**Stage 4 — Production (200+ parts)**
Same facility, same fixture, same tooling plan. No supplier transition, no re-approval process. The production standard carries through from prototype.

## Section 7: 5-Axis Capability for Complex Robot Geometry
**Design:** 2-column — text left, 5-axis machined component image right. White background.
**Heading:** Complex Geometry. One Setup.

Actuator housings, joint links, and torso frames for humanoid robots routinely require features on multiple faces — bearing seats, porting, bracket interfaces, cable routing channels, and weight-reduction pockets that all need to be positioned accurately relative to each other. In 3-axis machining, that means multiple setups and accumulated positional error between them. In 5-axis, it means one setup, one datum reference, consistent accuracy across every feature.

Our 5-axis machining handles simultaneous multi-axis motion for compound surface geometry and 3+2 positional machining for multi-face part access. For humanoid robot components — where bearing bore concentricity to external datum faces is a hard requirement, not a suggestion — the 5-axis approach produces results that multiple 3-axis setups can't reliably replicate at the tolerances the geometry demands.

| **Anodize Type II** | External visible components, sensor mounts, covers | Corrosion protection, colour (black standard for robotics). +5–25µm per side — account for on tight fits |

| **Polishing** | Harmonic drive contact surfaces, precision pivot interfaces | Ra down to 0.1µm — reduces friction and wear at high-cycle contact interfaces |

## Section 12: FAQ
**Design:** Accordion. White background. First item open.
**Heading:** Humanoid Robot CNC Machining — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: What's your lead time for humanoid robot prototype parts?**
From 3 business days for standard aluminium geometry. Complex 5-axis parts or titanium typically 5–7 days. If you're in a sprint before a demo or investor review, flag it in the RFQ — we'll tell you honestly what's achievable rather than quoting a lead time that slips.

**Q2: Can you machine harmonic drive components to the required tolerances?**
Yes. Harmonic drive geometry requires tight bore tolerances and specific surface finish on the wave generator contact surface. We machine flex spline housings, circular spline bores, and wave generator shafts regularly. Confirm the specific tolerance callouts and surface finish requirements on the drawing — if anything is at the edge of our verified capability, we'll tell you at quote stage rather than attempt and miss.

**Q3: How do you handle DFM feedback between design iterations?**
Every quote includes a DFM review — wall thickness flags, bore depth assessments, feature accessibility. When you upload a revised STEP after changing actuator housing geometry, you get DFM notes with the quote. An engineer reviews the file. The feedback comes with the number.

**Q4: Should I use 7075 or titanium for joint links?**
Depends on the weight budget and the loading. 7075-T6 at 572 MPa tensile is right for most joint links — significantly stronger than 6061, lighter than titanium, much cheaper to machine. Titanium Grade 5 is warranted where the cross-section needs to be small, the fatigue cycle count is high, or corrosion resistance matters at the joint interface. The cost difference is real — titanium machining takes longer and material cost is 5–8× aluminium. We'll give you a direct cost comparison if the decision is live.

**Q5: Can you match the surface finish on external visible robot components?**
Yes. External visible surfaces — covers, links, sensor mounts — get bead blast plus anodize as standard for a consistent matte or satin result. If a specific colour, gloss level, or texture is required for a product launch or investor demo, specify it in the RFQ. For primary A-surfaces, we agree cosmetic acceptance criteria before the batch runs.

**Q6: Can you scale from prototype to pre-production without a supplier change?**
Yes — that's deliberate. Prototype geometry, validation batches, and pre-production all run through the same facility with the same fixture design and tooling plan. When your geometry locks and volumes increase, the transition is a quantity change, not a supplier change. No re-approval, no new supplier audit, no re-running DFM from scratch.
