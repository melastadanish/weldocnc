## Section 3: What Does Humanoid Robot Finger Machining Require?

**H2:** What Does Humanoid Robot Finger Machining Require?

Weldo CNC machines humanoid robot finger phalanx segments — proximal, middle, and tip phalanx — in aluminium 6061-T6, aluminium 7075-T6, TC4 titanium (Ti-6Al-4V), PEEK, and 303 stainless steel, to pin bore tolerances of ±0.005 mm and joint face flatness of ±0.02 mm/50 mm, from its ISO 9001:2015 certified facility in Dongguan, China. Prototype finger segments ship in 1–5 business days. Every pin bore order includes a full CMM — coordinate measuring machine — dimensional inspection report, delivered with the parts.

Finger machining is defined by a single primary datum: the joint pin bore axis. Every other feature on the phalanx segment — the tendon routing channel, the mating joint faces, and the outer profile — is positioned relative to that bore axis. When pin bores across a multi-segment finger are held to ±0.005 mm and referenced to the same datum, joint play — the unwanted angular movement at each knuckle — stays within the kinematic specification. A tendon routing channel held to ±0.05 mm width passes the Bowden cable without friction buildup. Joint face flatness at ±0.02 mm/50 mm ensures the mating faces close without rocking under actuator load.

Phalanx segments are small parts: 15–60 mm in length, 2–8 g per segment. Small size makes fixturing the dominant process risk. Distortion from clamping pressure shifts bore position and invalidates the pin bore datum. Each phalanx geometry requires custom soft jaws — dedicated workholding ground to the part outer profile — so clamping load distributes evenly and the bore axis holds position through the full machining sequence.

## Section 6: How Weldo CNC Machines Finger Phalanx Segments

**H2:** The Finger Phalanx Machining Sequence — Datum to Final Part

Pin bore first. The joint pin bore is machined and measured before any other feature is cut. Establishing the bore datum early means every subsequent feature — the tendon channel, joint faces, outer profile — references the correct axis. A reversed sequence that machines the outer profile first and bores later introduces position error that no subsequent operation corrects.

Small part fixturing drives the setup design. Phalanx segments at 15–60 mm length do not grip reliably in standard vises. Custom soft jaws — aluminium workholding blocks machined to the exact outer profile of each phalanx geometry — hold the segment without line contact. Line contact from a flat jaw face concentrates clamping force at one point, deflects the part, and shifts the bore axis by 0.01–0.03 mm relative to the unclamped position. Soft jaws distribute the clamping load across the part face; bore axis stays fixed.

5-axis machining covers tendon channel geometry and curved outer profiles. Tendon routing channels on humanoid finger segments often run at compound angles relative to the pin bore axis — a 3-axis setup requires multiple repositions to cut the full channel length. Each reposition adds cumulative position error. A 5-axis simultaneous pass cuts the complete tendon channel in one clamping, with channel width held to ±0.05 mm along the full run.

TC4 titanium tip phalanx segments require low cutting speed and sharp carbide tooling. TC4 work-hardens under heat — aggressive cutting parameters build heat at the cutting edge, harden the surface ahead of the tool, and accelerate tool wear. Controlled cutting speed (typically 40–60 m/min for TC4), high-pressure coolant, and fresh carbide tooling prevent surface hardening on the bore wall where the PEEK bushing seats. PEEK bushings are press-fit into the bore after machining — bore diameter and surface finish Ra 0.8 µm are verified by CMM before bushing installation.

**Q6: What drives the cost of humanoid robot finger phalanx machining?**

Material (TC4 titanium and PEEK cost more per kilogram than 6061), cycle time (5-axis tendon channel geometry adds machining time over simple profiles), custom soft jaw setup (one-time per geometry — amortised across the order quantity), and CMM inspection (pin bore and joint face measurement adds time per part). **Q7: How does Weldo CNC verify pin bore diameter and joint face flatness?**

CMM — coordinate measuring machine — measures the pin bore diameter at three cross-sections along the bore depth, and reports diameter, roundness, and position relative to the joint face. Joint face flatness is verified by CMM surface scan across the full mating face area — not a single-point height check. Both measurements appear in the dimensional inspection report shipped with every precision bore order.

**Q8: How does Weldo CNC compare to a general CNC shop for humanoid robot finger machining?**

A general CNC shop machines finger phalanx segments as small milled parts — standard vise clamping, pin bore as a secondary operation after outer profile. That sequence accumulates position error between the outer profile datum and the bore axis. Weldo CNC uses pin bore datum first — the bore is the primary reference, all features cut relative to it. Custom soft jaws prevent clamping distortion on small parts. 5-axis simultaneous machining cuts tendon channels and outer profiles in one setup. The CMM inspection report verifies bore position, not just bore diameter. These are four separate process differences from general-purpose small-part machining.

**Q9: Can Weldo CNC machine the PEEK joint bushings and aluminium phalanx in the same order?**

Yes. PEEK bushings machine in the same facility as the aluminium and titanium phalanx segments. The bushing outer diameter is machined to match the measured bore in the phalanx — the press-fit interference is confirmed against the actual bore CMM reading before bushing delivery. Ordering bushing and phalanx together in one batch reduces the iteration cycle versus sourcing them separately and adjusting fit in the field.

**Q10: What thread standard applies to actuator attachment features on finger segments?**

Actuator attachment threads on finger phalanx segments are machined to M2–M4, class 6H fit. Thread class 6H — the standard internal thread tolerance — is confirmed by thread gauge inspection on every threaded feature. Specify thread size, pitch, depth, and class on the 2D drawing.
