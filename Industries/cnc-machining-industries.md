## Section 3: Why Industry-Specific Machining Matters
**Design:** 2-column — text left, image right. White background.
**Heading:** The Part Knows What Industry It Came From

An aerospace bracket and an automotive bracket can look identical on a drawing. The dimensions might be similar. The material might be the same alloy. But the aerospace bracket needs a mill cert with a heat number, a CMM report per feature, a finish process record, and material traceability that survives a quality audit two years later. The automotive bracket needs to be in production quantities at a competitive unit cost with dimensional consistency across 500 parts. Both are valid requirements. Neither is served well by treating them the same.

Industry context shapes how we quote, how we plan, and what documentation we generate. A medical device housing gets biocompatibility and traceability reviewed before anything else. A humanoid robot joint link gets DFM focused on weight and iteration speed — because the geometry is changing again next week. A downhole energy component gets material selection reviewed against pressure and temperature before the tolerances are even discussed. The machining process is similar across all of them. The engineering conversation around it is different every time.

That's why our industry pages exist — not to add pages to a website, but to have the right conversation from the start.

## Section 4: What's Consistent Across Every Industry
**Design:** 5-point icon list or 2-column grid. Light grey background.
**Heading:** The Standard, Regardless of Application

**DFM review with every quote.**
Before a price goes out, an engineer reviews the file. Wall thickness, feature accessibility, tolerance feasibility, finishing interactions. If something will cause a problem in machining, finishing, or at inspection — it's raised at quote stage, not after cutting starts.

**CMM inspection on every order.**
Not sampled. Every order is measured against the drawing. Nominal, tolerance, actual, pass/fail — per feature. The report ships with the parts.

**Material certificates as standard.**
Every material verified against a mill cert before cutting starts. Alloy grade, temper, heat number. For multi-part orders, each part is traceable to its stock cert.

**ISO 9001:2015 certified facility.**
ISO 9001:2015 is the framework. What it covers in practice: material receipt verification, in-process checks at defined intervals, CMM final inspection, and documentation control. Non-conformances stop the job before the next operation — not at final inspection.

**One point of contact.**
Your order is quoted, planned, machined, inspected, and shipped from our Dongguan facility. No brokering to subcontractors, no gap in accountability between operations. When a question comes up mid-production, one conversation resolves it.

## Section 5: Quality & Certifications
**Design:** RS-01. Dark navy background. White text.

**Pre-Production:**
Material verified against mill certificate before any job starts. Drawing reviewed for DFM issues, tolerance feasibility, and finish interactions. Industry-specific documentation requirements confirmed before the job enters the queue.

**In-Production:**
In-process checks at defined intervals using calibrated hand tools and CMM. Datum references verified after each setup change. Non-conformances trigger an NCR before the job continues.

**Final-Production:**
CMM inspection against drawing revision. Measured values recorded per feature — nominal, tolerance, actual, pass/fail. Material cert, dimensional report, and packing list ship with every order. Industry-specific documentation — FAI, PPAP, finish process records — where required.
