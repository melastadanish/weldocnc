## Section 3: What Does CNC Transmission Part Machining Require?

**H2:** What Does CNC Transmission Part Machining Require?

Weldo CNC machines transmission parts — gear blanks, synchroniser rings, shift forks, bearing housings, and selector hubs — in alloy steel 4140 (pre-hardened), 8620 (case-hardened for gear blanks), 4340, 17-4 PH stainless steel, and aluminium 6061, to gear bore tolerances of ±0.005 mm and bearing seat surface finish Ra 0.8 µm, from its ISO 9001:2015 certified facility in Dongguan, China. Prototype transmission parts ship in 1–5 business days. Every precision bore order includes a full CMM — coordinate measuring machine — dimensional inspection report delivered with the parts.

Transmission part machining is governed by three simultaneous tolerance relationships. Gear bores must achieve an interference fit with the shaft — ±0.005 mm — so the gear transmits torque without fretting. Bearing seats must hold ±0.005 mm diameter and surface finish Ra 0.8 µm — the oil film in a hydrodynamic bearing requires a surface that is neither too rough nor too smooth to sustain pressure. Shift fork bores must clear the selector rail at ±0.01 mm — tight enough to control axial play, loose enough to slide without binding.

The primary datum for transmission part machining is the gear pitch axis — not the housing outer face. Setting the pitch axis as the primary datum ties gear bore position, bearing seat concentricity, and shift groove position directly to the functional axis, not to a reference face that may not be square to the gears. Any deviation from this datum strategy accumulates as transmission noise, premature bearing failure, or missed shift.

## Section 6: Transmission Part Machining Process

**H2:** How Weldo CNC Machines Transmission Parts

Datum selection drives the transmission machining sequence. The primary datum is the gear pitch axis — established by the first bore operation and carried through every subsequent feature. Bearing seats, shift fork bores, and synchroniser ring fits all position relative to this axis. A housing outer face is convenient to fixture from, but it is not the functional reference — using it as the primary datum shifts tolerance accumulation onto the features that matter most.

For 8620 gear blanks, the machining sequence is: rough bore to leave 0.3–0.5 mm stock on critical diameters → send for carburising (case hardening) → return to machine → finish bore to final diameter and surface finish. Carburising adds a wear-resistant surface layer — 0.5–1.5 mm deep, typically 58–62 HRC surface hardness. The finish bore after case hardening cuts through any distortion from the heat treatment cycle and produces the final ±0.005 mm gear bore diameter.

4140 steel work-hardens under inconsistent cutting conditions. Interrupted cuts — across keyways, cross-drilled holes, or bolt patterns — accelerate tool wear at the entry and exit of each interruption. Consistent cutting speed and feed rate across the full bore cycle prevents localised work hardening at the tool re-entry point. Tooling is confirmed before production starts and replaced at defined intervals — not when surface finish degrades.

## Section 8: Why Engineers Specify Weldo CNC for Transmission Part Machining

**H2:** Why Engineers Specify Weldo CNC for Transmission Part Machining

**1. Gear pitch axis as the primary datum — not the housing face**
All transmission bore features — gear bores, bearing seats, shift fork bores — machine relative to the gear pitch axis. This ties tolerance directly to the functional reference, which is where transmission noise, bearing load, and shift feel originate. Fixturing from a convenient external face saves setup time but accumulates error on the features that drive transmission performance.

**2. Case hardening sequence built into the 8620 workflow**
8620 gear blanks follow a confirmed sequence: rough bore → carburise → finish bore. The finish bore after carburising removes the 0.05–0.15 mm of distortion introduced by the heat treatment cycle. Skipping this sequence — finishing before carburising — produces gear bores that are out of tolerance after hardening, with no recovery path without removing all the case depth.

**3. 4140 work hardening managed by consistent cutting parameters**
Interrupted cuts on 4140 — across keyways and cross-holes — accelerate tool wear at each re-entry. Consistent cutting speed and feed rate across the complete bore cycle prevent localised work hardening. Tooling is replaced on a defined interval, not reactively when surface finish starts to degrade.

**4. Bearing seat surface finish verified against Ra 0.8 µm — not estimated**
Bearing seats ship at Ra 0.8 µm, confirmed by surface profilometer measurement. A bearing seat at Ra 1.6 µm looks similar visually but cannot sustain a consistent hydrodynamic oil film — the result is premature bearing wear under load.

**5. CMM dimensional report and material certificate in every shipment**
Gear bore diameter, bearing seat diameter, bore concentricity, shift fork bore, and synchroniser ring inner diameter — all measured and recorded. Material certificate confirming steel grade and heat treatment condition ships with every order.

## Section 9: FAQ

**H2:** Transmission Part Machining — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: What tolerance can Weldo CNC hold on transmission gear bores?**

Gear bores hold to ±0.005 mm — an interference fit tolerance — verified by CMM after finish boring. Bearing seats hold ±0.005 mm diameter and surface finish Ra 0.8 µm, confirmed by profilometer measurement. Shift fork bores hold ±0.01 mm for clearance fit on the selector rail. Specify critical tolerances on your drawing — each feature class has a different tolerance requirement and they are measured independently.

**Q2: What materials do you machine for transmission parts?**

Confirmed materials for transmission parts include 4140 alloy steel (pre-hardened, for shift forks and bearing housings), 8620 alloy steel (case-hardened for gear blanks), 4340 alloy steel (for high-load shafts), 17-4 PH stainless steel (for fluid-contact selector components), and aluminium 6061-T6 (for transmission housing covers and retainer plates). Material selection depends on whether the part requires surface hardness, through-hardness, or corrosion resistance — confirm at the quote stage.

**Q3: How does case hardening affect the machining sequence for 8620 gear blanks?**

8620 gear blanks go through carburising — case hardening — after rough machining and before finish boring. Carburising adds a hard surface layer (typically 58–62 HRC, 0.5–1.5 mm deep) and distorts bores by 0.05–0.15 mm. Finish boring runs after carburising to remove this distortion and produce the final ±0.005 mm gear bore. If finish boring runs before carburising, the gear bore will be out of tolerance after hardening — with no recovery path available.

**Q9: What is the concentricity requirement for shaft bores in transmission parts?**

Shaft bore concentricity — the alignment of the shaft bore axis to the gear pitch axis — holds to ±0.01 mm TIR (total indicator runout) on precision orders. Weldo CNC measures and reports concentricity numerically in the CMM inspection report. A shaft bore that is correctly sized but eccentric to the pitch axis produces the same gear noise and bearing load as an undersized bore — concentricity is measured as a separate check, not inferred from bore diameter alone.

**Q10: What thread class applies to transmission shift mechanism threads?**

Shift mechanism threads use class 6H for internal threads and class 6g for external threads — the standard fit class for automotive selector mechanisms. These classes are verified by thread gauging. Specify thread class on the drawing; if no class is called out, 6H/6g is applied as the default.
