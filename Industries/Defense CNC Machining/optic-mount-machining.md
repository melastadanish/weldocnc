## Section 3: What Does Optic Mount Machining Require?

**H2:** What Does CNC Optic Mount Machining Require?

Weldo CNC machines optic mounts — scope rings, red dot bases, Picatinny rail mounts, and dovetail adapters — in aluminium 6061-T6, aluminium 7075-T6, TC4 titanium (Ti-6Al-4V), and 17-4 PH stainless steel (H900 condition), to a Picatinny cross-bolt bore tolerance of ±0.005 mm and rail contact face flatness of ±0.02 mm/100 mm, from its ISO 9001:2015 certified facility in Dongguan, China. Prototype optic mounts ship in 1–5 business days. Every precision bore order includes a full CMM — coordinate measuring machine — dimensional inspection report confirming bore position, rail face flatness, and cross-bolt hole location.

Optic mount machining is defined by one tolerance relationship above all others: the Picatinny rail interface. Rail contact face flatness, cross-bolt bore position, and locking lug clearance must all be held relative to the same primary datum — the rail contact surface itself, not the outer housing. A cross-bolt bore held to ±0.005 mm but referenced from the wrong datum will misalign the optic on the rail. Datum selection drives the machining sequence.

Hard anodize — MIL-A-8625 Type III — is the standard surface protection for aluminium optic mounts. The coating adds 10–50 µm of thickness inside every bore. Mounting bores are machined oversize before anodizing to maintain the correct interference or clearance fit after coating — a step that must be planned into the drawing before machining starts.

## Section 4: Materials for Optic Mount Machining

**H2:** Materials for CNC Machined Optic Mounts

| Material | Grade | Why Used | Best For |
|---|---|---|---|
| Aluminium | 6061-T6 / T651 | Lightweight, excellent machinability, anodize-ready | Standard scope rings, red dot bases, lightweight rail mounts |
| Aluminium | 7075-T6 | Higher strength than 6061 — 572 MPa tensile | Heavy optic mounts, high-recoil applications, structural clamping bodies |
| Titanium | TC4 / Ti-6Al-4V | Near-7075 strength with better corrosion resistance, weight saving | Mounts where both low weight and environmental durability are required |
| Stainless Steel | 303 | Corrosion resistant, free-machining | Cross-bolt inserts, elevation and windage screws, corrosion-exposed fasteners |
| Stainless Steel | 17-4 PH (H900) | High strength — 1310 MPa tensile — in H900 condition | Locking mechanism components, clamping screws, high-load retention parts |

**Material selection note — 7075-T6:** Aluminium 7075-T6 is susceptible to stress corrosion cracking at bore edges under sustained load. Chamfer and radius specifications at bore entries must be held; sharp edges are a stress riser. Confirm radii requirements on the drawing before quoting.

**Material selection note — TC4 titanium:** TC4 — Ti-6Al-4V, also written Ti6Al4V — work-hardens under interrupted cuts. Consistent feed rate and cutting speed are required for each pass; stopping mid-cut and resuming raises surface hardness. Insert change intervals are set conservatively to avoid tool pressure buildup.

## Section 6: How Weldo CNC Machines Optic Mounts

**H2:** The Optic Mount Machining Process at Weldo CNC

Datum setup comes first. The Picatinny rail contact surface is indicated and confirmed as the primary datum before any bore or hole is cut. All subsequent features — cross-bolt bore, elevation bore, dovetail slot, clamping screw holes — are positioned relative to that rail face. Setting the fixture from the outer housing instead would transfer datum error directly into rail-to-optic alignment.

Cross-bolt bore machining follows a roughing, semi-finishing, and finishing sequence. The finish pass uses a precision boring bar at a controlled feed and depth of cut — tool deflection must stay below the ±0.005 mm tolerance band, which keeps boring bar overhang within four times the bar diameter. After boring, CMM verifies bore diameter and position relative to the rail datum before the part moves to the next operation.

Hard anodize dimensional planning begins at the quoting stage. For aluminium 6061-T6 and 7075-T6 mounts, mounting bores and the cross-bolt bore are machined oversize by the expected coating build-up — typically 15–25 µm per face — so the bore after MIL-A-8625 Type III anodize lands on the drawing nominal. A bore machined to nominal and then anodized will be undersize. This is a machining protocol step, not a rework step.

Dovetail slot machining uses a dovetail cutter at controlled depth-of-cut increments. The slot width — held to ±0.010 mm — is confirmed by CMM before the part leaves the cell. Rail interface surface finish is measured by a profilometer and recorded in the inspection report alongside the CMM data.

## Section 8: Why Engineers Specify Weldo CNC for Optic Mount Machining

**H2:** Why Engineers Specify Weldo CNC for Optic Mount Machining

**1. Rail datum — features positioned from the Picatinny interface, not the housing**
Cross-bolt bore position and hole location are measured relative to the rail contact surface. This is the correct datum for a Picatinny optic mount — the dimension that matters is optic-to-rail alignment, not optic-to-housing. A bore position correct relative to the outer housing may still misalign the optic if the housing is not perfectly parallel to the rail face.

**2. Hard anodize dimensional planning from the quoting stage**
MIL-A-8625 Type III hard anodize adds 10–50 µm of coating thickness inside bores. Weldo CNC machines critical bores oversize before anodizing — by the calculated coating build-up for the specified bore diameter and coating specification. Bores machined to drawing nominal and then anodized will be undersize. Planning the oversize before machining is the correct method; reworking after anodizing is not.

**3. TC4 and 7075-T6 confirmed — with material-specific machining protocols**
Both high-strength alloys require different tooling and parameter strategies than 6061. For 7075-T6, stress riser control at bore edges — chamfer and radius hold — prevents stress corrosion cracking at locking surfaces. For TC4, consistent feed rate and conservative insert change intervals prevent work hardening. Both protocols are applied to every order — not managed per technician preference.

**4. CMM flatness scan on the rail contact face — not a single-point check**
Rail face flatness is verified by CMM surface scanning over the full contact area. A three-point check may pass while a localised high point or warp causes the optic to rock on the rail. The CMM surface scan map is included in the dimensional inspection report shipped with the parts.

**5. Direct facility — in-house machining from one quality system**
100+ CNC machines in Dongguan operate under one ISO 9001:2015 quality system. Optic mount machining — boring, milling, drilling — runs in-house. No subcontracting on standard orders.

## Section 9: FAQ

**H2:** Optic Mount Machining — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: What tolerance can Weldo CNC hold on Picatinny optic mount bores?**

Picatinny cross-bolt bore diameter holds to ±0.005 mm — confirmed by CMM after machining. Cross-bolt hole position relative to the rail datum holds to ±0.025 mm. Elevation and windage adjustment bores hold to ±0.010 mm. Rail contact face flatness holds to ±0.02 mm/100 mm, verified by CMM surface scan over the full rail interface — not by a single-point measurement. Specify critical tolerances and datum references on your drawing.

**Q2: What materials do you machine for optic mounts?**

Confirmed materials for optic mount machining include aluminium 6061-T6, aluminium 7075-T6, TC4 titanium (Ti-6Al-4V), 303 stainless steel, and 17-4 PH stainless in H900 condition. Aluminium 6061-T6 is standard for lightweight mounts and takes Type III hard anodize well. Aluminium 7075-T6 — 572 MPa tensile — covers heavy optics and high-recoil applications. TC4 provides near-7075 strength with added corrosion resistance where weight also matters. 17-4 PH in H900 condition, at 1310 MPa tensile, is used for locking mechanism components and high-load clamping screws.

**Q3: How long does it take to machine optic mounts?**

Prototype optic mounts ship in 1–5 business days from drawing approval. Parts requiring Type III hard anodize — which is a post-machining operation — may add 1–2 days for the anodize cycle, depending on the anodize schedule. **Q4: What is the minimum order quantity for optic mount machining?**

No minimum order quantity. Single-piece prototype optic mounts are accepted — common in defence product development where each design iteration changes bore position, rail interface geometry, or clamping mechanism configuration.

**Q5: What file formats do you accept for optic mount quotes?**

STEP (.stp / .step) is required for 3D geometry. Include a 2D drawing that specifies cross-bolt bore diameter and tolerance, bore position relative to the rail datum (not the housing), rail contact face flatness, dovetail slot width and tolerance, surface finish (Ra) on the rail interface, thread class on adjustment and clamping screws, and material grade with temper. Also specify whether Type III hard anodize is required — bore oversize planning depends on the anodize specification.

**Q6: What drives the cost of optic mount machining?**

Material — 7075-T6 and TC4 billet cost more than 6061. Cycle time — multiple bore operations, dovetail slot machining, and thread cutting take longer than simple profiles. Inspection — CMM bore and flatness measurement adds time. Post-process — Type III hard anodize adds an operation and requires oversize bore machining planning. Include material grade, bore count, thread requirements, and anodize specification in your quote enquiry for an accurate response.

**Q7: How does Weldo CNC compare to other optic mount machining suppliers on datum referencing?**

Many suppliers machine optic mount bores from the outer housing face — the largest, easiest-to-fixture surface. Weldo CNC fixtures and references from the Picatinny rail contact surface, which is the correct datum for optic alignment. A bore position measured from the housing face is correct relative to the housing; it is not necessarily correct relative to the rail. For a product where the rail-to-optic relationship drives point-of-impact accuracy, the datum matters. Request a datum reference confirmation from any supplier you evaluate — ask which surface the cross-bolt bore position is measured from.

**Q8: How is rail contact face flatness verified?**

Flatness of the Picatinny rail contact face is verified by CMM surface scanning over the complete contact area. The scanner generates a point cloud that reports maximum deviation, flatness across any 100 mm span, and any localised high or low points. A pass/fail result against the drawing specification is included in the CMM dimensional inspection report shipped with the parts. Three-point or four-corner checks are not used — they cannot detect centre warps or edge roll that cause optic rocking under recoil.

**Q9: Does Type III hard anodize affect bore tolerances on optic mounts?**

Yes — and the bore must be machined oversize before anodizing to account for it. MIL-A-8625 Type III hard anodize adds 10–50 µm of coating thickness per surface, with the typical build for optic mount applications in the 15–25 µm per face range. A 10 mm cross-bolt bore machined to 10.000 mm nominal and then anodized may measure 9.960–9.970 mm after coating — which puts it outside a ±0.005 mm tolerance. Weldo CNC calculates the expected coating thickness and machines bores oversize before anodizing so the finished dimension after coating lands on drawing nominal.

**Q10: What thread class is standard on optic mount adjustment screws?**

Elevation and windage adjustment screws use 6H internal thread class on the body and 6g external thread class on the screw — the standard metric thread fit for precision adjustment mechanisms. Thread class 6H/6g provides a controlled clearance fit with consistent feel and no play under recoil. A looser thread class — such as 7H/8g — introduces play that causes zero shift. Specify the thread class explicitly on your drawing; do not rely on the default.
