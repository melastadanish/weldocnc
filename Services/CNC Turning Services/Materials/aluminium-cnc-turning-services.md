## Section 3: What is Aluminium CNC Turning?

**H2:** What is Aluminium CNC Turning?

Weldo CNC turns aluminium — 6061-T6, 7075-T6, 2024-T3, 5052-H32, and 6082-T6 — to tolerances of ±0.005 mm on OD, ID, and length dimensions, from its ISO 9001:2015 certified facility in Dongguan, China. Prototype parts ship in 1–5 business days. Every tight-tolerance order includes a full CMM — coordinate measuring machine — dimensional inspection report, delivered with the parts.

Engineers specify aluminium for turned parts when weight-to-strength ratio drives the design. Tensile strength for 6061-T6 sits at 310 MPa with a density of 2.70 g/cm³ — roughly one-third the weight of steel at comparable structural stiffness for most shaft and housing geometries. Corrosion resistance is inherent to the alloy, and the machinability rating for 6061 is among the highest of any engineering metal.

What determines how well aluminium turns is alloy temper, not just alloy designation. Soft-temper aluminium (O-temper) gums tools and produces built-up edge on the cutting face. Heat-treated grades — T4, T6 — machine cleanly at high cutting speeds. Chip control matters: aluminium forms long, stringy chips at low feeds. Short-chip strategies — higher feed per revolution, correct tool nose radius — prevent chip wrapping on the spindle and workpiece.

Weldo CNC has turned aluminium components since 2012. Part types include hydraulic valve bodies, motor shafts, optical housings, pneumatic fittings, heat exchanger components, and precision bushings — across aerospace, medical, electronics, and industrial applications.

## Section 5: Material Trade-off Cards

**H2:** Choosing the Right Aluminium Grade for Turned Parts

**Subheading:** Grade selection is an engineering decision — the wrong alloy delivers a lower-performing part at the same machining cost.

**Card 1 — When 6061-T6 is the Right Choice**

6061-T6 is the default for turned aluminium for good reason. Its machinability rating is excellent — high cutting speed, clean chip formation, no built-up edge. Thread engagement is predictable, anodizing takes evenly, and the 310 MPa tensile strength covers most structural shaft and housing loads. For 80% of aluminium turning enquiries, 6061-T6 is the correct answer.

*Key spec: Tensile 310 MPa · Density 2.70 g/cm³ · Machinability: Excellent*

**Card 2 — When 7075-T6 Changes the Equation**

7075-T6 delivers 572 MPa tensile strength — nearly double 6061 — for parts that operate under high cyclic load, shock load, or in fittings and clevises where safety-critical load paths run through the turned geometry. It machines well but is slightly harder to thread cleanly than 6061. Anodizing produces a thinner, less uniform oxide layer than 6061, so if cosmetic anodizing is a deliverable, confirm the specification before ordering.

*Key spec: Tensile 572 MPa · Yield 503 MPa · Machinability: Good*

**Card 3 — When 2024-T3 is the Engineering Answer**

2024-T3 is the fatigue specialist. The copper-alloyed microstructure resists crack propagation better than 6061 or 7075 under alternating load — which is why it appears in rotating aerospace components where fatigue life is the governing failure mode. It is not weldable and has lower corrosion resistance than 6061, so anodizing or alodine (chromate conversion coating) is standard for exposed parts.

*Key spec: Tensile 483 MPa · Fatigue limit: superior to 6061 and 7075 under cyclic load*

## Section 10: Aluminium CNC Turning Design Guidelines

**H2:** Aluminium Turning Design Guidelines

**Subheading:** Rules specific to machining aluminium on a lathe — beyond standard DFM.

| Feature | Recommendation | Why It Matters |
|---|---|---|
| Length-to-diameter ratio | Keep L/D ≤ 4:1 unsupported. Use tailstock or steady rest above 4:1 | Long slender parts deflect under cutting forces, opening diameter tolerance mid-length |
| Wall thickness (thin-wall turned parts) | Minimum 0.8 mm for 6061-T6 | Thinner walls chatter and collapse under chuck pressure |
| Internal corner radii (bored features) | Min 0.5 mm on bored ID corners | Sharp internal corners concentrate stress and require specialist boring bars |
| Thread depth | 1.5× diameter min for M6 and below. 3× for M8+ | Shallow thread engagement in aluminium strips under load — design for pull-out, not just assembly fit |
| Undercuts for thread runout | Include a 1.0 mm × 0.5 mm runout groove at thread ends | Prevents interference at thread root and gives tool clearance on threading pass |
| Parting groove width | Min 3 mm for parts over Ø20 mm | Narrow parting on wide-diameter aluminium risks tool binding and part distortion at cutoff |
| Concentricity of multi-step ODs | Call out on drawing — do not assume | Multi-step shafts require datum re-chucking between steps unless live-centre supported |
| Surface finish for anodizing pre-treatment | Specify Ra 1.6 µm or better on surfaces to be anodized | Anodizing reveals machining marks — as-machined Ra 3.2 µm reads as visible texture under Type II |
| Bore size tolerance for bearing fits | Use H7 tolerance for bearing bore seats | H7 is the industry fit standard for bearing outer race interference. Loose tolerance = fretting failure |
| Runout callout on bearing journals | Specify total runout (not circular runout) | Total runout controls the entire journal surface, not just a single cross-section |

**Most common DFM failures in aluminium turning:**

Long-shaft orders with no steady-rest callout arrive with diameter errors in the 0.02–0.05 mm range at mid-length — exceeding the ±0.005 mm callout — because the part deflected under cutting forces without support. The fix is a drawing note: "Steady rest support required at mid-span — confirm setup before quote approval."

Thread stripping on M4 and M5 holes in 6061-T6 is the second common failure. Engineers design to 3× diameter depth on steel and apply it unchanged to aluminium, not accounting for aluminium's lower shear strength. Weldo CNC flags under-depth threads at DFM review — before the first cut — so the depth can be increased on the drawing or the thread class can be confirmed against the actual load case.

## Section 15: FAQ

**H2:** Aluminium CNC Turning — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: What tolerance can Weldo CNC hold on aluminium turned parts?**

Standard aluminium turning operates to ±0.05 mm on general dimensions, following ISO 2768-m. Precision features — bearing seats, shaft journals, close-fit bores — are held to ±0.005 mm and verified by CMM. Surface finish on turned aluminium is Ra 1.6 µm as standard on finish passes; Ra 0.8 µm is achievable with an additional finishing pass where specified. Specify the tolerance on your drawing — features without a callout are machined to the general tolerance.

**Q2: What is CNC turning for aluminium parts?**

CNC turning — computer numerical control turning — rotates the aluminium workpiece in a chuck while a fixed cutting tool removes material along a programmed G-code path. The result is cylindrical, conical, and complex rotational geometries: shafts, bores, threads, grooves, and tapers. Modern CNC lathes with live tooling add milling and drilling operations in the same setup — off-centre holes, cross-bores, and flat features — without re-chucking, which preserves concentricity between turned and milled features.

**Q3: What aluminium grades do you machine for turning?**

Confirmed aluminium grades for CNC turning include 6061-T6, 7075-T6, 2024-T3, 5052-H32, 6082-T6, and MIC-6 (cast tooling plate). 6061-T6 is the workhorse — highest machinability, predictable anodizing, cost-effective. 7075-T6 is specified for high-load and safety-critical parts. 2024-T3 for fatigue-critical rotating components. 5052-H32 for marine and thin-wall applications. Confirm your grade on the drawing — different grades behave differently in the chuck and in post-machining finishing.

**Q4: How long does it take to turn aluminium parts?**

Prototype aluminium turned parts ship in 1–5 business days from drawing approval. Small-batch production (up to 100 pieces) ships in 5–15 business days. Full production runs are quoted individually but typically complete in 2–5 weeks. Upload your STEP file and 2D drawing — a quote and lead time confirmation returns within 24 hours.

**Q5: What is the minimum order quantity for aluminium CNC turning?**

No minimum order quantity. Single-part prototype orders are accepted. The quote process is the same for 1 part as for 1,000 — upload your file, receive a 24-hour quote, confirm the order.

**Q6: What file formats do you accept for aluminium turning quotes?**

STEP files (.stp / .step) are the preferred 3D format — they carry full geometry with no translation loss. IGES, SolidWorks (.sldprt), and Parasolid (.x_t) are also accepted. 2D engineering drawings should accompany all tight-tolerance orders — PDF or DWG. For complex multi-step profiles, include a drawing even when STEP is supplied; G-code — the machine's programming language — is written from the 3D model but tolerances and surface finish callouts are read from the drawing.

**Q7: What drives the cost of aluminium CNC turning?**

Four factors set the price: material (7075 bar costs more per kilogram than 6061); cycle time (a 10-step shaft with thread, groove, and cross-bore takes longer than a plain shaft); tolerance (±0.005 mm features require additional inspection time); and finish (anodizing, hard coat, or plating adds a separate process step). Aluminium is among the fastest metals to turn — high cutting speeds and low tool wear reduce cycle time versus steel or titanium. Include material grade, required tolerance, and finish in your quote enquiry for an accurate return.

**Q8: How does Weldo CNC compare to other aluminium CNC turning suppliers?**

The gap most engineers notice is documentation. Many suppliers deliver the parts; Weldo CNC delivers the parts plus a CMM report showing every critical dimension, and a material certificate confirming alloy and heat number. On tolerance capability, ±0.005 mm on turned aluminium is a standard production tolerance here — not a premium service tier. ISO 9001:2015 certification with a 97.48% annual pass rate provides the audit trail. Customers in 100+ countries receive the same inspection documentation, regardless of order size.

**Q9: Can Weldo CNC anodize aluminium turned parts after machining?**

Yes. Anodizing — both Type II (decorative, 5–25 µm) and Type III hard anodize (20–100 µm) — is available as a post-machining finish for aluminium turned parts. Anodizing adds measurable thickness: Type II adds 5–15 µm per surface, Type III adds 10–50 µm per surface. Threads and precision bores are masked where anodizing dimensional growth would affect fit. Specify the anodize type, colour, and any masking requirements on your drawing before quoting — anodize-related dimensional planning happens at DFM review, not after turning is complete.

**Q10: What is the maximum size of aluminium part you can turn?**

The maximum turning envelope is Ø200 mm diameter × 500 mm length. Parts within those limits can be turned in standard lathe setups. Shafts approaching the length limit (above L/D = 4:1) require tailstock or steady rest support — specify on the drawing or flag at quote stage. For flat-face components larger than Ø200 mm, CNC milling is the appropriate process — confirm geometry with the quoting team.
