## Section 3: What is Stainless Steel CNC Turning?

**H2:** What is Stainless Steel CNC Turning?

Weldo CNC turns stainless steel — 303, 304, 316L, 431, and 17-4PH — to tolerances of ±0.005 mm on OD, ID, and thread features, from its ISO 9001:2015 certified facility in Dongguan, China. Prototype stainless turned parts ship in 1–5 business days. Every tight-tolerance order includes a full CMM — coordinate measuring machine — dimensional inspection report shipped with the parts.

Stainless steel is specified for turned components when corrosion resistance, hygienic surface requirements, or elevated operating temperature rules out aluminium. The austenitic grades — 304 and 316L — resist broad chemical environments. 316L's molybdenum content makes it resistant to chloride-induced pitting, which is why it appears in marine hardware, medical implants, and food-processing components. 17-4PH adds precipitation hardening to stainless chemistry — delivering tensile strength up to 1,310 MPa after H900 ageing.

Stainless steel work-hardens under the cutting tool. A worn tool nose radius — the rounded tip of the cutting insert — generates heat rather than shearing chip cleanly, which accelerates work hardening in the surface layer and degrades the next pass. Cutting speed, feed rate, and tool geometry are set to stay ahead of the hardened zone: cut deep enough on each pass to break through it, not skim across it. 303 machines most freely of all stainless grades; 316L is significantly more demanding; 17-4PH in the H900 condition requires carbide tooling, reduced cutting speed, and flood coolant.

Weldo CNC has turned stainless steel components since 2012 for customers in medical, food equipment, marine, chemical processing, and defence. Typical part types include valve bodies, surgical instrument handles, threaded fasteners, shaft couplings, and pressure fittings.

## Section 5: Material Trade-off Cards

**H2:** Choosing the Right Stainless Steel Grade for Turned Parts

**Subheading:** Grade selection is an engineering decision — corrosion resistance, strength, and machinability do not all peak in the same alloy.

**Card 1 — When 303 is the Right Choice**

303 is the free-machining stainless grade — sulphur additions break chips cleanly, reduce built-up edge on the tool nose, and allow faster spindle speeds than any other stainless. For high-volume turned parts where machining cost matters and the service environment is mild (not chloride-exposed), 303 is the economical correct choice. It does not weld cleanly and is not the right grade for passivation-critical medical parts, but for screws, fittings, spacers, and housings in clean environments, 303 turns faster and cheaper than any other stainless.

*Key spec: Tensile 620 MPa · Machinability: Excellent (best of all stainless grades)*

**Card 2 — When 316L is Required**

316L is the grade for corrosion-critical environments — chloride exposure, saline, body fluid contact, or aggressive cleaning chemicals. The "L" designation means low carbon content (≤0.03%), which prevents carbide precipitation at weld heat-affected zones and sensitisation during passivation. Medical implant components, food processing contact surfaces, and marine hardware are almost always 316L, not 304. Machining is slower and tool wear is higher than 303 or 304 — that cost is inherent to the material choice.

*Key spec: Tensile 485 MPa · Molybdenum content 2–3% · Chloride resistance superior to 304*

**Card 3 — When 17-4PH is the Engineering Answer**

17-4PH delivers stainless corrosion resistance at tensile strength approaching tool steel — up to 1,310 MPa in H900 condition. Aerospace shaft components, medical surgical tools, and high-load marine fittings specify 17-4PH where 316L's 485 MPa is insufficient and conventional high-strength steel's corrosion resistance is insufficient. It machines best in the annealed condition, then ages to final hardness — which means machined features do not change significantly in dimension during ageing, unlike carburised or through-hardened steel.

*Key spec: Tensile 1,310 MPa (H900) · Combines stainless corrosion resistance with high-strength structural capability*

## Section 10: Stainless Steel CNC Turning Design Guidelines

**H2:** Stainless Steel Turning Design Guidelines

**Subheading:** Stainless steel imposes specific tooling and setup constraints that don't apply to aluminium or mild steel.

| Feature | Recommendation | Why It Matters |
|---|---|---|
| Wall thickness minimum | 1.0 mm for austenitic grades (304, 316L) | Thinner stainless walls deflect and chatter during turning — dimensional error increases at thin sections |
| Tool nose radius | Use positive-rake carbide inserts with sharp edge | Stainless work-hardens under a blunt or negative-rake tool; built-up edge grows rapidly |
| Cutting depth | Do not skim — cut deep enough to break through the work-hardened layer | Skim passes amplify work hardening, raise surface temperature, and degrade dimensional control |
| Flood coolant | Mandatory on 316L and 17-4PH | Insufficient cooling accelerates tool wear and increases the risk of carbide tool fracture mid-feature |
| Thread design for stainless-on-stainless | Specify 6H/6g class and note anti-galling compound requirement | Stainless galls on stainless — tighter classes increase seizure risk at assembly |
| Parting stainless bar | Use high positive-rake parting blade, pecking cycle on diameters over Ø30 mm | Stainless binds on parting blades — galling causes tool breakage at cutoff |
| Surface finish for passivation | Specify Ra 0.8–1.6 µm pre-passivation | Passivation removes free iron from the surface; a rough finish traps contaminants that resist the acid bath |
| Bore diameter tolerance | Allow one extra roughing pass before finish bore on 316L | Work hardening from the roughing pass can tighten the bore on the finish pass if not accounted for |
| Runout on journal features | Specify total indicator runout (TIR) value on drawing | Total runout — the full sweep of the indicator across the journal — is the correct callout for shaft alignment, not circular runout |
| Thread engagement in stainless | Minimum 2× diameter for stainless threaded connections | Stainless strips at lower torque than alloy steel — deeper engagement compensates for lower shear strength relative to grade |

**Most common DFM failures in stainless turning:**

Undersized bore diameters are the most common stainless turning failure. 316L springs back slightly after the boring pass — more than aluminium or mild steel — and work hardening from aggressive roughing passes compresses the bore by 0.005–0.015 mm. The fix is a light final finishing pass at reduced feed with a sharp insert, followed by CMM measurement before the part leaves the chuck.

Galled threads on stainless-on-stainless assemblies are the second failure pattern. Engineers specify the correct tolerance class for steel and apply it unchanged to stainless. Weldo CNC flags stainless thread mating pairs at DFM review — the solution is a looser thread class plus assembly compound specification on the drawing before any bar is loaded.

## Section 15: FAQ

**H2:** Stainless Steel CNC Turning — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: What tolerance can Weldo CNC hold on stainless steel turned parts?**

Standard stainless turning operates to ±0.05 mm on general dimensions per ISO 2768-m. Precision features — bearing seats, shaft journals, valve seats, bored IDs — are held to ±0.005 mm and verified by CMM. Surface finish on turned stainless is Ra 1.6 µm on standard finish passes; Ra 0.8 µm is achievable with an additional finishing pass; Ra 0.4 µm is achievable after electropolishing. Specify tolerance and finish on your drawing.

**Q2: What is CNC turning for stainless steel?**

CNC turning — computer numerical control — rotates the stainless steel workpiece in a chuck while a carbide cutting insert removes material along a G-code programme. Stainless steel requires sharper tool geometry and tighter control of cutting speed than aluminium, because stainless work-hardens under the tool nose if feed rate or depth is insufficient. Live tooling on the lathe adds cross-holes, slots, and flats in the same setup — concentricity between turned and milled features is maintained because the part never leaves the chuck.

**Q3: What stainless steel grades do you machine for turning?**

Confirmed stainless grades for CNC turning include 303, 304, 316L, 431, and 17-4PH. 303 is the easiest to machine — free-machining sulphur additions produce clean chips. 304 for general corrosion resistance. 316L for medical, marine, and chloride environments. 431 for marine shafts combining corrosion resistance with higher tensile strength. 17-4PH for high-strength aerospace and medical applications requiring stainless chemical resistance. Specify your grade on the drawing — grade affects machining time, tool selection, and post-process compatibility.

**Q4: How long does it take to turn stainless steel parts?**

Prototype stainless turned parts ship in 1–5 business days from drawing approval. Small-batch production ships in 5–15 business days. Full production runs complete in 2–5 weeks. Stainless is slower to machine than aluminium — cycle time is longer, which affects per-part cost at prototype quantities. Upload your STEP file and drawing — a quote with lead time confirmation returns within 24 hours.

**Q5: What is the minimum order quantity for stainless steel CNC turning?**

No minimum order quantity. Single-piece prototypes accepted at the same quote process as production runs. Upload your file, confirm the quote within 24 hours, and the order enters the production queue.

**Q6: What file formats do you accept for stainless turning quotes?**

STEP (.stp / .step) is the preferred 3D format. IGES, SolidWorks (.sldprt), and Parasolid (.x_t) are also accepted. Include a 2D PDF drawing with all tolerance callouts, surface finish specifications, thread class, and passivation requirements. G-code is written from the 3D model; tolerances and finish callouts are read from the drawing. Both files are needed for tight-tolerance stainless work.

**Q7: What drives the cost of stainless steel CNC turning?**

Stainless costs more per cycle than aluminium for three reasons: the bar material is more expensive; cutting speed is lower (stainless removes material more slowly under safe cutting conditions); and tool wear is higher, so inserts are changed more frequently. 316L is the most expensive stainless to turn — 303 is the most economical. Surface finish requirements, passivation, and tight tolerances add inspection and finishing time. Include grade, tolerance, surface finish, and post-process requirements in your quote enquiry.

**Q8: How does Weldo CNC compare to other stainless steel turning suppliers?**

Most stainless turning suppliers deliver the part. Weldo CNC delivers the part plus a CMM dimensional report and material certificate — including carbon content confirmation for 316L. ISO 9001:2015 certification with a 97.48% annual pass rate covers the process. Grade-specific cutting parameters — not a single "stainless" programme — reduce work hardening and surface finish failures on 316L and 17-4PH. Customers in 100+ countries ship the same documentation package regardless of order volume.

**Q9: Does stainless steel need passivation after CNC turning?**

Passivation — typically per ASTM A967 or AMS 2700 — is recommended for stainless components operating in corrosive environments or used in medical and food applications. CNC turning exposes the stainless base metal and can embed free iron from tooling. Passivation removes free iron from the surface and restores the natural chromium oxide passive layer that gives stainless its corrosion resistance. Specify passivation on the drawing if required — masking of threads and precision features is planned at DFM review, not after machining.

**Q10: Can you turn 17-4PH stainless to tight tolerances in the H900 condition?**

Yes — but with a process note. 17-4PH is best rough-turned in the annealed (A) condition, then aged to H900, then finish-turned to final dimensions. Turning in the H900 condition is possible but requires slower cutting speeds and sharper inserts. Dimensional change during H900 ageing is small (typically 0.02–0.05% linear) — for ±0.005 mm tolerance features, plan for a finish-turning pass after ageing. Weldo CNC coordinates the ageing and finish-turning sequence at DFM review.
