## Section 3: What is CNC Turning
**Design:** 2-column — text left, image right. White background.

In CNC turning, the workpiece rotates and the cutting tool stays fixed. That's the fundamental difference from milling — and it's why turning is the faster, cheaper route for anything cylindrical. Shafts, bushings, pins, nipples, valves, nozzles — parts that are round in cross-section machine most efficiently when you spin them past a stationary tool rather than trace a rotating cutter around a fixed block.

The efficiency gap is real. A turned shaft that takes 8 minutes on a lathe might take 45 minutes to mill from round bar — same material removed, dramatically different cycle time. For high-volume cylindrical cnc turning parts, that difference determines whether the part is commercially viable.

What makes the difference between a competent turning operation and a capable one is what the machine can do beyond the basic OD and ID. Live tooling adds milling capability to the lathe — cross-drilled holes, flats, keyways, radial features — without moving the part to a second machine. Swiss-type lathes use a guide bushing that supports the material right at the cut, making it possible to hold tight tolerances on slender components that would deflect and chatter on a standard lathe.

We run all three configurations. The job tells us which one to use.

## Section 3b: Types of CNC Turning
**Design:** 3-column card grid. Light grey background.
**Heading:** Types of CNC Turning
**Subheading:** Standard lathes, live tooling centres, Swiss turning — each handles a different class of part.

**Card 1 — 2-Axis CNC Turning**
The standard lathe configuration: X and Z axes. Handles OD turning, facing, boring, grooving, undercutting, and threading. The right machine for the majority of turned parts — round in cross-section, no off-axis features. Fast cycle times, cost-effective for medium to high volumes, and capable of holding tight tolerances on diametral features.
- Best for: Shafts, bushings, spacers, pins, flanges
- Max diameter: 400mm | Max length: 1,000mm

**Card 2 — Live Tooling / Mill-Turn**
Adds rotating tool capability to the lathe. The spindle can index to a fixed position while a secondary milling head machines cross-holes, flats, hex features, keyways, and radial slots. What would otherwise require two setups on two machines — turning then milling — gets done in one. Fewer setups means fewer chances for datum error to compound.
- Best for: Shafts with flats or cross-holes, complex turned components with milled features
- Advantage: Single-setup complete machining

**Card 3 — Swiss CNC Turning**
Swiss-type lathes use a guide bushing that supports the material within 1–2mm of the cutting point. The result: even very long, slender parts hold tolerance because the support eliminates the deflection that kills accuracy on a standard lathe. The practical rule — if your part has a length-to-diameter ratio above 6:1, Swiss turning is worth considering. Above 12:1, it's usually the only way to hit a tight tolerance reliably.
- Best for: Miniature components, long slender shafts, medical pins, precision fasteners
- Capability: L:D ratios up to 20:1 at tight tolerance

## Section 3c: CNC Turning vs. CNC Milling
**Design:** 2-column comparison cards (RS-05 style). White background.
**Heading:** Turning or Milling — Which Process Fits Your Part?

**Choose Turning When:**
The part is predominantly cylindrical or rotational in form. OD, ID, threading, grooving, and facing are all native operations — faster and cheaper on a lathe than milled from bar. If the cross-section is round and the features are concentric, turning is the right starting point.
- Shafts, pins, bushings, nozzles, valves, nipples
- Faster cycle times than milling for round geometry
- Better surface finish on OD features as standard

**Choose Milling When:**
The part is prismatic — flat faces, pockets, slots, orthogonal holes. Or when the geometry can't be reached by a rotating workpiece: irregular profiles, asymmetric features, complex internal pockets. Milling handles the geometry that turning can't access.
- Brackets, housings, plates, manifolds
- Required for non-rotational geometry
- 3-axis to 5-axis depending on feature complexity

**Note:** Many real parts need both. A shaft with a milled keyway, a turned housing with bolt-hole patterns — live tooling handles these on one machine. If your part is on the boundary, send the STEP file and we'll advise the most efficient process before quoting.

## Section 9: Design Guidelines
**Design:** 3-column table + supporting copy. White background.
**Heading:** CNC Turning Design Guidelines

| Feature | Recommendation | Why It Matters |
|---|---|---|
| Min turned diameter | ≥ 2mm standard / ≥ 1mm Swiss | Below 2mm, standard lathes struggle to hold runout — Swiss turning required |
| Max L:D ratio (standard) | ≤ 6:1 unsupported | Longer parts deflect under cutting forces — affects OD tolerance and finish |
| Max L:D ratio (Swiss) | Up to 20:1 | Guide bushing supports material at the cut — slender parts hold tolerance reliably |
| Internal bore depth | ≤ 5× diameter | Deep bores require boring bars prone to chatter — flag if deeper is required |
| Undercut relief | Add 0.2mm relief before threads/shoulders | Eliminates tool interference, ensures full thread engagement to the shoulder |
| Knurling | Specify pitch and pattern on drawing | Diamond or straight — confirm before specifying fine pitch on hard materials |
| Thread tolerance | Specify class of fit (e.g. 6H/6g) | Go/No-Go gauged — ensure mating part tolerance is also called out |
| Runout / TIR | Specify explicitly on drawing if critical | Inspected separately from diameter tolerance — don't assume it's implied |
| Parting groove | Min 1.5mm width | Narrower parting tools are fragile on harder materials |
| Internal sharp corners | Add radius where possible | Sharp bore/face intersections cause stress concentrations and slow production |

The issue we see most on turning drawings is L:D ratio. An engineer designs a 200mm long shaft at 12mm diameter — on paper it looks straightforward. On a standard lathe it isn't. The part deflects under cutting force, the OD wanders, and you end up with a tapered shaft that fails inspection. Swiss turning solves it, but it needs to be the right call from the start, not a fix after the first batch is scrapped. We assess L:D on every turning quote and flag it if Swiss is the right route.

## Section 14: FAQ
**Design:** Accordion. White background. First item open.
**Heading:** CNC Turning Services — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: What tolerances can you hold on CNC turning parts?**
Turned parts can hold tighter diametral tolerances than milled features — the continuous cut of a lathe produces a more consistent surface than a rotating end mill tracing a profile. Standard work follows ISO 2768-m. For precision fits we hold ±0.01mm on diameters; for critical applications — bearing seats, valve seats, precision shafts — ±0.005mm is achievable and CMM-verified. Runout and concentricity are separate callouts from diameter tolerance. If they're critical, specify them explicitly on the drawing — we inspect and report both.

**Q2: What's the maximum size part you can CNC turn?**
Standard turning: 400mm diameter, 1,000mm length. Swiss turning: down to 1mm diameter, parts up to 20:1 length-to-diameter ratio. If your part is close to the limit in either direction — very large diameter or very slender — send the STEP file first. We'll confirm workholding and process suitability before quoting.

**Q3: When should I use CNC turning instead of CNC milling?**
If the part is cylindrical or predominantly rotational — shaft, bushing, pin, valve body, nozzle — turning is almost always faster and cheaper. The lathe spins the material past a fixed tool; a milling machine traces a rotating cutter around a fixed workpiece. For round geometry, the lathe wins on cycle time every time. Where it gets complicated is when the part has both rotational and prismatic features — that's where live tooling or a secondary milling setup comes in. If you're not sure, send the file and we'll advise.

**Q4: What is Swiss CNC turning and when do I need it?**
Swiss turning uses a guide bushing that supports the material within 1–2mm of the cutting point. On a standard lathe, a long slender workpiece deflects under cutting forces — the further from the chuck, the worse the runout and surface finish. The guide bushing eliminates that deflection. Practical threshold: parts with L:D ratios above 6:1 benefit from Swiss turning; above 12:1 it's usually the only way to hold tight tolerances reliably. Common applications include medical pins, precision fastener shanks, connector bodies, and long instrumentation shafts.

**Q5: Can you turn and mill a part in the same setup?**
Yes — with live tooling. Our turning centres with live tooling capability can machine rotational features on the lathe and then switch to driven tools for cross-holes, flats, hexagonal features, keyways, and radial slots — all without removing the part from the chuck. Single-setup complete machining is faster, eliminates re-fixturing error, and produces better datum alignment between turned and milled features than two separate operations on two machines.

**Q6: Do you offer CNC turning services for prototype quantities?**
Yes, no minimum order quantity. Single pieces quoted and produced to the same standard as production runs — same material verification, same inspection process, same documentation. The per-unit cost is higher on singles due to setup time, but the process and the output are identical. Engineers who need one shaft to validate a design before committing to production get the same quality output as a 500-piece batch.
