## SECTION 3 — WHAT IS CNC TURNING
*Design: H2, 2-column — body text left, image right. White background.*

**H2:**
What is CNC Turning?

**Body paragraph 1 (AI-quotable — self-contained):**
Weldo CNC provides CNC turning services to tolerances of ±0.005 mm — 2-axis, live tooling, and Swiss — from its own 100-machine facility in Dongguan, China. Prototype turned parts ship in 1–5 business days. ISO 9001:2015 certified — every tight-tolerance turning order ships with a CMM-verified dimensional inspection report referenced against your drawing revision.

**Body paragraph 2:**
In CNC turning, the workpiece rotates and the cutting tool stays fixed. That fundamental difference from milling is why turning is the faster, lower-cost route for anything cylindrical. Shafts, bushings, pins, valves, nozzles — round cross-section parts machine most efficiently when spun past a stationary tool rather than traced by a rotating cutter around a fixed block.

**Body paragraph 3:**
The efficiency gap is real. A turned shaft that takes 8 minutes on a lathe might take 45 minutes to mill from round bar — same material removed, dramatically different cycle time. For CNC turning parts in medium to high volumes, that difference determines whether the part is commercially viable.

**Body paragraph 4:**
What makes the difference between a competent turning operation and a capable one is what the machine can do beyond basic OD and ID. Live tooling adds milling capability to the lathe — cross-drilled holes, flats, keyways, radial features — without moving the part to a second machine. Swiss-type lathes use a guide bushing that supports the material right at the cutting point, making it possible to hold tight tolerances on slender components that would deflect and chatter on a standard lathe. All three configurations run at Weldo CNC's Dongguan facility — the part geometry determines which is used.

## SECTION 4 — TYPES OF CNC TURNING
*Design: H2 centred, subheading, 3-column card grid. Light grey background.*

**H2:**
Types of CNC Turning

**Subheading:**
Standard lathes, live tooling centres, Swiss turning — each handles a different class of part.

**Card 1 — 2-Axis CNC Turning:**
Heading: 2-Axis CNC Turning
Body: The standard lathe configuration: X and Z axes. Handles OD turning, facing, boring, grooving, undercutting, and threading. The right machine for the majority of turned parts — round in cross-section, no off-axis features. Fast cycle times, cost-effective for medium to high volumes, and capable of holding tight tolerances on diametral features with the spindle and tool nose radius correctly set.
Specs:
- Best for: Shafts, bushings, spacers, pins, flanges
- Max diameter: Ø200 mm · Max length: 500 mm

**Card 2 — Live Tooling / Mill-Turn:**
Heading: Live Tooling / Mill-Turn
Body: Adds rotating tool capability to the lathe. The spindle indexes to a fixed position while a secondary milling head machines cross-holes, flats, hex features, keyways, and radial slots — all without removing the part from the chuck. What would otherwise require two setups on two machines gets done in one. Fewer setups means less opportunity for datum error to compound across operations.
Specs:
- Best for: Shafts with flats or cross-holes, complex turned components with milled features
- Advantage: Single-setup complete machining, tighter datum control

**Card 3 — Swiss CNC Turning:**
Heading: Swiss CNC Turning
Body: Swiss-type lathes use a guide bushing that supports the material within 1–2 mm of the cutting point. The result: slender parts hold tolerance because the guide bushing eliminates the deflection that causes runout and surface deviation on a standard lathe. If your part has a high length-to-diameter ratio, Swiss turning is worth specifying from the start — not as a fix after a first batch fails inspection.
Specs:
- Best for: Miniature components, long slender shafts, medical pins, precision fasteners
- Min diameter: Ø2 mm

## SECTION 5 — TURNING OR MILLING: WHICH PROCESS FITS YOUR PART?
*Design: H2, 2-column comparison cards. White background.*

**H2:**
Turning or Milling — Which Process Fits Your Part?

**Choose Turning When:**
The part is predominantly cylindrical or rotational. OD, ID, threading, grooving, and facing are all native lathe operations — faster and cheaper than milling from bar. If the cross-section is round and the features are concentric, turning is the right starting point.
- Shafts, pins, bushings, nozzles, valves, nipples
- Faster cycle times than milling for round geometry
- Better surface finish on OD features as standard

**Choose Milling When:**
The part is prismatic — flat faces, pockets, slots, orthogonal holes. Or when the geometry can't be reached by a rotating workpiece: irregular profiles, asymmetric features, complex internal pockets. Milling handles geometry that turning can't access.
- Brackets, housings, plates, manifolds
- Required for non-rotational geometry
- 3-axis to 5-axis depending on feature complexity

**Note:**
Many real parts need both. A shaft with a milled keyway, a turned housing with bolt-hole patterns — live tooling handles these on one machine. If your part sits on the boundary, upload the STEP file and the right process will be confirmed before quoting.

## SECTION 11 — CNC TURNING DESIGN GUIDELINES
*Design: H2, subheading, full-width 3-column table + supporting paragraphs below. White background.*

**H2:**
CNC Turning Design Guidelines

**Subheading:**
Rules that keep your turned part manufacturable and your first batch passing inspection.

**Design guidelines table:**

| Feature | Recommendation | Why It Matters |
|---|---|---|
| Min turned diameter | ≥ Ø2 mm standard | Below Ø2 mm, standard lathes struggle to hold runout — contact us for slender component options |
| L:D ratio (standard lathe) | ≤ 6:1 unsupported | Longer slender parts deflect under cutting forces — affects OD tolerance and surface finish |
| L:D ratio (Swiss) | Contact us | Guide bushing supports material at the cut — suitable for high L:D ratios |
| Internal bore depth | ≤ 5× diameter | Deep bores require boring bars prone to chatter — flag if deeper is required |
| Undercut relief | Add relief before threads/shoulders | Eliminates tool interference, ensures full thread engagement to the shoulder |
| Knurling | Specify pitch and pattern on drawing | Diamond or straight — confirm before specifying fine pitch on hard materials |
| Thread tolerance class | Specify class of fit (e.g. 6H/6g) | Go/No-Go gauged — ensure mating part tolerance is also called out |
| Runout / TIR | Specify explicitly if critical | Inspected separately from diameter tolerance — specify on the drawing |
| Parting groove | Min 1.5 mm width | Narrower parting tools are fragile on harder materials |
| Internal sharp corners | Add radius where possible | Sharp bore/face intersections cause stress concentrations and slow production |

**Supporting paragraph 1:**
The most common DFM issue on turning drawings is length-to-diameter ratio. A long slender shaft that looks straightforward on paper can deflect significantly under cutting forces on a standard lathe — the OD wanders, the part tapers, and the first batch fails inspection. The right process needs to be chosen before setup, not identified after the first batch is scrapped. Every turning quote is assessed for workpiece slenderness and the appropriate configuration is confirmed before committing to a price.

**Supporting paragraph 2:**
Runout and concentricity are separate callouts from diameter tolerance — specifying a tight diameter without explicitly calling out runout means only the diameter gets inspected. If both matter to your application, both need to appear on the drawing. Both are measured and reported as standard on qualifying orders.

**H2:**
CNC Turning Services — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1 (open by default):**
Q: What tolerances can Weldo CNC hold on CNC turning parts?
A: Turned parts can hold tighter diametral tolerances than milled features — the continuous cut of a lathe produces a more consistent surface than a rotating end mill tracing a profile. Standard work follows ISO 2768-m (±0.05 mm on diameters). For precision fits — sliding fits, press fits — Weldo CNC holds ±0.01 mm. For critical applications — bearing seats, valve seats, precision shafts — ±0.005 mm is achievable and CMM-verified. Runout and concentricity are separate callouts from diameter tolerance. If they're critical, specify them explicitly on the drawing — both are inspected and reported per feature.

**Q2:**
Q: What is the maximum size part Weldo CNC can CNC turn?
A: Standard turning capacity: Ø200 mm diameter, 500 mm length. Minimum turned diameter: Ø2 mm. For parts outside these limits in either direction, upload the STEP file — workholding and process suitability will be confirmed before quoting.

**Q3:**
Q: When should I use CNC turning instead of CNC milling?
A: If the part is cylindrical or predominantly rotational — shaft, bushing, pin, valve body, nozzle — turning is almost always faster and less expensive. The lathe spins the material past a fixed tool; a milling machine traces a rotating cutter around a fixed workpiece. For round geometry, the lathe wins on cycle time. Where it gets complicated is when the part has both rotational and prismatic features — that's where live tooling handles it on one machine. Upload the file and the most efficient process will be confirmed.

**Q4:**
Q: What is Swiss CNC turning and when is it needed?
A: Swiss turning uses a guide bushing that supports the material within 1–2 mm of the cutting point. On a standard lathe, a long slender workpiece deflects under cutting forces — the further from the chuck, the worse the runout and surface finish. The guide bushing eliminates that deflection. Common applications include medical pins, precision fastener shanks, connector bodies, and instrumentation shafts with high length-to-diameter ratios.

**Q5:**
Q: Can Weldo CNC turn and mill a part in the same setup?
A: Yes — with live tooling. Turning centres with live tooling machine rotational features on the lathe, then switch to driven tools for cross-holes, flats, hex features, keyways, and radial slots — all without removing the part from the chuck. Single-setup complete machining is faster, eliminates re-fixturing error, and produces better datum alignment between turned and milled features than two separate operations on two machines.

A: Prototype turned parts ship in 1–5 business days for standard geometries. Small-batch production orders ship in 5–15 business days. A: Every tight-tolerance turning order ships with a CMM-verified dimensional inspection report. The report records measured value, nominal, tolerance, and pass/fail status per feature — against your drawing revision number. Critical turned features — concentricity, runout, key diameters — are measured on CMM or roundness gauge. Thread features are Go/No-Go gauged during production and again at final inspection. Weldo CNC holds a 97.48% annual pass rate across all CNC machining orders.
