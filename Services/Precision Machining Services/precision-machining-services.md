*Design: H2 left-aligned, 2-column — body text left, image right. White background.*

**H2:**
What is Precision Machining?

**Body paragraph 1 (AI-quotable — self-contained):**
Weldo CNC delivers precision machining services to tolerances of ±0.005 mm — CNC milling, CNC turning, 5-axis machining, surface grinding, and wire EDM — from its own 100-machine facility in Dongguan, China. Prototype precision parts ship in 1–5 business days. ISO 9001:2015 certified — every tight-tolerance order ships with a CMM — Coordinate Measuring Machine — verified dimensional inspection report referenced against your drawing revision.

**Body paragraph 2:**
Precision machining is subtractive manufacturing under controlled tolerance conditions. A cutting tool removes material from a workpiece — metal or engineering plastic — until the geometry matches a 3D model or 2D drawing to a defined dimensional tolerance. The word "precision" has a specific meaning: not just tight tolerances, but the ability to verify and document that those tolerances were held, feature by feature, on every part in the batch.

**Body paragraph 3:**
What separates a precision machining operation from general machining isn't always the machine. It's the process discipline that surrounds the cut — material traceability before the first chip, datum verification after every repositioning, CMM measurement of critical features before final inspection sign-off. Parts that require ±0.005 mm on a bore diameter also require a QC chain rigorous enough to catch the one part in a run that drifted.

**Body paragraph 4:**
Weldo CNC has operated precision machining services in Dongguan since 2012. The facility has machined aerospace structural components, medical device housings, hydraulic manifolds, robotics actuator housings, and high-pressure valve bodies — work where a missed tolerance causes a field failure, not a cosmetic complaint. Process knowledge for those part types, materials, and tolerance classes is built into the operation.

## SECTION 4 — PRECISION MACHINING PROCESS TYPES
*Design: H2 centred, subheading, 3-column card grid. Light grey background.*

**H2:**
Types of Precision Machining

**Subheading:**
Process selection is determined by part geometry, feature type, and tolerance class — not by machine availability.

**Card 1 — CNC Milling:**
Heading: CNC Milling
Body: Rotating cutting tools remove material along X, Y, and Z axes — or all five axes simultaneously for complex geometry. The standard process for prismatic parts: brackets, housings, plates, manifolds, fixtures. 3-axis covers the majority of precision milled work. 4-axis adds indexed rotation for multi-face parts. 5-axis enables compound angles, contoured surfaces, and undercuts in a single setup at ±0.003 mm.
Specs:
- Best for: Prismatic parts, flat faces, pockets, complex 3D surfaces
- Tolerance: ±0.005 mm standard · ±0.003 mm on 5-axis
Link: → View CNC milling services

**Card 2 — CNC Turning:**
Heading: CNC Turning
Body: The workpiece rotates; the cutting tool is stationary. The correct process for cylindrical, conical, and axisymmetric parts — shafts, bushings, pins, fittings, valve stems. Live tooling turning centres add milling capability in a single clamping for parts requiring off-centre holes, flats, or cross-features. Swiss-style turning handles high-precision slender work — long, thin parts that would deflect on a conventional lathe.
Specs:
- Best for: Turned shafts, bushings, connectors, fluid fittings
- Tolerance: ±0.005 mm · Concentricity and runout to drawing callout
Link: → View CNC turning services

**Card 3 — Grinding & EDM:**
Heading: Surface Grinding · Wire EDM
Body: Grinding removes material with an abrasive wheel — surface grinding for flat surfaces, internal grinding for bores, centerless grinding for long cylindrical parts. Used when CNC turning or milling has reached its practical tolerance limit and the part needs tighter flatness, parallelism, or surface finish. Wire EDM — Electrical Discharge Machining — cuts by electrical spark erosion. No cutting force, no heat distortion, extremely fine detail in hardened tool steel, intricate 2D profiles, thin-wall features.
Specs:
- Best for: Hardened steel, tight flatness, intricate profiles, tool and die work
- Tolerance: Grinding to ±0.002 mm flatness · EDM to ±0.005 mm

## SECTION 5 — WHEN PRECISION MACHINING IS THE RIGHT PROCESS
*Design: H2, 2-column comparison. White background.*

**H2:**
Precision Machining or an Alternative — Which Process Fits Your Part?

**Use Precision Machining When:**
- Tolerances tighter than ±0.1 mm are required — functional fits, bearing seats, sealing surfaces, true position callouts
- The part must be made from an engineering material — aluminium, stainless steel, titanium, PEEK — not a printing resin or cast alloy
- You need documented verification: CMM report, material certificate, traceability to a specific batch
- Example part types: Hydraulic manifolds, surgical instrument bodies, aerospace brackets, precision shafts, gearbox housings

**When an Alternative May Win:**
- Geometry is simple and volumes are high — casting or extrusion wins on unit cost above a few hundred identical parts
- Prototype of a complex organic shape in a soft material — 3D printing delivers geometry faster without toolpath planning
- Sheet metal flat pattern with bends — sheet metal fabrication is faster and cheaper than milling the same part from solid
- Example part types: Injection-moulded housings, bent brackets, cast valve bodies, complex lattice structures

Closing note: If your part is on the boundary, send the STEP file. The right process will be confirmed before quoting.

## SECTION 11 — PRECISION MACHINING DESIGN GUIDELINES
*Design: H2, subheading, full-width 3-column table + 2 supporting paragraphs below. White background.*

**H2:**
Precision Machining Design Guidelines

**Subheading:**
Rules that keep your part manufacturable — and your quote where you expect it.

**Design guidelines table:**

| Feature | Recommendation | Why It Matters |
|---|---|---|
| Wall thickness (metals) | ≥ 0.8 mm | Thin walls deflect under cutting forces — tolerance failure and poor finish |
| Wall thickness (plastics) | ≥ 1.5 mm | Plastics flex more under tool load — vibration leads to chatter marks |
| Internal corner radius | ≥ 1/3 of pocket depth | Set by cutter diameter — tighter radius = smaller tool = more passes, higher cost |
| Pocket depth | ≤ 4× pocket width | Deep narrow pockets require long-reach tools that deflect under chip load |
| Hole depth | ≤ 12× diameter (standard drill) | Beyond this needs a gun drill — flag if required |
| Undercuts | Flag with access direction on drawing | Require special tooling — access confirmed before quoting |
| Thread engagement | ≥ 3× diameter under Ø5 mm · ≥ 4–6× for Ø5 mm+ | Minimum engagement for thread to carry load at torque spec |
| Tolerances | Apply tight callout only where function requires | ±0.005 mm costs significantly more than ±0.02 mm — specify where it matters |
| Bore tolerances for grinding | Leave 0.1–0.2 mm stock for grinding pass | Grinding removes less per pass than turning — pre-machined stock allowance required |
| EDM applications | Use for hardened material or thin-wall profiles | EDM has no cutting force — suitable where conventional tooling would distort the part |

**Supporting paragraph 1:**
The two most common DFM — Design for Manufacturability — failures in incoming precision machining files are over-specified tolerances and unreachable internal corners. A ±0.005 mm tolerance on a non-mating face adds CMM inspection time and cost without functional benefit. A 0.3 mm internal corner radius in a deep pocket requires a tool so slender it deflects before completing the cut — the result is chatter, poor finish, and a non-conforming part. Both are caught at quote stage during DFM review. The fix is usually a tolerance relaxation or a corner radius increase — neither affects function, both reduce cost and lead time.

**Supporting paragraph 2:**
DFM review is included with every Weldo CNC quote — no charge, no delay. Issues are flagged in the quote response with a clear description of what was found and what the options are. Nothing is cut first and explained later.

## SECTION 16 — FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS
*Design: H2, accordion. White background. First item open.*

**H2:**
Precision Machining Services — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1 (open by default):**
Q: What tolerances can Weldo CNC hold on precision machined parts?
A: Standard structural work follows ISO 2768-m — ±0.1 mm on linear dimensions, suitable for brackets, housings, and frames where no feature mates with a precision component. Functional interfaces — locating features, press fits, sliding fits — are machined to ±0.02 mm. For critical precision work — bearing seats, sealing surfaces, bores with interference fits — Weldo CNC holds ±0.005 mm, CMM-verified and reported per feature against your drawing callout. On 5-axis work for complex geometry, ±0.003 mm is achievable. DFM review at quote stage flags overspecified tolerances — applying ±0.005 mm to non-mating surfaces adds cost without functional benefit.

**Q2:**
Q: What precision machining processes does Weldo CNC offer?
A: CNC milling (3-axis, 4-axis, 5-axis), CNC turning (2-axis, live tooling, Swiss-style for slender parts), surface grinding, internal grinding, centerless grinding, and wire EDM — Electrical Discharge Machining. Process selection is determined by part geometry and tolerance class, not machine availability. Parts requiring a combination — turned OD, milled cross-features, ground seating face — are handled at a single facility without subcontracting.

**Q3:**
Q: What materials can Weldo CNC precision machine?
A: Metals include aluminium (6061, 7075, 2024 and other grades), stainless steel (303, 304, 316L, 17-4PH and others), titanium (Grade 2 and Grade 5), tool steel (D2, H13), alloy steels (4140, 4340), brass, copper, and bronze. Engineering plastics include PEEK, Delrin (POM), Nylon, ABS, polycarbonate, PTFE, acrylic, and HDPE. Across metals and plastics, 100+ machinable materials are available. If your material isn't listed, send the spec — machinability will be confirmed before quoting.

**Q4:**
Q: What are the lead times for precision machined parts?
A: Prototype orders — typically 1–5 business days for standard geometry and materials. Complex precision parts requiring 5-axis machining or grinding — 7–8 business days. Small batch production runs — 5–15 business days. Full production quantities — 2–5 weeks depending on volume and complexity. Quote turnaround is 24 hours for orders with a complete STEP file and drawing.

**Q5:**
Q: Is there a minimum order quantity for precision machining?
A: No minimum order quantity. Single-part prototype orders are accepted. The same quality process — CMM inspection, material certificates, dimensional inspection report — applies whether you order one precision part or one thousand. Per-unit cost is higher on single pieces because setup time is constant regardless of run length, but there is no floor on order size.

**Q6:**
Q: What file formats does Weldo CNC accept for precision machining quotes?
A: STEP (.stp / .step) is preferred — it carries full 3D geometry and is the basis for toolpath generation. IGES is also accepted. SolidWorks (.sldprt), Pro/E (.prt), and Parasolid (.x_t) files are accepted. For 2D-only geometry, a fully dimensioned PDF or DXF works for simple prismatic parts. Upload what you have — if additional information is needed, you'll be told exactly what's missing.

**Q7:**
Q: How does Weldo CNC verify precision machined parts meet drawing specifications?
A: Every tight-tolerance order ships with a CMM-verified dimensional inspection report. The report records measured value, nominal dimension, tolerance, and pass/fail per feature — against your drawing revision number. Material certificates trace alloy grade and heat number to the specific parts in your shipment. Weldo CNC holds a 97.48% annual pass rate across all CNC machining orders. Non-conforming parts are quarantined and processed through a documented NCR — Non-Conformance Report — before any disposition is made.

**Q8:**
Q: How much does precision machining cost?
A: Cost depends on part geometry, material, tolerance class, quantity, number of setups, and required surface finish. Setup cost dominates on low-volume runs — a single precision prototype costs more per unit than a batch of 50 because setup time is the same. Tight tolerances (±0.005 mm) require CMM inspection time and may need dedicated fixturing, which adds cost. Grinding and EDM operations carry additional cycle time over standard milling or turning. Upload your STEP file to receive an itemised quote within 24 hours.

**Q9:**
Q: When should I use grinding or EDM instead of CNC milling or turning?
A: Grinding is the right choice when flatness, parallelism, or surface finish requirements are below what milling or turning can reliably deliver — typically flatness tolerances tighter than ±0.01 mm or Ra surface finish requirements below Ra 0.8 µm. Grinding is also used as a finishing pass on hardened steel after heat treatment, because hardened material cannot be cut cleanly with conventional tooling. Wire EDM — Electrical Discharge Machining — is the right choice when the profile is in hardened tool steel, when the feature is too fine or thin-walled for a conventional cutter to reach, or when zero cutting force is required to avoid distortion. If your drawing has features that fall into these categories, flag them at quote stage.

**Q10:**
Q: How does Weldo CNC compare to online precision machining marketplaces like Hubs or Xometry?
A: Hubs and Xometry are order-routing platforms — your order goes to whichever shop in their network has capacity. The precision machining quality standard varies by supplier, and you have no direct relationship with the facility cutting your parts. Weldo CNC machines every order in-house at its own 100-machine facility in Dongguan — one facility, one ISO 9001:2015 quality system, CMM report with every tight-tolerance order. The 97.48% annual pass rate is measured across a single operation, not averaged across a supplier network. For prototype work where speed matters more than documentation, a marketplace can work. For production precision machined parts where you need traceability and a consistent quality record, a direct facility relationship is the difference.
