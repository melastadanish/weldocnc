## Section 3: What is Aluminium 5-Axis CNC Machining?

**H2:** What is Aluminium 5-Axis CNC Machining?

Weldo CNC machines aluminium — 6061-T6, 7075-T6, 2024-T3, 5052-H32, and 6082-T6 — using simultaneous 5-axis CNC machining to tolerances of ±0.003 mm on complex geometries, from its ISO 9001:2015 certified facility in Dongguan, China. Parts with compound angles, undercuts, blended surfaces, and multi-face features complete in a single setup. Prototype parts ship in 1–5 business days, with a full CMM — coordinate measuring machine — dimensional report in every tight-tolerance shipment.

Five-axis machining adds two rotary axes to the standard X, Y, Z linear motion of 3-axis milling. The trunnion — a tilting and rotating table — repositions the part continuously while the spindle cuts. This matters for aluminium aerospace and robotics components where multiple angled faces, pocket undercuts, and compound-radius blends appear on the same part. A bracket that would require four separate 3-axis setups — and accumulate datum shift error at each re-clamping — machines in one 5-axis setup, holding all features to a single datum.

Aluminium machines exceptionally well in 5-axis configurations. Its high machinability rating allows faster spindle speeds and deeper passes than titanium or stainless, reducing cycle time on complex parts. Chip control at aggressive 5-axis feed rates is managed with high-pressure through-spindle coolant and proper tool selection — preventing chip re-cutting on pocket floors, which degrades surface finish below Ra 1.6 µm.

Weldo CNC has machined 5-axis aluminium components since 2012. Typical part types include camera housings, UAV structural frames, robotic arm links, avionics enclosures, optical instrument bodies, and impeller-like geometries requiring simultaneous multi-axis interpolation.

## Section 5: Material Trade-off Cards

**H2:** Choosing the Right Aluminium Grade for 5-Axis Parts

**Subheading:** 5-axis unlocks geometry that 3-axis cannot reach — but grade selection still governs load capacity and finish quality.

**Card 1 — When 6061-T6 is the Right Choice**

6061-T6 covers the majority of 5-axis aluminium work. Its machinability rating is excellent at the spindle speeds that 5-axis programmes require — aggressive feed rates, long-reach tools, and continuous contour cuts all proceed without built-up edge or chatter. For enclosures, brackets, and housings where the 5-axis feature access is the design driver (not extreme load capacity), 6061-T6 delivers the right result at the best cycle time and cost.

*Key spec: Tensile 310 MPa · Density 2.70 g/cm³ · Machinability: Excellent*

**Card 2 — When 7075-T6 Changes the Equation**

UAV frames, aerospace structural brackets, and high-load robotics links specify 7075-T6 because the 572 MPa tensile strength — nearly double 6061 — carries the load at minimum part mass. Five-axis machining is frequently the only way to produce the thin webs, compound-angle pockets, and curved rib geometry that make 7075 parts weight-efficient. Machining is good but slightly slower than 6061, and anodizing requires careful specification — 7075 produces a thinner oxide than 6061 under identical anodizing conditions.

*Key spec: Tensile 572 MPa · Density 2.81 g/cm³ · Machinability: Good*

**Card 3 — When 2024-T3 is the Engineering Answer**

2024-T3 is the grade for fatigue-loaded complex aluminium geometry — curved spars, ribs, and structural skins that cycle through bending loads. Five-axis machining produces the blended radii and compound-face geometry that minimise stress concentrations in these parts. 2024 does not weld and has lower corrosion resistance than 6061, so alodine (chromate conversion coating) or anodizing is standard on exposed surfaces.

*Key spec: Tensile 483 MPa · Fatigue crack resistance: superior to 6061 and 7075 under cyclic load*

## Section 10: Aluminium 5-Axis CNC Machining Design Guidelines

**H2:** Aluminium 5-Axis Machining Design Guidelines

**Subheading:** 5-axis capability changes what is possible — but aluminium's material behaviour still constrains what is optimal.

| Feature | Recommendation | Why It Matters |
|---|---|---|
| Undercut features | Design undercuts accessible to a 5-axis indexed or simultaneous tool path — avoid re-entrant geometries requiring 6-axis access | True re-entrant undercuts (facing back on themselves) require wire EDM or cannot be machined — flag at DFM before programme is written |
| Wall thickness in 5-axis pockets | Minimum 0.8 mm for walls under 50 mm height. Thicker for walls over 50 mm height | Slender walls deflect under the cutting force at the tool's contact point — deflection opens the tolerance on the opposite face |
| Tool reach planning | Keep feature depth within 4× tool diameter where possible. Longer reaches require reduced feed | Long-reach cutting increases tool deflection — position tolerance degrades with reach. Flag deep features at quote stage |
| Datum selection for 5-axis parts | Nominate a primary datum face on the drawing — the trunnion indexes to this face | Without a nominated datum, each setup interpretation differs between CAM programmer and quality inspection |
| Compound angle tolerances | Specify the angular tolerance on compound faces (e.g. ±0.05°) | Compound angles are not verifiable by caliper — CMM is required. Without a callout, compound faces default to the general angular tolerance |
| Blended radius transitions | Minimum R2.0 mm at surface blend transitions | Tighter blends require smaller-diameter ball-end tools and slower feeds — add significant cycle time. R2.0 is achievable at standard cycle rates |
| Internal pocket corners | Minimum R1.0 mm for internal pocket corners on aluminium 5-axis parts | Sharp internal corners at compound angles require specialist grinding or EDM — not achievable by standard 5-axis milling |
| Surface finish on contoured faces | Specify Ra value — default finishing pass achieves Ra 1.6 µm on curved aluminium surfaces | Ra 0.8 µm on compound surfaces requires smaller ball-end step-over — adds cycle time. Specify only where functionally required |

**Most common DFM failures on 5-axis aluminium parts:**

Unmasked anodizing of pocket interiors is the most common failure on complex aluminium 5-axis parts. Engineers specify anodize on the drawing with no masking notes, and the anodizing shop anodizes all surfaces uniformly. Tight bores and threaded features then fail fit-check due to the 5–15 µm dimensional growth from anodizing. The fix: specify masking on the drawing before the part is quoted — masking planning is part of DFM review, not an afterthought at the finisher.

Insufficient datum definition on multi-face 5-axis parts is the second pattern. When the drawing has no primary datum face called out, the CAM programmer and CMM inspector interpret the part geometry differently, producing a datum discrepancy that shows up as angular errors on compound faces. One nominated datum — clearly annotated on the drawing — eliminates this class of error before the first trunnion index.

**Step 1 — Pre-Production: Material Verified Before Cutting Starts**

Mill certificate checked — alloy grade, temper, heat number confirmed before the billet is fixtured on the trunnion. CAM — computer-aided manufacturing — programme verified against the 3D model before first cut. Datum face confirmed between CAM and CMM programmes to ensure inspection reads from the same reference as the machining setup.

**Step 2 — In-Production: In-Process Checks at Every Setup Change**

CMM — coordinate measuring machine — checks on compound-face features and critical pocket dimensions at defined intervals. Non-Conformance Report (NCR) triggered on deviation. For long-run 5-axis programmes, first-article inspection — measuring all features on the first part off the programme — confirms CAM accuracy before subsequent parts run.

**Step 3 — Final Inspection: CMM Report Ships With Every Tight-Tolerance Order**

Every critical dimension measured: compound angles, pocket depths, position of features relative to the datum. Full report — nominal, tolerance, actual, pass/fail — in every tight-tolerance shipment alongside the material certificate.

## Section 15: FAQ

**H2:** Aluminium 5-Axis CNC Machining — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: What tolerance can Weldo CNC hold on aluminium 5-axis parts?**

Five-axis aluminium machining holds ±0.003 mm on critical features — tighter than the ±0.005 mm achievable in standard 3-axis milling, because 5-axis eliminates repositioning error between setups. General dimensions follow ISO 2768-m at ±0.05 mm. Complex surface profiles and compound-angle features are verified by CMM — a coordinate measuring machine — not caliper or visual inspection. Specify the tolerance on your drawing and identify which features are critical.

**Q2: What is the advantage of 5-axis machining for aluminium parts?**

Five-axis simultaneous machining allows compound angles, undercuts, and blended surfaces to be cut in a single clamping. For aluminium parts — UAV frames, camera housings, aerospace brackets — this eliminates the datum shift error that accumulates when a part is repositioned between 3-axis setups. Each repositioning introduces up to 0.01–0.02 mm of datum uncertainty; on a tight-tolerance bracket with three compound faces, those errors add. Five-axis removes the repositioning and holds all features to one datum.

**Q3: What aluminium grades do you use for 5-axis machining?**

Confirmed grades for 5-axis aluminium machining include 6061-T6, 7075-T6, 2024-T3, 5052-H32, 6082-T6, and MIC-6. 6061-T6 covers general complex geometry. 7075-T6 for high-load aerospace and UAV structures. 2024-T3 for fatigue-critical curved forms. Grade selection follows the same engineering logic as 3-axis work — 5-axis changes the geometry achievable, not the material properties required.

**Q9: Can 5-axis machining produce internal undercut features in aluminium?**

Yes — with geometry limits. Five-axis indexed positions can reach undercut faces inaccessible to 3-axis machines, using the trunnion to tilt the part until the undercut face is approachable by the spindle. Simultaneous 5-axis interpolation can machine sweeping undercut surfaces in one continuous path. True re-entrant undercuts — where the feature faces back toward the entry direction at an acute angle — are not reachable by 5-axis milling and require EDM. Weldo CNC identifies re-entrant geometry at DFM review and proposes the correct process.

Anodizing — Type II and Type III — bead blasting, alodine, powder coating, and polishing are all compatible with 5-axis aluminium parts. Complex geometry adds masking complexity: pocket interiors, threaded features, and precision bores must be masked before anodizing to prevent dimensional growth in critical areas. Specify masking requirements on the drawing or in the quote notes. Surface finish planning for complex geometry is part of DFM review — not a post-machining decision.
