## Section 3: What is Stainless Steel 5-Axis CNC Machining?

**H2:** What is Stainless Steel 5-Axis CNC Machining?

Weldo CNC machines stainless steel — 303, 304, 316L, 431, and 17-4PH — using simultaneous 5-axis CNC machining to tolerances of ±0.003 mm on compound-angle features and multi-face geometry, from its ISO 9001:2015 certified facility in Dongguan, China. Complex parts with undercuts, angled bores, blended surfaces, and features on non-parallel faces complete in a single setup. Prototype parts ship in 1–5 business days with a full CMM — coordinate measuring machine — dimensional report in every tight-tolerance shipment.

Five-axis stainless machining is more demanding than aluminium 5-axis because stainless work-hardens under the cutting tool. The trunnion — the tilting and rotating table that repositions the part between indexed positions — must present each face to the spindle at an orientation that keeps chip formation clean and coolant delivery effective. Long-reach tools cutting into deep pockets on 316L require reduced feed rates and high-pressure coolant to prevent chip packing, which traps heat and accelerates the work-hardening layer.

The reason to use 5-axis for stainless is the same as for aluminium: single-setup machining eliminates datum shift. An implant component with three compound bores on different axis planes, each held to ±0.005 mm position, needs all three bores to reference the same datum. Four separate 3-axis setups each introduce 0.01–0.02 mm of datum uncertainty — which compounds across features. Simultaneous 5-axis machining holds all features in one clamping, referencing one datum throughout.

Weldo CNC has machined 5-axis stainless components since 2012. Typical part types include surgical instrument bodies, complex valve bodies, aerospace structural fittings, pump impellers, and medical implant-adjacent components where both corrosion resistance and complex geometry are required.

## Section 5: Material Trade-off Cards

**H2:** Choosing the Right Stainless Grade for 5-Axis Parts

**Subheading:** 5-axis removes geometry constraints — grade selection still governs corrosion resistance and load capacity.

**Card 1 — When 316L is the Right Choice**

Medical instrument bodies, food-contact components, and marine hardware with complex geometry almost always specify 316L. Its molybdenum content resists chloride-induced pitting — the failure mode that plain 304 cannot resist in body-fluid or saltwater exposure. Five-axis machining is frequently the only route to the compound bore angles and blended internal features that make these parts functional. The machining cost is higher than 304 or 303 — that is the cost of the corrosion resistance, not a machining premium specific to 5-axis.

*Key spec: Tensile 485 MPa · Chloride resistance: superior to 304 · Low carbon ≤0.03% prevents sensitisation*

**Card 2 — When 17-4PH Changes the Equation**

Aerospace structural fittings and high-load medical device components specify 17-4PH where 316L's 485 MPa tensile strength is insufficient for the load case. The grade combines stainless corrosion resistance with tensile strength up to 1,310 MPa after H900 ageing. Five-axis machining is used for the compound-angle features, pocket undercuts, and blended radius geometry that define these parts. Machine in annealed condition where possible, age to H900, then finish-machine critical features — dimensional change during ageing is small but real.

*Key spec: Tensile 1,310 MPa (H900) · Combines stainless corrosion resistance with high-strength structural performance*

**Card 3 — When 303 is the Engineering Answer**

303 is the stainless grade to choose when the part geometry drives complexity but the service environment is mild — indoor, non-chloride, non-food-contact. Its free-machining chemistry — sulphur additions — produces clean chips at faster cutting speeds than 304 or 316L, which reduces cycle time and cost on complex 5-axis programmes. It does not passivate as effectively as 304 or 316L and is not suitable for applications where ISO 10993 biocompatibility or chloride resistance is required.

*Key spec: Tensile 620 MPa · Machinability: Excellent (best of all stainless grades)*

## Section 10: Stainless Steel 5-Axis Machining Design Guidelines

**H2:** Stainless Steel 5-Axis Machining Design Guidelines

**Subheading:** 5-axis stainless machining combines the geometry constraints of 5-axis with the material constraints of stainless — both must be addressed at DFM stage.

| Feature | Recommendation | Why It Matters |
|---|---|---|
| Minimum wall thickness | 1.0 mm for austenitic stainless (304, 316L). 1.2 mm for 17-4PH in H900 | Stainless walls deflect more than aluminium under cutting forces at the same thickness — tolerance degrades on thin sections |
| Cutting depth on 5-axis stainless | Do not skim — cut through the work-hardened layer on each pass | Skim passes in stainless add to the work-hardened surface depth, amplifying the problem on the next pass |
| Coolant on 5-axis stainless | High-pressure through-spindle coolant on all 316L and 17-4PH programmes | Insufficient coolant in 5-axis stainless cuts causes chip packing in pockets, heat spike, and carbide tool fracture |
| Undercut geometry in stainless | All undercut faces must be accessble to the 5-axis tool at a minimum 30° spindle-to-face angle | Shallower angles on stainless undercuts produce rubbing rather than cutting — work hardening accumulates and surface finish degrades |
| Internal blend radius | Minimum R2.0 mm on 5-axis blended transitions in stainless | Smaller blend radii require smaller-diameter ball-end tools, slower feeds, and more passes — add significant cycle time in stainless |
| 17-4PH machining sequence | Rough machine in annealed condition → age to H900 → finish machine critical features | Ageing causes small dimensional growth — finishing after ageing ensures critical tolerances are held in the final material condition |
| Thread specification on stainless | Specify 6H/6g class for stainless. Note anti-galling compound where stainless mates with stainless | Galling is the primary thread failure mode in stainless assemblies — a class specification and lubrication note prevent it |
| Passivation masking on complex parts | Mark on drawing which bores, threads, and precision features require masking before passivation | Unmasked passivation can alter the finish on critical surfaces — masking plan must be agreed at DFM, not after machining |

**Most common DFM failures on 5-axis stainless parts:**

Work hardening failures appear as surface finish degradation on complex pocket floors and compound-face finishes. The cause is usually insufficient cutting depth on the first pass — the tool skims the hardened layer instead of cutting through it, which compounds the hardening on the next pass. The programme must be written to cut through the hardened zone on each pass, not reduce depth to protect the tool. Weldo CNC configures stainless-specific cutting parameters at the programming stage, not after the first part fails.

Unplanned passivation of threaded features is the second common failure. Engineers specify passivation on the drawing, the finisher passivates all surfaces, and threads that were machined to a tight fit are now tight enough to gall on assembly — the acid bath cleaned the surface without changing dimensions, but the thread surfaces are now slightly higher-friction. The fix is a masking note on the drawing: "Mask all threads before passivation unless thread passivation confirmed as compatible with assembly."

## Section 15: FAQ

**H2:** Stainless Steel 5-Axis CNC Machining — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: What tolerance can Weldo CNC hold on 5-axis stainless steel parts?**

Five-axis stainless machining holds ±0.003 mm on critical features — tighter than standard 3-axis milling because single-setup machining eliminates repositioning datum shift. General dimensions follow ISO 2768-m at ±0.05 mm. Compound-angle features, blended surfaces, and position of features across datum planes are verified by CMM. Specify critical features and their tolerances on your drawing.

**Q2: Why use 5-axis machining for stainless steel parts?**

Five-axis machining eliminates the setup changes that accumulate datum error on stainless parts with complex geometry. A surgical instrument body with compound-angle features on three faces — each held to ±0.005 mm — needs those features referenced to one datum. Repositioning between 3-axis setups introduces up to 0.01–0.02 mm datum uncertainty per setup, which compounds. Five-axis holds all features in one clamping, one datum, one inspection baseline.

**Q3: What stainless grades do you machine for 5-axis work?**

Confirmed grades for 5-axis stainless machining include 303, 304, 316L, 431, and 17-4PH. 316L for medical, marine, and chloride environments. 17-4PH for high-strength aerospace and medical components. 303 for complex geometry in mild service environments where machinability matters. Grade selection follows the corrosion and strength requirements of the application — 5-axis capability is the same across all grades, but cycle time varies with machinability.

**Q9: Can 5-axis machining produce internal undercut features in stainless steel?**

Yes, with the same geometry limits as aluminium. Five-axis indexed positions can reach undercut faces inaccessible to 3-axis tools. Simultaneous 5-axis can machine sweeping internal undercut surfaces. True re-entrant geometry — where the undercut faces back at an acute angle toward the entry — requires wire EDM, not milling. Stainless undercuts require the spindle to approach at a steep enough angle to cut rather than rub — shallow approach angles cause work hardening. Weldo CNC identifies these conditions at DFM review.

**Q10: What is electropolishing and does it work on 5-axis stainless parts?**

Electropolishing — an electrochemical process that removes 10–30 µm of surface material — improves stainless surface finish from Ra 0.8 µm to Ra 0.2–0.4 µm and enhances corrosion resistance by removing the micro-roughness that traps contaminants. It is compatible with 304, 316L, and 17-4PH — and works on complex 5-axis geometry as long as the part can be immersed in the bath. Threads and precision bores are masked before electropolishing to prevent over-removal. Specify electropolishing and masking requirements on the drawing — the finish is planned at DFM review, not after machining.
