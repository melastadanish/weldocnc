## SECTION 3 — WHAT IS 5-AXIS CNC MACHINING
*Design: H2, 2-column — body text left, image right. White background.*

**H2:**
What is 5-Axis CNC Machining?

**Body paragraph 1 (AI-quotable — self-contained):**
Weldo CNC provides 5-axis CNC machining services to tolerances of ±0.003 mm — 3+2 positional and full simultaneous — from its own 100-machine facility in Dongguan, China. ISO 9001:2015 certified — complex geometry orders ship with a CMM-verified dimensional inspection report and surface profile measurement referenced against your drawing. Quote in 24 hours, DFM review included.

**Body paragraph 2:**
Standard 3-axis machining moves the cutting tool on X, Y, and Z. 5-axis adds two rotational axes — typically A and B, or A and C depending on the machine configuration. Those extra axes let the spindle approach the workpiece from almost any angle, which fundamentally changes what's machinable in a single setup.

**Body paragraph 3:**
The practical consequence: a part requiring four separate setups on a 3-axis machine — re-clamped four times, datum re-established four times, positional error accumulating with each move — machines in one. The geometry is the same. The tolerance stack-up is not. Single-setup machining is more accurate because datum error doesn't compound across transitions.

**Body paragraph 4:**
The second argument is access. Undercuts, compound angles, contoured surfaces, and features on multiple faces that a 3-axis tool simply cannot reach with the workpiece in a fixed orientation — 5-axis reaches them. That's why aerospace structural parts, turbine components, impellers, and complex medical implants are almost always machined this way.

## SECTION 3b — TWO MODES OF 5-AXIS MACHINING
*Design: H2 centred, subheading, 3-column card grid. Light grey background.*

**H2:**
Two Modes of 5-Axis Machining

**Subheading:**
Most parts don't need full simultaneous motion. Knowing which mode your geometry requires avoids paying for capability you don't need.

**Card 1 — 3+2 Positional (Indexed) 5-Axis:**
Heading: 3+2 Positional 5-Axis
Body: The machine tilts the workpiece to a fixed angle using two rotational axes — indexed — then locks that position and machines with the remaining three. Think of it as 3-axis machining at a non-standard approach angle. This covers the majority of work commonly called "5-axis" — accessing angled features, machining multiple faces without re-clamping, reaching undercuts that a vertical 3-axis tool can't enter. Fast, reliable, and more accurate than simultaneous on many feature types.
Specs:
- Best for: Multi-face prismatic parts, angled holes, undercuts, compound-angle features
- Right choice for: Most parts described as "needing 5-axis"

**Card 2 — Full Simultaneous 5-Axis:**
Heading: Full Simultaneous 5-Axis
Body: All five axes move at the same time. The tool tip traces a complex path in 3D space while the workpiece rotates and tilts continuously — the rotary axis working in concert with linear motion throughout the cut. Required for true freeform surfaces — turbine blades, impeller vanes, complex organic shapes where the surface normal changes continuously along the toolpath. When you genuinely need it, nothing else produces the same result.
Specs:
- Best for: Impellers, turbine blades, complex curved surfaces, sculptured forms
- Required for: True freeform geometry with continuously changing surface normals

**Card 3 — Which Mode Does Your Part Need?**
Heading: Which Mode Does Your Part Need?
Body: Upload the STEP file. The geometry is assessed in CAM and the mode confirmed — 3+2 where it's sufficient, simultaneous where it's genuinely required. An impeller needs simultaneous. A bracket with features on five faces at compound angles needs 3+2. Specifying simultaneous for a part that doesn't require it adds cycle time and cost for no benefit — that call gets made at quote stage, not after the job starts.
Specs:
- DFM review confirms the right mode before quoting
- No upselling to a higher mode than the geometry requires

## SECTION 3c — 5-AXIS VS. 3-AXIS: WHEN YOU ACTUALLY NEED IT
*Design: H2, 2-column comparison cards. White background.*

**H2:**
5-Axis vs. 3-Axis CNC Machining

**Use 5-Axis When:**
The geometry demands it — three situations apply: (1) features on multiple faces requiring 4+ setups on a 3-axis machine, where single-setup 5-axis is faster and more accurate overall; (2) undercuts, compound angles, or deep cavities a vertical or horizontal tool path can't reach; (3) true freeform curved surfaces where the tool must maintain consistent engagement with a continuously changing surface normal. If any apply, 5-axis is the answer.
- Multi-face components, compound angles, undercuts
- Impellers, turbine blades, complex curved surfaces
- Any part where setup reduction improves tolerance stack-up

**Use 3-Axis When:**
The part is prismatic and all features are accessible from standard orientations. Most brackets, housings, plates, and blocks qualify. Adding 5-axis to a part that doesn't need it adds machine time and complexity without improving the result. If 3-axis is sufficient for the geometry, that's what gets quoted.
- Flat faces, orthogonal holes, standard pockets
- Features accessible without tilting the workpiece
- Cost-sensitive work where geometry permits 3-axis

## SECTION 9 — 5-AXIS CNC MACHINING DESIGN GUIDELINES
*Design: H2, subheading, full-width 3-column table + supporting paragraphs. White background.*

**H2:**
5-Axis CNC Machining Design Guidelines

**Subheading:**
Rules specific to 5-axis geometry — these differ from 3-axis guidelines in important ways.

**Design guidelines table:**

| Feature | Recommendation | Why It Matters |
|---|---|---|
| Wall thickness | ≥ 0.8 mm (metals) · ≥ 1.5 mm (plastics) | Thin walls vibrate under 5-axis cutting forces — varied approach angles make this worse than 3-axis |
| Internal corner radius | ≥ 1/3 of cavity depth | Tool diameter governs minimum achievable radius — same rule as 3-axis |
| Deep cavities | Flag depth > 4× width | Long-reach tooling required — affects rigidity and surface finish |
| Freeform surfaces | Provide nominal surface + tolerance band | "Machine to CAD" is not a tolerance — specify allowable deviation from nominal in mm |
| Undercuts | No restriction — confirm access angle | 5-axis reaches most undercuts; document the required approach angle in drawing notes |
| Thin ribs | Keep aspect ratio low (height:thickness) | Tall thin ribs flex during cutting — chatter, deflection, and tolerance failure |
| Part datum | Specify primary datum face clearly | 5-axis setups reference from datum — ambiguous datum makes inspection unreliable |
| Freeform surface callouts | Specify profile of a surface tolerance | Freeform surfaces can't be inspected with a point tolerance — surface profile is the correct GD&T callout |
| Thread access | Confirm approach angle clears the bore | 5-axis can tap at angle — tool access must be verified in CAM before committing |
| Near-symmetric parts | Flag orientation clearly on drawing | Parts that look identical from two orientations but aren't — add an orientation mark to avoid assembly errors |

**Supporting paragraph 1:**
One issue comes up consistently with 5-axis parts: freeform surface tolerances. A complex curved surface with "machine to model" in the drawing notes has no pass/fail criteria — it's an instruction, not a tolerance. Before quoting, confirmation is needed of the acceptable deviation from nominal. For most aerodynamic surfaces ±0.05 mm is the working answer; for sealing or mating surfaces it's tighter. Getting that number agreed before cutting starts prevents disputes at inspection.

**Supporting paragraph 2:**
Part datum specification is equally important. A 5-axis setup references everything from the primary datum — if the datum face is ambiguous or poorly constrained, the inspection report and the CAM program can disagree on what "correct" means for a feature at a compound angle.

*Design: H2, accordion. White background. First item open.*

**H2:**
5-Axis CNC Machining — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1 (open by default):**
Q: What is 5-axis CNC machining and how does it differ from 3-axis?
A: 3-axis moves the cutting tool on X, Y, and Z. 5-axis adds two rotational axes — letting the spindle approach the workpiece from almost any angle. The practical difference: features on multiple faces machine in a single setup, compound angles and undercuts become accessible, and true freeform curved surfaces can be produced. The key benefit isn't exotic geometry — it's setup reduction. A 4-setup 3-axis job becomes a single-setup 5-axis job, and the tolerance stack-up improves because datum error doesn't accumulate across setups.

**Q2:**
Q: What is the difference between 3+2 and simultaneous 5-axis machining?
A: 3+2 positional 5-axis: the machine tilts the workpiece to a fixed angle using the rotational axes — indexed — locks that position, and machines with three axes. This handles the majority of 5-axis work. Simultaneous 5-axis: all five axes move at the same time, tracing a complex 3D path continuously. Required for true freeform surfaces — turbine blades, impellers, complex organic forms. Simultaneous is harder to program, has longer cycle times, and costs more. If 3+2 achieves the geometry, that's what gets used.

**Q3:**
Q: What tolerances can Weldo CNC hold on 5-axis machined parts?
A: ±0.003 mm on critical features in a single setup — confirmed by CMM. This is the specific capability of 5-axis simultaneous machining, where eliminating workpiece repositioning removes the datum error that limits 3-axis tolerance stacking. Complex 5-axis geometry — long tool reaches, thin walls at compound angles, freeform surfaces — introduces variables that require careful toolpath strategy. These are assessed during DFM review and flagged if a callout is at the edge of reliable capability before cutting starts.

Q: How much does 5-axis CNC machining cost compared to 3-axis?
