## Section 3: What is 5-Axis CNC Machining
**Design:** 2-column — text left, image right. White background.

Standard 3-axis machining moves the cutting tool on X, Y, and Z. 5-axis adds two rotational axes — typically A and B, or A and C depending on the machine configuration. Those two extra axes let the spindle approach the workpiece from almost any angle, which changes what's machinable in a single setup fundamentally.

The practical consequence: a part that would require four separate setups on a 3-axis machine — re-clamped four times, datum re-established four times, accumulating positional error with each move — machines in one. The geometry is the same. The tolerance stack-up is not. Single-setup machining is tighter because datum error doesn't compound.

That's the core argument for 5 axis cnc machining. Not that it can machine exotic shapes nobody else can touch — though it can. The first argument is simpler: it machines ordinary complex parts with fewer opportunities for error to enter.

The second argument is access. Undercuts, compound angles, contoured surfaces, and features on multiple faces that a 3-axis tool simply cannot reach with the workpiece fixed — 5-axis reaches them. That's why aerospace structural parts, turbine components, impellers, and complex medical implants are almost always machined this way.

## Section 3b: 3+2 Positioning vs. Full Simultaneous 5-Axis
**Design:** 3-column card grid. Light grey background.
**Heading:** Two Modes of 5-Axis Machining
**Subheading:** Most parts don't need full simultaneous motion. Knowing which mode your geometry requires avoids paying for capability you don't need.

**Card 1 — 3+2 Positional (Indexed) 5-Axis**
The machine tilts the workpiece to a fixed angle using two rotational axes, then locks that position and machines with the remaining three. Think of it as 3-axis machining at a non-standard approach angle. This covers the majority of what people call "5-axis work" — accessing angled features, machining multiple faces without re-clamping, reaching undercuts that a vertical 3-axis tool can't enter. Fast, reliable, and more accurate than full simultaneous on many feature types.
- Best for: Multi-face prismatic parts, angled holes, undercuts, compound-angle features
- Covers 70–80% of 5-axis applications

**Card 2 — Full Simultaneous 5-Axis**
All five axes move at the same time. The tool tip traces a complex path in 3D space while the workpiece rotates and tilts continuously. Required for true freeform surfaces — turbine blades, impeller vanes, complex organic shapes where the surface normal changes continuously along the toolpath. Harder to program, longer cycle times, and demands tighter machine calibration. When you actually need it, nothing else will do.
- Best for: Impellers, turbine blades, complex curved surfaces, sculptured forms
- Required for true freeform geometry

**Card 3 — Which Does Your Part Need?**
Send us the STEP file. We assess the geometry and specify the mode — 3+2 where it's sufficient, simultaneous where it's genuinely required. An impeller needs simultaneous. A bracket with features on five faces at compound angles needs 3+2. Specifying simultaneous for a part that doesn't need it adds cycle time and cost for no benefit. We won't do that.
- DFM review confirms the right mode before quoting
- No upselling to a higher mode than the geometry requires

## Section 3c: 5-Axis vs. 3-Axis — When You Actually Need It
**Design:** 2-column comparison. White background.
**Heading:** 5-Axis vs. 3-Axis CNC Machining

**Use 5-Axis When:**
The geometry demands it — not because it sounds impressive. Three situations apply: (1) features on multiple faces that would require 4+ setups on a 3-axis machine, making single-setup 5-axis faster and more accurate overall; (2) features that can't be accessed with a vertical or horizontal tool path — undercuts, compound angles, deep cavities with restricted access; (3) freeform curved surfaces where the tool must maintain a consistent engagement angle with a continuously changing surface normal. If any of these apply, 5-axis is the answer.
- Multi-face components, compound angles, undercuts
- Impellers, turbine blades, complex curved surfaces
- Any part where setup reduction improves tolerance stack-up

**Use 3-Axis When:**
The part is prismatic and all features are accessible from standard orientations. Most brackets, housings, plates, and blocks qualify. Adding 5-axis to a part that doesn't need it adds machine time and complexity without improving the result. We'll always tell you if 3-axis is sufficient — we allocate by geometry, not by margin.
- Flat faces, orthogonal holes, standard pockets
- Features accessible from top, front, and side without tilting
- Cost-sensitive work where geometry permits 3-axis

## Section 9: Design Guidelines
**Design:** 3-column table + supporting copy. White background.
**Heading:** 5-Axis CNC Machining Design Guidelines

| Feature | Recommendation | Why It Matters |
|---|---|---|
| Wall thickness | ≥ 0.8mm metals / ≥ 1.5mm plastics | Thin walls vibrate under 5-axis cutting forces — varied approach angles make this worse than 3-axis |
| Internal corner radius | ≥ 1/3 of cavity depth | Tool diameter governs minimum achievable radius — same rule as 3-axis |
| Deep cavities | Flag depth > 4× width | Long-reach tooling required — affects rigidity and surface finish |
| Freeform surfaces | Provide nominal surface + tolerance band | "Machine to CAD" isn't a tolerance — specify allowable deviation from nominal |
| Undercuts | No restriction — confirm access angle | 5-axis reaches most undercuts; document required approach angle in drawing notes |
| Thin ribs | Aspect ratio ≤ 8:1 (height:thickness) | Tall thin ribs flex during cutting — chatter, deflection, and tolerance failure |
| Part datum | Specify primary datum face clearly | 5-axis setups reference from datum — ambiguous datum makes inspection difficult |
| Freeform surface callouts | Specify profile of a surface tolerance | Freeform surfaces can't be inspected with a point tolerance — surface profile is the correct GD&T callout |
| Thread access | Confirm approach angle clears the bore | 5-axis can tap at angle — but tool access must be verified in CAM before committing |
| Near-symmetric parts | Flag orientation clearly on drawing | Parts that look the same from two orientations but aren't — we'll add an orientation mark |

One thing that comes up regularly with 5-axis parts: freeform surface tolerances. An engineer specifies a complex curved surface and writes "machine to model" in the drawing notes. That isn't a tolerance — it's an instruction with no pass/fail criteria. We ask what the acceptable deviation from nominal is before quoting. For most aerodynamic surfaces ±0.05mm is the working answer; for sealing or mating surfaces it's tighter. Getting that number agreed before cutting starts prevents a dispute at inspection.

## Section 14: FAQ
**Design:** Accordion. White background. First item open.
**Heading:** 5-Axis CNC Machining — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: What is 5-axis CNC machining and how does it differ from 3-axis?**
3-axis moves the cutting tool on X, Y, and Z. 5-axis adds two rotational axes — letting the spindle approach the workpiece from almost any angle. The practical difference: features on multiple faces can be machined in a single setup, compound angles and undercuts become accessible, and freeform curved surfaces can be produced. The key benefit isn't exotic geometry — it's setup reduction. Fewer setups means fewer datum transitions and less accumulated positional error. A 4-setup 3-axis job becomes a single-setup 5-axis job, and the tolerance stack-up improves as a result.

**Q2: What's the difference between 3+2 and simultaneous 5-axis machining?**
3+2 (positional 5-axis): the machine tilts the workpiece to a fixed angle, locks it, and machines with three axes. This handles the majority of 5-axis work — angled features, multi-face access, undercuts. Simultaneous 5-axis: all five axes move at the same time, tracing a complex 3D path continuously. Required for true freeform surfaces — turbine blades, impellers, complex organic forms. Simultaneous is harder to program, has longer cycle times, and costs more. If 3+2 achieves the geometry, that's what we'll use.

**Q3: What tolerances can you hold on 5-axis machined parts?**
±0.005mm on critical features, CMM-verified — same as our 3-axis capability. The honest note: complex 5-axis geometry introduces variables that don't exist in 3-axis work. Long tool reaches, thin walls at compound angles, and freeform surfaces all require careful toolpath strategy to maintain tolerance. We assess these during DFM and will tell you if a callout is at the edge of what's reliable before cutting starts.

**Q4: How much does 5-axis CNC machining cost compared to 3-axis?**
5 axis cnc machining price depends on part complexity, number of setups, and whether 3+2 or simultaneous motion is needed. For parts that genuinely require 5-axis, the cost comparison against 3-axis is often misleading — 3-axis might need 4 setups and 4 fixture builds to achieve the same result, making apparent machine-rate savings illusory. For parts that don't require 5-axis, we'll say so and quote accordingly. Send the file — we'll advise the right process and the honest cost.

**Q5: What industries use 5-axis CNC machining most?**
Aerospace is the biggest driver — structural components with multiple mounting faces, compound-angle features, and GD&T callouts that 3-axis can't hold in a multi-setup sequence. Medical is second — implants and surgical instruments where complex geometry meets tight tolerance and full CMM documentation. Energy (impellers, turbine components), defence, motorsport, and high-end robotics round out the primary applications.

**Q6: How do I know if my part needs 5-axis machining?**
Three questions: (1) Does the part have features on more than two faces requiring separate setups? (2) Are there undercuts or compound angles a vertical tool path can't reach? (3) Are there true freeform curved surfaces? If any answer is yes, 5-axis is likely the right process. If none apply, 3-axis will do it. Send the STEP file — we'll assess during DFM review and recommend the right machine allocation before quoting.
