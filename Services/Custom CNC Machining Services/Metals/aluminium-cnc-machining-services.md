## Section 3: Why Aluminium for CNC Machining
**Design:** 2-column — text left, image right. White background.
**Heading:** The Go-To Material for a Reason

Aluminium cuts fast, holds tolerances well, and finishes cleanly. That combination is why it shows up across aerospace brackets, medical housings, automotive components, electronics enclosures, and robotics structural parts. When a project needs tight dimensions, a good surface finish, and parts back in days rather than weeks, aluminium is usually the right call.

The machinability is the starting point. Aluminium clears chips well at high cutting speeds — where stainless steel might need conservative feed rates to avoid work hardening, aluminium can be pushed significantly harder. That speed translates directly to cost on high-volume jobs. It also reduces cutting forces, which matters when you're holding thin walls or long unsupported features.

Weight-to-strength ratio is the other factor engineers cite. A 7075-T6 aluminium bracket can replace a steel one at roughly one-third the weight with comparable strength. For aerospace and robotics applications where every gram has to be justified, that matters. For automotive and consumer electronics, it matters because aluminium looks and feels like a premium material once anodized.

What aluminium doesn't do well: it's not stainless. Bare aluminium oxidises, and that oxidation accelerates in aggressive environments. Most aluminium CNC machining applications include a finish — anodize Type II for general corrosion and colour, hardcoat Type III for wear interfaces, chromate conversion where electrical conductivity needs to be maintained. We plan finishing alongside machining, not as an afterthought.

## Section 5: Grade Selection Guide
**Design:** 3-column card grid. Light grey background.
**Heading:** Which Aluminium Grade Do You Need?

**Card 1 — Strength First**
If the part is structural, load-bearing, or subject to fatigue loading:
- General structural use → 6061-T6 (strong enough for most applications, easy to source, well-documented)
- Maximum strength → 7075-T6 (use where 6061 isn't strong enough — note: not weldable, and harder to machine than 6061)
- Fatigue-critical aerospace → 2024-T3 (high fatigue resistance, but plan for protective finish — bare 2024 corrodes faster than 6061)

**Card 2 — Finish Quality First**
If the part will be anodized and the visual result matters:
- Standard anodizing → 6061-T6 (anodizes well, good colour consistency)
- Premium cosmetic result → 6063-T5 (finer grain structure, anodizes with exceptional clarity and uniformity — noticeably better surface than 6061 for colour-critical parts)
- Avoid 7075 for colour anodizing → the zinc content affects colour consistency and uniformity; clear anodize is fine, coloured results can be inconsistent batch to batch

**Card 3 — Stability and Flatness First**
If the part is a tooling plate, fixture, or base that must remain flat after machining:
- Use MIC-6 (Alca-5) cast tooling plate → stress-relieved at cast, which means heavy stock removal doesn't release internal stress and cause warping. Wrought 6061 plate has residual stress from rolling — machine away enough material and you'll see movement. MIC-6 doesn't do that.
- For general flat parts (not fixtures) → 6061-T651 (stress-relieved by stretching — better than standard 6061 plate, but not as stable as MIC-6)

## Section 7: Surface Finishes for Aluminium
**Design:** Full-width table. Light grey background.
**Heading:** Surface Finishes for Aluminium Parts

| Finish | Dimensional Impact | Best For | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **As Machined** | None | Internal components, tooling, fixturing | Ra 0.8–3.2 µm, visible toolmarks — fine for non-cosmetic applications |
| **Bead Blasted** | Negligible (<0.01 mm) | Cosmetic uniformity, hiding toolmarks | Isotropic matte texture. Good base for anodizing. |
| **Anodize Type II** | +5–25 µm per side | General corrosion protection, colour | Clear, black, blue, red, gold. Electrically insulating. Adjust tight-fit features before coating. |
| **Anodize Type III (Hardcoat)** | +25–50 µm per side | Wear interfaces, aggressive environments | HV 400–600 hardness. Must pre-machine bores/shafts to allow for coating thickness. |
| **Chromate Conversion (Alodine)** | Negligible (<0.001 mm) | Electrical conductivity required | Retains conductivity unlike anodizing. MIL-DTL-5541. Good corrosion protection for aerospace chassis. |
| **Powder Coat** | +50–100 µm per side | Colour, impact resistance | Mask threads and precision bores. Not suitable for close-tolerance fits without pre-machining offsets. |
| **Polishing** | Removes minor surface layer | Mirror or near-mirror finish | Used for optical mounts, cosmetic consumer parts. |

**Anodizing and tolerances — read this before your drawing is finalised.**
Anodizing adds material. Type II adds 5–25 µm per side; Type III hardcoat adds 25–50 µm per side. A bore machined to H7 tolerance and then anodized without adjustment is no longer H7. Options: machine undersized by the coating allowance, mask the feature, or machine after coating. Flag precision-fit features at quote stage and we'll specify the right approach before the job starts.

## Section 8: Design Guidelines for Aluminium
**Design:** 3-column table. White background.
**Heading:** Design Guidelines for Aluminium CNC Machining

| Feature | Recommendation | Why It Matters |
|---|---|---|
| Min wall thickness | 0.8 mm (structural), 1.0 mm (recommended) | Thinner walls deflect under cutting forces and can vibrate, affecting surface finish and dimensional accuracy |
| Internal corner radius | Min 1/3 of cavity depth | Aluminium cuts cleanly but tool geometry still limits how sharp an internal corner can be — undersize radii require slower, smaller tools and add cost |
| Hole depth | Max 10× diameter (standard drill) | Beyond this, drill wander becomes significant; specify through-holes where possible, or flag deep holes for specialist tooling |
| Thread engagement | Min 1.5× diameter | Aluminium is softer than steel — short thread engagement strips under load. For structural threads, 2× diameter is safer |
| Tolerances | Only call out tight tolerance where function requires | Every ±0.005 mm callout adds cost; ISO 2768-m covers most non-critical features and keeps the job moving at normal rates |
| Anodize allowance | Pre-machine or mask precision bores | Type II adds 5–25 µm per side; Type III adds 25–50 µm — both will take an H7 bore out of tolerance without allowance |
| MIC-6 vs 6061 plate | Use MIC-6 for fixtures and tooling | 6061 plate has rolling stress — remove material asymmetrically and the part can spring. MIC-6 is stress-relieved at cast and stays flat |
| Thin rib features | Keep rib height to width ratio <8:1 | Tall thin ribs in aluminium can chatter during cutting; above 8:1 aspect ratio, discuss fixturing strategy at quote stage |

## Section 9: Applications
**Design:** 2-column icon+text grid. Light grey background.
**Heading:** Where CNC Machined Aluminium Parts Are Used

**Aerospace & Defence**
Structural brackets, wing rib components, avionics housings, hydraulic manifolds. 7075-T6 and 2024-T3 for high-load structural applications; 6061-T6 for secondary structures. Material traceability and CMM reporting standard.

**Medical Devices**
Surgical instrument housings, imaging equipment frames, dental device components. 6061-T6 anodized for biocompatibility and cleanability. Electropolishing on stainless inserts in aluminium assemblies. Full material certs with every order.

**Automotive & Motorsport**
Engine components, suspension brackets, gearbox housings, heat exchangers. Aluminium's weight-to-strength ratio matters here. 6061-T6 for structural parts; 7075-T6 for performance applications where weight reduction is critical.

**Electronics & Semiconductor**
Enclosures, heatsinks, PCB frames, wafer handling equipment. Aluminium's thermal conductivity and lightweight are the primary drivers. MIC-6 for precision flat plates in semiconductor tools; 6061 for general enclosures; chromate conversion where conductivity must be maintained.

**Robotics & Automation**
Structural links, end effectors, camera mounts, motor brackets. Weight matters — every gram carried in the arm is load on the motor. 6061-T6 is standard; 7075-T6 for high-load joints. Anodized for durability in industrial environments.

**Industrial Machinery**
Fixtures, tooling plates, machine bases, guard panels. MIC-6 tooling plate for flatness-critical fixtures; 6061-T6 for general structural use. Longer lead times less common here — production runs with repeat ordering.

## Section 10: Pros & Cons
**Design:** 2-column — Pros left, Cons right. White background.
**Heading:** Aluminium: Honest Assessment

**Where aluminium works well:**
- Machines faster than most metals — lower cost per part on volume runs
- Excellent strength-to-weight ratio, particularly 7075-T6
- Anodizes well — functional corrosion protection and wide colour range
- Good thermal conductivity for heat dissipation applications
- Widely available in multiple alloys and stock sizes

**Where to think twice:**
- Not suitable for high-temperature environments — strength drops significantly above 150°C; use stainless or titanium for elevated temperature applications
- Lower hardness than steel — wear interfaces need hardcoat anodize or will wear faster than expected
- 7075 is not weldable — if your design needs post-machine welding, specify 6061
- Bare aluminium corrodes in marine or chemically aggressive environments — plan for finishing from the start, not as an afterthought
- Galvanic corrosion risk when in contact with steel or copper fasteners — use insulating sleeves or specify compatible materials

## Section 14: FAQ
**Design:** Accordion. White background. First item open.
**Heading:** Aluminium CNC Machining — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: Which aluminium grade should I use for CNC machined parts?**
Depends on what the part actually does. For general structural and engineering applications, 6061-T6 is the default — good machinability, solid strength, anodizes well, and widely available. If you need maximum strength and weight isn't the only driver, 7075-T6 is 2× stronger than 6061 but not weldable and more expensive to source. For cosmetic parts that will be colour-anodized, 6063-T5 anodizes with a noticeably finer, more uniform result than 6061. For fixtures and tooling plates where flatness after heavy stock removal is critical, MIC-6 is the right answer — wrought 6061 plate has residual rolling stress that can cause movement when you remove material asymmetrically. Tell us the application and we'll advise.

**Q2: Does anodizing affect the dimensions of aluminium CNC parts?**
Yes, and this is one of the most common mistakes on drawings. Type II anodizing adds 5–25 µm per side; Type III hardcoat adds 25–50 µm per side. On a bore with an H7 fit tolerance, that's enough to take the part out of specification without adjustment. The fix: machine the bore undersized by the coating allowance, mask it during anodizing, or machine after coating. Flag any precision-fit features on your drawing at quote stage and we'll specify the approach — not after the parts come back from finishing and don't fit.

**Q3: Can you machine 7075 aluminium to tight tolerances?**
Yes. 7075-T6 is harder and less free-cutting than 6061, but it's still very machinable compared to stainless or titanium. The key differences: it requires sharper tooling, more conservative chip load management to avoid tool deflection on tight features, and different fixturing on thin-wall geometry. Our tolerance capability on 7075 is the same as 6061 — ±0.005 mm on critical features, CMM verified. The cost is slightly higher than 6061 due to material cost and machining time, which we'll reflect in the quote.

**Q4: What's the best surface finish for aluminium CNC machined parts?**
Depends on the function. For corrosion protection with colour options, anodize Type II is the standard choice. For hard-wearing surfaces that take impact or sliding contact, hardcoat anodize Type III (HV 400–600). For parts that need to stay electrically conductive — enclosures that need EMI bonding continuity, for example — chromate conversion (Alodine) is right, because anodizing is electrically insulating. For the cleanest cosmetic result on colour anodizing, bead blast before anodizing to eliminate toolmarks and get a uniform surface. For purely cosmetic uniformity with no dimensional concern, bead blasting alone is often sufficient.

**Q5: How do you handle aluminium parts that require both tight tolerances and anodizing?**
Planning. Any aluminium CNC machining job involving anodizing and tight-tolerance features gets a finishing review at quote stage. We identify every feature where coating thickness will affect fit — bores, shafts, sealing faces, threaded interfaces — and agree the approach before machining starts. Options: pre-machine with offset (machine undersized, coat, end up at nominal), mask (keep the feature bare), or machine after coating (practical for some geometry, not all). This is planned on the drawing, not discovered at inspection.

**Q6: What aluminium grades do you stock for CNC machining?**
6061-T6 in plate and bar is in-house stock — fastest lead times. 7075-T6 plate and bar is standard stock. 6063-T5 in bar is stocked. MIC-6 tooling plate is stocked in common thicknesses. 2024-T3 and 5052-H32 are procured to order — add 1–2 days for material sourcing. If your project requires a specific temper or form not listed, confirm at quote stage and we'll advise lead time.
