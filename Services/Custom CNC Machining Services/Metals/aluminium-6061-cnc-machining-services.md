## Section 3: What is Aluminium 6061 CNC Machining?

**H2:** What is Aluminium 6061 CNC Machining?

Weldo CNC machines aluminium 6061 — primarily in T6 temper — to tolerances of ±0.005 mm across milling, turning, and 5-axis operations, from its ISO 9001:2015 certified facility in Dongguan, China. Prototype parts ship in 1–5 business days. Every tight-tolerance order includes a full CMM — coordinate measuring machine — dimensional inspection report, delivered with the parts.

Aluminium 6061-T6 is the default CNC aluminium grade because it balances machinability, strength, and post-process compatibility better than any other aluminium alloy. Tensile strength of 310 MPa and yield strength of 276 MPa cover the structural requirements of most housing, bracket, shaft, and enclosure designs. The magnesium-silicon chemistry machines cleanly — chips break predictably, built-up edge on the end mill is rare, and cutting speeds can be set aggressively without thermal damage.

6061-T6 anodizes more evenly than 7075 and accepts a wider range of anodizing colours and oxide thicknesses. Thread engagement is predictable and reliable. Weldability is acceptable with proper filler selection — 4043 or 5356 filler rods. For 80% of aluminium CNC machining requirements — enclosures, housings, brackets, shafts, fittings — 6061-T6 is the correct grade.

Weldo CNC has machined aluminium 6061 components since 2012. Typical part types include heatsinks, robotic arm links, optical mounts, pneumatic manifolds, motor housings, structural brackets, camera bodies, and precision fixtures.

## Section 5: Material Trade-off Cards

**H2:** When 6061-T6 is Right — and When It Isn't

**Subheading:** 6061-T6 covers most aluminium machining requirements. The cases where it doesn't are specific and worth knowing.

**Card 1 — When 6061-T6 is the Correct Choice**

6061-T6 delivers 310 MPa tensile, clean machinability, predictable anodizing, and weldability — in one grade. For housings, enclosures, structural brackets, heat sink bodies, pneumatic manifolds, and optical mounts, 6061-T6 satisfies the engineering requirement at the best price-performance ratio of any aluminium alloy. If the service load doesn't require 7075-T6's strength, and the application doesn't require 2024's fatigue resistance, 6061-T6 is the answer.

*Key spec: Tensile 310 MPa · Density 2.70 g/cm³ · Machinability: Excellent · Anodizing: Excellent*

**Card 2 — When 7075-T6 Changes the Equation**

7075-T6's 572 MPa tensile — nearly double 6061 — matters when the part carries a safety-critical structural load: UAV frame members, aerospace fastener-critical brackets, high-load clevis fittings. The machining cost is similar but slightly higher. Anodizing is less predictable — 7075 produces a thinner, less uniform oxide. If your design requires 6061-grade corrosion resistance and weldability, 7075 is not a drop-in upgrade — it is a different engineering choice.

*Key spec: Tensile 572 MPa · Use when structural load exceeds 6061's safety margin*

**Card 3 — When 6061 Itself Becomes the Constraint**

Thermal applications that need higher conductivity (pure aluminium 1050 or 1100 series) and parts operating above 150°C (where T6 strength degrades with exposure time) are the two cases where 6061-T6 is the wrong material. Cast tooling plate — MIC-6 — is the right answer when flatness and stress relief matter more than tensile strength, such as fixture bodies and optical mounting bases.

*Key spec: 6061 upper thermal limit — strength begins degrading above 150°C exposure*

## Section 10: Aluminium 6061 CNC Machining Design Guidelines

**H2:** Aluminium 6061 Design Guidelines

**Subheading:** 6061-T6 machines cleanly — but several design patterns still produce avoidable failures.

| Feature | Recommendation | Why It Matters |
|---|---|---|
| Minimum wall thickness (milling) | 0.8 mm for walls under 30 mm height | Thinner walls deflect during end-milling — tolerance opens at the wall tip |
| Minimum wall thickness (turning) | 0.8 mm for turned thin-wall tubes | Chuck pressure collapses walls below 0.8 mm on small OD parts |
| Corner radii in milled pockets | Minimum R equal to tool radius — R2.0 mm for standard pocket milling | Square internal corners are impossible — all CNC milled pockets have a corner radius equal to the tool radius |
| Anodize dimensional planning | Add 5–15 µm per surface for Type II. 10–50 µm per surface for Type III hard anodize | Anodizing grows the dimension — precision bores, shaft diameters, and threads must account for anodize thickness |
| Thread engagement depth | Minimum 1.5× diameter for M6 and below. 2.5× for M8+ | Aluminium threads strip at lower torque than steel — sufficient engagement depth is critical for threaded connections |
| Deep pocket depth-to-width ratio | Maximum 4:1 depth to width in 6061-T6 | Beyond 4:1, the end mill deflects at the pocket floor — dimensional error increases with depth |
| Thermal stabilisation before measurement | Allow 15–20 minutes after machining before measuring critical features | 6061-T6's thermal expansion (23.6 × 10⁻⁶/°C) means a part direct off the machine reads differently than a room-temperature part |
| Flatness on large face-milled parts | Specify flatness callout — do not assume | Face-milled aluminium plates can have 0.05–0.1 mm bow if residual stress releases during machining. T651 plate reduces this |

**Most common DFM failures on aluminium 6061 parts:**

Anodize-related fit failures are the most frequent. Engineers design a bore to H7 tolerance, anodize the part, and the anodize thickness closes the bore below the H7 minimum. The fix is to machine the bore 10–30 µm oversize per surface (depending on anodize spec), then anodize — the final bore is H7 after anodizing. Weldo CNC flags this conflict at DFM review and confirms the pre-anodize bore diameter before machining.

Insufficient thread engagement for M4 and smaller threads in 6061-T6 is the second pattern. Engineers apply the same thread depth as they would for steel and discover the threads strip at lower torque than expected. For M4 through M6 in 6061-T6, 2.0× diameter minimum engagement is the correct target — not the steel-designed 1.5× that many drawings specify.

## Section 15: FAQ

**H2:** Aluminium 6061 CNC Machining — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: What tolerance can Weldo CNC hold on aluminium 6061 parts?**

Aluminium 6061 machines to ±0.005 mm on precision features — bore diameters, OD fits, positional callouts — verified by CMM. General dimensions follow ISO 2768-m at ±0.05 mm. Surface finish on milled 6061-T6 is Ra 1.6 µm as standard on finish passes; Ra 0.8 µm with an additional finishing pass. 5-axis machining holds ±0.003 mm on complex geometry. Specify the tolerance and which features are critical on your drawing.

**Q2: Why is 6061-T6 the most common CNC aluminium grade?**

6061-T6 balances three properties that no other aluminium grade matches simultaneously: excellent machinability (fast cutting, clean chips, no built-up edge), predictable anodizing (even oxide layer, wide colour range), and adequate structural strength (310 MPa tensile, 276 MPa yield) for most housing, bracket, and shaft applications. 7075-T6 is stronger but harder to anodize; 2024-T3 is better for fatigue but doesn't weld and has lower corrosion resistance. 6061-T6 is the general-purpose correct answer.

**Q3: What is the difference between 6061-T6 and 6061-T651?**

T651 is T6 that has been stress-relieved by controlled stretching after solution heat treatment. Residual stress from the rolling and heat treatment process can cause large aluminium plates to warp when material is removed — the stress releases and the part moves. T651 reduces this residual stress, making it the preferred specification for precision blocks, tooling plates, and large milled structures where flatness matters. For smaller parts and thin profiles, T6 is equivalent.

**Q7: What drives the cost of aluminium 6061 CNC machining?**

Four factors set the price: part complexity (pockets, undercuts, tight tolerances add programme time); material (6061-T6 bar and plate are cost-effective — one of the most economical engineering metals); surface finish (anodizing, hard coat, and powder coating add separate process steps); and inspection (±0.005 mm features require CMM verification time). 6061 is the most cost-effective aluminium to machine — its high cutting speed reduces cycle time compared to any other metal.

ISO 9001:2015 certification with a 97.48% annual pass rate covers the production system. Customers in 100+ countries receive the same inspection documentation on orders from 1 piece to 10,000.

**Q9: Can aluminium 6061 be anodized after CNC machining?**

Yes. 6061-T6 anodizes better than any other engineering aluminium alloy — even, consistent oxide layer, full colour range, both Type II (5–25 µm) and Type III hard anodize (20–100 µm) available. Anodizing adds thickness: precision bores and shaft fits must be machined oversize pre-anodize to hit the drawing dimension after anodizing. Threads, precision bores, and sealing faces are masked where anodize growth would affect fit. All of this is coordinated at DFM review — not after the part is machined.
