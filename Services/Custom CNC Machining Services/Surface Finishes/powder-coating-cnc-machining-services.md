## Section 3: What is Powder Coating?

**H2:** What is Powder Coating?

Weldo CNC applies powder coating to CNC machined steel, aluminium, and alloy steel parts — polyester, epoxy, and polyurethane powder formulations — from its ISO 9001:2015 certified facility in Dongguan, China. Powder coating applies 60–100 µm of uniform colour and corrosion protection across external surfaces. Parts with no precision fit requirements on coated faces receive full uniform coverage. Where precision bores, threads, or sealing faces must remain uncoated, masking is applied before the powder gun and confirmed on the drawing at quote stage.

Powder coating is a dry finishing process. Dry thermosetting powder — a mixture of resin, hardener, pigment, and flow additives — is electrostatically charged and sprayed onto the grounded metal part. The electrostatic attraction wraps the powder around edges and into recesses. The coated part then enters an oven — typically at 180–200°C for polyester and epoxy formulations — where the powder melts, flows, and cross-links into a continuous film. The result is a harder, more chemically resistant coating than liquid paint of the same thickness, with no solvent emissions and near-zero VOC.

What separates a quality powder coat from a poor one is substrate preparation. Powder that adheres to a contaminated or oxidised surface delaminates under mechanical load or UV exposure within months. Proper pre-treatment — chemical degreasing, phosphate conversion coating or chromate pre-treatment for aluminium, shot blasting for steel — provides the adhesion profile that keeps the powder bonded. A pre-treatment failure is invisible until the finish fails in service.

Powder coating is the standard decorative and corrosion-protective finish for steel and alloy steel CNC machined structural components, industrial machine frames, enclosures, and assemblies where the colour must be durable and the cost must be controlled. Weldo CNC has applied powder coating to machined parts since 2012.

## Section 4: Types of Powder Coating

**H2:** Types of Powder Coating

**Subheading:** Powder formulation is determined by the operating environment — outdoor UV exposure, chemical resistance, and flexibility requirements drive the choice.

**Card 1 — Polyester Powder Coating**

Polyester is the most common powder coating formulation for outdoor and general-purpose industrial applications. It provides excellent UV resistance — colour holds without significant chalking or fading over years of outdoor exposure. Impact resistance and flexibility are good. Polyester is the standard for architectural aluminium, industrial enclosures, outdoor equipment frames, and automotive accessory components. It is not the best choice for environments with aggressive chemical exposure — epoxy outperforms polyester in chemical resistance.

*Spec: Thickness 60–80 µm · UV resistance: Excellent · Chemical resistance: Good · Cure: 180–200°C*

**Card 2 — Epoxy Powder Coating**

Epoxy powder provides superior chemical resistance and adhesion compared to polyester — it bonds more tenaciously to metal substrates and resists exposure to oils, hydraulic fluids, and mild acids better than polyester. The trade-off is UV degradation — epoxy chalks and fades outdoors faster than polyester. Epoxy is specified for internal machine components, electrical panels, industrial equipment in chemical environments, and parts where maximum adhesion to the substrate is required, such as primer coats under topcoats.

*Spec: Thickness 60–100 µm · UV resistance: Moderate · Chemical resistance: Excellent · Cure: 160–180°C*

**Card 3 — Choosing the Right Powder Specification**

Specify powder type, colour (RAL or NCS reference), finish texture (gloss, semi-gloss, matte, texture), and film thickness on the drawing or in the order notes. For outdoor applications, specify polyester. For chemical-resistance applications, specify epoxy. For parts requiring both UV and chemical resistance, specify polyurethane (a specialty formulation combining both properties). Note masking requirements for all threads, precision bores, and sealing faces that must remain uncoated. Weldo CNC confirms the specification and masking plan at quote stage.

*Spec: Colour: RAL or NCS reference · Texture: gloss / semi-gloss / matte / fine texture · Masking: confirm all masked features on drawing*

## Section 5: Powder Coating or Anodizing — Which Finish Fits Your Part?

**H2:** Powder Coating or Anodizing — Which Finish Fits Your Part?

**Specify Powder Coating When:**
- Base material is steel or alloy steel — anodizing is aluminium only; powder coating covers all metals
- Colour uniformity across a thick, chip-resistant coating is the priority — powder coat at 60–100 µm is more impact-resistant than anodize at 5–25 µm
- Part is a structural panel, frame, or enclosure with no precision fits on exterior surfaces
- Cost is a primary driver for corrosion protection on non-critical machined components
- Part operates in UV-exposed outdoor environments — polyester powder coat holds colour better than most wet paints

Example parts where powder coating wins: steel machine frames, equipment enclosures, industrial brackets and panels, structural weldments, outdoor equipment components

**When Anodizing May Win:**
- Base material is aluminium and the integral oxide is preferred over a surface coating — anodize cannot chip off at edges
- Precision features (bores, shafts) are on the coated surface — anodizing adds precisely calculable thickness; powder coat adds 60–100 µm imprecisely, ruling it out on precision features
- Thin coating is required — anodize at 5–15 µm is practical on complex geometry; 60–100 µm powder coat is not
- Hard surface wear resistance is required — Type III hard anodize at 60–70 HRC equivalent outperforms any powder coat in abrasion resistance

Closing note: for aluminium parts with both cosmetic colour and precision bore requirements, anodizing with proper pre-machining allowance is almost always the correct process. For steel structural components requiring corrosion protection and colour, powder coating is the standard answer. Send the STEP file with finish specification — the right process will be confirmed before quoting.

## Section 6: Compatible Materials for Powder Coating

**H2:** Compatible Materials for Powder Coating

Powder coating bonds through electrostatic adhesion — the part must be electrically conductive. All metals are compatible. Some surface conditions and alloys require specific pre-treatment.

| Material | Compatible | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Carbon Steel (1018, 1045) | ✅ Yes | Shot blast to Sa 2.5 + phosphate conversion coat for best adhesion |
| Alloy Steel (4140, 4340) | ✅ Yes | Same pre-treatment as carbon steel. Confirm cure temperature vs temper specification |
| Stainless Steel | ✅ Yes | Shot blast + aluminium phosphate pre-treatment. Adhesion lower than on carbon steel — confirm application |
| Aluminium 6061 | ✅ Yes | Chromate or phosphate pre-treatment. Powder coat is a viable alternative to anodizing on non-precision surfaces |
| Aluminium 7075 | ✅ Yes | Chromate pre-treatment. Adhesion is good but confirm for applications with mechanical load on the coated surface |
| Cast Aluminium (ADC12) | ✅ Yes | Outgassing from casting porosity can cause pinhole defects in powder coat — degassing pre-bake recommended |
| Brass / Copper | ⚠️ Conditional | Adhesion is lower on non-ferrous copper alloys without specialist pre-treatment. Confirm for adhesion-critical applications |
| Titanium | ⚠️ Conditional | Powder coat can be applied but is rarely specified on titanium — anodizing or as-machined is more common |
| PEEK / Engineering Plastics | ❌ No | Cure temperature (180–200°C) exceeds the service limit of most engineering plastics — not applicable |

## Section 8: Design Guidelines for Powder Coating

**H2:** Design Guidelines for Powder Coating

**Subheading:** Powder coating is applied after machining — design decisions made before cutting determine whether the coated part meets its dimensional and functional requirements.

| Feature | Recommendation | Why It Matters |
|---|---|---|
| Precision bore pre-allowance | Machine all bores 2× coating thickness oversize if coating interior — for 80 µm coating: bore 0.16 mm oversize | 60–100 µm powder coat inside a bore closes it by the full thickness on each surface — a H7 bore machined to nominal will be below minimum after coating |
| Thread masking | Mask all threads — always | Powder coat inside threads causes assembly failure. Coating breaks on threading, producing fragments that contaminate the assembly. No exceptions |
| Sealing face masking | Mask sealing surfaces — O-ring grooves, mating faces, valve seats | Coating on sealing faces changes seal geometry and prevents metal-to-metal contact at precision interfaces |
| Sharp edges | Break all sharp edges to minimum R0.5 mm before coating | Film at sharp edges is thin and brittle — chips and delaminates under impact loading. A small radius allows the powder to flow and build uniform thickness |
| Hanging hole provision | Include a 3–5 mm hanging hole or specify location for racking wire | Parts are hung during coating and curing — without a hanging point, the rack wire leaves a bare spot on the part surface |
| Pre-treatment surface condition | Shot blast carbon and alloy steel to Sa 2.5 before powder coating | Adhesion to mill scale or oxidised surface is poor — the coating delaminates under load or UV exposure within months |
| Cure temperature compatibility | Confirm part temper spec vs cure temperature (180–200°C) for high-strength alloy steel | 4340 or spring steel parts heat treated to specific hardness may be affected by 180–200°C cure. Confirm at DFM review |
| Electrical contact areas | Mask all electrical contact surfaces, grounding points, and contact pads | Powder coat is an electrical insulator. Grounding contacts and contact areas must remain bare metal |

**Most common powder coating failures caused by design decisions:**

Threads coated without masking is the most frequent powder coating failure. The powder flows into threads during application. During curing, it becomes a hard, brittle coating inside the thread geometry. Tapping breaks the coating into flakes — the thread is functionally blocked and contaminated. The fix is a masking note on the drawing: "Mask all threaded features before powder coating." This is confirmed at quote stage, not improvised at the coating line.

Unplanned dimensional impact on precision fits is the second pattern. An engineer specifies powder coating on an aluminium enclosure with close-fit bores for pressed-in inserts. The 80 µm coating closes each bore by 0.08 mm on each surface — 0.16 mm total — converting an interference fit into a free-running fit. The fix is to machine all bore diameters to the drawing dimension plus 2× intended coating thickness before coating, so the final post-coat dimension is the drawing dimension. This calculation is done at DFM review — before the first cut.

## Section 11: Quality & Finish Inspection

**H2:** Quality Control & Finish Inspection

**Step 1 — Pre-Finish: Part Condition Verified Before Coating**

Machined part inspected before powder coating — dimensional check on features adjacent to coated surfaces, masking confirmed against the drawing callout, and surface condition checked. Shot blasting and pre-treatment verified complete before the powder gun. Any contamination, bare spots from incomplete pre-treatment, or unmasked features flagged before the part enters the coating booth.

**Step 2 — In-Process: Powder Application and Cure Monitored**

Electrostatic gun settings (voltage, flow rate) confirmed at setup. Oven temperature and cure time monitored throughout the cure cycle — temperature recorder confirms the part reaches the required cure temperature and holds it for the full cure duration. Non-Conformance Report (NCR) triggered on any oven temperature deviation before the batch is considered cured.

**Step 3 — Post-Finish Inspection: Film Thickness and Adhesion Verified**

Film thickness measured on representative surfaces using a magnetic film gauge — confirmed within specified range (e.g. 60–80 µm for polyester). Cross-hatch adhesion test on a representative test coupon from each batch. Visual inspection for coverage uniformity, edge coverage, colour consistency, and pinhole defects. Dimensional check on critical bores post-coating — confirmed against drawing dimension. Results documented and shipped with the part.

## Section 14: FAQ

**H2:** Powder Coating — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: How much does powder coating change the dimensions of a machined part?**

Standard powder coating applies 60–100 µm per coated surface. A bore coated on its interior narrows by 60–100 µm on each surface — 120–200 µm total. For precision bores, this means machining the bore 120–200 µm oversize before coating, so the final post-coat dimension is the drawing dimension. For external ODs, the part grows by the same amount. Weldo CNC calculates pre-coat machining targets at DFM review — the machining target is different from the drawing final dimension.

**Q2: What is powder coating and how does it work?**

Powder coating is a dry finishing process where a thermosetting polymer powder is electrostatically charged and sprayed onto a grounded metal part. The electrostatic attraction causes the powder to wrap around edges and into recesses. The coated part then cures in an oven at 160–200°C, where the powder melts, flows, and cross-links into a continuous film. The result is harder than liquid paint — typically H pencil hardness — with no solvent emissions and better impact resistance at equivalent thickness.

**Q3: What base materials can be powder coated?**

All electrically conductive metals can be powder coated — carbon steel, alloy steel, stainless steel, aluminium, and most non-ferrous metals with appropriate pre-treatment. Engineering plastics cannot be powder coated — the cure temperature (160–200°C) exceeds the service limit of most engineering polymers. For aluminium, powder coating is an alternative to anodizing on non-precision external surfaces. For steel, powder coating is the standard decorative and corrosion-protective finish.

**Q4: How long does powder coating add to the total lead time?**

Powder coating adds 1–3 working days to the machining lead time. Shot blasting, pre-treatment, coating, cure, and inspection run in sequence — typically one to two days per batch. Total order lead time for machined and powder-coated steel parts is typically 5–10 business days for prototypes, 10–20 days for small-batch production. **Q5: What is the minimum order quantity for powder coating?**

No minimum order quantity. Single-piece prototype orders accepted with the same masking and inspection process as production runs.

**Q6: How do I specify powder coating on my drawing?**

Specify the powder type (polyester or epoxy), colour (RAL or NCS reference), finish texture (gloss, semi-gloss, matte, fine texture), film thickness target, and masking requirements. Example drawing note: "Powder coat: polyester, RAL 9005 gloss black, 60–80 µm. Mask M8 threaded holes and Ø32H7 bore." Upload the STEP file and annotated PDF drawing — Weldo CNC confirms the masking plan and pre-coat dimensions at quote review.

**Q7: What drives the cost of powder coating?**

Part size (larger surface area = more powder and longer cure time), masking complexity (multiple masked features increase prep time), primer requirements (primer under topcoat doubles the coating passes), and colour (standard black and grey cost less than custom or special-effect colours). Powder coating is a batch process — per-part cost reduces significantly at production quantities.

**Q8: How does powder coating compare to wet paint?**

Powder coating is harder, more impact-resistant, and more chemically resistant than conventional liquid paint of equivalent thickness. Powder coat at 80 µm outperforms most liquid paint systems at 50–80 µm on all mechanical properties. It is a one-step process (no primer needed for most applications), zero-VOC during application, and cure is faster than the drying time of many liquid paints. The trade-off is that powder coating requires baking at 160–200°C — not suitable for heat-sensitive parts or materials.

**Q9: Will powder coating withstand outdoor UV exposure?**

Polyester powder coating provides excellent UV resistance — colour and gloss retention outdoors is significantly better than epoxy or many liquid paints. Five-plus years of outdoor colour retention is achievable with polyester on properly pre-treated steel or aluminium. Epoxy degrades under UV exposure — chalking and colour fading occur faster than polyester outdoors. For outdoor applications, specify polyester. For indoor chemical-resistance applications, specify epoxy.

**Q10: Can powder coating be applied over anodized aluminium?**

Powder coating over anodized aluminium is technically possible but not commonly specified. The anodized oxide provides a corrosion barrier; adding powder coat over it provides colour and impact resistance that anodizing alone may not. The anodized surface must be chemically etched before powder coating to provide a mechanical adhesion profile — otherwise the powder coat adhesion to the smooth oxide is poor. Weldo CNC reviews this combination at quote stage and confirms the pre-treatment approach.
