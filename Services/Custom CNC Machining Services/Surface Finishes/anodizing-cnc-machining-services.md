## Section 3: What is Anodizing?

**H2:** What is Anodizing?

Weldo CNC anodizes aluminium CNC machined parts — 6061, 7075, 2024, 5052, and 6082 — using Type II standard anodizing and Type III hard anodizing, from its ISO 9001:2015 certified facility in Dongguan, China. Type II produces an oxide layer 5–25 µm thick. Type III hard anodize produces 20–100 µm of dense aluminium oxide. Both processes add measurable thickness to the part — dimensional impact is calculated and machined-in before the part enters the anodizing bath.

Anodizing is an electrochemical process, not a coating. The aluminium surface is converted to aluminium oxide (Al₂O₃) — the same material as industrial sapphire — by passing an electric current through the part while it is submerged in an acid electrolyte bath, typically sulphuric acid. The oxide layer grows inward and outward from the original aluminium surface in roughly equal parts: approximately half the thickness penetrates the substrate, half builds above it. This is why anodizing affects dimensions less than a plated coating of the same nominal thickness.

The quality of an anodized finish depends on surface preparation before the bath. Aluminium machined to Ra 3.2 µm anodizes with visible machining marks — the oxide conforms to the surface. A pre-anodize finish of Ra 1.6 µm produces a cleaner result; Ra 0.8 µm is specified for decorative or functional sealing surfaces. Masking precision is the second quality factor — an unmasked precision bore gains 5–15 µm of oxide on each surface, closing the bore below the fit tolerance if not planned at machining.

Weldo CNC has applied anodizing to CNC machined aluminium parts since 2012. Typical applications include aerospace structural housings, camera bodies, electronic enclosures, medical device housings, and industrial components where corrosion resistance, appearance, and abrasion resistance are all required.

## Section 4: Types of Anodizing

**H2:** Types of Anodizing

**Subheading:** Variant selection is determined by the functional requirement — hardness, appearance, and operating environment drive the choice.

**Card 1 — Type II Standard Anodizing**

Type II anodizing produces an aluminium oxide layer 5–25 µm thick, with a surface hardness of approximately 30–35 HRC equivalent. It provides good corrosion resistance (500+ hours salt spray in dyed finishes with proper sealing) and accepts a wide range of dye colours. Type II is the standard specification for consumer electronics, camera bodies, enclosures, and decorative aluminium components. It is not suitable for applications requiring maximum abrasion resistance — for wear surfaces, Type III is correct.

*Spec: Thickness 5–25 µm · Hardness ~30–35 HRC equivalent · Colour: wide range · Standard reference: MIL-A-8625 Type II*

**Card 2 — Type III Hard Anodizing**

Type III hard anodizing produces 20–100 µm of dense aluminium oxide — significantly harder than Type II, at 60–70 HRC equivalent. Abrasion resistance is dramatically higher — hard anodized surfaces are used on sliding wear surfaces, hydraulic bore inserts, and aerospace components subject to abrasion loading. The finish is naturally dark grey to black. Dye colours are possible but colour uniformity is lower than Type II. Type III is thicker — dimensional impact must be planned more carefully, with precision bores machined up to 0.1 mm oversize per surface depending on oxide thickness specified.

*Spec: Thickness 20–100 µm · Hardness ~60–70 HRC equivalent · Colour: dark grey/black standard · Reference: MIL-A-8625 Type III*

**Card 3 — Colour Anodizing and Sealing**

Anodizing without sealing produces a porous oxide layer that accepts dye. Dye is absorbed into the pores, then sealed by immersion in hot deionised water or nickel acetate solution — the pores close, trapping the colour and providing maximum corrosion resistance. Common colours include black, blue, red, gold, green, and clear (natural aluminium appearance). Colour matching between batches and between 6061 and 7075 alloys is not always exact — 7075 anodizes to a slightly different base tone than 6061. Confirm colour specification and batch consistency requirements at order.

*Spec: Colour range — black, blue, red, gold, green, clear · Batch colour consistency: within one part family, same alloy grade · 7075 vs 6061 colour match: not guaranteed*

## Section 5: Anodizing or Alternative — Which is Right for Your Part?

**H2:** Anodizing or Powder Coating — Which Finish Fits Your Part?

**Specify Anodizing When:**
- Base material is aluminium and the anodized oxide must be part of the aluminium substrate (not a separate coating that can chip off)
- Dimensional tolerance of the machined features is tight — anodizing adds a precisely calculable thickness, powder coating does not
- Abrasion or wear resistance on the surface is required (Type III for wear-critical surfaces)
- Thin, consistent coating on complex geometry with internal features — anodizing covers internal bores and pockets uniformly
- Colour stability under UV exposure — anodized finishes are more UV-stable than most organic powder coatings in aerospace and outdoor applications

Example parts where anodizing wins: camera housings, aerospace brackets, drone frames, hydraulic bore inserts, medical device housings, precision instrument bodies

**When Powder Coating May Win:**
- Base material is steel, stainless steel, or a non-aluminium metal — anodizing is aluminium only
- Thick decorative colour layer is required — powder coating applies 60–100 µm uniformly across flat external surfaces
- Part has no precision features sensitive to coating build-up — flat structural panels, welded frames, brackets without tight-tolerance bores
- Cost is the primary driver for non-critical cosmetic finish — powder coating over mild steel is less expensive than anodizing over machined aluminium

Closing note: if the part is aluminium with both decorative colour and precision bore requirements, anodizing with proper pre-machining allowance is the correct process. Send the STEP file with anodize spec — dimensional impact is confirmed before machining.

## Section 6: Compatible Materials for Anodizing

**H2:** Compatible Materials for Anodizing

Not all metals are compatible with anodizing — the process is specific to aluminium and certain aluminium alloys.

| Material | Compatible | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Aluminium 6061 (wrought) | ✅ Yes | Best anodizing result — even oxide, full colour range, Type II and Type III |
| Aluminium 7075 (wrought) | ✅ Yes | Anodizes well for Type II. Thinner oxide than 6061; colour less uniform. Confirm for decorative applications |
| Aluminium 2024 (wrought) | ⚠️ Conditional | Copper content reduces oxide uniformity. Alodine is often preferred for 2024. Confirm spec before ordering |
| Aluminium 5052 (wrought) | ✅ Yes | Good anodizing result — suitable for Type II standard and colour |
| Aluminium 6082 (wrought) | ✅ Yes | Similar to 6061 — good oxide uniformity, full Type II and Type III range |
| Aluminium cast (ADC12/A380) | ⚠️ Conditional | High silicon content in casting alloys interferes with oxide formation — streaky, non-uniform result. Not recommended for decorative anodizing |
| Stainless Steel | ❌ No | Anodizing is not applicable to stainless. Use passivation or electropolishing |
| Carbon / Alloy Steel | ❌ No | Anodizing is not applicable to steel. Use powder coating, black oxide, or nickel plating |
| Titanium | ⚠️ Conditional | Titanium anodizing (Type II) is possible but a specialist process — produces interference colour, not aluminium oxide. Confirm spec separately |
| Copper / Brass | ❌ No | Anodizing is not applicable to copper or brass |
| PEEK / Engineering Plastics | ❌ No | Anodizing is a metal-only process |

## Section 8: Design Guidelines for Anodizing

**H2:** Design Guidelines for Anodizing

**Subheading:** Anodizing is applied after machining — design and tolerance decisions made before cutting directly affect whether the anodized part meets its dimensional requirements.

| Feature | Recommendation | Why It Matters |
|---|---|---|
| Precision bore pre-allowance | Machine bore 2× (dimensional growth per surface) oversize before anodizing — e.g. for Type II at 12.5 µm growth: machine bore 0.025 mm oversize | Anodizing closes bores by the growth amount on each surface. A bore machined to nominal dimension anodizes undersized |
| Precision shaft OD pre-allowance | Machine OD 2× (dimensional growth per surface) undersize before anodizing | ODs grow by the same amount. An OD machined to nominal dimension anodizes oversize |
| Thread masking | Mask all threads before anodizing — internal and external | Anodize inside threads causes assembly failure — the oxide is brittle and breaks on threading, producing flakes that contaminate the assembly |
| Sealing face masking | Mask sealing surfaces where oxide growth would affect seal performance | O-ring grooves and sealing faces gain oxide — the groove depth changes, affecting seal compression |
| Alloy selection for colour consistency | Specify the same alloy grade across all parts in a colour-matched assembly | 6061 and 7075 anodize to different base tones — colour matching between grades in a single assembly is unreliable |
| Pre-anodize surface finish | Specify Ra 1.6 µm or better on surfaces to be decoratively anodized | Type II anodize reveals machining marks — Ra 3.2 µm as-machined reads as visible texture under the oxide layer |
| Sharp edges | Break all sharp edges to minimum R0.2 mm before anodizing | Oxide thickness at a sharp edge is thin and fragile — edges without a radius show premature oxide wear and chipping in service |
| Material: 2024 aluminium | Substitute 6061 or 5052 for 2024 where anodize quality is required | 2024's copper content produces streaky, non-uniform oxide — not suitable for decorative or functional-quality anodize without specialist chemistry |

**Most common anodizing failures caused by design decisions:**

Undersized bores after anodizing are the single most common issue. Engineers design a bore to H7 tolerance, machine to nominal, anodize without planning the dimensional growth, and discover the bore is below the minimum at incoming inspection. The fix is simple — machine the bore oversize by the confirmed oxide growth per surface — but it must be calculated before the first cut, not discovered after anodizing. Weldo CNC calculates the pre-anodize bore dimension at DFM — Design for Manufacturability — review and confirms the machining target before cutting starts.

Unmasked threads are the second pattern. An order arrives with no masking callout on threads. The anodizer coats all surfaces uniformly. Assembly taps break the brittle oxide in the thread roots; fragments contaminate the assembly. The fix is a masking note on the drawing: "Mask M6 threaded holes and all thread ODs before anodizing." Masking is confirmed on the drawing at quote stage — not improvised at the finish line.

## Section 11: Quality & Finish Inspection

**H2:** Quality Control & Finish Inspection

**Step 1 — Pre-Finish: Part Condition Verified Before Anodizing**

Machined part inspected before entering the anodizing bath — bore diameters and ODs confirmed against the pre-anodize machining target, surface finish (Ra) verified on decorative surfaces, and masking confirmed against the drawing callout. Any contamination, tool marks below specified Ra, or unmasked features flagged before the part enters the bath.

**Step 2 — In-Process: Bath Chemistry and Current Monitored**

Electrolyte bath chemistry — sulphuric acid concentration, temperature, and current density — monitored at defined intervals throughout the anodizing run. Non-Conformance Report (NCR) triggered on any process deviation before the batch continues.

**Step 3 — Post-Finish Inspection: Thickness and Dimensions Verified**

Oxide thickness measured on representative surfaces — confirmed within specified range. Visual inspection for colour uniformity, coverage, and adhesion. Bore diameters and OD fits re-measured post-anodize — confirmed against the final drawing dimension, not the pre-anodize machining target. Results documented and shipped with the part.

## Section 14: FAQ

**H2:** Anodizing — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: How much does anodizing change the dimensions of my machined part?**

Type II standard anodizing grows the part by 2.5–12.5 µm per surface — half the total oxide thickness is above the original surface, half penetrates the substrate. At 5 µm growth per surface, a precision bore narrows by 0.010 mm total (2 × 5 µm). Type III hard anodize grows 10–50 µm per surface — more significant. Weldo CNC calculates the pre-anodize machining target at DFM review, so the bore is machined oversize by exactly the confirmed growth amount, achieving the drawing dimension after anodizing.

**Q2: What is anodizing and how does it work?**

Anodizing converts the aluminium surface to aluminium oxide (Al₂O₃) through an electrochemical process. The aluminium part is connected as the anode in an acid electrolyte bath — typically sulphuric acid — and an electric current drives oxygen ions from the electrolyte to combine with aluminium atoms at the surface, forming a hard, dense oxide layer. Because the oxide forms from the aluminium itself, it is integral to the part — it cannot peel or chip like a plated coating. The process takes minutes to hours depending on the target oxide thickness.

**Q3: What aluminium alloys can be anodized?**

Wrought aluminium alloys — 6061, 7075, 5052, 6082, 2024 — are all anodizable. 6061-T6 produces the best anodize result: uniform oxide, even colour, full Type II and Type III range. 7075 anodizes slightly thinner and less uniformly — acceptable for Type II but confirm for cosmetic colour applications. 2024's copper content makes it the most difficult wrought grade to anodize uniformly; alodine (chromate conversion) is often preferred for 2024. Cast aluminium (ADC12, A380) anodizes poorly due to high silicon content — not recommended for decorative or precision anodizing.

**Q4: How long does anodizing add to the total lead time?**

Anodizing adds 1–3 working days to the machining lead time. The anodizing process itself runs for several hours per batch; pre-treatment, masking, and post-anodize inspection add one to two days. Total order lead time for machined and anodized aluminium parts is typically 3–8 business days for prototypes, 8–20 days for small-batch production. Upload your STEP file with finish specification for a 24-hour confirmed lead time.

**Q5: What is the minimum order quantity for anodizing?**

No minimum order quantity. Single-piece prototype orders accepted with the same masking and inspection process as production orders.

**Q6: How do I specify anodizing on my drawing?**

Specify the anodize type (Type II or Type III), colour (black, blue, natural, etc.), any applicable standard (MIL-A-8625 Type II or Type III), and masking requirements. Example drawing note: "Anodize Type II per MIL-A-8625, black dyed and sealed. Mask M6 threaded holes and Ø25H7 bore." Upload the STEP file and annotated PDF drawing — Weldo CNC confirms the masking plan and pre-anodize dimensions at quote review.

**Q7: What drives the cost of anodizing?**

Part size (larger surface area = longer bath time), masking complexity (multiple masked features increase prep time), colour (standard black or clear costs less than special-match colours), and oxide thickness (Type III at 100 µm takes longer than Type II at 15 µm). Anodizing is a batch process — multiple parts run together. Single-piece prototype anodizing costs more per part than production runs due to the fixed setup cost per batch.

**Q8: How does anodizing compare to powder coating for aluminium parts?**

Anodizing is integral to the aluminium — the oxide cannot peel or chip. Powder coating is a surface coating that can chip at edges under impact. Anodizing is thinner and dimensionally more precise; powder coating applies 60–100 µm per surface, which is more difficult to control on precision features. For machined aluminium with tight-tolerance features, anodizing is the correct choice. For cosmetic finishes on fabricated steel or non-critical aluminium structures, powder coating may be more economical.

**Q9: Can Type III hard anodize be applied to 7075 aluminium?**

Yes, with a conditional note. 7075-T6 can be hard anodized, but the oxide uniformity is lower than 6061 at the same conditions. The zinc and copper alloying elements in 7075 interfere with oxide formation — the result may show slight colour variation or reduced oxide thickness in some areas. For safety-critical hard anodized wear surfaces in 7075, confirm the specification at quote stage and run a sample piece before committing to production quantity.

**Q10: Does anodizing affect the fatigue life of aluminium parts?**

Anodizing can reduce fatigue life on highly stressed aluminium parts — the oxide layer is brittle and can initiate fatigue cracks at the part surface under cyclic stress. This is most significant for Type III hard anodize, which is thicker and harder. For fatigue-critical aerospace aluminium parts (spars, ribs, structural brackets under cyclic load), confirm with your structural engineer whether anodizing is appropriate, or whether alodine (which does not affect fatigue life significantly) is the preferred corrosion protection.
