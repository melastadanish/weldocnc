## Section 3: Why ABS for CNC Machining
**Design:** 2-column — text left, image right. White background.
**Heading:** A Practical Material That Does Most Things Well

ABS — acrylonitrile butadiene styrene — is a terpolymer. Each component does a specific job: acrylonitrile provides chemical resistance and hardness, butadiene gives impact toughness (particularly at low temperatures), and styrene contributes rigidity and surface finish quality. The combination produces a material that machines cleanly, holds reasonable tolerances, paints well, and costs significantly less than engineering plastics like PEEK or Delrin.

The machinability comes from the thermoplastic structure — ABS softens rather than shatters. It cuts with sharp tooling at high spindle speeds, produces continuous chips rather than brittle fragments, and leaves a smooth surface with the right parameters. The risk is heat buildup. ABS softens around 100°C — well below what metals routinely tolerate at the cutting interface. Sharp tools, appropriate feed rates, good chip clearance: these aren't optional on thermoplastics. A part that comes off the machine with melted burrs and a waxy surface was cut with dull tooling or at the wrong parameters. That's fixable. It's also entirely avoidable.

One property that distinguishes ABS from most engineering plastics: it's soluble in acetone. That's practically useful. ABS assemblies can be solvent-cemented — clean joints with near-parent-material bond strength, no mechanical fasteners needed. ABS surfaces can be smoothed with controlled acetone vapour where Ra needs to improve without mechanical polishing. And it means ABS parts that will contact acetone-based solvents or adhesives in service need to be assessed — it's not always a problem, but it should be a known quantity.

For prototyping and functional parts in moderate-duty applications — enclosures, housings, brackets, jigs, fixtures, consumer and medical device components — ABS is frequently the first plastic considered. Reasonable strength, low cost, available in multiple grades and colours, and easier to paint than almost any other engineering plastic.

## Section 5: Grade Selection Guide
**Design:** 3-column card grid. Light grey background.
**Heading:** Which ABS Grade Do You Need?

**Card 1 — Standard ABS**
The default choice for most applications. If the part is a prototype, an enclosure, a bracket, a housing, or a fixture that doesn't have elevated temperature, flame rating, or ESD requirements — standard ABS is right. Lower cost, easiest to machine, best surface finish after cutting, and takes paint better than any of the other grades. Available in beige/natural, black, and white as standard.

**Card 2 — ABS+PC Blend**
The polycarbonate content does two things: it raises the heat deflection temperature to 110–120°C and improves impact resistance at low temperatures — both of which standard ABS struggles with. It's a meaningful upgrade for parts that sit in hot enclosures, automotive interiors, or anywhere the ambient temperature regularly pushes past 80°C. Harder to machine, slightly more expensive, slightly less clean surface finish after cutting. Worth it if standard ABS has failed at temperature.

**Card 3 — FR or ESD Grade**
FR and ESD grades are compliance-driven choices, full stop. Flame retardant when the application has a UL 94 requirement — common in consumer electronics housings, electrical enclosures, and aerospace interior parts. ESD when the part handles or is adjacent to electronics where static discharge would cause damage. Both grade families behave slightly differently in the machine (FR grades can be more brittle; ESD grades vary by brand) — flag the specific requirement at quote stage and we'll confirm grade suitability for the geometry.

| Thread Standards | Metric (M), UNC, UNF | — | Brass inserts recommended for load-bearing or repeated-assembly threads |

**A note on tolerances for ABS.**
ABS is not aluminium. The same ±0.05mm callout on a plastic part carries different implications than on a metal one. ABS absorbs atmospheric moisture and expands — typically 0.2–0.5% over time in humid conditions. Its coefficient of thermal expansion is approximately 7× higher than aluminium. A part machined to ±0.025mm at 20°C in a controlled workshop will behave differently at 40°C in a sealed electronics enclosure or 30°C in a humid factory. For critical-fit features — bores that mate with metal shafts, sealing faces, precision guides — discuss the service environment at quote stage. We'll advise on what's realistically achievable and whether ABS is the right material for that level of precision, or whether Delrin or another plastic would serve better.

## Section 7: Surface Finishes for ABS
**Design:** Full-width table. Light grey background.
**Heading:** Surface Finishes for ABS Parts

| Finish | Best For | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **As Machined** | Prototypes, internal components, jigs and fixtures | Ra 1.6–3.2µm, visible toolmarks. Fine for non-cosmetic applications. |
| **Bead Blasted** | Cosmetic uniformity, hiding toolmarks | Matte isotropic texture. Use cautiously on thin or fine-detail features — bead blasting at incorrect pressure can damage detail on plastics. |
| **Painted** | Colour-critical cosmetic parts | ABS accepts primer and paint with better adhesion than almost any other engineering plastic. Key for consumer product prototypes requiring colour matching to production moulded parts. |
| **Vapour Smoothed (Acetone)** | Reducing surface Ra without mechanical polishing | Controlled acetone vapour exposure smooths the machined surface. Not dimensionally precise — keep away from tight-tolerance features. Cosmetic surfaces only. |
| **Solvent Cemented** | Bonded ABS assemblies | ABS bonds to itself with MEK or acetone-based cement. Near-parent-material joint strength. Mark bond faces on drawing — surface finish at joint interface matters for bond quality. |
| **Plating (Chrome/Nickel)** | Decorative or EMI shielding applications | ABS can be electroplated after a specialist etching process. Good adhesion. Used for automotive decorative trim and EMI shielding enclosures. |

**Note on powder coating:** Standard ABS HDT is 95–100°C; powder coat cure temperatures reach 180–200°C. Standard ABS will distort in the cure oven. If powder coating is required, specify ABS+PC and confirm with us before the drawing is finalised — we'll assess the geometry and wall thickness before committing.

## Section 8: Design Guidelines for ABS CNC Machining
**Design:** 3-column table. White background.
**Heading:** Design Guidelines for ABS

| Feature | Recommendation | Why It Matters |
|---|---|---|
| Min wall thickness | 1.0mm recommended; 1.5mm for load-bearing walls | Thinner walls deflect under cutting forces; ABS below 1mm vibrates during machining, affecting dimensional accuracy and surface finish |
| Internal corner radius | Min 0.5mm; prefer 1.0mm+ | Sharp internal corners create stress concentration in ABS under impact or load. Larger radii also allow faster toolpaths and reduce tool wear |
| Thread engagement | Use brass inserts (M3 and above) for structural threads | ABS threads strip under repeated assembly and disassembly cycles. Brass helicoil or press-fit inserts give metal-thread performance in plastic parts |
| Tolerance on mating features | Allow for thermal expansion in the fit design | ABS CTE is ~70–90 µm/m·°C vs ~23 for aluminium. A bore mating with an aluminium shaft at 20°C will behave differently at 60°C — design the fit with the operating temperature in mind |
| Powder coating | Do not specify for standard ABS | Cure temperature (180–200°C) exceeds standard ABS HDT (95–100°C) — parts will distort. Use ABS+PC if powder coating is required and confirm with us first |
| Deep pockets | Max depth-to-width ratio 4:1 recommended | Chip clearance and heat removal become difficult in deep pockets in thermoplastics. Beyond 4:1, discuss toolpath strategy at quote stage |
| Acetone exposure | Flag in application review | ABS is solvent-attacked by acetone and some ketone-based cleaners. If the part contacts these chemicals in service, confirm compatibility or consider an alternative material |
| Solvent cementing | Mark bond faces on drawing | ABS assemblies can be solvent-cemented with MEK or acetone-based cement. Surface finish at bond interfaces affects joint strength — flag these features and we'll confirm finish requirements |

## Section 9: Applications
**Design:** 2-column icon+text grid. Light grey background.
**Heading:** Where CNC Machined ABS Parts Are Used

**Consumer Electronics & Enclosures**
Keyboards, remote controls, instrument cases, appliance housings — ABS is the default plastic for consumer electronics enclosures for good reason. CNC machined ABS is used for prototypes, pre-production samples, and short-run production where injection moulding tooling cost isn't justified. Its paint adhesion means colour-matched prototypes that look and feel like production moulded parts.

**Automotive Interior**
Dashboard components, switch housings, duct connectors, trim panels. ABS+PC blend is preferred for parts that sit in hot cabin environments. CNC machined for prototype validation and low-volume production; injection moulded at volume. ABS's paint adhesion is a genuine advantage for colour-matched interior parts — priming and painting produces a finish that's difficult to distinguish from production tooled parts.

**Medical Devices**
Equipment housings, diagnostic device enclosures, mounting brackets, protective covers. ABS is not implant-grade — for external-contact medical equipment only. Its combination of impact resistance, machinability, and paint adhesion makes it practical for housings and structural covers. ESD grade for electronics-adjacent assemblies. Full material certs and traceability with every order.

**Jigs, Fixtures, and Tooling**
ABS is cost-effective for CNC machined jigs and fixtures where metal isn't required — lighter than aluminium, faster to machine, easy to modify between design iterations. Drill guides, assembly fixtures, inspection aids. One caveat: ABS will creep under sustained stress. For precision fixtures that hold load over long periods, Delrin or aluminium is the better choice.

**Robotics and Automation**
Structural housings, sensor mounts, prototype mechanisms, cable management parts. ABS machines cleanly and is easy to post-process — paint, solvent cement, bond. For structural robotics components that need to survive repeated impact, the butadiene toughness is the right trade-off at this cost point.

**Education and Prototyping**
ABS is the workhorse material for prototype development across industries. Fast to machine, easy to modify, low cost. When a design is being iterated — form check, fit check, functional prototype — ABS gets parts on the bench quickly without committing to expensive materials. Switch to the correct production material once the geometry is confirmed.

## Section 10: Pros & Cons
**Design:** 2-column. White background.
**Heading:** ABS: Honest Assessment

**Where ABS works well:**
- Low cost relative to most engineering plastics — significant advantage over Delrin, PEEK, polycarbonate
- Machines cleanly with sharp tooling — good surface finish achievable without secondary operations
- Accepts paint and primer better than almost any other engineering plastic — critical for colour-matched prototypes
- Acetone and MEK solvent cementing enables strong bonded assemblies without mechanical fasteners
- Available in standard, FR, ESD, and ABS+PC grades to cover most moderate-duty requirements
- Impact resistant — butadiene content gives good toughness compared to brittle plastics like acrylic or polystyrene

**Where to think twice:**
- Temperature limit — standard ABS deflects at ~95°C. Not suitable for elevated temperature applications. Use ABS+PC (110–120°C HDT) or polycarbonate if heat resistance is required
- UV degradation — ABS yellows and becomes brittle with prolonged UV exposure. Outdoor applications need a UV-stabilised grade or protective coating
- Powder coating is problematic with standard ABS — cure temperatures exceed HDT and parts will distort. Requires ABS+PC and careful process assessment
- Dimensional stability — higher moisture absorption and thermal expansion than metals means tight-tolerance ABS features need the service environment considered
- Creep under sustained load — ABS deforms slowly under constant stress. Not suitable for precision load-bearing features; use metal or Delrin for those
- Solvent sensitivity — acetone and some other ketone-based solvents attack ABS. Know the chemical environment before specifying

## Section 14: FAQ
**Design:** Accordion. White background. First item open.
**Heading:** ABS CNC Machining — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: Is ABS easy to CNC machine?**
Yes — with the right tooling. ABS machines faster and more freely than most engineering plastics. The one thing to manage is heat: ABS softens around 100°C, and dull tooling or aggressive feeds will melt the cutting interface rather than cut it. Sharp high-speed tooling, good chip clearance, and appropriate spindle speeds produce clean surfaces and accurate dimensions. Under the right conditions, ABS is one of the more forgiving plastics to machine.

**Q2: What tolerances can you hold on ABS CNC parts?**
Standard tolerance is ±0.1mm; precision work to ±0.05mm. Tighter than ±0.05mm on ABS needs a qualification: ABS absorbs moisture and has a thermal expansion coefficient approximately 7× higher than aluminium. A part machined to ±0.025mm at 20°C in a controlled shop can shift measurably in service at elevated temperature or humidity. For critical-fit features, specify the service environment and fit requirement at quote stage — we'll advise on what's achievable and whether ABS is the right material for that precision level.

**Q3: What's the difference between standard ABS and ABS+PC?**
ABS+PC is a blend of ABS and polycarbonate. The PC content raises the heat deflection temperature from ~95°C to 110–120°C and improves low-temperature impact resistance. It's harder to machine and costs slightly more. Specify ABS+PC when the part sits near heat-generating electronics, in automotive interior environments, or anywhere standard ABS has shown temperature-related failure. For general enclosures and prototypes that won't see elevated temperatures, standard ABS is usually the right choice.

**Q4: Can ABS be painted after CNC machining?**
Yes, and better than most engineering plastics. ABS accepts primer and paint with excellent adhesion — this is one of its genuine advantages for prototype work. For consumer product prototypes that need to colour-match production moulded parts, ABS is the natural choice. Surface preparation matters: clean, lightly sanded or bead-blasted surface with a suitable primer gives the best paint adhesion and the most durable result.

**Q5: When should I use ABS instead of Delrin for CNC machined parts?**
Different materials for different problems. Delrin is stiffer, dimensionally more stable, has lower moisture absorption, and handles sustained loads without creep — it's right for precision mechanical components, gears, bushings, and parts where dimensional stability under load is critical. ABS is tougher in impact, costs less, machines slightly faster, and takes paint and solvent cement. Use ABS for housings, enclosures, brackets, and prototypes. Use Delrin for precision mechanical components. If you're unsure, send the application details and we'll advise.

**Q6: Can ABS be used for flame-retardant applications?**
Flame retardant ABS grades (UL 94 V-0 and V-1 rated) are available and we machine them regularly. FR grades are slightly more brittle than standard ABS and properties vary by manufacturer — confirm the specific grade requirement at quote stage for complex or thin-walled geometry. If you have a UL 94 compliance requirement, flag it in the RFQ and we'll confirm the appropriate grade and assess the geometry before quoting.
