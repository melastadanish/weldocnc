## Section 3: What is Delrin CNC Machining?

**H2:** What is Delrin CNC Machining?

Weldo CNC machines Delrin — POM acetal, polyoxymethylene — in Delrin 150, Delrin 500, POM-C copolymer, and food-grade acetal variants, to tolerances of ±0.005 mm, from its ISO 9001:2015 certified facility in Dongguan, China. Prototype parts ship in 1–5 business days. Every tight-tolerance order includes a full CMM — coordinate measuring machine — dimensional inspection report, delivered with the parts.

Engineers specify Delrin for mechanical precision parts because it is the most dimensionally stable common engineering plastic below 100°C. A Delrin gear holds its tooth profile in humid conditions, a Delrin bushing maintains its bore diameter after months of operation, and a Delrin food-contact component maintains both its dimension and its regulatory compliance without special treatment. Tensile strength of 68–70 MPa, low friction coefficient (0.20–0.35 against steel), and negligible moisture absorption (0.2% maximum) make it the default polymer for slide rails, cams, and load-bearing mechanical components where nylon's moisture sensitivity would cause dimensional drift.

Delrin machines exceptionally well — it is one of the easiest engineering plastics to CNC machine. Sharp carbide tooling produces clean chips at high cutting speeds. The material does not melt or gum at the cutting edge as ABS or polycarbonate can. Surface finish achieves Ra 0.8 µm as standard on finishing passes. The one constraint specific to Delrin is its tendency to warp in thick-section homopolymer (Delrin 150, 500) when asymmetric stock removal releases internal stress — the fix is controlled stress-relief annealing at 70°C for two hours before finish machining.

Weldo CNC has machined Delrin components since 2012. Typical part types include precision gears, cam followers, conveyor wear strips, guide rails, food-contact fittings, pneumatic valve components, and medical device plastic housings.

## Section 5: Delrin vs Alternative Materials

**H2:** Delrin or an Alternative — Which Material Fits Your Part?

**Choose Delrin When:**
- Dimensional stability in humid environments is required — Delrin absorbs less than 0.2% moisture versus nylon's 2.5–3.5%
- Sliding or bearing surface with metal counterparts — low friction coefficient (0.20–0.35) and low wear rate
- Food contact compliance is required — specify food-grade acetal (FDA 21 CFR 177.2470)
- Operating temperature is below 90°C continuous
- Precision gears, cams, and guides where tooth or profile tolerance must hold over time

Example part types where Delrin wins: conveyor wear components, food-contact fittings, cam followers, precision gear blanks, slide rail inserts, valve actuator components

**When an Alternative May Win:**
- Operating temperature above 100°C → PEEK is required; Delrin softens
- Impact-critical application where tough failure mode matters → Nylon PA6 or PA66 has better notched impact resistance than Delrin homopolymer
- Electrical insulation in high-frequency applications → PTFE has superior dielectric properties
- Prototype or short-life component → ABS is more economical for non-mechanical applications

Closing note: if the application sits on the boundary between Delrin and PEEK — 80–120°C, moderate chemical exposure — send the STEP file with operating temperature and chemical environment. The correct grade will be confirmed before quoting.

## Section 10: Delrin Machining Design Guidelines

**H2:** Delrin Machining Design Guidelines

**Subheading:** Delrin is one of the easiest engineering plastics to machine — but specific design and process rules prevent warping and stress failures.

| Feature | Recommendation | Why It Matters |
|---|---|---|
| Minimum wall thickness | 1.5 mm for structural Delrin features | Thinner walls deflect under cutting force — dimensional instability appears at the feature tip |
| Stress relief for thick blocks | Anneal at 70°C for 2 hours before finish machining on billets over 30 mm thickness | Delrin homopolymer (150, 500) carries internal residual stress from extrusion — asymmetric stock removal releases it as warping |
| Tool selection | Sharp positive-rake HSS or carbide tooling | Blunt tools generate frictional heat at the chip root — Delrin softens and gums, ruining surface finish |
| Coolant | Air blast or light mist — no flood coolant on Delrin | Delrin absorbs minimal moisture but prolonged flood coolant contact can affect surface quality on fine-finish parts |
| Internal corner radii | Minimum R0.5 mm in milled pockets | Sharp Delrin corners initiate cracking under cyclic load — all pockets have a corner radius equal to at least half the tool radius |
| Thread design | Cut threads to ISO metric standard. For M6 and below with load-bearing requirement, use helicoil inserts | Delrin threads creep under sustained clamp load — metal inserts eliminate creep in threaded connections |
| Gear tooth surface finish | Specify Ra 0.8 µm on tooth flanks | Gear tooth finish below Ra 1.6 µm is achievable in Delrin and directly affects gear efficiency and wear life |
| Food-grade traceability | Specify FDA 21 CFR 177.2470 compliant grade on drawing — natural/ivory colour only | Coloured Delrin formulations may not satisfy food contact regulations — confirm compliance before specifying coloured grades |
| Dimensional measurement timing | Allow 15–20 minutes at room temperature before measuring | Delrin's CTE of 110 × 10⁻⁶/°C means parts measured direct off the machine read differently than parts at thermal equilibrium |
| Wall symmetry in turned parts | Design turned thin-wall parts symmetrically about the turning axis | Asymmetric wall sections in turned Delrin release stress unequally when unclamped — the bore goes oval |

**Most common DFM failures on Delrin parts:**

Warped large blocks are the most common Delrin machining failure. An engineer orders a 200 × 100 × 50 mm block from Delrin 150 stock, the machinist removes material from one side, and the block bows 0.3–0.5 mm across its length as the internal stress released by that cut re-distributes. The fix is: specify POM-C copolymer for large blocks (lower internal stress than Delrin homopolymer), and anneal at 70°C for 2 hours before finish machining. Weldo CNC flags this at DFM review for all Delrin blocks over 30 mm section thickness.

Creeping threads under assembly torque are the second pattern. Engineers design M5 or M6 threads directly in Delrin for load-bearing joints. Under sustained clamp force, Delrin threads cold-flow — the joint loosens over hours to days. Helicoil metal inserts installed in the Delrin bore provide a metal-to-metal thread interface that doesn't creep. Weldo CNC recommends inserts at DFM review for any Delrin thread carrying static or dynamic load.

## Section 15: FAQ

**H2:** Delrin CNC Machining — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: What tolerance can Weldo CNC hold on Delrin parts?**

Delrin machines to ±0.005 mm on precision features — gear pitch diameters, bore fits, guide rail profiles — verified by CMM after thermal stabilisation. General dimensions follow ISO 2768-c at ±0.1 mm. Surface finish on machined Delrin is Ra 1.6 µm on standard finish passes; Ra 0.8 µm on gear tooth flanks and precision bearing surfaces with a finishing pass. Specify critical features and tolerances on your drawing.

**Q2: What is Delrin CNC machining and how does it differ from machining metals?**

Delrin — POM acetal — is a semi-crystalline thermoplastic that machines by shearing cleanly with sharp positive-rake carbide or HSS tooling. Unlike metals, Delrin generates chips that are brittle and short, clearing from the cutting zone easily. The primary machining challenge is heat management — frictional heat from blunt tools softens Delrin at the chip root, gumming the surface rather than producing a clean chip. At correct cutting speed and feed rate, Delrin machines faster than most metals and achieves excellent surface finish without grinding.

**Q3: What Delrin grades do you machine?**

Confirmed grades include Delrin 150 (homopolymer, maximum toughness), Delrin 500 (homopolymer, general purpose), POM-C copolymer (lower internal stress for large sections), food-grade acetal (FDA 21 CFR 177.2470 compliant), and glass-filled POM (GF20/GF30 for higher stiffness). Delrin 150 and 500 are the homopolymer grades (Dupont trade name). POM-C is the copolymer alternative — preferred for large blocks where internal stress is a warping concern. Specify grade explicitly on the drawing.

**Q4: How long does it take to machine Delrin parts?**

Prototype Delrin parts ship in 1–5 business days. Small-batch production ships in 5–15 business days. Delrin is one of the fastest-machining engineering plastics — cycle times are short compared to metals or PEEK. For thick-section parts requiring stress-relief annealing, add one working day for the annealing cycle. Upload your STEP file for a 24-hour quote.

**Q5: What is the minimum order quantity for Delrin CNC machining?**

No minimum order quantity. Single-piece prototypes accepted. The quote and production process is the same for 1 part as for 1,000.

**Q6: What file formats do you accept for Delrin machining quotes?**

STEP (.stp / .step) is the preferred 3D format. Include a 2D PDF drawing with tolerance callouts, surface finish specifications, grade requirement (Delrin 150, 500, POM-C, food-grade), and any FDA compliance requirements. For gears, include tooth profile specification — module, pressure angle, number of teeth, and tooth face surface finish.

**Q7: What drives the cost of Delrin CNC machining?**

Delrin raw material is cost-effective — less expensive per kilogram than PEEK or engineering metals. Machining cycle time is short due to high cutting speeds. The main cost factors are part complexity (deep pockets, tight tolerances, gear profiles) and whether stress-relief annealing is required for thick sections. Food-grade acetal costs a small premium over standard grades. Overall, Delrin is one of the most cost-effective precision plastic machining options.

**Q8: How does Delrin compare to PEEK and nylon for machined parts?**

Delrin is the most dimensionally stable option below 90°C in humid environments — it absorbs less than 0.2% moisture, compared to nylon's 2.5–3.5%. PEEK is required above 100°C or when biocompatibility per ISO 10993 is a regulatory requirement. Nylon has superior impact resistance but dimensional instability in humid conditions that Delrin avoids. For precision mechanical components — gears, bushings, guides — Delrin is almost always the correct choice below 90°C. It costs significantly less than PEEK for the same geometry.

**Q9: Is Delrin suitable for food contact applications?**

Food-grade acetal (compliant with FDA 21 CFR 177.2470) is suitable for direct food contact in machined components — conveyor guides, star wheels, fittings, and valve components. Standard Delrin 150 and 500 are NOT confirmed as food-grade by default. The food-grade grade must be specified on the drawing, and Weldo CNC provides the material certificate confirming FDA 21 CFR 177.2470 compliance with every food-contact Delrin order.

**Q10: Does Delrin warp after machining?**

Delrin homopolymer (Delrin 150, 500) carries internal residual stress from the extrusion process. When material is removed asymmetrically — machining a flat face on one side of a thick block — that stress releases and the part bows. POM-C copolymer has lower internal stress and is preferred for large machined blocks. Stress-relief annealing at 70°C for 2 hours before finish machining eliminates most of the warping risk in homopolymer grades. Weldo CNC applies this protocol by default on Delrin homopolymer blocks over 30 mm section thickness and flags the recommendation at DFM review.
