## Section 3: What is PEEK CNC Machining?

**H2:** What is PEEK CNC Machining?

Weldo CNC machines PEEK — polyether ether ketone — in standard unfilled grade, GF30 (30% glass-filled), and CF30 (30% carbon-filled) variants, to tolerances of ±0.005 mm, from its ISO 9001:2015 certified facility in Dongguan, China. Prototype PEEK parts ship in 1–5 business days. Every tight-tolerance order includes a full CMM — coordinate measuring machine — dimensional inspection report, delivered with the parts.

Engineers specify PEEK when no other engineering plastic satisfies the combination of requirements. Continuous service temperature to 250°C, chemical resistance to most organic solvents and acids, inherent flame retardancy, and biocompatibility of standard unfilled PEEK — per ISO 10993 and USP Class VI — make it the polymer of last resort before titanium or stainless steel. At 1.32 g/cm³, PEEK is one-fifth the weight of stainless steel, which matters in medical implant and aerospace weight-critical applications.

PEEK machines well under the right conditions. Sharp, positive-rake carbide tooling is required — neutral or negative-rake tools generate heat rather than shear at the cutting edge, which causes localised melting at the chip root. Surface finish (Ra — roughness average) on PEEK is excellent at Ra 0.8 µm with a standard finishing pass; Ra 0.4 µm is achievable with a polishing pass on flat faces. Chip clearance matters — PEEK chips are short and brittle but accumulate quickly in deep pockets, so high-pressure air blast or light flood coolant is used to evacuate the cutting zone.

Weldo CNC has machined PEEK components since 2012. Typical part types include valve seats and seals for chemical processing, spinal implant components, bearing cages, electrical insulators, structural brackets in aerospace, and test equipment housings requiring low outgassing.

## Section 4: PEEK Grades & Variants

**H2:** PEEK Grades We Machine

Grade selection determines strength, stiffness, and biocompatibility — not all PEEK grades are equivalent for regulated applications.

| Grade / Variant | Key Difference | Temperature Limit | Best For |
|---|---|---|---|
| Standard PEEK (unfilled) | Biocompatible per ISO 10993 / USP Class VI. Chemically transparent | 250°C continuous | Medical devices, implants, FDA-regulated food contact, chemical processing |
| PEEK GF30 (30% glass-filled) | Higher stiffness and compressive strength than unfilled. NOT biocompatible | 260°C continuous | Structural brackets, pump components, high-load bearing surfaces |
| PEEK CF30 (30% carbon-filled) | Highest stiffness and wear resistance. Electrically conductive. NOT biocompatible | 260°C continuous | Aerospace structural, semiconductor handling, high-wear bearing and seal surfaces |

> **Critical note:** Only standard unfilled PEEK satisfies ISO 10993 biocompatibility and USP Class VI. GF30 and CF30 are NOT biocompatible — do not specify for implant or direct body-contact applications. Confirm grade and compliance requirement on the drawing before quoting.

## Section 5: PEEK vs Alternative Materials

**H2:** PEEK or an Alternative Material — Which Fits Your Part?

**Choose PEEK When:**
- Continuous operating temperature exceeds 150°C (above Delrin and nylon limits)
- Biocompatibility per ISO 10993 / USP Class VI is required — standard PEEK only
- Chemical resistance to organic solvents, steam, or aggressive acids is the design driver
- Sterilisation compatibility required — PEEK autoclaves at 121°C/134°C, withstands gamma and ETO
- Part replaces metal in a weight-critical application where plastic temperature resistance is marginal in other grades

Example part types where PEEK wins: spinal cages, chemical valve seats, semiconductor pick-up wands, aerospace electrical insulators, continuous-use bearing liners

**When an Alternative May Win:**
- Operating temperature below 100°C and chemical resistance is mild → Delrin (POM) is cheaper and easier to machine
- Operating temperature below 120°C and strength is moderate → Nylon PA66 costs significantly less
- Application is electrical insulation only at low temperature → PTFE is easier to machine and has superior dielectric properties
- Part is a prototype or low-cycle-life component → ABS or polycarbonate machines more economically

Closing note: if the application sits at the boundary — 150–180°C, moderate chemical exposure, regulated application — send the STEP file with material spec and operating conditions. The correct grade will be confirmed before quoting.

## Section 10: PEEK Machining Design Guidelines

**H2:** PEEK Machining Design Guidelines

**Subheading:** PEEK machines better than most engineering plastics but requires specific tool selection and heat management.

| Feature | Recommendation | Why It Matters |
|---|---|---|
| Minimum wall thickness | 1.5 mm for structural PEEK features | Thinner PEEK walls deflect under cutting force and produce dimensional instability at the feature tip |
| Tool selection | Sharp positive-rake carbide only | Neutral or negative-rake tools generate heat at the PEEK chip root — localised melting and material adhesion to the tool |
| Coolant | Light air blast or water-soluble emulsion at low concentration | PEEK absorbs minimal moisture (0.1% max) — flood coolant is acceptable; avoid oil-based cutting fluids which contaminate biocompatible-grade PEEK |
| Chip clearance | Ensure chip evacuation from deep pockets — PEEK chips are brittle and accumulate | Chip packing re-cuts the debris, degrades surface finish to below Ra 1.6 µm, and generates heat |
| Thread design | Use helicoil inserts for load-bearing threads in PEEK | PEEK threads are strong for the material but creep under sustained clamp load — metal inserts eliminate creep |
| Thermal stabilisation before measurement | Allow 30 minutes at room temperature before measuring critical features | PEEK expands at 47 × 10⁻⁶/°C — measuring direct off the machine reads thermal expansion, not actual room-temperature dimension |
| Internal corner radii | Minimum R0.5 mm in milled PEEK pockets | Sharp corners concentrate stress in PEEK and initiate cracking under cyclic load |
| Biocompatibility requirements | Specify "standard unfilled PEEK" on drawing and confirm grade requirement explicitly | GF30 and CF30 are NOT biocompatible — grade confusion in a medical supply chain is a regulatory non-conformance |
| Surface finish pre-polishing | Achieve Ra 1.6 µm from machining before polishing to Ra 0.8 µm | Attempting to polish from Ra 3.2 µm to Ra 0.8 µm in one pass leaves machining marks visible at shallow light angles |
| Clamping on thin PEEK parts | Use soft jaws or vacuum fixture — avoid metal-jaw direct clamp on thin PEEK | Hard jaw pressure on PEEK parts under 5 mm wall creates localised stress that releases as spring-back after unclamping, opening tolerance |

**Most common DFM failures on PEEK parts:**

Grade mix-up is the most consequential failure specific to PEEK. An engineer specifies standard PEEK for a medical application; the purchase order says "PEEK"; the supplier ships PEEK GF30 because it is in stock. The glass-filled grade is not biocompatible — the parts are non-conforming before they are machined. The fix is explicit grade specification on every drawing: "PEEK — standard unfilled grade — ISO 10993 biocompatibility required" leaves no ambiguity.

Thermal measurement error is the second pattern. PEEK at ±0.005 mm tolerance measured immediately after machining reads 0.005–0.015 mm differently than the same part at room temperature equilibrium, depending on part size and machining heat input. Weldo CNC flags this on inspection reports — all PEEK tight-tolerance measurements are taken after 30-minute thermal stabilisation.

## Section 15: FAQ

**H2:** PEEK CNC Machining — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: What tolerance can Weldo CNC hold on PEEK parts?**

PEEK machines to ±0.005 mm on precision features — bore diameters, OD fits, sealing faces — verified by CMM after thermal stabilisation. General dimensions follow ISO 2768-c at ±0.1 mm. Surface finish on machined PEEK is Ra 1.6 µm on standard finish passes; Ra 0.8 µm with a finishing pass; Ra 0.4 µm on lapped sealing surfaces. Specify critical features and tolerances on your drawing.

**Q2: What is PEEK CNC machining and how does it differ from machining metals?**

PEEK — polyether ether ketone — is a semi-crystalline high-performance polymer that machines by shearing with sharp positive-rake carbide tools, similar to soft metals. Unlike metal machining, however, PEEK generates heat at the tool-chip interface more readily — the material softens and re-welds rather than forming clean chips if the tool is blunt or the feed rate is too low. Cutting speed, feed, and tool sharpness are managed as a system to maintain chip-shearing mode throughout the cut, producing the Ra 0.8 µm surface finish that the material is capable of.

**Q3: What PEEK grades do you machine?**

Confirmed PEEK grades include standard unfilled PEEK, PEEK GF30 (30% glass-filled), and PEEK CF30 (30% carbon-filled). Standard unfilled PEEK is the only biocompatible grade — satisfying ISO 10993 and USP Class VI. GF30 and CF30 are NOT biocompatible. GF30 is specified for structural and pump applications requiring higher stiffness than unfilled PEEK. CF30 for aerospace and semiconductor applications where highest stiffness, wear resistance, and electrical conductivity are required. Specify grade explicitly on the drawing.

**Q4: How long does it take to machine PEEK parts?**

Prototype PEEK parts ship in 1–5 business days. Small-batch production ships in 5–15 business days. PEEK machines at moderate cycle times — faster than titanium, slower than aluminium. PEEK bar and sheet stock is more expensive than common engineering plastics, which affects material cost at prototype quantities. Upload your STEP file for a 24-hour quote.

**Q5: What is the minimum order quantity for PEEK CNC machining?**

No minimum order quantity. Single-piece prototypes accepted. The quote and production process is the same for 1 part as for 500.

**Q6: What file formats do you accept for PEEK machining quotes?**

STEP (.stp / .step) is the preferred 3D format. Include a 2D PDF drawing with tolerance callouts, surface finish specifications, grade requirement (standard PEEK / GF30 / CF30), and any compliance requirements (ISO 10993, USP Class VI). For medical applications, the compliance documentation requirement should be confirmed at order — not requested after delivery.

**Q7: What drives the cost of PEEK CNC machining?**

PEEK raw material is expensive — typically 10–20× the cost of nylon or Delrin per kilogram. That material cost dominates the part price at prototype quantities. Machining time is moderate — PEEK cuts at reasonable speeds with the right tooling. Tight tolerances and CMM inspection add time. Filled grades (GF30, CF30) are similar in raw material cost to standard PEEK. If cost is the constraint, confirm whether the temperature and chemical requirements actually require PEEK — Delrin or nylon may satisfy the operating condition at significantly lower cost.

**Q8: How does PEEK compare to Delrin and nylon for CNC machined parts?**

PEEK, Delrin, and nylon serve different temperature and chemical regimes. Delrin (POM) is the most dimensionally stable below 100°C — excellent for gears, bushings, and mechanical components in mild environments. Nylon (PA) absorbs moisture, which affects dimensional stability but provides toughness at moderate temperature. PEEK is specified when the continuous temperature exceeds 150°C, chemical resistance to solvents or steam is required, or biocompatibility per ISO 10993 is a regulatory requirement. PEEK costs 5–15× more than Delrin or nylon for the same geometry — choose it when the operating condition requires it, not as a default.

**Q9: Is standard PEEK biocompatible for medical device applications?**

Standard unfilled PEEK satisfies ISO 10993 biocompatibility and USP Class VI requirements — making it one of the few engineering polymers accepted for long-term implant and device-contact applications. PEEK GF30 and CF30 are NOT biocompatible — the glass and carbon fibres are not biologically inert. Specify the grade explicitly on the drawing and confirm the compliance documentation requirement at order confirmation. Weldo CNC tracks standard PEEK lot numbers and can provide material certificates — ISO 10993 test reports are the raw material supplier's documentation.

**Q10: What sterilisation methods are compatible with PEEK?**

Standard unfilled PEEK is compatible with autoclaving at both 121°C and 134°C, gamma radiation sterilisation, and ETO (ethylene oxide) sterilisation — all three common medical device sterilisation methods. GF30 and CF30 are thermally stable but are not used in sterilisation contexts due to the non-biocompatible filler. Specify the sterilisation method on the drawing if it affects the required surface finish (autoclaving and gamma do not affect PEEK surface — ETO leaves trace residue in deep pockets if not evacuated properly).
