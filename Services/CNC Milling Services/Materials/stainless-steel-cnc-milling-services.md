Stainless steel is specified when corrosion resistance, cleanroom compatibility, or food-grade surface hygiene requirements make aluminium or carbon steel inadequate. The passive oxide layer on austenitic grades (304, 316L) and precipitation-hardening grades (17-4PH) provides corrosion resistance in the majority of engineering environments without additional surface treatment — a significant operational advantage in humid, chloride-bearing, or chemically aggressive environments. Tensile strengths ranging from approximately 515 MPa (316L annealed) to 1310 MPa (17-4PH H900) make stainless applicable across a wide range of structural applications from food processing equipment to high-performance precision shafts.

Stainless steel is more demanding to machine than aluminium. The combination of work-hardening tendency (particularly pronounced in 304 and 316L), low thermal conductivity, and tendency to built-up edge on tooling means that cutting parameters, tooling geometry, and coolant strategy are more consequential. 316L machines somewhat better than 316 but requires sharp carbide tooling, consistent feed rates, and through-coolant. 303 — the free-machining grade with sulphur addition — is significantly more machinable and is the correct specification for parts where machinability and cost matter more than maximum corrosion resistance. 17-4PH in the H900 condition requires harder carbide grades and more conservative parameters than austenitic stainless.

Weldo CNC has been milling stainless steel for medical instruments, energy sector valve bodies, food processing equipment, and precision defence hardware since 2012. Passivation per ASTM A967 is performed in-house, with process records shipped alongside the CMM report. For medical, energy, and food applications where material traceability and surface treatment documentation are part of the quality record, the complete documentation set ships with every order.

## Section 5: Choosing the Right Stainless Steel Grade
**Design:** 3-column card grid. White background.
**Subheading:** Stainless steel grade selection is an engineering decision — the correct grade is determined by the corrosion environment, structural loads, and machinability requirements.

**When 316L Is Required**
316L is the correct specification for components in contact with chloride-bearing environments — seawater, physiological fluids, saline cleaning solutions, and process chemicals with chloride content. The molybdenum addition (2–3%) provides pitting corrosion resistance that 304 cannot match in chloride exposure; the low-carbon "L" designation prevents carbide precipitation at weld heat-affected zones and during sterilisation. For medical surgical instruments, pump and valve bodies in marine service, and food processing equipment in wash-down environments, 316L is the default specification. Passivation per ASTM A967 removes free iron from the surface and builds the passive oxide layer — standard on every 316L order.
*Tensile: ~515 MPa · Mo content: 2–3% · Machinability: Good*

**When 303 Makes Sense**
303 is the free-machining grade — a sulphur addition (0.15% minimum) acts as a chip breaker, dramatically improving machinability relative to 304 and 316L. The trade-off is corrosion resistance: 303 is less resistant than 304 in pitting and crevice conditions, and significantly less resistant than 316L in chloride environments. For machined shaft components, fittings, and threaded hardware where the operating environment is mild (dry, non-chloride) and machinability and cost are the primary drivers, 303 is the correct specification. For parts in demanding environments, 303 is not the right choice.
*Tensile: ~515 MPa · Sulphur: 0.15% min · Machinability: Excellent*

**When 17-4PH Is the Engineering Answer**
17-4PH (SUS630) is specified when structural performance requirements exceed what austenitic stainless grades provide, while maintaining adequate corrosion resistance. In the H900 condition, tensile strength reaches approximately 1310 MPa — more than double 316L — with corrosion resistance that exceeds 304 in most environments. For precision shafts in corrosive environments, aerospace and defence fasteners requiring both strength and corrosion resistance, and medical device components requiring high fatigue life, 17-4PH H900 is the engineering-correct specification. Machining requires harder carbide grades, more conservative parameters, and greater heat management than austenitic grades.
*Tensile: ~1310 MPa (H900) · Hardness: ~38 HRC · Machinability: Moderate*

## Section 10: Stainless Steel Machining Design Guidelines
**Design:** 3-column table + 2 supporting paragraphs. White background.
**Subheading:** Rules specific to machining stainless steel — beyond standard DFM.

| Feature | Recommendation | Why It Matters |
|---|---|---|
| Minimum wall thickness | 1.0 mm minimum for stable milling | Stainless deflects in thin walls — thicker minimum than aluminium |
| Internal corner radii | ≥ 0.5 mm; prefer ≥ 1.0 mm for deep pockets | Sharp corners in stainless cause work-hardening stress concentration and tool chatter |
| Thread design | Avoid fine-pitch threads in 304/316L; prefer coarse pitch | Stainless galls more readily than steel on fine-pitch threads under load |
| Thread engagement | 1.5–2× diameter minimum | Stainless galling risk at thread engagement face requires sufficient length |
| Coolant requirement | Flood coolant required; through-coolant for deep pockets | Low thermal conductivity means heat accumulates at cutting zone — accelerates tool wear |
| Tool material | Sharp PVD-coated carbide — never HSS on 316L or 17-4PH | Work-hardening destroys HSS tooling rapidly; carbide resists wear on the hardened layer |
| Feed rate | Maintain consistent cutting engagement — avoid low-feed rubbing | Low feeds cause rubbing, which work-hardens faster than cutting |
| Deep holes | Peck drilling with through-coolant above 5× diameter | Chip evacuation in stainless drilling — chips recut and cause drill failure in deep blind holes |
| Electropolish pre-condition | Degrease and remove embedded contamination before electropolishing | Embedded particles prevent uniform electrochemical removal — causes surface defects |
| Passivation timing | Passivate after all machining and deburring is complete | Post-passivation mechanical work breaks the passive layer; repassivation required |

**Most common DFM failure — thread galling in 316L:** Fine-pitch threads in 316L under load, or threaded holes assembled with steel fasteners, are prone to galling. The fix: specify coarse-pitch threads where the load case allows; specify anti-gall lubricant (Molykote or equivalent) on assembly; or switch to 303 for non-corrosive-environment applications. Identifying thread galling risk at the DFM review prevents a failure often discovered only at assembly.

**How stainless DFM issues are handled at Weldo CNC:** DFM — design for manufacture — review is included with every stainless steel quote. Work-hardening risk, thread galling concerns, electropolish pre-conditioning requirements, and deep pocket coolant access are flagged before the job enters the machine queue. The engineer receives the DFM note at quoting.

## Section 15: Stainless Steel CNC Milling — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: What tolerances does Weldo CNC hold on stainless steel milled parts?**
±0.005 mm is the confirmed standard for stainless steel CNC milling — applicable to 303, 304, 316L, 431, 17-4PH, and 440C. For 5-axis simultaneous milling, ±0.003 mm. CMM verification is included on all critical-tolerance features and reported per dimension on the inspection report. For general structural milling without tight-tolerance callouts, ISO 2768-m applies.

**Q2: What does stainless steel CNC milling involve?**
CNC milling of stainless steel removes material using rotating carbide cutting tools under flood or through-coolant. Feed rate and toolpath strategy are more critical than in aluminium — work-hardening tendency means the tool must maintain consistent cutting engagement and cannot dwell or rub. 3-axis handles prismatic features; 5-axis handles multi-face and complex geometry from a single datum. Post-machining, passivation is applied where specified, and CMM reports critical features.

**Q3: What stainless steel grades does Weldo CNC mill?**
303 (free-machining), 304, 316, 316L, 431, 17-4PH (H900, H925, H1025 conditions), and 440C. Grade 201 and 430 also available. Mill certificates covering alloy grade, heat number, carbon content, and specification reference ship with every order.

**Q4: What is the lead time for stainless steel CNC milled parts?**
Prototype quantities: 1–5 business days manufacturing. Small batch (10–50 parts): 5–15 business days. Passivation or electropolishing adds 1–2 business days. DDP delivery to the UK: 3–5 business days. Quote within 24 hours.

**Q5: Is there a minimum order quantity?**
No minimum. Single-piece prototypes in 316L, 17-4PH, or 440C are handled under the same quality and documentation standard as production batches.

**Q6: What file formats does Weldo CNC accept for stainless milling?**
STEP is preferred. Also accepted: IGES, SolidWorks (.sldprt), Parasolid (.x_t), DXF, and compressed packages. Include a 2D drawing with all tolerance callouts, GD&T requirements, surface finish Ra specifications, and thread specifications.

**Q7: How is pricing structured for stainless steel CNC milling?**
Key pricing drivers: grade (17-4PH and 440C carry material and tooling premium over 303 and 304), geometry complexity (5-axis and complex pocketing add cost), tolerance requirements (±0.005 mm requires additional CMM time), post-process finishing (passivation and electropolish add cost), and quantity. Upload STEP for a quote with DFM review.

**Q8: How does stainless steel compare to aluminium for structural parts?**
Stainless steel is approximately three times denser than aluminium (8.0 g/cm³ vs 2.7 g/cm³). Where corrosion resistance in chloride or chemical environments is the primary requirement and weight is secondary, stainless is correct. Where weight-to-strength ratio drives the design and the environment is mild, aluminium is typically correct. 17-4PH approaches 7075-T6's specific strength but at three times the density — specified for applications requiring both high strength and corrosion resistance simultaneously.

**Q9: Can Weldo CNC provide passivation documentation for medical and pharmaceutical applications?**
Yes — passivation per ASTM A967 (citric acid or nitric acid) is performed in-house. The finish process record confirms bath type, chemistry parameters, and exposure duration. For medical device programmes where passivation documentation supports device history records, the process record is structured to support inclusion. Confirm specific format requirements at quoting.

**Q10: How does work-hardening affect stainless steel milling tolerances?**
Work-hardening in austenitic stainless (304, 316L) occurs when the tool rubs rather than cuts — at low feed rates, after tool wear, or with inconsistent cutting engagement. The work-hardened layer is harder than the substrate and causes rapid tool degradation and dimensional inconsistency. At Weldo CNC, stainless toolpaths maintain consistent cutting engagement; fresh tooling is used at the start of tight-tolerance runs; in-process CMM checks confirm dimensional stability mid-run. Work-hardening is a process parameter issue — managed through correct toolpath and feed strategy, not by slowing down.
