## Section 3 — What Is Polycarbonate CNC Machining?

Polycarbonate CNC machining is the precision subtractive manufacturing of polycarbonate (PC) — an amorphous thermoplastic engineering plastic with outstanding impact strength and optical clarity — using CNC milling, turning, and 5-axis machining centres. At Weldo CNC, located in Dongguan, Guangdong, China, polycarbonate parts are machined to tolerances of ±0.005 mm on 3-axis and 4-axis platforms and ±0.003 mm on 5-axis centres, with Coordinate Measuring Machine (CMM) inspection reports supplied with every tight-tolerance order. Polycarbonate is specified for machined transparent parts — inspection windows, light covers, protective guards, optical instrument housings, and test viewing panels — and for any structural component where the combination of optical clarity, high impact resistance (Izod impact 600–900 J/m), and continuous operating temperature to 120 °C is required in a single material. Weldo CNC holds ISO 9001:2015 certification, with prototype delivery in 1–5 business days and small-batch production in 5–15 business days.

Engineers specify polycarbonate when acrylic (PMMA) is too brittle — polycarbonate is approximately 30× more impact-resistant than acrylic at equivalent thickness — and when ABS's opacity disqualifies it. PC is the correct choice for face shields, machine guards, instrument lens panels, lighting diffusers, and any application where the part must transmit light and survive accidental impact. PC also carries relevant flame ratings (UL94 V-0 available in some formulations) that make it the standard for electrical enclosure covers and protective panels.

Polycarbonate is amorphous and therefore has no sharp melting point, softening progressively above its glass transition temperature (~147 °C). It machines cleanly with sharp tooling and appropriate feed rates, producing good surface finish as-milled. The critical constraint is solvent sensitivity: polycarbonate crazes — develops surface microcracking — on contact with cutting fluids containing alcohol, ketone, or aromatic compounds. Weldo CNC uses only PC-compatible coolants (typically water-soluble emulsions or dry air blast) for polycarbonate machining.

Weldo CNC has machined polycarbonate for machine guarding, medical device light covers, automotive interior components, and electronic instrument panels since 2012. On transparent parts, surface quality failure is immediately visible — every polycarbonate order is reviewed for crazing, tool marks, and surface clarity before shipment.

## Section 5 — Polycarbonate or Alternative Material — Which Fits Your Part?

**Choose Polycarbonate When:**
- Optical clarity AND impact resistance are both required — no other common engineering plastic combines these properties
- Operating temperature reaches 120 °C and transparency is required
- A machine guard, inspection window, or protective shield must survive impact without fracturing
- Flame-rated transparent cover (UL94 V-0 formulations available) is required for electrical enclosures
- Example parts: machine guard panels, inspection windows, light covers, face shields, protective lenses, transparent housings for optical instruments

**When an Alternative May Win:**
- Maximum optical clarity and scratch resistance, impact is not a concern → **Acrylic (PMMA)** (higher clarity, better scratch resistance, but 30× less impact-resistant than PC)
- Structural housing with no transparency requirement → **ABS** (less expensive, easier to machine, electroplatable)
- Maximum impact toughness without transparency → **Nylon PA66** or **ABS+PC blend**
- Operating temperature above 120 °C → **PEEK** (handles 260 °C continuous) — no transparent engineering plastic handles high temperature well
- UV exposure over many years without yellowing → **UV-stabilised PC** or **PMMA** (PMMA has better long-term UV stability than even UV-PC)

*If the application sits on the boundary between polycarbonate and acrylic, send the STEP file with impact requirement and operating temperature — the right material will be confirmed before quoting.*

## Section 8 — Surface Finishes

`As-Machined` `Polishing (Optical-Grade)` `Bead Blasting` `Laser Engraving` `Pad Printing` `Flame Polishing` `Tumbling / Deburring`

> **Optical polishing note:** Polycarbonate can be polished to Ra 0.4 µm for optically clear instrument windows and lens surfaces. Flame polishing is an alternative method for restoring optical clarity to edges and machined surfaces — it heat-softens the surface layer, causing flow that smooths the machined texture. Flame polishing is most effective on simple flat or cylindrical surfaces; complex geometries are diamond-polished. Do NOT list anodizing, passivation, powder coating, or electroplating as standard finishes for PC — these are not compatible without special pre-treatment.

## Section 10 — Polycarbonate Machining Design Guidelines

| Feature | Recommendation | Why It Matters |
|---|---|---|
| Wall thickness | Minimum 2.0 mm; 3.0 mm+ for impact-rated guards | Thinner PC panels flex under cutting force — minimum 2.0 mm for dimensional stability during machining |
| Internal corners | Minimum radius 0.5 mm; use largest practical radius | PC is notch-sensitive — sharp internal corners are crack initiation points under impact loads |
| Tooling | Sharp, polished, single-flute or two-flute carbide; positive rake; O-flute or upcut spiral | PC machines cleanly with correct tooling; dull tools cause surface smearing and subsurface stress |
| Coolant — CRITICAL | Air blast only or water-soluble emulsion confirmed PC-compatible — NEVER use alcohol, ketone, or aromatic solvents | PC crazes (surface microcracking) on contact with alcohol, acetone, MEK, and similar solvents — cleaning and cutting fluid selection is critical |
| Surface pre-inspection | Check all transparent PC surfaces under raking light before final inspection — crazing is invisible in flat light | Crazing from solvent contact or internal stress appears as fine surface cracking only visible at oblique angles |
| Optical surface finish | Machine optical surfaces to Ra 0.8 µm minimum before polishing; polish to Ra 0.4 µm in sequence | Polishing directly from Ra 1.6 µm takes significantly more time and risks thermal stress — machine to Ra 0.8 µm first |
| Frame clearance | Design 1.0–1.5 mm clearance between PC panel and metal frame per metre of span | CTE mismatch between PC and aluminium or steel frames causes buckling or cracking without clearance |
| Thread design | Brass press-fit inserts or helicoil preferred for load-bearing fastener locations | PC has moderate thread strength — press-fit brass inserts provide metal-quality thread engagement in PC housings |
| Stress relief | For large or complex PC panels: anneal at 120 °C for 2 hours before finish machining | Reduces residual machining and extrusion stress; improves dimensional stability and reduces craze risk |
| Chip clearance | Upcut spiral geometry on router-style operations; through-cutting with climb milling preferred | PC chips clear well with correct helix; downward chip packing causes re-heating and smearing on deeper pockets |

**Most common DFM failure — solvent crazing from cleaning:** The most frequent cause of scrap on machined PC parts is craze damage from cleaning operations using isopropyl alcohol (IPA), acetone, or aromatic cleaning agents after machining. Crazing appears as fine surface microcracking that is invisible in overhead light but renders optically clear panels unusable. Weldo CNC uses only PC-compatible coolants during machining and delivers parts in protective wrapping — specify that downstream cleaning must use water-based or PC-compatible agents. Provide this note to the assembly team.

**DFM review for polycarbonate covers solvent and frame clearance first:** Weldo CNC reviews PC drawings for coolant compatibility, minimum wall thickness on impact-rated panels, frame clearance for thermal expansion, and optical surface finish specification. These are flagged at Design for Manufacturability (DFM) review before production — solvent and CTE failures are the two causes of most PC part scrap that reaches assembly.

## Section 15 — FAQ

**Q1: What tolerances can Weldo CNC hold on polycarbonate?**
Standard tolerances on polycarbonate milling are ±0.005 mm, verified by CMM. ISO 2768-c is the baseline for plastics. Polycarbonate is dimensionally stable — it does not absorb moisture significantly, so tolerances specified at machining apply in service. The main dimensional consideration for PC in metal frames is thermal expansion: design panel clearance for the full operating temperature range, as PC expands approximately 3× more than aluminium per degree.

**Q2: What is polycarbonate CNC machining and how does it differ from machining metals?**
Polycarbonate CNC machining removes material from PC sheet or rod using CNC milling, turning, and drilling. Compared to metals, PC is soft and machines quickly — cutting forces are low, and surface finish from the cutter is good with sharp, polished tooling. The critical difference is solvent sensitivity: coolants containing alcohol or ketone craze polycarbonate immediately. Only PC-compatible water-soluble emulsions or air-blast cooling are used. Polycarbonate's amorphous structure also means it has no sharp melting point — it softens progressively — so feed rates must be set to keep heat at the cut zone below the glass transition temperature (~147 °C).

**Q3: What polycarbonate grades are available?**
Weldo CNC machines standard polycarbonate (optically clear, highest impact strength), UV-stabilised polycarbonate (outdoor-rated, resists yellowing), and PC+ABS blend (higher toughness than ABS, opaque or lightly translucent, not optically clear). Specify grade on the drawing — "polycarbonate" without grade designation defaults to standard optically clear PC. UV-stabilised PC is specified for outdoor or prolonged UV-exposure applications.

**Q4: What are the lead times for polycarbonate parts?**
Prototype delivery is 1–5 business days from drawing approval. Small-batch production is 5–15 business days. For parts requiring optical polishing, add 1–2 business days for polishing operations. Quote turnaround is 24 hours from STEP file and drawing submission.

**Q5: Is there a minimum order quantity for polycarbonate machining?**
No minimum order quantity. Single prototype PC parts are accepted — polycarbonate guards, inspection windows, and instrument covers are commonly machined as single pieces for prototype validation before committing to injection moulding. Same quality standards and CMM inspection apply to single-piece orders.

**Q6: What file formats does Weldo CNC accept for polycarbonate parts?**
STEP (.stp, .step) is the preferred format. IGES (.igs), Parasolid (.x_t), and SolidWorks (.sldprt) files are also accepted. 2D drawings should be supplied as PDF to confirm tolerances, surface finish callouts (especially Ra specification for optical surfaces), PC grade, and any UL94 flame rating requirements. For transparent parts, note which surfaces must be optically clear.

**Q7: What drives the cost of polycarbonate CNC machining?**
Polycarbonate raw material is moderately priced relative to engineering metals. Machining cost is driven by part complexity, number of setups, and surface finish requirement — optical polishing to Ra 0.4 µm is a significant additional operation on large panels. PC+ABS is typically less expensive per part than pure PC for opaque structural applications. UV-stabilised PC has a modest material premium. Providing grade, optical surface requirements, and quantity at quoting ensures accurate pricing.

**Q8: When should I choose polycarbonate over acrylic (PMMA)?**
Choose polycarbonate when impact resistance is required — PC is approximately 30× more impact-resistant than acrylic at equivalent thickness. PC is the standard for safety guards, face shields, and any transparent cover where fracture must be avoided. Choose acrylic when scratch resistance and optical purity are the priority and impact loads are low — acrylic has higher surface hardness, better long-term UV stability, and is less expensive. Both materials craze with solvents; both require sharp tooling and careful heat management. Acrylic fractures catastrophically under impact; polycarbonate flexes and absorbs the blow.

**Q9: Can polycarbonate be machined to optical clarity — suitable for lenses and windows?**
Polycarbonate can be machined to optically useful surface quality for windows, sight glasses, and instrument lenses, but it is not an optical-grade lens material for imaging applications (that requires diamond-turned acrylic or glass optics). For machined PC windows and inspection panels, Ra 0.4 µm surface finish is achievable with polishing after CNC milling, and transmission is approximately 88–90% of visible light. For applications where Ra 0.8 µm as-milled is adequate (diffuse lighting covers, protective windows), no polishing is required.

**Q10: Is polycarbonate suitable for outdoor applications?**
Standard polycarbonate yellows under prolonged UV exposure — within 2–5 years in direct outdoor sunlight without UV protection. UV-stabilised polycarbonate contains UV absorbers that resist yellowing significantly longer and is the correct grade for outdoor machine guards, solar equipment covers, and exterior lighting panels. For maximum outdoor UV stability, acrylic (PMMA) outperforms even UV-stabilised PC over very long exposure periods — consider acrylic for parts expected to remain outdoors for 10+ years without replacement.
