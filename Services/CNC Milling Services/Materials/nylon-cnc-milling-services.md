## Section 3 — What Is Nylon CNC Machining?

Nylon CNC machining is the precision subtractive manufacturing of polyamide (PA) — a semi-crystalline engineering thermoplastic family — including PA6, PA66, PA12, and glass-filled (GF) variants, using CNC milling, turning, and 5-axis machining centres. At Weldo CNC, located in Dongguan, Guangdong, China, nylon parts are machined to tolerances of ±0.005 mm on 3-axis and 4-axis platforms and ±0.003 mm on 5-axis centres, with Coordinate Measuring Machine (CMM) inspection reports supplied with every tight-tolerance order. Nylon's outstanding toughness, impact resistance, and fatigue strength make it the correct engineering choice for components that must survive shock loads, vibration, and repeated flex cycles — properties where Delrin (POM) and PEEK are less competitive. Weldo CNC holds ISO 9001:2015 certification, with prototype delivery in 1–5 business days and small-batch production in 5–15 business days.

Engineers specify nylon for structural gears, bearing cages, bracket structures, fuel system components, and sliding mechanical assemblies that require toughness over dimensional precision. Nylon's tensile strength (70–85 MPa dry), elongation at break (20–50%), and excellent fatigue resistance allow it to absorb impact and cyclic loading that would crack Delrin or require metal at higher cost and weight. The self-lubricating property of nylon (especially PA6) makes it suitable for bushings, rollers, and gear teeth running with minimal lubrication.

Nylon's key limitation for precision machining is moisture absorption. PA6 absorbs 2.5–3.5% moisture by weight in humid conditions; PA66 absorbs slightly less; PA12 absorbs the least (approximately 1.5%). As nylon absorbs moisture, it swells — dimensions increase measurably, and the absorbed moisture also reduces stiffness. Parts machined dry and measured dry may be outside tolerance once they have equilibrated to ambient humidity. For tight-tolerance nylon work, Weldo CNC machines from pre-conditioned or pre-dried stock and specifies measurement at the equilibrated state relevant to the application environment.

Weldo CNC has machined nylon for automotive drivetrain components, industrial machinery gears, food processing equipment parts, and marine hardware since 2012. These are applications where dimensional failure — a gear bore that swells tight on the shaft in humid conditions — has direct operational consequences.

## Section 4 — Nylon Grades

Weldo CNC machines PA6, PA66, PA12, and glass-filled nylon. Grade selection is driven by toughness requirements, dimensional stability needs, moisture sensitivity, and temperature range.

| Grade / Variant | Key Difference | Max Continuous Temp | Best For |
|---|---|---|---|
| PA6 (Nylon 6) | Highest toughness and impact resistance, highest moisture absorption | 80–100 °C | Gears, bearing cages, impact-loaded structural parts |
| PA66 (Nylon 66) | Higher stiffness and temperature resistance than PA6, slightly less tough | 100–120 °C | Automotive structural brackets, higher-temp mechanical parts |
| PA12 (Nylon 12) | Lowest moisture absorption (~1.5%), best dimensional stability, reduced mechanical properties vs PA66 | 80–100 °C | Precision parts where dimensional stability in humidity is required |
| Glass-filled nylon (PA66-GF30) | 30% glass fibre — significantly higher stiffness, lower CTE, reduced toughness | 120–140 °C | Structural housings, high-stiffness brackets, under-bonnet automotive |

> **Moisture note:** PA6 and PA66 absorb moisture significantly and swell. For tight-tolerance parts, specify PA12 or glass-filled nylon where dimensional stability in service conditions is critical. Always specify the moisture conditioning state for inspection — "dry-as-machined" or "conditioned to 50% RH equilibrium" are different dimensions.

## Section 5 — Nylon or Alternative Material — Which Fits Your Part?

**Choose Nylon When:**
- Impact toughness and fatigue resistance are the primary mechanical requirements
- The part will see shock loading, vibration, or repeated flex cycles that would crack Delrin
- Sliding wear applications with intermittent or insufficient lubrication — nylon self-lubricates
- Moderate temperature resistance (PA66 to 120 °C; PA12 to 100 °C) and light chemical exposure
- Example parts: gear blanks for moderate-load gears, structural brackets, bearing retainer cages, cable ties and clips, food machinery sliding components

**When an Alternative May Win:**
- Tight dimensional tolerances with humidity variation → **Delrin (POM)** or **PA12** (Delrin absorbs minimal moisture; PA12 is the most dimensionally stable nylon)
- Operating temperature above 120 °C → **PEEK** (handles 260 °C continuous) or **PA66-GF30** (to 140 °C)
- Maximum stiffness and dimensional stability → **PEEK CF30** or **aluminium 6061** — nylon is too flexible for highly loaded structural applications
- Extreme chemical resistance → **PEEK** or **PTFE** — nylon has poor resistance to strong acids and some solvents
- Optical clarity required → **Polycarbonate (PC)** or **Acrylic (PMMA)** — nylon is opaque

*If the application sits on the boundary between nylon and Delrin or nylon and PEEK, send the STEP file with operating conditions, humidity environment, and load data — the right material will be confirmed before quoting.*

## Section 10 — Nylon Machining Design Guidelines

| Feature | Recommendation | Why It Matters |
|---|---|---|
| Pre-machining drying | Dry nylon stock at 80 °C for 4–8 hrs before machining tight-tolerance parts | Wet nylon cuts differently from dry nylon — moisture in the stock causes chip tearing and dimensional variation |
| Wall thickness | Minimum 1.5 mm; 2.0 mm+ for load-bearing walls | Nylon's lower stiffness vs Delrin means thin walls deflect more under cutting forces |
| Internal corners | Minimum radius 0.5 mm; match end mill radius | Zero-radius corners require EDM and create stress concentrations in a material already prone to stress relaxation |
| Tooling | Sharp, polished, single-flute or two-flute carbide | Dull tools generate heat that smears nylon and causes gumming; nylon melts at 215–265 °C depending on grade |
| Coolant | Air blast preferred — avoid flood coolant on PA6/PA66 as moisture absorption during machining affects dimensions | Flood cooling adds moisture to nylon stock mid-operation, causing dimensional shift on precision features |
| Bore tolerances | Machine nylon bores in the dry-machined state; specify the conditioning state for the dimension on the drawing | Bore may be correct dry but outside tolerance after moisture conditioning — must align measurement condition with service condition |
| Thread design | Coarse threads preferred — fine threads in nylon strip under torque; use helicoil inserts for high-torque assembly | Nylon threads creep under sustained load — inserts distribute load across a larger area |
| Moisture allowance | For parts assembled in metal housings: design clearance to account for nylon swell from dry-machined to conditioned state | Nylon bored to H7 fit dry may become an interference fit when conditioned at 65% RH |
| Gear design | Specify tooth surface finish Ra 0.8 µm; wider face width vs Delrin for equivalent load capacity | Nylon is tougher but more flexible than Delrin — wider face compensates for reduced load per unit area |
| Glass-filled nylon | Reduce cutting speed 20–30% vs unfilled nylon; use harder carbide grade — GF30 is abrasive | Glass fibre rapidly abrades standard carbide tooling; filled nylon tool life is significantly shorter than unfilled |

**Most common DFM failure — tight-tolerance nylon bores in humid environments:** PA6 and PA66 bores machined to H7 dry can become H6 or tighter interference after 24–48 hours of moisture conditioning at 50–65% relative humidity. This is the most common cause of assembly failure on nylon parts made to metal-equivalent tolerances. The fix is simple: specify the measurement condition on the drawing ("measure dry-as-machined" or "measure at 50% RH conditioned"), design clearance to account for swell, or switch to PA12 for the most dimensional stability in humid environments.

**DFM review for nylon covers moisture first:** Weldo CNC flags moisture-related dimensional risk at Design for Manufacturability (DFM) review before cutting — specifically whether the specified bore tolerance is achievable and maintainable given the service humidity environment. Grade selection (PA6, PA66, PA12, GF30) is confirmed against the application before quoting.

## Section 15 — FAQ

**Q1: What tolerances can Weldo CNC hold on nylon?**
Standard tolerances on nylon milling are ±0.005 mm, verified by CMM. ISO 2768-c is the baseline for plastics. The key constraint is moisture: tolerances specified dry-as-machined will change as the part conditions to ambient humidity — by up to 0.1–0.3 mm per 100 mm length for PA6 in humid environments. Specify the conditioning state for inspection dimensions on the drawing; Weldo CNC confirms measurement conditions at quoting.

**Q2: What is nylon CNC machining and how does it differ from machining metals?**
Nylon CNC machining removes material from nylon (polyamide) stock using CNC milling, turning, and drilling. Compared to metals, nylon requires sharp, polished tooling with positive rake angles and lower cutting speeds to prevent heat-related melting and gumming. Flood coolant is avoided on PA6 and PA66 — the moisture absorbed during machining can shift dimensions on tight-tolerance features. Air blast is the preferred cooling method. Nylon is significantly softer than engineering metals — cutting forces are low, but the material's flexibility increases deflection risk on thin walls and deep pockets.

**Q3: What nylon grades are available?**
Weldo CNC machines PA6 (Nylon 6 — highest toughness), PA66 (Nylon 66 — higher temperature, slightly less tough), PA12 (Nylon 12 — lowest moisture absorption, best dimensional stability), and glass-filled nylon (PA66-GF30 — highest stiffness and temperature resistance). Specify grade on the drawing; "nylon" without grade designation defaults to PA6. PA12 is recommended for tight-tolerance applications in humid environments.

**Q4: What are the lead times for nylon parts?**
Prototype delivery is 1–5 business days from drawing approval. Pre-machining drying (4–8 hours at 80 °C) adds to the schedule for tight-tolerance parts requiring dry-as-machined dimensions — confirm at quoting. Small-batch production is 5–15 business days. Quote turnaround is 24 hours.

**Q5: Is there a minimum order quantity for nylon machining?**
No minimum order quantity. Single prototype nylon parts are accepted with the same quality standards and CMM inspection as production runs. Nylon's machinability and low material cost make single-part orders economical.

**Q6: What file formats does Weldo CNC accept for nylon parts?**
STEP (.stp, .step) is the preferred format. IGES (.igs), Parasolid (.x_t), and SolidWorks (.sldprt) files are also accepted. 2D drawings should be supplied as PDF to confirm tolerances, surface finish callouts, conditioning state for dimensions, and nylon grade — all critical inputs for accurate quoting of nylon parts.

**Q7: What drives the cost of nylon CNC machining?**
Nylon raw material is inexpensive relative to engineering metals. Machining cost is driven by part complexity, number of setups, and grade: glass-filled PA66-GF30 has higher tooling cost than unfilled nylon due to fibre abrasion. Tight tolerances requiring full CMM inspection add inspection time. Pre-machining drying and dimensional measurement at specified conditioning state add process steps on tight-tolerance programmes. Providing grade, conditioning state, and dimensional requirements at quoting ensures accurate pricing.

**Q8: When should I choose nylon over Delrin or PEEK?**
Nylon is the correct choice when impact toughness and fatigue resistance are the primary requirements — nylon absorbs more shock and vibration than Delrin without cracking. Delrin is preferred when dimensional stability in humid environments matters more than toughness: Delrin absorbs minimal moisture and holds tighter tolerances in service. PEEK is required when temperature exceeds 120 °C, chemical resistance to strong acids or solvents is needed, or sterilisation compatibility is required. Nylon is typically less expensive than PEEK and tougher (but less dimensionally stable) than Delrin.

**Q9: How does moisture absorption affect nylon part dimensions in service?**
PA6 can absorb 2.5–3.5% moisture by weight in humid conditions, causing linear dimensional growth of 0.1–0.3 mm per 100 mm in highly humid environments. PA66 absorbs slightly less; PA12 approximately 1.5% — the most dimensionally stable of the common nylon grades. For parts assembled with metal counterparts in a fixed housing, design clearance to account for nylon swell from dry-machined to service-conditioned state. Weldo CNC can machine to pre-conditioned dimensions if the service conditioning state is specified at quoting. For applications where moisture-related swell is unacceptable, switch to PA12 or glass-filled nylon.

**Q10: Is nylon suitable for food contact applications?**
Food-grade nylon (PA6 or PA12 in natural unfilled form, without pigments or additives) is available for food-contact applications complying with FDA 21 CFR 177.1500 and EU 10/2011. PA12 is preferred for food-contact applications requiring dimensional stability in wet or humid processing environments. Specify "food-grade nylon" or "natural PA12" on the drawing and at quoting — Weldo CNC sources compliant material and supplies the food-contact certificate with every food-grade nylon order.
