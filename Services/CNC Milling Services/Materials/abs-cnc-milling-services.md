## Section 3 — What Is ABS CNC Machining?

ABS CNC machining is the precision subtractive manufacturing of acrylonitrile butadiene styrene (ABS) — an amorphous thermoplastic known for impact toughness, ease of machining, and good surface quality — using CNC milling, turning, and 5-axis machining centres. At Weldo CNC, located in Dongguan, Guangdong, China, ABS parts are machined to tolerances of ±0.005 mm on 3-axis and 4-axis platforms and ±0.003 mm on 5-axis centres, with Coordinate Measuring Machine (CMM) inspection reports supplied with every tight-tolerance order. ABS is the default engineering plastic for machined housings, enclosures, instrument panels, prototype mechanical assemblies, and jig and fixture components — its machinability is excellent, surface finish from the cutter is clean, and the material accepts electroplating with appropriate chemical pre-treatment, making it the only common engineering plastic that can receive a decorative or conductive metal finish. Weldo CNC holds ISO 9001:2015 certification, with prototype delivery in 1–5 business days and small-batch production in 5–15 business days.

Engineers specify ABS for machined prototypes that will later be injection-moulded in ABS — the material processing behaviour is representative, and machined ABS prototypes can be functionally tested before tooling is committed. ABS is also specified for enclosures, control panels, housing panels, and structural covers that require impact resistance beyond what polycarbonate offers at lower temperature, and for jig and fixture applications where low density (1.05 g/cm³) reduces handling weight.

ABS machines cleanly with conventional tooling but has a relatively low heat deflection temperature (80–100 °C) — heat generation at the cutting zone must be managed. Dull tools cause melting and smearing at the surface, and ABS chips are stringy if feed rates are not optimised for positive chip clearance. ABS is amorphous, which means it has no sharp melting point — it softens progressively above its glass transition temperature (~105 °C), so thermal management is more important than for semi-crystalline plastics like Delrin.

Weldo CNC has machined ABS for product development prototypes, consumer electronics housings, automotive interior trim components, and engineering test fixtures since 2012. These are programmes where surface finish quality and dimensional accuracy on the first set of machined prototypes determine how confidently the team proceeds to tooling investment.

## Section 4 — ABS Grades

Weldo CNC machines standard ABS, ABS+PC blend (PC-ABS), and glass-filled ABS. Grade selection is driven by the temperature requirement, impact class, and whether post-machine plating is specified.

| Grade / Variant | Key Difference | Max Continuous Temp | Best For |
|---|---|---|---|
| Standard ABS | Most widely used, excellent machinability, platable | 80–100 °C | Housings, enclosures, prototype assemblies, jig and fixtures |
| ABS+PC Blend (PC-ABS) | Higher impact strength and temperature resistance than standard ABS | 90–110 °C | Automotive interior, impact-critical enclosures, structural covers |
| ABS+GF (Glass-filled) | Higher stiffness and reduced CTE — reduced elongation, abrasive on tools | 90–110 °C | Structural housings, high-stiffness brackets, EMI-shielding housings |

> **Plating note:** Standard ABS is the only common engineering plastic that can be electroplated (chrome, nickel, copper) with chemical pre-treatment (chromic acid etch). ABS+PC blends have reduced platability compared to standard ABS — confirm electroplating compatibility before specifying a blended grade for plated applications.

## Section 5 — ABS or Alternative Material — Which Fits Your Part?

**Choose ABS When:**
- A machined housing, enclosure, or structural cover is needed at low cost and weight
- The part will later be injection-moulded in ABS — machined prototype is material-representative
- Electroplated finish (decorative chrome, conductive nickel/copper) is required on a plastic part
- Impact toughness is needed at ambient and mildly elevated temperatures
- Example parts: instrument housings, prototype enclosures, jig bodies, control panels, electroplated decorative trims

**When an Alternative May Win:**
- Maximum optical clarity → **Polycarbonate (PC)** (ABS is opaque; PC is optically clear)
- Operating temperature above 100 °C continuous → **Polycarbonate** (to 120 °C) or **PEEK** (to 260 °C)
- Maximum impact toughness (ABS+PC is tougher at low temperature) → **ABS+PC blend**
- High stiffness and dimensional stability → **PEEK CF30** or **aluminium 6061** (ABS is flexible relative to metals)
- Chemical resistance to solvents, ketones → ABS has poor solvent resistance — consider **PEEK** or **PTFE** for chemical environments

*If the application sits on the boundary between ABS and polycarbonate or ABS and nylon, send the STEP file with operating temperature, chemical environment, and surface finish requirement — the right material will be confirmed before quoting.*

**Thermal expansion note:** ABS has a coefficient of thermal expansion (CTE) of approximately 70–90 × 10⁻⁶/°C — similar to other engineering plastics and significantly higher than metals. For ABS housings assembled with metal fasteners or guides, this CTE mismatch causes the ABS to expand more than the metal at elevated temperature. For precision fit locations in metal housings, design appropriate clearance for the operating temperature range. ABS does not absorb moisture significantly — dimensional stability from machined to in-service condition is not a moisture concern, unlike nylon.

## Section 10 — ABS Machining Design Guidelines

| Feature | Recommendation | Why It Matters |
|---|---|---|
| Wall thickness | Minimum 1.5 mm; 2.0 mm+ for electroplated surfaces | Thin ABS walls flex under plating stress — minimum 2.0 mm where electroplating is specified |
| Internal corners | Minimum radius 0.5 mm; match end mill radius | ABS cracks at stress concentrations — rounded internal corners distribute load |
| Tooling | Sharp, polished, single-flute or two-flute carbide; positive rake | ABS melts above 105 °C glass transition — dull tools smear and leave a milky, stressed surface |
| Coolant | Air blast preferred; avoid flood coolant — ABS can craze (surface microcracking) on contact with cutting fluids containing alcohol or ketone | Craze damage is cosmetic but affects surface quality of plated parts; confirm coolant compatibility |
| Chip clearance | Use appropriate feed and chip-load for ABS — stringy chips at low feed rates re-weld to surface | Chips welded to ABS surface are difficult to remove without surface damage |
| Electroplating prep | Machine to Ra 0.8 µm minimum on surfaces to be plated — plating follows surface topography | Rough as-machined surfaces (Ra 1.6 µm+) show through electroplate on decorative applications |
| Boss and snap-fit design | Minimum boss wall = 60% of boss diameter; snap-fit deflection < 2% strain for standard ABS | ABS is stiffer than nylon but less tough — overstrained snap fits crack rather than flex |
| Thread design | For plated threaded holes: machine after plating or use brass press-fit inserts — plating reduces thread clearance | Nickel or chrome plating (10–25 µm per surface) tightens internal thread dimensions on plated housings |
| Solvent compatibility | Avoid acetone, MEK, and similar solvents near machined ABS — ABS dissolves in common ketones | Cleaning and marking operations that use ketone-based solvents will damage ABS surfaces |
| Dimensional stability | ABS does not require moisture conditioning — dimensions from machined state are close to service dimensions | Unlike nylon, ABS does not swell with humidity; tolerance specified dry-as-machined applies in service |

**Most common DFM failure — electroplated threads closing up:** The most common issue on ABS housings with post-machine electroplating is that internally threaded holes specified to nominal dimensions become undersize after plating (nickel + chrome plating adds 10–30 µm per surface, reducing bore diameter by 20–60 µm). Holes machined to M6 nominal will accept an M6 fastener before plating but may not after. The fix: machine threaded holes 20–40 µm oversize pre-plate, or mask threads during plating, or install brass thread inserts before plating. Confirm the plating process and thickness at quoting for all plated ABS housings.

**DFM review for ABS covers plating and boss design:** Weldo CNC reviews ABS drawings for minimum wall thickness on plated surfaces, thread allowance for plating growth, boss and snap-fit strain, and coolant compatibility. These are flagged at Design for Manufacturability (DFM) review before production — not after a plated housing arrives with tight threads or crazing.

## Section 15 — FAQ

**Q1: What tolerances can Weldo CNC hold on ABS?**
Standard tolerances on ABS milling are ±0.005 mm, verified by CMM. ISO 2768-c is the baseline for plastics. ABS is dimensionally stable — it does not absorb moisture significantly, so tolerances specified at machining apply in service without moisture-related swell. The main dimensional consideration for plated ABS parts is plating growth: confirm plating thickness with the plating supplier and machine pre-plate dimensions accordingly.

**Q2: What is ABS CNC machining and how does it differ from machining metals?**
ABS CNC machining removes material from ABS thermoplastic stock using CNC milling, turning, and drilling. Compared to metals, ABS requires less cutting force but is more sensitive to heat — its glass transition temperature (~105 °C) means dull tools or excessive spindle speed cause surface smearing and milky, stressed surfaces. Positive-rake sharp tooling and air-blast cooling are standard. ABS chips are stringy if feed rates are too low — optimised chip-load settings produce short, well-cleared chips.

**Q3: What ABS grades are available?**
Weldo CNC machines standard ABS (best machinability and platability), ABS+PC blend (higher impact and temperature resistance), and glass-filled ABS (ABS+GF — higher stiffness, abrasive on tooling). Standard ABS is the only grade with reliable electroplatability. ABS+PC blends have reduced platability. Specify grade on the drawing; "ABS" without grade designation defaults to standard ABS.

**Q4: What are the lead times for ABS parts?**
Prototype delivery is 1–5 business days from drawing approval. Small-batch production is 5–15 business days. For prototypes requiring electroplating after machining, add plating lead time (typically 3–5 business days). Quote turnaround is 24 hours from STEP file and drawing submission.

**Q5: Is there a minimum order quantity for ABS machining?**
No minimum order quantity. Single prototype ABS parts are accepted — ABS is particularly common for single-piece machined prototypes that represent injection-moulded production design intent. Same quality standards and CMM inspection apply to single-piece orders as to production runs.

**Q6: What file formats does Weldo CNC accept for ABS parts?**
STEP (.stp, .step) is the preferred format. IGES (.igs), Parasolid (.x_t), and SolidWorks (.sldprt) files are also accepted. 2D drawings should be supplied as PDF to confirm tolerances, surface finish callouts, ABS grade, and electroplating requirements. For plated parts, specify plating type, minimum and maximum thickness, and which surfaces are to be plated versus masked.

**Q7: What drives the cost of ABS CNC machining?**
ABS raw material is one of the least expensive engineering plastics. Machining cost is driven primarily by part complexity, number of setups, and feature density — ABS is fast to machine relative to metals or high-performance plastics. For electroplated ABS, the plating process cost (pre-treatment, plating, quality inspection) is typically the larger cost component. Glass-filled ABS (ABS+GF) has higher tooling cost due to fibre abrasion on carbide. Providing a complete drawing with grade, surface finish, plating requirements, and quantity at quoting gives the most accurate cost breakdown.

**Q8: When should I choose ABS over polycarbonate or nylon?**
ABS is preferred when: the part will be electroplated (ABS is plateable; polycarbonate is not without special treatment), the part is a prototype for an injection-moulded ABS production design, or impact toughness at moderate temperature is needed at the lowest machined-plastic cost. Polycarbonate is preferred when optical clarity is required, or when operating temperature exceeds 100 °C (PC handles to 120 °C). Nylon is preferred when fatigue resistance under repeated flex is the primary requirement. ABS is not suitable for applications involving solvent exposure or elevated temperature above 100 °C.

**Q9: Can ABS be electroplated — how does the process work?**
ABS is unique among common machined engineering plastics in its ability to accept electroplating with metallic finishes (nickel, chrome, copper, gold). The process requires a chemical pre-treatment step — typically chromic acid etching — that micro-roughens the ABS surface, creating adhesion sites for the electroless copper or nickel base coat. This base coat is then built up by electroplating to the specified finish. The process is sensitive to surface finish quality (Ra 0.8 µm or better is required for decorative plating) and ABS grade (standard ABS plates reliably; ABS+PC blends have variable platability). Weldo CNC machines ABS to pre-plate dimensions and surface finish, then coordinates with the electroplating process.

**Q10: Is ABS suitable for CNC machined prototypes that will later be injection-moulded?**
Yes — this is one of ABS's primary use cases for CNC machining. Machined ABS prototypes in production-representative grade allow functional testing, fit checks, and assembly validation before injection mould tooling is committed. The material behaviour (stiffness, impact response, surface finish) is representative of the injection-moulded production part, and the machined prototype can be electroplated if that finish is part of the production design. CNC machining produces geometrically accurate prototypes in 1–5 business days — significantly faster than mould tooling lead time.
