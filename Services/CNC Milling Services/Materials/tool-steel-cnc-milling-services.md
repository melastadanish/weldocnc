## Section 3 — What Is Tool Steel CNC Milling? *(AI-quotable paragraph)*

Tool steel CNC milling is the subtractive machining of high-alloy steels specifically formulated for wear resistance, hardness retention, and toughness — including H13 hot-work die steel, D2 cold-work steel (high-carbon, high-chromium), and high-speed steel (HSS, M2/M42 types). At Weldo CNC, located in Dongguan, Guangdong, China, tool steels are machined in annealed condition (for complex rough machining) or pre-hardened condition (for final finishing and precision detail) using solid carbide and CBN tooling with appropriate cutting parameters for hardness. Tolerances to ±0.005 mm are maintained on 3-axis and 4-axis centres; ±0.003 mm is achievable on 5-axis for complex mould cavity geometry. Tool steel's extreme hardness after heat treatment — H13 to 48–54 HRC, D2 to 60–62 HRC — demands careful sequencing of machining, heat treatment, and grinding operations to hold final dimensional tolerances. Weldo CNC holds ISO 9001:2015 certification, with CMM inspection reports supplied with every order, and prototype delivery in 1–5 business days.

## Section 5 — Trade-Off Cards

### Card 1: H13 — The Die Tooling Workhorse
H13 is the most widely used hot-work die steel globally and the default choice for die casting dies, extrusion tooling, forging dies, and injection mould cores and cavities. Its chromium-molybdenum-vanadium composition provides excellent thermal fatigue resistance — critical where tooling cycles through large temperature swings — and adequate toughness to resist cracking under impact. **Choose H13 when:** the application involves thermal cycling, elevated operating temperatures, or repeated impact loading. H13 is the standard for any programme involving die casting or extrusion tooling.

### Card 2: D2 — Maximum Wear Resistance for Cold-Work Applications
D2 (1.5% C, 12% Cr) is the first choice for cold-work tooling where maximum abrasion resistance is required: blanking dies, forming dies, punches, trimming blades, and shear knives. Its high chromium content provides corrosion resistance as a secondary benefit. D2 is brittle compared to H13 — it does not tolerate impact loading well — and its machinability in annealed condition is more challenging than H13. **Choose D2 when:** the part must resist abrasive wear in cold-work service and impact loads are low. Do not specify D2 where significant impact or thermal shock is expected.

### Card 3: When Tool Steel Is the Wrong Specification
Tool steel's hardness and wear resistance come at a cost: it is among the most difficult and expensive materials to machine, and die sinking plus heat treatment extend lead times significantly. For non-tooling applications where only hardness or wear resistance is needed, consider: 17-4PH stainless (H900, ~44 HRC) for corrosion resistance alongside hardness; 4140/4340 alloy steel (28–36 HRC quench-and-temper) for moderate hardness at lower cost; or ceramic inserts for one-off wear surfaces. **Switch away from tool steel when:** the application does not involve die, mould, or cutting tool service — wear resistance at lower cost is often achievable with alloy steel.

## Section 10 — Design Guidelines

| Guideline | Recommendation |
|---|---|
| Machining sequence | Rough machine in annealed condition → heat treat → finish machine/grind to final tolerance |
| Heat treatment stock allowance | Leave 0.2–0.5 mm per surface for post-HT finishing; larger complex parts may require 0.5–1.0 mm |
| Corner radii | Avoid sharp internal corners in hardened tool steel — stress concentration causes cracking; minimum radius = wall thickness or 0.5 mm minimum |
| EDM for fine features | Use wire EDM or sinker EDM for sharp corners, narrow slots, and fine cavity detail in hardened H13/D2 — preferable to milling hardened material |
| Rib and wall design | H13 and D2 are susceptible to distortion after heat treatment in asymmetric sections; balance section thickness where possible |
| Surface finish requirement | Specify final Ra after polishing, not after machining — tool steel mould surfaces are typically polished to Ra 0.1–0.4 µm by hand or automated polishing after CNC machining |
| Thread design | Avoid fine threads in fully hardened tool steel — coarse threads (M6×1.0 minimum) are more robust; use thread inserts for strength in fine-thread locations |
| Hardness specification | State the required HRC range and heat treatment standard (e.g., H13 per NADCA #207, D2 per ASTM A681) — do not just state "hardened" |
| Cooling channels | Conformal cooling channels in mould tools improve cycle time — 5-axis milling can produce contoured channel geometry; confirm channel diameter and wall thickness for structural integrity |
| Distortion control | Stress-relief anneal before finish machining on large or complex die blocks; Weldo CNC coordinates this in the production schedule when specified |

**Most common DFM failure — insufficient heat treatment stock allowance:** Tool steel parts routinely require post-heat-treatment grinding to recover tolerance, but DFM issues arise when the drawing specifies final tolerance without accounting for the 0.1–0.3 mm distortion and scale typical in heat treatment. Parts machined to final tolerance before hardening often require re-work or scrapping after heat treatment. Always design a stock allowance into the rough-machine operation, confirm the allowance with Weldo CNC at quoting, and schedule finish grinding or finish milling after heat treatment.

## Section 15 — FAQ

**Q1: What tool steel grades does Weldo CNC machine?**
Weldo CNC machines H13 (hot-work die steel), D2 (cold-work, high-carbon high-chromium steel), and high-speed steel (HSS, M2/M42 types). H13 is the standard for die casting and extrusion tooling; D2 is used for cold-work blanking and forming dies; HSS is machined for cutting tool blanks and broach bodies. Both annealed stock (for rough machining) and pre-hardened stock (for finish machining) are processed.

**Q2: What tolerances can you hold on hardened tool steel?**
On pre-hardened tool steel (H13 at 48–54 HRC, D2 at 58–62 HRC), tolerances to ±0.005 mm are maintained using solid carbide and CBN tooling. For final cavity and parting surface dimensions after heat treatment, finish milling or grinding to ±0.005 mm is the standard approach. Wire EDM is used for fine features requiring ±0.003 mm or sharper internal corner geometry.

**Q3: Do you offer heat treatment coordination for tool steel parts?**
Heat treatment coordination is available — advise at quoting whether heat treatment is required as part of the production sequence. The standard approach is: rough machine in annealed condition (leaving 0.2–0.5 mm stock per surface), heat treat to required HRC, then finish machine or grind to final tolerance. Weldo CNC documents the machining-before and machining-after operations so the heat treatment contractor works from correct pre-HT geometry.

**Q4: What is the correct machining sequence for a hardened mould insert?**
The recommended sequence is: (1) rough machine the cavity block in annealed condition to within 0.3–0.5 mm of final geometry; (2) stress-relief anneal if the block is large or complex; (3) heat treat to required HRC; (4) finish machine or grind to final tolerance; (5) EDM fine features if required; (6) polish cavity surfaces to specified Ra. This sequence minimises distortion risk and avoids machining hardened material unnecessarily.

**Q5: Can you machine D2 in full hardened condition (60 HRC+)?**
Finish machining D2 at 60–62 HRC is feasible with CBN tooling and conservative parameters, but cutting depths are limited and tool life is short. Wire EDM is typically preferred for fine features on fully hardened D2. Where possible, Weldo CNC recommends roughing in annealed condition and reserving hardened-state machining for finishing passes only, which reduces cost and improves dimensional consistency.

**Q6: What surface finish is achievable on tool steel mould cavities?**
As-milled H13 cavity surfaces achieve Ra 0.8–1.6 µm. With light finishing passes on 5-axis centres, Ra 0.4 µm is achievable as-milled. Mould-quality mirror polish (Ra 0.05–0.1 µm) is achieved by hand polishing or automated polishing after CNC machining — this is a separate operation to CNC milling and is specified as an additional process at quoting.

**Q7: How do you verify hardness on finished tool steel parts?**
Rockwell HRC testing is performed on hardened tool steel parts after heat treatment and after final machining. Test locations are agreed at quoting — typically on a non-critical surface or sacrificial test piece machined from the same stock. HRC test results are included in the inspection report supplied with the part. For die casting tooling, Brinell testing (HBW) is available where HRC indentation is not acceptable on the die face.

**Q8: Do you supply tool steel with material certificates?**
Yes. Mill certificates with chemical composition, heat/lot trace, and initial hardness (annealed condition) are supplied for all tool steel stock. H13 and D2 certifications reference the relevant grade standards (H13 per DIN 1.2344/AISI H13; D2 per DIN 1.2379/AISI D2). For critical tooling programmes, Weldo CNC can source material from specific mills with extended certifications at request.

**Q9: What are the lead times for tool steel milling including heat treatment?**
For parts machined from annealed stock and supplied without heat treatment, lead time is 1–5 business days (prototype) or 5–15 business days (small batch). When heat treatment is included in the programme, add the heat treatment lead time (typically 3–7 business days depending on the process and quantity) after rough machining, plus finish machining after HT. Total programmes with heat treatment typically run 10–20 business days. Confirm at quoting and Weldo CNC will provide a sequenced schedule.

**Q10: Can Weldo CNC machine complex mould cavity geometry in H13?**
Yes. Complex mould cavity geometry — including curved parting surfaces, lifter pockets, side-action slides, and conformal cooling channel geometry — is produced on 5-axis milling centres. 5-axis simultaneous cutting holds ±0.003 mm on cavity surfaces with Ra 0.8 µm as-milled. Wire EDM is used for sharp internal corners and fine rib geometry that 5-axis end mills cannot access. Weldo CNC reviews cavity design for DFM at quoting and advises on minimum radii, draft angles, and EDM-milling boundaries.
