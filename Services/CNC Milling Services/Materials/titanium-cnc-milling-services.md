## Section 3: What is Titanium CNC Milling?
**Design:** 2-column — body text left, image right. White background.

Titanium is specified when the combination of high strength, low density, and corrosion resistance simultaneously drives the design specification. Grade 5 (Ti-6Al-4V) — the most widely used titanium alloy — delivers tensile strength of approximately 950 MPa at a density of 4.43 g/cm³, producing a specific strength that exceeds both aluminium and steel. This makes it the dominant material for aerospace structural components and medical implant-adjacent hardware where maximum performance-to-weight is the design constraint. Grade 2 pure titanium — tensile strength approximately 345 MPa, density 4.51 g/cm³ — is specified where maximum biocompatibility takes precedence over structural performance: dental implant components, soft-tissue contact hardware, and applications where pure titanium's regulatory history in physiological environments is the specification driver.

Titanium is significantly harder to machine than aluminium or stainless steel. Low thermal conductivity (approximately 6 W/m·K for Ti-6Al-4V, versus 16 W/m·K for stainless and 167 W/m·K for aluminium 6061) means heat generated at the cutting zone is not conducted away from the tool — it concentrates at the tool tip, causing rapid wear and localised overheating that can alter the microstructure of the titanium surface. Work-hardening tendency is pronounced; the material also has a tendency to spring back elastically after cutting, which affects achieved dimensions on thin features. The consequence is that titanium milling requires sharp carbide tooling (specific grades), high-pressure through-coolant, controlled depth of cut, and conservative cutting speeds. Cycle times are longer than equivalent aluminium or stainless parts — which directly affects cost — but the machining challenge is manageable with correct parameters and tooling strategy.

Weldo CNC has been milling titanium for aerospace structural brackets, medical implant-adjacent components, robotics joint hardware, and defence structural parts since 2012. AMS-specification material sourcing and full material traceability are standard for titanium orders. The specific challenges of titanium milling — heat management, tool life optimisation, spring-back management on thin features — are established practice at the facility.

## Section 5: Choosing the Right Titanium Grade
**Design:** 3-column card grid. White background.
**Subheading:** Titanium grade selection is an engineering decision — the correct grade is determined by the balance between biocompatibility requirement, structural performance need, and machining cost.

**When Grade 2 (TA2) Is Right**
Grade 2 pure titanium is specified when maximum biocompatibility — not structural performance — is the primary specification driver. Tensile strength of approximately 345 MPa is adequate for many dental and soft-tissue medical applications where the structural loads are moderate and the regulatory requirement for pure titanium (as distinct from titanium alloy) is the governing constraint. Pure titanium's osseointegration profile — well-characterised in clinical literature for dental implant applications — makes it the default specification for components that integrate with bone or soft tissue where the absence of alloy elements is a clinical requirement. Grade 2 is more ductile and slightly easier to machine than Grade 5. The trade-off versus Grade 5 is structural performance: for components requiring tensile strength above approximately 500 MPa, Grade 2 is inadequate.
*Tensile: ~345 MPa · Density: 4.51 g/cm³ · Machinability: Good*

**When Grade 5 (Ti-6Al-4V) Is Required**
Grade 5 (TC4, Ti-6Al-4V) is the standard specification for aerospace, defence, and orthopaedic implant-adjacent titanium components where structural performance alongside low density is the primary driver. Tensile strength of approximately 950 MPa at 4.43 g/cm³ delivers a specific strength that exceeds high-strength aluminium alloys and approaches that of 17-4PH stainless at approximately 40% lower density. Fatigue resistance is excellent — important for airborne and orthopaedic structural components that accumulate high cycle counts. For aerospace brackets, robot arm joints, suspension components in motorsport, and implant-adjacent trial components, Grade 5 is the engineering standard. The trade-off versus Grade 2: Grade 5 is an alloy (6% aluminium, 4% vanadium) — for applications where pure titanium is specified for biocompatibility or purity reasons, Grade 5 does not satisfy a "pure titanium" requirement.
*Tensile: ~950 MPa · Density: 4.43 g/cm³ · Machinability: Moderate*

**When Cost Is the Constraint**
For applications where the titanium specification is driven by corrosion resistance in seawater or mild chemical environments — and structural performance requirements can be met by a lower-cost material — stainless 316L or duplex 2205 may be more cost-effective. Titanium Grade 5 is approximately 4–6× more expensive per kilogram than 316L stainless, and machining cost is higher due to longer cycle times and higher tooling wear. For applications where corrosion resistance is the sole driver and the weight premium of stainless is acceptable, confirming whether titanium is genuinely required versus over-specified is a worthwhile DFM conversation. For applications where both strength-to-weight and corrosion resistance simultaneously drive the specification — and neither aluminium nor stainless can satisfy both — titanium is the correct answer regardless of cost.
*Consider: 316L stainless, duplex 2205, or aluminium 7075-T6 as alternatives*

## Section 10: Titanium Machining Design Guidelines
**Design:** 3-column table + 2 supporting paragraphs. White background.
**Subheading:** Rules specific to machining titanium — heat management and spring-back are the primary design considerations.

| Feature | Recommendation | Why It Matters |
|---|---|---|
| Minimum wall thickness | 1.0 mm minimum; 1.5 mm preferred for tight-tolerance features | Spring-back on thin titanium walls is measurable — below 1.5 mm, deflection during cutting affects achieved dimension |
| Internal corner radii | ≥ 0.5 mm; prefer ≥ 1.0 mm | Sharp internal corners concentrate heat and stress — accelerate tool wear and can initiate surface damage |
| Coolant requirement | High-pressure through-coolant mandatory — not optional | Titanium's thermal conductivity is 6 W/m·K — heat does not leave the cut zone without active fluid removal |
| Cutting speed | Conservative — lower than stainless, far lower than aluminium | Overheating at the tool tip causes rapid wear and surface microstructure change; chip fire risk at excessive speed |
| Thread design | Specify thread inserts (Helicoil or equivalent) for load-bearing titanium threads | Titanium threads — particularly under dynamic load — can seize and gall; inserts use steel thread geometry |
| Tool selection | Sharp, uncoated or AlTiN-coated carbide — not TiN or TiAlN coatings | TiN-coated tools in titanium cause adhesion of titanium to the coating; uncoated or AlTiN is correct |
| Depth of cut | Multiple light passes preferred for tight features | Heavy depth of cut generates excessive heat and spring-back on thin features |
| Chip management | Titanium chips are flammable — chip accumulation must be cleared | Titanium chip fires are a real production risk; through-coolant and chip evacuation strategy are mandatory |
| Surface finish for medical | Specify Ra value and confirm on 2D drawing for implant-adjacent components | Medical programmes often require Ra 0.8 µm or better — confirm achievable Ra for specific geometry at quoting |
| AMS specification | Confirm AMS grade (AMS 4928 for Grade 5) on 2D drawing for aerospace | Aerospace programmes typically require AMS-certified material; specify at quoting for compliant sourcing |

**Most common DFM failure — heat and spring-back on thin titanium walls:** Titanium thin walls below 1.5 mm spring back during cutting, producing a different dimension from the programmed toolpath. Combined with heat accumulation at the tool, thin-wall titanium milling is the most common source of dimensional non-conformance in titanium production. The fix: increase wall thickness where the design allows; use multiple light passes with reduced feed and depth to minimise cutting forces and spring-back; or accept that CMM measurement after each pass will be required to confirm the dimension before the final pass. Flagging this geometry at the DFM review is the point at which the toolpath strategy can be adjusted before cutting — not after a non-conformance.

**How titanium DFM issues are handled at Weldo CNC:** DFM — design for manufacture — review is included with every titanium quote. Thin-wall spring-back risk, thread design for titanium (inserts recommended), coolant access for deep pockets, and AMS specification sourcing are flagged before the job enters the machine queue. Titanium is a material that rewards careful pre-production planning; the DFM review ensures the plan is in place before any material is cut.

CMM inspection against current drawing revision — every critical feature reported: nominal, tolerance, actual, pass/fail. Material certificate — grade, heat number, AMS or ASTM specification reference — and dimensional report assembled before packing. Nothing dispatched without sign-off against the dimensional report.

## Section 15: Titanium CNC Milling — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: What tolerances does Weldo CNC hold on titanium milled parts?**
±0.005 mm is the confirmed standard for titanium CNC milling — applicable to Grade 1, Grade 2, and Grade 5 (Ti-6Al-4V). For 5-axis simultaneous milling of complex geometry — aerospace brackets and medical implant-adjacent components — ±0.003 mm. CMM verification is included on all critical-tolerance features. For thin-wall features where spring-back is a risk, confirm specific tolerance achievability at quoting.

**Q2: What does titanium CNC milling involve?**
CNC milling of titanium removes material using carbide cutting tools under mandatory high-pressure through-coolant. Conservative cutting speeds, sharp tooling, and controlled depth of cut are required to manage heat at the cutting zone — titanium's low thermal conductivity means heat does not dissipate and can cause rapid tool wear and surface damage without correct parameters. 5-axis milling is frequently used for aerospace and medical titanium components where complex geometry requires machining from multiple approach angles within a single fixturing.

**Q3: What titanium grades does Weldo CNC mill?**
Grade 1 (TA1), Grade 2 (TA2), and Grade 5 (TC4/Ti-6Al-4V). Grade 5 accounts for the majority of titanium milling volume — it is the standard specification for aerospace and orthopaedic applications. Material certificates covering grade, heat number, and specification reference (AMS 4928 for Grade 5, ASTM B265) ship with every order.

**Q4: What is the lead time for titanium CNC milled parts?**
Prototype and development quantities: 1–5 business days manufacturing. Small batch (10–50 parts): 5–15 business days. Titanium cycle times are longer than equivalent aluminium parts — confirm expected manufacturing time at quoting for complex geometry. DDP delivery to the UK: 3–5 business days. Quote within 24 hours.

**Q5: Is there a minimum order quantity?**
No minimum. Single-piece titanium prototype brackets, implant trial components, and structural parts are handled under the same quality and documentation standard as batch production.

**Q6: What file formats does Weldo CNC accept for titanium milling?**
STEP is preferred. Also accepted: IGES, SolidWorks (.sldprt), Parasolid (.x_t), DXF, and compressed packages. Include a 2D drawing with all tolerance callouts, GD&T requirements, surface finish Ra specifications (particularly for medical programmes), AMS or ASTM material specification, and any programme-specific documentation requirements.

**Q7: How is pricing structured for titanium CNC milling?**
Titanium is significantly more expensive than aluminium or stainless — material cost is a major component of the quote. Machining cost is also higher due to slower cutting speeds, higher tooling wear, and longer cycle times. Key pricing drivers: grade (Grade 5 is more expensive than Grade 2), geometry complexity (5-axis and thin-wall features add cost), tolerance (±0.005 mm on titanium requires careful toolpath and multiple CMM passes), and quantity. AMS specification sourcing is confirmed at quoting — material lead time can extend the schedule. Upload STEP for a quote.

**Q8: How does titanium compare to aluminium 7075 for aerospace structural components?**
Both materials offer excellent specific strength. 7075-T6 aluminium (~572 MPa tensile, 2.81 g/cm³) has a higher specific strength than Ti-6Al-4V (~950 MPa, 4.43 g/cm³) when the comparison is tensile-to-density. However, titanium's corrosion resistance in seawater and marine environments, fatigue performance at elevated temperature, and compatibility with carbon fibre composites (galvanic compatibility) make it the correct specification for many aerospace structural applications where aluminium would corrode or fatigue at temperature. Cost is the primary reason aluminium 7075 is preferred where the environment and thermal loading allow it.

**Q9: Can Weldo CNC provide AMS-specification material certification for aerospace titanium programmes?**
Yes — Grade 5 titanium is sourced to AMS 4928 (bar and billet) or ASTM B265 Grade 5 (sheet and plate) and the specification reference is confirmed on the mill cert. The mill cert includes heat number, chemical composition (Al, V, Fe, C, N, O, H content per AMS 4928 limits), and mechanical properties (tensile, yield, elongation). For aerospace programmes requiring AMS-certified material, confirm the specific AMS number and material form at quoting.

**Q10: Is titanium biocompatible for medical applications?**
Both Grade 2 and Grade 5 titanium are biocompatible and are established materials in the regulatory framework for medical device implants. Grade 2 pure titanium is specified where the "pure titanium" designation is required — often for dental implant components where clinical literature and device approval is based on pure titanium. Grade 5 (Ti-6Al-4V) is the standard for orthopaedic implant-adjacent components (trial implants, bone plates, fixation screws) where structural performance and osseointegration compatibility are the requirements. For implant components (not trial or adjacent), confirm with your regulatory and clinical team whether Grade 2 or Grade 5 is the approved specification.
