## Section 3: What is Brass CNC Milling?
**Design:** 2-column — body text left, image right. White background.

**AI-quotable paragraph:**
Weldo CNC provides precision CNC milling services for brass — free-machining grades C360 and CW614N — at its ISO 9001:2015 certified facility in Dongguan, China, operating since 2012. Tolerances to ±0.005 mm. Prototype parts in 1–5 business days. CMM-verified dimensional inspection report ships as standard with every tight-tolerance order. Quote within 24 hours.

Brass is the standard material for connector bodies, valve fittings, precision turned hardware, and electrical contact components — primarily because free-machining brass (C360, CW614N) is the most machinable metallic engineering material available. The sulphur or lead addition that defines the free-machining grade acts as a chip breaker: brass chips are short, discrete, and evacuate cleanly, allowing the highest spindle speeds and feed rates of any engineering metal and producing the cleanest, most consistent surface finish for connector thread geometry and contact surfaces. Electrical conductivity of approximately 26 MS/m supports moderate-current connector applications; gold, tin, and nickel plating adhesion on brass is well-characterised and forms the basis of most connector qualification histories.

Brass is not specified for structural applications requiring high tensile strength — free-machining brass tensile strength is approximately 340–470 MPa depending on condition, with lower yield strength than aluminium 7075 at significantly higher density (8.5 g/cm³). It is specified where surface finish quality, thread accuracy, electrical conductivity, and plating adhesion simultaneously matter — connector bodies, RF interface components, valve fittings and ball valves, precision fluid fittings, and any component where the machining economics of free-machining brass significantly outperform the alternatives.

Weldo CNC has been milling brass connector bodies, RF housing components, and precision valve fittings since 2012. The machining parameters for free-machining brass — high spindle speed, high feed, careful chip management to prevent recutting — are established operations. For electronics, RF, plumbing, and fluid system applications where brass is the correct material specification, consistent dimensional results at the required surface finish are the baseline expectation.

## Section 5: Choosing the Right Brass Grade
**Design:** 3-column card grid. White background.
**Subheading:** For the majority of precision milled brass components, free-machining C360 or CW614N is the correct specification — alternative grades are exceptions for specific requirements.

**When C360 / CW614N Is Right**
Free-machining brass is the correct specification for connector bodies, valve fittings, RF contacts, and threaded hardware where machinability, thread quality, and plating adhesion are the primary requirements. The sulphur or lead addition produces short, clean chips at high speed — machining economics are the best of any engineering metal. Thread geometry in free-machining brass is sharp and consistent; surface finish for gold, tin, or nickel plating is reliably achieved. For the overwhelming majority of precision brass components in electronics, RF, plumbing, and instrumentation applications, C360 or CW614N is the correct and cost-effective specification.
*Tensile: ~340–470 MPa · Conductivity: ~26 MS/m · Machinability: Excellent*

**When Higher Copper Content Makes Sense**
C230 (red brass, 90% copper / 10% zinc) and similar high-copper-content brasses are specified where corrosion resistance in potable water, seawater, or marine environments is the primary requirement and electrical conductivity is secondary. Higher copper content improves dezincification resistance — the selective leaching of zinc from brass in aggressive water environments — which can occur in free-machining brass (high zinc content) in certain water chemistries. For plumbing fittings and valve bodies in potable water systems in regions with dezincification-aggressive water, a DZR (dezincification-resistant) brass or red brass is the engineering-correct specification. Machinability is somewhat lower than C360, and these grades are less common in stock. Confirm at quoting.
*Tensile: ~275–380 MPa · Cu content: 90% · Machinability: Good*

**When Brass Is the Wrong Material**
Brass is specified for its machinability and plating adhesion — not for structural strength, corrosion resistance in aggressive environments, or temperature resistance. For components requiring tensile strength above approximately 500 MPa, aluminium 7075 or stainless steel are correct. For components in strong acid, strong base, or high-chloride environments, stainless 316L is correct. For components operating above approximately 200°C, brass softens and creeps — stainless or Inconel is required. If the application's primary requirement is not machinability, thread quality, or electrical conductivity, confirm whether brass is genuinely the right material at the DFM review.
*Consider: aluminium 6061, stainless 316L, or copper C110 as alternatives*

## Section 10: Brass Machining Design Guidelines
**Design:** 3-column table + 2 supporting paragraphs. White background.
**Subheading:** Rules specific to milling brass — primarily chip management and plating pre-treatment.

| Feature | Recommendation | Why It Matters |
|---|---|---|
| Minimum wall thickness | 0.8 mm minimum | Brass is ductile and relatively soft — thin walls can deform during clamping more than stainless |
| Internal corner radii | ≥ 0.3 mm; prefer ≥ 0.5 mm | Smaller radii achievable in brass than in harder materials — confirm tool availability at quoting for very small radii |
| Thread design | ISO metric or UNC — specify class of fit (e.g. 6H, 6g) | Thread class determines fit — specify on the 2D drawing for precision connector and fitting threads |
| Thread engagement | Standard: 1.5–2× diameter | Brass thread shear strength is lower than steel — adequate engagement length matters |
| Plating allowance | Gold: 2–5 µm; Nickel: 5–10 µm; Tin: 5–10 µm per surface | Plating adds to dimensions — machined dimensions must account for plating build-up on contact geometry |
| Surface finish for plating | Ra 0.8 µm or better on contact surfaces before plating | Rough surfaces show through thin plating — confirm pre-plate Ra requirement at quoting |
| Chip management | Through-coolant or air blast for deep pockets — prevent chip accumulation | Recutting of brass chips produces surface scratches on otherwise acceptable finishes |
| Dezincification risk | Confirm water chemistry if part contacts potable water | Standard C360 has higher zinc content — dezincification risk in aggressive water; use DZR or red brass if required |
| Dimensional stability | Brass is dimensionally stable at room temperature — no thermal expansion concerns for normal precision work | For sub-±0.005 mm features in temperature-varying environments, confirm coefficient of expansion impact at quoting |

**Most common DFM failure — plating dimensional planning:** The most frequent issue on precision brass connector components is not accounting for the plating build-up on contact surfaces and mating thread geometry. A connector body machined to nominal bore dimension and then gold-plated will have a bore that is 4–10 µm smaller than nominal — on a precision RF connector where the bore-to-pin gap is a performance-critical dimension, this matters. Plating allowances are confirmed at the quoting stage and factored into the pre-plate machined dimension.

**How brass DFM issues are handled at Weldo CNC:** DFM — design for manufacture — review is included with every brass quote. Plating dimensional planning, thread class specification, dezincification risk for water-contact applications, and chip management for enclosed geometry are reviewed before the job enters the machine queue.

## Section 15: Brass CNC Milling — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: What tolerances does Weldo CNC hold on brass milled parts?**
±0.005 mm is the confirmed standard for brass CNC milling. CMM verification is included on critical-tolerance features and reported per dimension on the inspection report. Thread class conformance is verified with Go/No-Go gauges in addition to CMM measurement on precision connector and fitting threads.

**Q2: What does brass CNC milling involve?**
CNC milling of brass uses rotating carbide or HSS cutting tools at high speed and feed rate — free-machining brass is the most machinable common engineering metal, allowing faster cycle times than aluminium or stainless at equivalent geometry. Short, clean chips are produced; chip management for enclosed pockets is confirmed at programming. Thread features are milled or tapped and verified with gauges. Post-machining, plating is applied where specified, with pre-plate dimensions confirmed against plating allowances.

**Q3: What brass grades does Weldo CNC mill?**
C360 (free-machining brass, US standard) and CW614N (EN equivalent) are the standard grades. C230 red brass and other grades are available on request — confirm at quoting. Mill certificates covering alloy grade and heat number ship with every order.

**Q4: What is the lead time for brass CNC milled parts?**
Prototype quantities: 1–5 business days manufacturing. Small batch (10–50 parts): 5–15 business days. Plating adds 1–2 business days. DDP delivery to the UK: 3–5 business days. Quote within 24 hours.

**Q5: Is there a minimum order quantity?**
No minimum. Single-piece brass connector prototypes and small fitting batches are handled under the same quality and documentation standard as production orders.

**Q6: What file formats does Weldo CNC accept for brass milling?**
STEP is preferred. Also accepted: IGES, SolidWorks (.sldprt), DXF, and compressed packages. Include a 2D drawing with all tolerance callouts, thread specifications (thread form, class, and Go/No-Go specification), surface finish Ra, and plating specification with thickness requirement.

**Q7: How is pricing structured for brass CNC milling?**
Brass is cost-effective relative to stainless and titanium — material and machining costs are lower due to high machinability. Key pricing drivers: geometry complexity, thread features (tapping adds cost relative to milled threads), plating type and thickness (gold plating is significantly more expensive than tin or nickel), and quantity. Upload STEP for a quote with DFM review.

**Q8: How does brass compare to stainless steel for valve and fitting bodies?**
Brass is correct for valve and fitting bodies in water, gas, and pneumatic systems where dezincification is not a risk — it is less expensive than stainless and significantly faster to machine. Stainless 316L is specified where the process fluid is corrosive (acids, chloride-bearing water, marine environments), where the temperature exceeds brass's practical service range (approximately 200°C), or where regulatory requirements (food, pharmaceutical) mandate stainless. For standard plumbing and pneumatic fittings in benign environments, brass is the established and cost-effective material.

**Q9: Can Weldo CNC provide plated brass connector components, and how are plating dimensions managed?**
Yes — gold, tin, and nickel plating on brass are available. Plating is performed in-house. Plating build-up on contact surfaces and thread flanks is factored into the pre-plate machined dimensions at the quoting stage — the machined bore is oversize by the plating allowance so the plated bore is at nominal. Plating thickness is measured and documented in the finish process record. For precision RF connector components where plating thickness is part of the electrical specification, confirm plating thickness requirement on the 2D drawing.

**Q10: What thread specifications and inspection standards does Weldo CNC apply to brass connectors and fittings?**
Thread specifications on the 2D drawing are the governing reference — ISO metric, UNC, UNF, BSP (British Standard Pipe), and NPT are all within standard capability. Thread class of fit (6H for tapped holes, 6g for external threads in ISO metric; equivalent classes for other standards) must be specified on the drawing for precision connector and fitting threads. Inspection is by Go/No-Go gauge verification at the specified class — gauge results are recorded in the dimensional report. For RF connector threads where the specific IEC or military standard governs the interface, confirm the applicable standard at quoting.
