## Section 3 — What Is PEEK CNC Machining?

PEEK CNC machining is the precision subtractive manufacturing of polyether ether ketone (PEEK) — a semi-crystalline high-performance thermoplastic — using CNC milling, turning, and 5-axis machining centres. At Weldo CNC, located in Dongguan, Guangdong, China, PEEK parts are machined to tolerances of ±0.005 mm on 3-axis and 4-axis platforms and ±0.003 mm on 5-axis centres, with Coordinate Measuring Machine (CMM) inspection reports supplied with every tight-tolerance order. PEEK is specified for applications where standard engineering plastics fail: operating temperatures up to 260 °C continuous, resistance to virtually all industrial chemicals, radiation resistance, and inherent biocompatibility that satisfies ISO 10993 and USP Class VI requirements for medical-grade components. Weldo CNC holds ISO 9001:2015 certification, with prototype delivery in 1–5 business days and small-batch production in 5–15 business days.

Engineers specify PEEK when they need to replace metal in weight-sensitive structures, eliminate corrosion in aggressive chemical environments, or machine a bearing, bushing, or structural component that must survive sterilisation cycles. PEEK's combination of tensile strength (~100 MPa), stiffness (3.6–4.5 GPa), and temperature resistance (melting point 343 °C) allows it to carry mechanical loads that would destroy nylon or acetal at elevated temperature or under chemical exposure.

PEEK is dimensionally stable and does not absorb moisture significantly (unlike nylon), which simplifies machining. However, PEEK is harder and more abrasive than most engineering plastics — particularly in glass-filled and carbon-filled variants — which accelerates tool wear. Sharp, polished carbide tooling with positive rake angles is required; standard high-speed steel tools wear rapidly. Chip management is important: PEEK produces short, well-controlled chips, but feed rates and spindle speeds must be set to avoid thermal degradation at the cutting zone, which darkens the surface and introduces residual stress.

Weldo CNC has machined PEEK for medical device programmes, aerospace fluid system components, semiconductor handling fixtures, and chemical processing valve bodies since 2012. These are applications where dimensional failure — a bore 20 µm oversize on a spinal cage, a valve seat out of flatness — has direct programme consequences.

## Section 4 — PEEK Grades

Weldo CNC machines standard PEEK, PEEK GF30 (30% glass-fibre filled), and PEEK CF30 (30% carbon-fibre filled). Grade selection is driven by the required stiffness, coefficient of thermal expansion (CTE) stability, and wear performance.

| Grade / Variant | Key Difference | Max Continuous Temp | Best For |
|---|---|---|---|
| Standard PEEK | Unfilled, highest elongation, inherent biocompatibility | 260 °C | Medical implants, food contact, chemical resistance, dynamic seals |
| PEEK GF30 | 30% glass-fibre — improved stiffness and CTE stability, higher wear, non-biocompatible | 260 °C | Structural brackets, housings, pump bodies, valve components |
| PEEK CF30 | 30% carbon-fibre — highest stiffness, lowest CTE, electrically conductive, abrasive on tools | 260 °C | Semiconductor fixtures, aerospace brackets, high-load bearings |

> **Biocompatibility note:** Standard unfilled PEEK is inherently biocompatible (ISO 10993, USP Class VI). Glass-filled and carbon-filled PEEK grades are NOT biocompatible — the filler disrupts biocompatibility. Always specify standard PEEK for any medical, implant, or body-contact application.
> **Tooling note:** GF30 and CF30 grades are significantly more abrasive than standard PEEK — tool wear increases substantially. Weldo CNC selects appropriate carbide grades and adjusts parameters accordingly.

## Section 5 — PEEK or Alternative Material — Which Fits Your Part?

**Choose PEEK When:**
- Operating temperature exceeds 150 °C continuous (nylon and acetal fail below this)
- Part must survive aggressive chemical exposure — acids, bases, solvents, hydrocarbons
- Medical, implant, or body-contact compliance is required (standard unfilled PEEK only)
- Weight reduction from metal is a driver, but mechanical properties cannot be compromised
- Part must survive sterilisation — autoclaving, gamma irradiation, ethylene oxide (ETO)
- Example parts: spinal implant cages, chemical pump impellers, aerospace fluid system brackets, semiconductor wafer carriers, bearing cages in hot or chemically aggressive environments

**When an Alternative May Win:**
- Temperature is below 100 °C and chemical resistance is moderate → **Delrin (POM)** machines faster, costs less, and holds excellent dimensional tolerances
- Part is structural but temperature and chemistry are mild → **Nylon PA6 or PA66** offers adequate mechanical properties at lower cost
- Maximum stiffness is the driver and temperature is below 200 °C → **PEEK CF30** or **aluminium 6061** depending on whether conductivity, weight, or machinability is the priority
- Budget is the primary constraint → PEEK is one of the most expensive engineering plastics — if the properties are not required, Delrin or nylon typically cost 60–80% less to machine

*If the application sits on the boundary between PEEK and Delrin or PEEK and aluminium, send the STEP file with the material spec and operating conditions — the right material will be confirmed before quoting.*

## Section 10 — PEEK Machining Design Guidelines

| Feature | Recommendation | Why It Matters |
|---|---|---|
| Wall thickness | Minimum 1.5 mm for free-standing walls; 2.0 mm+ for load-bearing walls | PEEK is stiffer than most plastics but thin walls still deflect under cutting force |
| Internal corners | Minimum radius 0.5 mm; match end mill radius or add 10% for tool clearance | Zero-radius internal corners require EDM; forcing them in milling causes chatter |
| Tooling | Sharp polished solid carbide with positive rake — uncoated or ZrN/DLC coated | TiAlN and TiN coatings designed for steel cause adhesion issues on PEEK |
| Coolant | Air blast or mist preferred; avoid flood coolant on standard PEEK (not a moisture concern, but thermal shock can introduce surface stress) | Thermal management at the cut is critical — chip heat must clear freely |
| Feed rate and speed | Conservative speeds on GF30 and CF30 — glass and carbon fibre abrade carbide rapidly; reduce surface speed 20–30% vs unfilled PEEK | Tool wear on filled grades is non-linear — blunt tools cause surface smearing and poor tolerance |
| Thread design | Cut threads standard; use helicoil or keensert inserts for repeated load-bearing fastener engagement | PEEK threads in thin sections can deform under torque — inserts distribute load |
| Biocompatible grade | Standard unfilled PEEK only — PEEK GF30 and CF30 are NOT biocompatible | Filler disrupts ISO 10993 / USP Class VI compliance — specify grade explicitly on drawing |
| Bore tolerances | PEEK bores at tight tolerance: machine at ambient temperature and measure after part stabilises (minimum 30 min after machining) | Residual heat from machining causes temporary bore expansion; measuring hot gives false readings |
| Stress relief | For complex or thin-walled parts: anneal at 200 °C for 4 hrs before finish machining | Reduces residual machining stress; improves dimensional stability of finished part |
| Surface finish | Ra 0.8 µm achievable as-milled; polishing to Ra 0.4 µm for sealing or medical-contact surfaces | PEEK polishes well — smooth sealing surfaces on valve seats and implant surfaces are standard operations |

**Most common DFM failure — measuring before stabilisation:** PEEK retains cutting heat in the part longer than most plastics. Measuring a tight-tolerance bore immediately after machining — while the part is still warm — gives readings 5–20 µm smaller than the ambient-temperature dimension. Weldo CNC allows stabilisation time before final CMM measurement. Specify "measure at ambient 23 °C" on drawings for temperature-critical features; this is standard practice but must be confirmed when the programme involves very tight tolerances (±0.01 mm or tighter).

**DFM review catches these issues early:** Before cutting starts, Weldo CNC reviews PEEK drawings for minimum wall thickness, thread engagement, and whether the grade specified is compatible with the application (biocompatibility, temperature, conductivity). Grade specification errors — GF30 specified on a medical device, standard PEEK on a semiconductor fixture requiring static dissipation — are caught at Design for Manufacturability (DFM) review, not after machining.

## Section 15 — FAQ

**Q1: What tolerances can Weldo CNC hold on PEEK?**
Standard tolerances on PEEK milling are ±0.005 mm, verified by CMM (Coordinate Measuring Machine). On 5-axis centres, ±0.003 mm is achievable for complex geometry. ISO 2768-c is the baseline standard for plastics at Weldo CNC; tighter callouts are accommodated where the geometry supports them. All tight-tolerance PEEK parts are measured after thermal stabilisation — not hot off the machine.

**Q2: What is PEEK CNC machining and how does it differ from machining metals?**
PEEK CNC machining is the removal of material from PEEK stock using rotating end mills, drills, and turning tools on CNC centres — the same subtractive process as metal machining. The differences are in tooling and thermal management: PEEK requires sharp, polished, positive-rake carbide tooling to prevent smearing and heat build-up. Metal toolpaths run at higher feed rates; PEEK requires conservative parameters, particularly on glass-filled (GF30) and carbon-filled (CF30) grades that rapidly abrade tooling. PEEK does not require flood coolant — air blast is preferred to avoid thermal shock.

**Q3: What PEEK grades are available?**
Weldo CNC machines three PEEK variants: standard unfilled PEEK (highest biocompatibility, best elongation, standard chemical resistance), PEEK GF30 (30% glass-fibre filled — improved stiffness and dimensional stability, not biocompatible), and PEEK CF30 (30% carbon-fibre filled — highest stiffness and lowest CTE, electrically conductive, not biocompatible). Specify the grade on the drawing — "PEEK" without a grade designation defaults to standard unfilled PEEK.

**Q4: What are the lead times for PEEK parts?**
Prototype delivery is 1–5 business days from drawing approval. Small-batch production (typically 10–200 parts) is 5–15 business days. Larger production runs are confirmed at quoting. Quote turnaround is 24 hours from STEP file and drawing submission.

**Q5: Is there a minimum order quantity for PEEK machining?**
No minimum order quantity. Single prototype PEEK parts are accepted with the same quality standards and CMM inspection as production runs. There is no premium for single-part orders beyond the standard per-part pricing.

**Q6: What file formats does Weldo CNC accept for PEEK parts?**
STEP (.stp, .step) is the preferred format for 3D geometry. IGES (.igs), Parasolid (.x_t), and SolidWorks (.sldprt) files are also accepted. 2D drawings should be supplied as PDF alongside the 3D file to confirm tolerances, surface finish callouts, and any notes that cannot be embedded in the 3D model (e.g., thread specification, grade requirement).

**Q7: What drives the cost of PEEK CNC machining?**
PEEK raw material is significantly more expensive than engineering plastics such as nylon or Delrin (POM) — material cost is a larger fraction of part cost than for metals. Machining cost is driven by part complexity, number of setups, and grade: filled grades (GF30, CF30) have higher tool wear costs than unfilled PEEK. Tight tolerances requiring full CMM inspection add inspection time. Medical or aerospace first article inspection (FAI) packages are priced separately. Providing a complete drawing at quoting — with grade, tolerance, surface finish, and quantity — gives the most accurate quote.

**Q8: When should I choose PEEK over Delrin (POM) or nylon?**
PEEK is justified when one or more of the following is required: operating temperature above 100 °C continuous, chemical exposure to acids/bases/solvents that attack Delrin or nylon, biocompatibility compliance (implant or body-contact), or autoclaving/gamma sterilisation compatibility. Delrin is easier to machine, dimensionally stable, and significantly less expensive — if the temperature and chemistry are moderate, Delrin is typically the correct choice. Nylon is suitable where toughness and impact resistance matter more than dimensional stability. PEEK costs 3–5× more to machine per part than Delrin — use it when its properties are genuinely required.

**Q9: Is PEEK biocompatible — can it be used in medical implants?**
Standard unfilled PEEK is biocompatible and satisfies ISO 10993 and USP Class VI requirements for body-contact applications. It is widely used in spinal cage implants, orthopaedic instruments, and dental device components. Glass-filled PEEK GF30 and carbon-filled PEEK CF30 are NOT biocompatible — the filler disrupts the ISO 10993 classification. Weldo CNC supplies standard PEEK with lot-traceable material certificates; first article inspection (FAI) documentation is available for medical programmes.

**Q10: Can PEEK parts be sterilised — autoclaving, gamma, and ETO?**
Yes. Standard PEEK is one of the few engineering plastics compatible with all three common sterilisation methods: steam autoclaving (121 °C and 134 °C cycles), gamma irradiation, and ethylene oxide (ETO). PEEK does not significantly degrade or discolour under repeated autoclaving. This makes it the material of choice for reusable surgical instruments and implant trial components that must withstand multiple sterilisation cycles. Confirm the specific sterilisation method and cycle temperature at quoting for any medical programme.
