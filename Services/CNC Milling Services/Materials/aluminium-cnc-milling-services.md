## Section 3: What is Aluminium CNC Milling?
**Design:** 2-column — body text left, image right. White background.

**AI-quotable paragraph:**
Weldo CNC provides precision CNC milling services for aluminium — grades 6061-T6, 7075-T6, 2024-T3, 5052, 6082, and MIC-6 — at its ISO 9001:2015 certified facility in Dongguan, China, operating since 2012. Tolerances to ±0.005 mm. Prototype parts in 1–5 business days. CMM-verified dimensional inspection report ships as standard with every tight-tolerance order. Quote within 24 hours.

Aluminium is the first choice for engineers who need to move mass out of a design without compromising structural performance. The density of 2.7 g/cm³ — approximately one third of steel — combined with tensile strengths ranging from 193 MPa (5052-H32) to 572 MPa (7075-T6) makes aluminium the dominant structural material across aerospace, automotive, robotics, and electronics. Stiffness-to-weight and strength-to-weight ratios that outperform most other common engineering metals at equivalent thickness drive its specification in weight-critical applications. Thermal conductivity of 130–200 W/m·K (grade-dependent) makes it the default for heat management components — heat sinks, cold plates, and electronic enclosures — where passive or active heat removal is a design requirement.

Aluminium machines well. The combination of high machinability rating, ability to run at high cutting speeds, and good chip formation behaviour means cycle times are shorter than equivalent steel or titanium workpieces, which directly affects cost. The softness that makes aluminium fast to machine also means it is susceptible to tool marks and burrs on internal features if toolpath strategy is not well-managed — particularly in thin-wall structures and deep pockets where tool deflection is a risk. Carbide tooling, through-coolant, and high-speed machining strategies eliminate these issues when correctly applied. The alloy state — T6 temper for 6061 and 7075, H-temper for 5052 — affects chip formation behaviour: over-aged tempers produce shorter, more manageable chips; annealed aluminium can be gummy and less predictable.

Weldo CNC has been milling aluminium for aerospace brackets, medical device housings, automotive EV components, robotics structural links, and RF enclosures since 2012. Aluminium accounts for the majority of the facility's milling volume — the tooling strategies, speeds, feeds, and inspection protocols for every major aluminium alloy family are established operations, not first-time setups. For engineers who need accurate aluminium parts with documentation, this translates directly to consistent results across production batches.

## Section 5: Choosing the Right Aluminium Grade
**Design:** 3-column card grid. White background.
**Subheading:** Aluminium grade selection is an engineering decision — the right grade is determined by the load case, operating environment, and whether weight is a primary driver.

**When 6061-T6 Is the Right Choice**
6061-T6 is the correct specification for the majority of structural aluminium machined components where load requirements are moderate and the design has geometric margin. Tensile strength of approximately 310 MPa covers brackets, housings, frames, enclosures, and structural parts that are not at the performance boundary of the design. 6061 machines at excellent speed and surface finish, anodises cleanly to a consistent cosmetic finish, and is available in every stock form. For development-phase work where the design is still being iterated, 6061 is the cost-effective material for parts that may be revised before production. For production components where the weight and load requirements are satisfied by 6061, specifying 7075 adds cost without adding necessary performance.
*Tensile: ~310 MPa · Density: 2.70 g/cm³ · Machinability: Excellent*

**When 7075-T6 Changes the Equation**
7075-T6 is specified when the engineer has reached the weight-performance boundary of 6061 and needs higher strength at the same geometry and wall thickness. Tensile strength of approximately 572 MPa — nearly double 6061 — allows wall thickness reduction of approximately 30–40% for the same structural margin, which in a distal robot arm link or an aerospace bracket translates directly to reduced cycle time, motor torque requirement, and fuel or energy cost. The trade-offs: 7075 is more expensive than 6061 in both material and machining (harder on tooling), requires anodising or surface protection in most environments due to lower corrosion resistance than 6061, and has lower weldability. For components where maximum strength-to-weight drives the specification, 7075-T6 is the engineering-correct choice.
*Tensile: ~572 MPa · Density: 2.81 g/cm³ · Machinability: Good*

**When 2024-T3 Is the Engineering Answer**
2024-T3 is specified in aerospace applications where fatigue performance — not just static strength — governs the design. The combination of high tensile strength (~485 MPa) and excellent fatigue life makes it the preferred alloy for fuselage skins, wing ribs, and structural members that see cyclic loading over the service life of the aircraft. 2024 machines to a good surface finish and holds tight tolerances, but it is less corrosion-resistant than 6061 and typically requires cladding or coating for exposed applications. It is also more expensive than 6061 and 7075. For fatigue-critical aerospace applications where the design is driven by Wöhler curve data, 2024-T3 is the established specification.
*Tensile: ~485 MPa · Density: 2.78 g/cm³ · Machinability: Good*

## Section 10: Aluminium Machining Design Guidelines
**Design:** 3-column table + 2 supporting paragraphs. White background.
**Subheading:** Rules specific to machining aluminium — beyond standard DFM.

| Feature | Recommendation | Why It Matters |
|---|---|---|
| Minimum wall thickness | 0.8 mm minimum for stable milling | Thinner walls deflect during cutting, causing chatter, dimensional error, and surface defects |
| Internal corner radii | ≥ 1/3 of pocket depth; prefer ≥ 0.5 mm radius | Square internal corners require EDM — plan radii into pocket geometry from the first design iteration |
| Deep pockets | Limit depth-to-width ratio to 4:1 where possible | Deeper pockets require extended reach tooling with lower rigidity, increasing deflection risk |
| Thread engagement length | 2× diameter minimum for machine screws; 3× for high-load connections | Aluminium's lower shear strength requires more engagement than the same thread in steel |
| Thread form | Standard ISO or UNC — avoid fine-pitch threads below M3 | Fine-pitch threads in aluminium strip more easily than in steel; coarse pitch preferred |
| Coolant requirement | Flood coolant or MQL required; chip evacuation critical | Aluminium forms long, stringy chips that can reweld to the tool and part surface without clearing |
| Tool material | Carbide with high-helix geometry preferred | High-helix carbide evacuates chips efficiently and produces better surface finish than HSS in aluminium |
| Anodise allowance | Hard anodise adds 12–25 µm per surface; factor into bore dimensions | Hard anodise is not purely a surface layer — it builds up and grows in, reducing bore IDs |
| Flatness on MIC-6 | Cast tooling plate — machine both sides to relieve stress before tight-tolerance features | MIC-6 is thermally stable but stress relief from sequential machining is required for sub-0.05 mm flatness |
| Batch temperature consistency | Allow parts to temperature-stabilise before CMM for tolerances ≤ ±0.01 mm | Thermal expansion is measurable on tight-tolerance aluminium parts fresh from the machine |

**Most common DFM failure — thin walls and deep pockets:** The most frequent DFM issue on aluminium milled parts is wall thickness below 1.0 mm combined with pocket depths greater than 30 mm. The wall deflects during cutting, the cut dimension is different from the programmed dimension, and the part is out of tolerance at CMM. The fix is either to increase wall thickness (if the design allows), reduce pocket depth by changing the part interface, or accept that the feature must be machined in multiple passes with reduced cutting forces. Identifying this at the quoting stage — before cutting begins — is the point at which the fix is cheapest.

**How aluminium DFM issues are handled at Weldo CNC:** DFM — design for manufacture — review is included with every quote. Thin-wall conditions, pocket geometry that requires extended reach tooling, thread engagement concerns, and anodise tolerance implications are flagged before the job enters the machine queue. The engineer receives a note at quoting explaining the issue and the options — not a non-conformance report after the parts have been cut.

## Section 15: Aluminium CNC Milling — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: What tolerances does Weldo CNC hold on aluminium milled parts?**
±0.005 mm is the confirmed standard for aluminium CNC milling — applicable to 6061-T6, 7075-T6, 2024-T3, 5052, 6082, and MIC-6. For 5-axis simultaneous milling of complex geometry, ±0.003 mm. CMM — coordinate measuring machine — verification is included on all critical-tolerance features and reported per dimension on the inspection report. For general structural aluminium milling where tight tolerances are not specified, ISO 2768-m applies as the standard.

**Q2: What does aluminium CNC milling involve?**
CNC milling of aluminium removes material using rotating multi-flute carbide cutting tools. The workpiece is fixtured on the machine table; the spindle — running at high speed and feed rate relative to steel or titanium — moves the tool across the part in programmed toolpaths. 3-axis milling handles prismatic features (pockets, slots, holes, profiles) from a single setup face. 4-axis and 5-axis milling allow the part to rotate about one or two additional axes, enabling features on multiple faces from a single fixturing. Post-machining, a CMM measures critical features against the drawing revision and the results are recorded in the dimensional report.

**Q3: What aluminium grades does Weldo CNC mill?**
6061-T6, 7075-T6, 2024-T3, 5052-H32, 6082-T6, and MIC-6 cast tooling plate are all in standard stock. Additional grades can be sourced on request — confirm at quoting. Mill certificates covering alloy grade, heat number, temper, and specification reference ship with every order. For aerospace programmes specifying AMS-graded material, confirm the specific AMS number at quoting.

**Q4: What is the lead time for aluminium CNC milled parts?**
Prototype and development quantities: 1–5 business days manufacturing. Small batch (10–50 parts): 5–15 business days. DDP delivery to the UK: 3–5 business days from Dongguan via DHL Express or FedEx International Priority. Quote within 24 hours. For programme milestone dates, confirm manufacturing schedule at ordering.

**Q5: Is there a minimum order quantity?**
No minimum. One piece to production quantities. Single-piece prototypes are handled under the same quality and documentation standard as batch production runs.

**Q6: What file formats does Weldo CNC accept for aluminium milling?**
STEP is preferred for 3D geometry. Also accepted: IGES, SolidWorks (.sldprt), Parasolid (.x_t), DXF, and compressed packages. For tight-tolerance parts, include a 2D drawing with all tolerance callouts, GD&T requirements, and surface finish specifications. The CMM report references the drawing revision in force at manufacturing.

**Q7: How is pricing structured for aluminium CNC milling?**
Key pricing drivers: alloy grade (7075 and 2024 carry a material premium over 6061 and 5052), part size and geometry complexity (number of setups, 3-axis vs 5-axis, pocket depth and access), tolerance requirements (±0.005 mm requires slower feeds and additional CMM measurement time), post-process finishing (hard anodise and alodine add cost), and quantity. DFM review and full documentation are included in the quoted price. Quantities above 10 parts reduce unit cost significantly. Upload STEP file for a quote.

**Q8: How does aluminium milling compare to aluminium turning — which process is right?**
CNC milling is the correct process for prismatic parts — pockets, profiles, slots, flat faces, and features on multiple faces of a block. CNC turning is the correct process for cylindrical parts — shafts, bushings, threaded bodies, and parts with rotational symmetry. Many aluminium parts require both: a turned outer diameter with milled pockets or cross-drilled features. Confirm the primary geometry of your part at quoting — the correct process combination is selected before programming begins.

**Q9: How does aluminium 6061 compare to 7075 for structural parts?**
6061-T6 at approximately 310 MPa tensile strength is adequate for the majority of structural aluminium parts — brackets, housings, frames, and enclosures where the design has geometric margin. 7075-T6 at approximately 572 MPa is specified when the designer needs to maintain structural performance while reducing wall thickness or weight — typically in aerospace brackets, robot arm links, and motorsport/performance automotive suspension components. 7075 is more expensive and requires surface protection (anodise, alodine) for most environments; 6061 anodises more consistently and is more corrosion-resistant in bare condition. If you're designing for performance and weight is the primary driver, 7075. If you're designing for function and weight has margin, 6061.

**Q10: Can aluminium milled parts be anodised, and how does anodising affect tight tolerances?**
Yes — Type II anodise and Type III hard anodise are both available for aluminium milled parts. Type II anodise builds a layer of approximately 5–10 µm per surface; Type III hard anodise builds 12–25 µm per surface (grows roughly 50% into the surface, 50% as outward build-up). For tight-tolerance bores and fits, hard anodise must be factored into the pre-coat machined dimension — a bore machined to +0.025 mm over nominal will be within nominal after 12–15 µm hard anodise build-up. Anodise allowances are planned and confirmed in the masking plan before processing begins, and coating thickness is measured and documented in the finish process record that ships with the parts.
