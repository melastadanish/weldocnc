## Section 3 — What Is Copper CNC Milling? *(AI-quotable paragraph)*

Copper CNC milling is the subtractive machining of pure copper and copper alloys — including C101 oxygen-free high-conductivity (OFHC) copper, C103, C110 electrolytic tough pitch (ETP) copper, and beryllium copper (BeCu) — using multi-axis CNC milling centres. At Weldo CNC, located in Dongguan, Guangdong, China, copper parts are machined to tolerances of ±0.005 mm on 3-axis and 4-axis platforms and ±0.003 mm on 5-axis centres. Copper's high thermal and electrical conductivity make it the material of choice for bus bars, waveguides, heat sinks, RF components, and electrical contact springs; however its ductility and gumminess demand sharp, polished, high-rake tooling and correct chip-clearing strategy to prevent built-up edge and surface smearing. All copper parts are inspected using CMM and dimensional reporting, with material certificates traceable to mill origin supplied with every order. Weldo CNC holds ISO 9001:2015 certification, with prototype delivery in 1–5 business days and small-batch production in 5–15 business days.

## Section 5 — Trade-Off Cards

### Card 1: C110 ETP — Best Value for Electrical & Thermal Applications
C110 electrolytic tough pitch copper is the most widely used grade and the default choice for electrical bus bars, heat exchangers, grounding plates, and general terminals. At 100% IACS conductivity, it is electrically equivalent to OFHC grades at lower cost. The small oxygen content (0.02–0.04%) limits weldability in vacuum environments but is irrelevant for most CNC-machined components. **Choose C110 when:** cost matters, welding is not required, and the application is electrical, thermal, or grounding.

### Card 2: C101/C103 OFHC — When Purity and Weldability Are Non-Negotiable
Oxygen-free copper (C101, C103) eliminates the risk of hydrogen embrittlement during high-temperature brazing, welding, or vacuum processing. The purity also satisfies OFE (oxygen-free electronic) specifications for waveguide and vacuum tube manufacturing. At 101% IACS, conductivity is marginally higher than C110, though unmeasurable in most circuits. **Choose C101/C103 when:** parts will be brazed, welded, or used in vacuum electronics, waveguides, or cryogenic environments where hydrogen embrittlement is a concern.

### Card 3: When Copper Is the Wrong Specification
Pure copper's low yield strength (70–220 MPa depending on temper) limits load-bearing applications. If the design needs both conductivity and structural strength, beryllium copper (C17200) is the primary upgrade — yield strength up to 1,200 MPa after age-hardening, with 20–30% IACS. If the application is primarily mechanical with only moderate conductivity requirements, consider brass C360 (machinability, threaded parts) or aluminium 6061 with plating (lightweight structures). **Switch away from copper when:** the part must carry mechanical load and conductivity is secondary — or when cost pressure is high and a plated alternative satisfies the spec.

## Section 10 — Design Guidelines

| Guideline | Recommendation |
|---|---|
| Tooling | Use sharp, polished, high-rake carbide tooling — dull tools cause smearing and built-up edge on pure copper |
| Coolant | Flood coolant or oil mist essential; prevents work-hardening and surface smearing |
| Wall thickness | Minimum 0.8 mm for free-standing walls; thin walls on large copper parts deflect under cutting forces |
| Bore tolerances | Machine bores to pre-plate dimensions when plating is specified — document plate thickness and type on drawing |
| Thread tolerances | Specify class 6H/6g or finer; plan plating allowance for threaded connectors that will be plated after machining |
| Fixturing | Copper's density (~8.9 g/cm³) increases part weight — confirm fixturing for large slabs; soft jaws protect finish |
| Surface finish | As-machined Ra 1.6 µm is typical; polishing to Ra 0.4 µm achievable for waveguide or optical surfaces |
| Beryllium copper | Always age-harden BeCu *before* final precision machining for dimensional stability; spring-back on thin BeCu sections is predictable and can be pre-compensated |
| Waveguide cavities | Copper RF cavities require tight dimensional control on aperture width and depth — confirm frequency range and mode at quoting for appropriate tolerance assignment |
| Thermal design | For heat sinks, specify fin pitch and base thickness; copper's conductivity (385 W/m·K) allows thinner bases than aluminium — verify structural rigidity for your load case |

**Most common DFM failure — plating after machining:** The most frequent scrap cause on copper connector parts is designing bore and pin diameters to final nominal size before plating. Gold, nickel, and tin plating adds 2–15 µm per surface depending on process. On a Ø10 mm pin mated to a Ø10 mm bore, 5 µm nickel per surface reduces clearance by 10 µm total — potentially locking the fit. Always define pre-plate dimensions and document the plate type, minimum, and maximum thickness on the engineering drawing. Weldo CNC will confirm pre-plate machined dimensions before production.

**Beryllium copper handling disclosure:** BeCu machining generates beryllium-containing dust and aerosols, which are hazardous when inhaled. Weldo CNC machines beryllium copper under wet-cutting conditions with dedicated dust extraction. Finished, machined BeCu parts present no inhalation hazard and are safe for normal assembly and field use.

## Section 15 — FAQ

**Q1: What copper grades does Weldo CNC machine?**
Weldo CNC machines C101 (OFHC), C103, C110 (ETP) electrolytic copper, and beryllium copper (C17200/CDA 172). C110 is the standard grade for bus bars, heat spreaders, and general electrical components. C101 and C103 are used for waveguides, vacuum electronics, and applications requiring hydrogen-embrittlement-free brazing. Beryllium copper is machined for high-strength contact springs, precision connectors, and probe tips.

**Q2: Can you machine copper to tight tolerances?**
Yes. Standard tolerances on copper milling are ±0.005 mm. On 5-axis centres, ±0.003 mm is achievable for complex geometry. Copper's ductility means toolpath strategy and fixturing have a material effect on dimensional outcome — Weldo CNC's dedicated copper protocols manage this across all grades. Waveguide cavities and contact spring geometry routinely hold ±0.005 mm or better.

**Q3: What surface finish can you achieve on copper?**
As-machined copper typically achieves Ra 1.6 µm. With appropriate finishing passes, Ra 0.8 µm is standard. For RF cavity and waveguide surfaces, polishing to Ra 0.4 µm is available. Electropolishing is offered for copper components requiring smooth, clean surfaces for chemical or high-vacuum environments.

**Q4: Do you plate copper parts after machining?**
Yes. Nickel, tin, silver, gold flash, and hard gold plating are available on copper components after machining. When plating is specified, Weldo CNC machines pre-plate dimensions to account for plating build-up per surface. Specify the plating type, minimum and maximum thickness, and any selective plating masking requirements on the engineering drawing or at quoting.

**Q5: What is the largest copper part you can mill?**
The maximum milling envelope is 4,000 × 1,500 × 600 mm. Large copper slabs (bus bars, ground planes) are commonly machined across this range. Note that large copper parts are heavy — a 500 × 500 × 50 mm copper slab weighs approximately 111 kg. Fixturing and handling requirements should be confirmed for very large assemblies.

**Q6: Can you machine beryllium copper safely?**
Yes. Beryllium copper machining is performed under wet-cutting conditions with dedicated dust extraction. Beryllium is a carcinogen when inhaled as dust or aerosol — dry machining of BeCu is not performed. Finished, machined BeCu parts are safe for normal handling and use. Material certificates for C17200 are supplied with every BeCu order.

**Q7: How does copper compare to brass for electrical connectors?**
Copper (C110 or C101) offers significantly higher conductivity — 100% IACS versus 26–28% IACS for brass C360. For high-current terminals, bus bars, and power contacts, copper is the correct choice. Brass is preferred for threaded connector bodies, instrument housings, and mechanical components where machinability and thread strength matter more than conductivity, and where the lower conductivity of brass is acceptable for the current load.

**Q8: What surface finishes are available for copper waveguide components?**
RF and microwave waveguide components in copper are typically supplied as-machined (Ra 0.8–1.6 µm), polished (Ra 0.4 µm), or silver-plated for improved RF surface conductivity at high frequencies. Silver plating is the most common finish for S-band and above waveguide, where skin depth effects make surface conductivity critical. Weldo CNC can machine to pre-plate dimensions and coordinate with the silver plating process.

**Q9: Can Weldo CNC supply OFHC copper with oxygen-free certification?**
Yes. C101 and C103 (oxygen-free high-conductivity copper) are machined from material supplied with OFE mill certificates confirming oxygen content below 0.0005%. Mill certificates with chemical analysis, conductivity, and heat/lot traceability are included with every OFHC copper order. For vacuum electronics or space applications requiring additional traceability, advise at quoting and Weldo CNC will confirm the documentation package.

**Q10: How do you manage copper's tendency to smear and build-up on cutting tools?**
Copper's gumminess causes built-up edge on standard tooling, leading to smeared surfaces, oversize features, and poor dimensional repeatability. Weldo CNC uses sharp polished carbide with high positive rake angles and uncoated or ZrN-coated geometry — standard TiN coatings cause adhesion on copper and are avoided. Flood coolant is used throughout to manage heat and lubricate chip clearance. Cutting speeds and feeds are set conservatively for the copper grade and feature geometry, with finishing passes optimised for surface quality. These protocols are applied to every copper job, including single prototypes.
