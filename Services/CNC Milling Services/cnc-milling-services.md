Prototype milled parts ship in 1–5 business days. ISO 9001:2015 certified — every tight-tolerance milling order ships with a CMM-verified dimensional inspection report referenced against your drawing revision.

**Body paragraph 2:**
CNC milling removes material using rotating cutting tools — end mills, face mills, drills — mounted in a spindle. The workpiece is clamped to a table, the machine moves the cutter across multiple axes, and material is removed until the geometry matches your 3D model. No moulds, no hand finishing. Just the toolpath and the part.

**Body paragraph 3:**
What separates capable CNC milling machine services from average ones isn't the machine — it's the setup. How the part is fixtured — held rigid against cutting forces — how many setups it takes to access all features, which tool handles which operation. Get those decisions right and a complex part machines cleanly in one shift. Get them wrong and tolerance errors compound across every repositioning.

**Body paragraph 4:**
Weldo CNC has operated CNC milling in Dongguan since 2012. The facility has machined everything from simple flat plates to multi-setup titanium aerospace brackets with 12 geometric callouts on a single face. ## SECTION 4 — TYPES OF CNC MILLING
*Design: H2 centred, subheading, 3-column card grid. Light grey background.*

**H2:**
Types of CNC Milling

**Subheading:**
3-axis to 5-axis — the right axis count is determined by your geometry, not by machine hourly rate.

**Card 1 — 3-Axis CNC Milling:**
Heading: 3-Axis CNC Milling
Body: Three axes: X, Y, Z. The spindle moves, the table moves, material comes off. This covers the majority of prismatic CNC milling parts — flat faces, holes, pockets, slots, external profiles, counterbores. Fast, cost-effective, and the right choice for most orders. If your part can be done in 3-axis without compromising access to any feature, that's what gets quoted.
Specs:
- Best for: Plates, brackets, housings, jig bases
- Standard tolerance: ±0.1 mm · Critical: ±0.005 mm CMM-verified

**Card 2 — 4-Axis CNC Milling:**
Heading: 4-Axis CNC Milling
Body: Adds a rotational A-axis. The part can be indexed — rotated to a new face — without removing it from the fixture. That matters for components with features on multiple faces. Machining face 1, rotating 90°, machining face 2 — all in a single setup. Fewer re-clampings means less opportunity for datum error to accumulate.
Specs:
- Best for: Cylindrical parts with milled features, multi-face components
- Advantage: Eliminates datum error from multiple setups

**Card 3 — 5-Axis CNC Machining:**
Heading: 5-Axis CNC Machining
Body: Two additional rotational axes with simultaneous motion on all five. Required for complex curved surfaces, compound angles, and undercut features that can't be reached with the part in a fixed position. Also the most efficient route for intricate geometries that would otherwise need four or more separate 3-axis setups — each of which introduces re-fixturing time and cumulative positional error.
Specs:
- Best for: Aerospace structures, medical implants, complex housings
- Tolerance: ±0.003 mm in a single setup

## SECTION 5 — WHEN CNC MILLING IS THE RIGHT PROCESS
*Design: H2, 2-column — paragraphs left, supporting image right. White background.*

**H2:**
When CNC Milling is the Right Process

**Body paragraph 1:**
CNC milling isn't the answer for everything. High volumes of a simple shape — casting or extrusion wins on unit cost. A complex freeform surface in a soft material for a single prototype — 3D printing may get you there faster. But for the part most precision engineers actually specify — prismatic geometry, functional tolerances, a real engineering material, quantities in the tens to low thousands — milling is difficult to beat.

**Body paragraph 2:**
No hard tooling required. That's the first reason. STEP file to machined part without a mould, a die, or a forming tool. The capital cost comparison between cast aluminium and milled aluminium is straightforward for anything under a few hundred pieces — often under a few thousand.

**Body paragraph 3:**
The second advantage is material range. Same process, same machines, same tolerances — aluminium one week, titanium the next, PEEK after that. The cutting parameters and chip load change. The process doesn't. That matters when you're developing a part and haven't locked down the final material.

**Body paragraph 4:**
Surface finish is underrated as a milling advantage. An as-milled surface at Ra 1.6 µm is functional for most applications without secondary operations. For features needing tighter finish — bearing bores, sealing faces — a finishing pass reaches Ra 0.8 µm on the same machine, in the same setup. Repeatability is the final point: part one and part five hundred are cut from the same program to the same tolerance.

## SECTION 11 — CNC MILLING DESIGN GUIDELINES
*Design: H2, subheading, full-width 3-column table + 2 supporting paragraphs below. White background.*

**H2:**
CNC Milling Design Guidelines

**Subheading:**
Rules that keep your part manufacturable — and your quote where you expect it.

**Design guidelines table:**

| Feature | Recommendation | Why It Matters |
|---|---|---|
| Wall thickness (metals) | ≥ 0.8 mm | Thinner walls deflect under cutting forces — poor finish, tolerance failure |
| Wall thickness (plastics) | ≥ 1.5 mm | Plastics flex more; thin walls vibrate during cutting |
| Internal corner radius | ≥ 1/3 of pocket depth | Set by tool diameter — smaller radius = smaller tool = more passes, higher cost |
| Pocket depth | ≤ 4× pocket width | Deep narrow pockets need long-reach end mills that deflect under chip load |
| Hole depth | ≤ 12× diameter (standard drill) | Beyond this requires a gun drill — flag on drawing if needed |
| Undercuts | Flag on drawing with access direction | Require special tooling — access must be confirmed before quoting |
| Thread engagement | ≥ 3× diameter for threads under Ø5 mm · ≥ 4–6× for Ø5 mm and above | Minimum engagement for thread to carry load under service conditions |
| Tolerances | Tight callout only where function requires | ±0.005 mm costs significantly more than ±0.02 mm — only specify where it matters |
| Text / engraving | ≥ 0.5 mm stroke width | Narrower features require very small end mills — fragile, slow, expensive |
| Chamfers vs. radii | Prefer chamfers on part edges | Easier to machine than external radii — same deburring result at lower cost |

**Supporting paragraph 1:**
Two things account for most DFM failures in incoming files. First: internal corner radii specified tighter than the tooling can physically reach. A 0.3 mm corner radius in a 25 mm deep pocket requires a tool so slender it deflects before finishing the cut — the result is chatter, poor surface finish, and a rejected part. The fix is usually a 1 mm radius, which costs nothing functionally and machines cleanly. Second: tight tolerances applied to every dimension regardless of function. ±0.005 mm on a non-critical face costs significantly more to inspect and verify than ±0.1 mm. If the surface doesn't mate with anything or carry a load, it doesn't need that callout.

**Supporting paragraph 2:**
Both issues get caught at DFM review. They're flagged in the quote — you decide whether to update the drawing or proceed as-is. Nothing is cut first and explained later.

## SECTION 16 — FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS
*Design: H2, accordion. White background. First item open.*

**H2:**
CNC Milling Services — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1 (open by default):**
Q: What tolerances can Weldo CNC hold on CNC milled parts?
A: Standard structural work follows ISO 2768-m — ±0.1 mm on linear dimensions, sufficient for brackets, housings, frames, and plates where no feature mates with a precision component. Functional interfaces — sliding fits, press fits, locating features — are machined to ±0.02 mm. For tight-tolerance work — bearing seats, sealing surfaces, critical bores — Weldo CNC holds ±0.005 mm, measured by CMM and reported per feature against your drawing callout. Specifying ±0.005 mm across every dimension adds cost for no functional benefit where it isn't needed. DFM review flags overspecified tolerances — you can accept the note or keep the callout, either way you go in with clear information.

**Q2:**
Q: What is the difference between 3-axis, 4-axis, and 5-axis CNC milling?
A: 3-axis moves the cutter on X, Y, and Z — the right choice for the majority of prismatic parts. 4-axis adds rotation, letting the part be indexed to access multiple faces without re-clamping — useful for cylindrical parts or components with features on three or more faces. 5-axis adds full simultaneous motion on two rotational axes, enabling complex curved surfaces, compound angles, and undercuts in a single setup. Weldo CNC assesses which is right for each geometry. If 3-axis gets it done cleanly, that's what gets quoted.

**Q3:**
Q: Can Weldo CNC ship CNC milled parts to the US or Europe?
A: Yes. Parts ship to customers in the US, UK, Germany, France, Australia, and 100+ countries. Most customers are not local to Dongguan — they need accurate, documented, on-time parts with clear communication. Export documentation, commercial invoices, and DDP (Delivered Duty Paid — buyer pays no customs fees at destination) freight are available on request. International transit typically takes 3–7 days after production, depending on destination and service level.

**Q4:**
Q: What materials can Weldo CNC mill?
A: Metals include aluminium (6061, 7075, 2024), stainless steel (303, 304, 316L), titanium (Grade 2 and Grade 5), tool steel (D2, H13), brass, copper, and bronze. Engineering plastics include PEEK, Delrin (POM), Nylon (PA6/PA66), ABS, polycarbonate, and PTFE. If your application calls for a material not listed here, send the material spec with your quote request and machinability will be confirmed before you commit.

**Q5:**
Q: What file format does Weldo CNC need for a CNC milling quote?
A: STEP (.stp / .step) is preferred — it carries full 3D geometry and is what toolpaths are generated from. IGES is also accepted. For 2D-only work, a fully dimensioned PDF or DXF works for simpler prismatic geometry. Upload what you have. If additional information is needed, you'll be told exactly what's missing.

**Q6:**
Q: How does Weldo CNC handle tight tolerances on aluminium vs. titanium?
A: Material behaviour during cutting changes what's achievable and how to get there. Aluminium machines fast with low cutting forces — tight tolerances are straightforward on most feature types. Titanium generates high cutting forces, work-hardens at the surface, and retains heat in the cut — all of which affect tolerance holding. Appropriate tooling, feed rates, and cutting fluid strategies are applied per material. The ±0.005 mm capability applies to both, but the process to achieve it differs. If a design is moving from aluminium to titanium with the same tolerance callouts, flag it at quote stage — a material-specific DFM review will be run.

**Q7:**
Q: How does Weldo CNC verify that CNC milled parts meet my drawing specifications?
A: Every tight-tolerance milling order ships with a CMM-verified dimensional inspection report. The report records measured value, nominal dimension, tolerance, and pass/fail status per feature — against your drawing revision number. Material certificates trace alloy grade and heat number to the specific parts in your order. Weldo CNC holds a 97.48% annual pass rate across all CNC machining orders. Non-conforming parts do not ship — they are quarantined and processed through a documented NCR before any disposition is made.

**Q8:**
Q: What is the minimum order quantity for CNC milling at Weldo CNC?
A: There is no minimum order quantity. Single-part prototype orders are accepted. The same quality process — CMM inspection, material certs, dimensional report — applies whether you order one milled part or one thousand. Per-unit cost is higher on single pieces because setup time is the same regardless of run length, but there is no floor on order size.

**Q9:**
Q: How much does CNC milling cost?
A: Cost depends on part geometry, material, tolerance class, quantity, number of setups, and surface finish required. Setup cost dominates on low-volume runs — a single prototype costs more per unit than a batch of 50. Tight tolerances (±0.005 mm) require CMM inspection time and may need dedicated fixturing, which adds cost. Upload your STEP file to receive an itemised quote within 24 hours. No obligation to proceed.

**Q10:**
Q: How does Weldo CNC's CNC milling compare to Hubs or Xometry?
A: Hubs quotes CNC milling tolerances to ±0.020 mm as standard — Weldo CNC holds ±0.005 mm, CMM-verified. Xometry routes orders through a marketplace network, meaning your part goes to whichever shop has capacity with no single facility quality standard. Weldo CNC mills every order in-house at its own dedicated facility in Dongguan — one facility, one quality system, CMM report with every tight-tolerance order.
