## Section 3: What is CNC Milling
**Design:** 2-column — text left, image right. White background.

CNC milling removes material using rotating cutting tools mounted in a spindle. The workpiece is clamped to a table, the machine moves the cutter across multiple axes, and material is removed until the geometry matches your 3D model. No moulds, no hand finishing — just the toolpath and the part.

What separates capable cnc milling machine services from average ones isn't the machine — it's the setup. How the part is fixtured, how many setups it takes to access all features, which tool is chosen for which operation. Get those decisions right and a complex part machines cleanly in one shift. Get them wrong and you're resetting, re-measuring, and calling the customer about lead time.

We've been running CNC milling operations in Dongguan for over 20 years. In that time we've machined everything from simple flat plates to multi-setup titanium aerospace brackets with 12 geometric callouts on a single face. The setups and the tooling knowledge are built up over that period — not something you acquire from a manual.

## Section 3b: Types of CNC Milling
**Design:** 3-column card grid. Light grey background.
**Heading:** Types of CNC Milling
**Subheading:** We run 3-axis, 4-axis, and 5-axis milling. The right choice depends on your geometry — not on upselling.

**Card 1 — 3-Axis CNC Milling**
Three axes: X, Y, Z. The spindle moves, the table moves, material comes off. This covers the majority of prismatic cnc milling parts — flat faces, holes, pockets, slots, external profiles, counterbores. Fast, cost-effective, and completely sufficient for most orders we receive. If your part can be done in 3-axis without compromising access to any feature, that's what we'll quote.
- Best for: Plates, brackets, housings, jig bases
- Standard tolerance: ±0.1mm | Critical: ±0.005mm CMM-verified

**Card 2 — 4-Axis CNC Milling**
Adds a rotational axis (A-axis). The part can be indexed — rotated to a new face — without removing it from the fixture. That matters for components with features on multiple faces: machining face 1, rotating 90°, machining face 2, all in a single setup. Fewer re-clampings means fewer opportunities for datum error to accumulate across setups.
- Best for: Cylindrical parts with milled features, multi-face components
- Advantage: Eliminates datum error from multiple setups

**Card 3 — 5-Axis CNC Machining**
Two additional rotational axes, with simultaneous motion on all five. Required for complex curved surfaces, compound angles, and features that simply can't be reached with the workpiece in a fixed position. Also the most efficient route for intricate parts that would otherwise demand four or more separate setups on a 3-axis machine — each of those setups introduces re-fixturing time and potential error.
- Best for: Aerospace structures, medical components, complex housings

## Section 3c: Advantages of CNC Milling
**Design:** 2-column — paragraphs left, supporting image right. White background.
**Heading:** When CNC Milling is the Right Process

CNC milling isn't the answer for everything. High volumes of a simple shape? Casting or extrusion wins on unit cost. A complex freeform surface in a soft material? 3D printing may get you there faster for a prototype. But for the category of part that most precision engineers are actually specifying — prismatic geometry, functional tolerances, a real material, production quantities in the tens to low thousands — milling is difficult to beat.

No hard tooling. That's the first thing. You go from STEP file to machined part without a mould, a die, or a forming tool. The capital cost of a small batch in cast aluminium versus milled aluminium is not a comparison — milling wins for anything under a few hundred pieces, often under a few thousand.

The second thing is the material range. Same process, same machines, same tolerances — aluminium one week, titanium the next, PEEK after that. The cutting parameters change. The process doesn't. That flexibility matters when you're developing a part and haven't locked down the final material yet.

Surface finish is underrated. An as-milled surface at Ra 1.6µm is functional for most applications without any secondary operation. Compare that to 3D printing, where the as-built surface requires significant post-processing to reach the same result. And for features that need tighter finish — bearing bores, sealing faces — a finishing pass brings you to Ra 0.4µm or better on the same machine, in the same setup.

The repeatability point is straightforward: part one and part five hundred are cut from the same program. The tolerance your prototype was qualified to is the tolerance your production batch ships to. No re-tooling, no requalification cycle.

## Section 9: Design Guidelines
**Design:** Full-width 3-column table + supporting copy. White background.
**Heading:** CNC Milling Design Guidelines
**Subheading:** Rules that keep your part manufacturable — and your quote where you expect it.

| Feature | Recommendation | Why It Matters |
|---|---|---|
| Wall thickness (metals) | ≥ 0.8mm | Thinner walls deflect under cutting forces — poor finish, tolerance failure |
| Wall thickness (plastics) | ≥ 1.5mm | Plastics flex more; thin walls vibrate during cutting |
| Internal corner radius | ≥ 1/3 of pocket depth | Set by tool diameter — smaller radius = smaller tool = more passes, higher cost |
| Pocket depth | ≤ 4× pocket width | Deep narrow pockets require long-reach tools that deflect under load |
| Hole depth | ≤ 10× diameter (standard drill) | Beyond this, use a gun drill — flag on drawing if required |
| Undercuts | Flag on drawing with access direction | Require special tooling — need to confirm access before quoting |
| Thread length | ≥ 1.5× diameter | Minimum engagement for thread to carry load |
| Tolerances | Tight callout only where function requires | ±0.005mm costs significantly more than ±0.02mm — only specify where it matters |
| Text / engraving | ≥ 0.5mm stroke width | Narrower features need very small tools — fragile, slow, expensive |
| Chamfers vs. radii | Prefer chamfers on part edges | Easier to machine than external radii — same deburring result at lower cost |

Two things account for the majority of DFM issues we see in incoming files. First: internal corner radii specified tighter than the tooling can practically reach. A 0.3mm corner radius in a 25mm deep pocket requires a tool so slender it will deflect before it finishes the cut — the result is chatter, poor surface finish, and a rejected part. The fix is usually a 1mm radius, which costs nothing functionally and machines cleanly. Second: tight tolerances applied to every dimension on the drawing regardless of function. ±0.005mm on a non-critical face costs three times more to inspect and verify than ±0.1mm. If the surface doesn't mate with anything or carry a load, it doesn't need that callout.

Both issues get caught at DFM review. We raise them in the quote — you decide whether to update the drawing or proceed as-is. We don't cut first and explain later.

## Section 14: FAQ
**Design:** Accordion. White background. First item open.
**Heading:** CNC Milling Services — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: What tolerances can you hold on CNC milled parts?**
Depends what the part needs. Structural components and non-critical geometry we machine to ISO 2768-m — ±0.1mm on linear dimensions, sufficient for most brackets, housings, frames, and plates where no feature mates with a precision component. Step up to functional interfaces — sliding fits, press fits, locating features — and we're working to ±0.02mm. For the tight end: bearing seats, sealing surfaces, critical bores — we hold ±0.005mm, measured by CMM, reported per feature against your drawing callout. Worth being direct: specifying ±0.005mm across every dimension adds cost and lead time for no functional benefit where it isn't needed. We flag overspecified tolerances during DFM review — you can accept the note or keep the callout, either way you go in with eyes open.

**Q2: What's the difference between 3-axis, 4-axis, and 5-axis CNC milling?**
3-axis moves the cutter on X, Y, and Z — the right choice for the majority of prismatic parts. 4-axis adds rotation, letting us index the part to access multiple faces without re-clamping — useful for cylindrical parts or components with features on 3+ faces. 5-axis adds full simultaneous motion on two rotational axes, enabling complex curved surfaces, compound angles, and undercuts in a single setup. We assess which is right for your specific geometry. If 3-axis gets it done cleanly, that's what we'll use. If the geometry demands 5-axis, we'll tell you why.

**Q3: Do you offer CNC milling services near me if I'm based in the US or Europe?**
We ship globally — US, UK, Germany, France, Australia, and beyond. Most of our customers aren't local to Dongguan; they're engineers who need accurate, documented, on-time parts with clear communication. We handle export documentation, commercial invoices, and DDP freight on request. Lead time from 3 business days ex-works; international transit typically 3–7 days depending on destination and service level.

**Q4: What materials can you CNC mill?**
Metals: aluminium (6061, 7075, 2024), stainless steel (303, 304, 316L), titanium (Grade 2 and Grade 5), tool steel (D2, H13), brass, copper, and bronze. Plastics: PEEK, Delrin (POM), Nylon (PA6/PA66), ABS, polycarbonate, PTFE, UHMW-PE. If your application calls for something not on this list — Inconel, Hastelloy, a specific composite — send the material spec with your RFQ and we'll confirm machinability before you commit.

**Q5: What file format do you need for a CNC milling quote?**
STEP is preferred — it carries full 3D geometry and is what we generate toolpaths from. IGES also accepted. For 2D-only work, a fully dimensioned PDF or DXF works for simpler prismatic geometry. Send what you have. If we need additional information, we'll tell you exactly what's missing — not a generic "insufficient information" response.

**Q6: How do you handle tight tolerances on aluminium vs. titanium?**
Material behaviour during cutting changes what's achievable and how you get there. Aluminium machines fast with low cutting forces — tight tolerances are straightforward on most feature types. Titanium generates high cutting forces, work-hardens at the surface, and retains heat in the cut — all of which affect tolerance holding. We use appropriate tooling, feeds, speeds, and cutting fluid strategies per material. The ±0.005mm capability applies to both, but the process to achieve it differs. If you're moving a design from aluminium to titanium and keeping the same tolerance callouts, tell us — we'll review the DFM for that material specifically.
