## Section 8: Importing CNC Parts to France
**Design:** 2-column — editorial text left (60%), summary table right (40%). Light grey background.
**Heading:** Importing CNC Machined Parts to France — What to Expect

Understanding EU customs and import rules when sourcing CNC machined parts from China prevents delays and unexpected costs. Here is what applies when ordering with Weldo CNC for delivery to France.

**DDP means we handle it**
All Weldo CNC shipments to France are sent DDP — Delivered Duty Paid. Weldo CNC files the EU import declaration, pays the applicable duty, and handles clearance before goods reach your French address. No courtier en douane required on your side.

**EU import duty on CNC machined parts**
France applies the EU Common External Tariff (CET) on goods imported from China. Most CNC machined metal parts import under CN code 8466.93. Typical MFN duty rates:
- Precision machined components (CN 8466.93): 2.7–3.7%
- Machined aluminium parts (CN 7616.99): 2.7%
- Machined stainless steel (CN 7326.90): 2.7%
- Titanium parts (CN 8108.99): 0%

Under DDP, these duties are paid by Weldo CNC and included in your quoted price.

**TVA — French import VAT**
French import VAT (TVA) is 20%. Under DDP, TVA is settled at EU border entry and included in the delivered price. French businesses registered for TVA (numéro de TVA intracommunautaire) can recover import TVA through their standard TVA return — confirm with your comptable.

**No customs broker needed**
DDP covers customs declaration, duty payment, and clearance. Parts arrive at your French address without any customs hold or douane documentation required from you.

**CE marking**
CNC machined parts are components — they do not require CE marking. The CE obligation applies to finished products placed on the EU market, not to machined parts used in their manufacture. If you are incorporating Weldo CNC parts into a product requiring CE marking, that obligation sits with you as the product manufacturer.

**REACH**
Standard aluminium alloys, stainless steel, and titanium machined at Weldo CNC are not subject to REACH substance restrictions. If your application involves REACH-sensitive requirements, request material data sheets at quote stage. Specific surface treatments — chromate conversion coating, for instance — may be relevant depending on the application.

**Transit to France**
DHL Express and FedEx International Priority: 3–5 business days from Dongguan. Paris, Lyon, and Marseille are the fastest delivery destinations. No EORI number required on your end under DDP.

## Section 11: Engineering in France
**Design:** Full-width editorial section. White background. Large heading. 2-column text on desktop.
**Heading:** Engineering in France — Where We Deliver

France is home to some of Europe's most technically demanding engineering clusters. From the Airbus commercial aircraft hub in Toulouse to the semiconductor and deep tech ecosystem around Grenoble, French engineers operate in environments where tolerance consistency, material traceability, and documented inspection are baseline requirements — not differentiators. That context shapes what a CNC machining supplier must provide to be credible.

**Île-de-France — Aerospace and Defence Prime Contractors**
The Paris region hosts the procurement offices and tier 1 suppliers of Airbus, Safran, Thales, and Dassault Aviation. Demand centres on precision aluminium aerospace structural parts, titanium actuation brackets, and sensor housings for defence systems. Quality expectations at tier 1 are high: full material traceability, CoC documentation, and CMM-verified dimensional reports are standard. Engineers in this cluster have a detailed understanding of what they're specifying — technical dialogue, not just pricing, is the norm. ISO 2768 tolerance callouts and metric drawings are universal. NF norms may appear alongside ISO references on older drawing sets.

**Toulouse — Airbus Commercial Aircraft Hub**
Toulouse is the single largest aerospace manufacturing cluster in continental Europe. Airbus final assembly lines, together with a dense supply chain of tier 2 and tier 3 suppliers, generate sustained demand for structural aluminium (7075-T6, 2024-T3), titanium fastener-adjacent components, precision ribs, and bracket assemblies. Material traceability to mill cert and heat number is expected at every tier. CMM inspection report and certificate of conformity ship with every order — non-negotiable in this supply chain. For tier 2 and tier 3 Airbus suppliers sourcing prototype and pre-production parts outside their primary supply chain, Weldo CNC's ISO 9001:2015 certified QMS and 97.48% annual pass rate provide the documentation baseline to support qualification.

**Auvergne-Rhône-Alpes — Lyon, Grenoble, and Advanced Manufacturing**
Lyon anchors a strong automotive and industrial manufacturing base, with Renault, Stellantis, and Michelin supply chain activity generating demand for precision aluminium powertrain and suspension components. Grenoble is a different market: STMicroelectronics, Soitec, and the CEA-Leti research campus create demand for precision aluminium tooling plates, wafer handling components, and clean-room compatible finishes — hard anodise and electropolish. Tolerances are tight: ±0.005 mm or tighter on critical features. Surface finish Ra values are specified on the drawing, not left to the supplier. The medtech cluster across Lyon and Grenoble — medical device manufacturers sourcing PEEK, titanium, and 316L stainless components — expects biocompatibility documentation alongside dimensional reports. ISO 2768-m/c is the standard baseline across this region.

**Occitanie — Toulouse and the Aerospace Supply Chain**
Beyond the Airbus campus itself, the Occitanie region hosts a second ring of aerospace suppliers and engineering services firms covering composite manufacturing support, test equipment, and avionics hardware. Machined aluminium and titanium parts with compound geometry — features requiring 5-axis simultaneous machining to maintain position tolerance across multiple faces — are a recurring requirement. Weldo CNC's 5-axis capability holds ±0.003 mm on complex geometry, with all positional features CMM-verified and reported per feature.

**Brittany and Pays de la Loire — Naval and Maritime**
The naval engineering base around Brest, Lorient, and Saint-Nazaire — Naval Group shipbuilding, DCNS design offices — generates demand for precision components in duplex stainless, super-duplex, and high-performance alloys. Corrosion resistance and pressure-rating documentation are standard requirements. The Saint-Nazaire and Nantes industrial zone adds civil shipbuilding and offshore energy hardware. Material traceability and pressure-rated documentation are expected across both sub-sectors.

**Strasbourg, Bordeaux, and the Broader French Market**
Bordeaux hosts Thales, Dassault Systèmes design offices, and Naval Group naval defence procurement — a mix of aerospace precision parts and naval stainless hardware. Strasbourg sits within the wider Rhine Valley advanced manufacturing corridor and sources precision industrial machinery components. French engineers across all regions share a common expectation: drawings are metric, ISO 2768 is the general tolerance standard, M-thread series is universal, and documentation accompanies every delivery.

**Heading:** Sourcing CNC Machining from China — France FAQs

**Q1: How long does delivery take from China to France?**
Production runs 1–5 business days depending on part complexity. Shipping via DHL Express or FedEx International Priority adds 3–5 business days from Dongguan — total 4–10 business days to France. All shipments are DDP: EU import duty and TVA are included in the price, and EU customs clearance is handled by Weldo CNC. Parts arrive at your French address with no additional charges and no douane administration required from you.

**Q2: Who handles EU customs and import duties when shipping to France?**
Weldo CNC handles it. DDP — Delivered Duty Paid — means Weldo CNC files the EU import declaration, pays the applicable import duty (typically 2.7–3.7% on precision machined components under CN code 8466.93), and settles TVA at the EU border. You receive a cleared shipment. No courtier en douane required on your side.

**Q3: Is TVA reclaimable on parts imported under DDP?**
Yes, for TVA-registered French businesses. Under DDP, Weldo CNC pays the 20% French TVA at EU import. Your business can recover that TVA through your standard TVA return (déclaration de TVA) — the same process as any other import. Confirm the exact mechanism with your comptable, as recovery depends on your TVA registration status and the nature of the goods.

**Q4: How do I protect my IP when sending STEP files to a supplier in China?**
Weldo CNC signs an NDA before any file transfer, on request. STEP files and 2D drawings are used for quoting and manufacturing only — not shared with third parties. ISO 9001:2015 certification includes document control procedures governing how customer data is handled and stored.

**Q5: What if the parts don't meet the drawing when they arrive?**
Parts ship with a CMM dimensional inspection report — coordinate measuring machine — checked feature by feature against your drawing. If a part is non-conforming and the report confirms it, Weldo CNC remakes or credits without dispute. Non-conformances are identified at the inspection stage before parts leave the Dongguan facility. The 97.48% annual pass rate reflects consistent first-time quality, not a returns process.

**Q6: Can Weldo CNC hold the tolerances required for Airbus supply chain parts?**
Tolerances to ±0.005 mm are confirmed standard for CNC milling and turning. For complex 5-axis geometry — compound angles, undercuts, multi-face positional features common in aerospace structural parts — ±0.003 mm is confirmed. All critical-tolerance features are CMM-verified and reported per feature on the dimensional inspection report. GD&T callouts on your 2D drawing — positional tolerance, flatness, runout, perpendicularity — are read and inspected against stated values.

**Q7: What tolerances and standards do French drawings typically use?**
French engineering drawings use ISO 2768 for general tolerances — ISO 2768-m for metals, ISO 2768-c for plastics. Metric dimensions and M-thread series are standard. Some older drawings reference NF norms alongside ISO — Weldo CNC reads both. ISO 2768-m is the baseline tolerance class applied unless a tighter callout is specified on the drawing.

**Q8: What materials can Weldo CNC machine for French engineers?**
100+ metals and engineering plastics. For aerospace: aluminium 7075-T6, 2024-T3, 6061-T6; titanium Grade 2 and Grade 5 (Ti-6Al-4V). For medtech: 316L stainless, titanium Grade 2, PEEK. For industrial and automotive: 303 and 304 stainless, 6061 aluminium, brass C360, tool steel H13 and D2. For semiconductor tooling: MIC-6 tooling plate, 6061 aluminium with hard anodise or electropolish. Confirm your specific grade and finish at quote stage.

**Q9: Is there a minimum order quantity for CNC machined parts?**
No minimum. Single-part prototype orders to full production quantities — all accepted. Per-unit cost is higher on single pieces because setup time is fixed regardless of quantity, but no floor exists on order size. French prototype and pre-production engineers working on 1–20 parts are a normal part of Weldo CNC's workload.

**Q10: How does Weldo CNC compare to French precision machine shops?**
French precision ateliers — particularly in Île-de-France and Occitanie — are the right choice when parts are needed in 24–48 hours, or the programme requires a locally auditable supplier with AS9100D or ISO 13485 certification. Weldo CNC is typically the stronger choice for tight-tolerance work (±0.005 mm), full CMM documentation, specialist alloys, complex 5-axis geometry, or quantities of 5–500 parts where French precision shop lead times (2–4 weeks typical) and unit pricing become a constraint. Many French engineers run both — a local shop for critical-path one-offs, Weldo CNC for the documented prototype build or pre-production run.
