## Section 8: Importing CNC Parts to the USA
**Design:** 2-column — editorial text left (60%), summary table right (40%). Light grey background.
**Heading:** Importing CNC Machined Parts to the USA — What to Expect

Understanding the customs and import process when sourcing CNC parts from China prevents budget surprises and delivery delays. Here is what to expect when ordering with Weldo CNC.

**DDP means we handle it**
All Weldo CNC shipments to the USA are sent DDP — Delivered Duty Paid. Weldo CNC files the import declaration, pays the applicable duty and tariff, and handles customs clearance before the parcel reaches your address. Your team receives a package, not a customs hold notice or broker invoice.

**Section 301 tariffs — the key consideration**
The US Trade Representative imposed Section 301 tariffs on Chinese goods starting in 2018. Most CNC machined metal parts fall under HTS Chapters 73, 76, or 84 — which carry a 25% additional tariff on top of the standard MFN import duty rate. Standard MFN rates for machined components classified under HS code 8466.93 are typically 0–3.9% depending on material. Under DDP, Weldo CNC pays both the standard import duty and the Section 301 tariff. Both are included in your quoted price. No additional charges arrive on delivery.

**Standard US import duty on CNC machined parts**
Typical duty rates under standard MFN rates, before Section 301 surcharges:
- Precision machined components (HS 8466.93): 0–3.9% depending on material
- Machined aluminium parts (HTS 7616.99): 0–3%
- Machined stainless steel parts (HTS 7326.90): 2.9%
- Titanium machined parts (HTS 8108.99): 0%

All applicable duties are included under DDP and paid before shipment departs.

**ISF filing**
US Customs and Border Protection requires an Importer Security Filing (ISF 10+2) for ocean freight at least 24 hours before vessel loading. Weldo CNC orders ship by air freight — DHL Express or FedEx International Priority. Advance cargo information on air shipments is handled electronically by the carrier. No filing action required from your team.

**FDA context for medical device component buyers**
CNC machined parts do not require FDA registration. FDA oversight under 21 CFR Part 820 applies to finished medical devices placed in commerce, not to machined components used in their manufacture. If your programme is subject to FDA 21 CFR Part 820, that regulatory obligation sits with you as the device manufacturer. Weldo CNC supplies ISO 9001:2015 compliant machined components with full material traceability and CMM inspection reports to support your quality records.

**ITAR**
Weldo CNC is not ITAR registered. Components subject to International Traffic in Arms Regulations — defence articles, technical data, or defence services on the USML — cannot be sourced through Weldo CNC. Commercial and industrial parts not controlled under ITAR are unaffected.

**ASTM and AMS material specifications**
Weldo CNC works from ASTM and AMS material specifications. Specify ASTM B209 for aluminium sheet, AMS 2770 for heat treatment requirements, or any standard ASTM alloy designation on your drawing — material certs reference the specified standard. If your drawing calls up a military or aerospace spec (e.g. MIL-A-8625 for anodising), confirm at quote stage.

## Section 11: Engineering in the USA
**Design:** Full-width editorial section. White background. Large heading. 2-column text on desktop.
**Heading:** Engineering in the USA — Where We Deliver

The United States is the largest CNC machining market in the world. Across California, Texas, Michigan, Massachusetts, Ohio, and Washington State, the engineers specifying precision machined parts are working in industries — aerospace, medical devices, defence, automotive, energy — where the tolerance requirement, material traceability standard, and documentation expectation are as demanding as anywhere on earth. That context defines what a credible CNC supplier needs to deliver.

**California — Silicon Valley, Bay Area, and Los Angeles**
Two distinct engineering clusters define California's machining demand. Silicon Valley and the Bay Area generate sustained requirements for semiconductor tooling in MIC-6 aluminium tooling plate, precision electronics enclosures, robotics actuator housings, and prototype parts for deep tech companies iterating on weekly design cycles. Tolerances are tight, surface finish requirements are specific, and engineers arrive knowing exactly what they are asking for. Los Angeles and the surrounding aerospace corridor — home to SpaceX, Northrop Grumman, Raytheon, and Boeing's supply chain — produces a different workload: 7075-T6 aluminium structural brackets, titanium actuation hardware, complex 5-axis geometry with full CMM documentation and material traceability to heat number. ASME Y14.5 GD&T is standard on drawings. DFM feedback is expected before machining starts.

**Texas — Energy and Defence**
Houston is the centre of US oil and gas engineering. Downhole tooling, valve bodies, manifold assemblies, and subsea hardware in Inconel, duplex stainless, and high-performance alloys define the workload. Pressure-rated documentation and material traceability to heat cert are baseline expectations, not premium add-ons. San Antonio and Fort Worth support a defence and aerospace supply chain — structural components and precision hardware where documentation requirements match the operating environment. Austin adds a growing technology hardware cluster: robotics, automation, and precision electronics generating iterative aluminium prototype work at short cycle times.

**Michigan and the Midwest — Automotive and Industrial Machinery**
Michigan's automotive supply chain, anchored around Detroit, centres increasingly on EV powertrain development: motor housings, battery cooling plates, transmission components, and prototype tooling running from concept to first-article in compressed schedules. Aluminium is the dominant work material, alongside powertrain machining in stainless and alloy steel. Ohio's industrial machinery sector generates steady demand for gearbox housings, bearing blocks, hydraulic components, and machine frame parts where dimensional accuracy and surface finish drive the specification. US engineers in this market routinely specify inch and metric dimensions on the same drawing — Weldo CNC reads both natively, with UNC and UNF threads handled alongside M-series callouts.

**Massachusetts — Medtech and Robotics**
Boston and the Route 128 corridor hold one of the world's highest concentrations of medical device and life sciences engineering. Surgical instrument prototypes, implant component housings, diagnostic device assemblies, and catheter-adjacent hardware in 316L stainless, titanium Grade 5, and PEEK define the work. ISO 9001:2015 compliance and full material traceability are the minimum bar. FDA 21 CFR Part 820 context is present on most projects — buyers require documentation that supports their own device quality records. Cambridge-area robotics and automation companies add a parallel stream of tight-tolerance aluminium and stainless work with faster iteration cycles and lower documentation overhead.

**Washington State — Aerospace**
Seattle is the centre of Boeing's commercial aircraft supply chain. Demand across tier 1 through tier 3 runs to structural aluminium, titanium brackets, actuation components, and precision machined assemblies where ASME Y14.5 GD&T is mandatory and material traceability to AMS specification is expected. SpaceX's Starlink ground equipment, Blue Origin's propulsion hardware, and the broader Pacific Northwest aerospace supplier base add further demand for 7075, titanium, and Inconel parts with full CMM dimensional reports.

US engineers across all sectors share one constant expectation: the drawing is the spec. ASME Y14.5 GD&T callouts are applied as written. Material certs reference the ASTM or AMS designation. The CMM report maps to drawing callouts by feature number. Weldo CNC operates to that standard.

## Section 13: FAQ
**Design:** Accordion. White background. First item open.
**Heading:** Sourcing CNC Machining from China — USA FAQs

**Q1: How long does delivery take from China to the USA?**
Total lead time to a US address is typically 6–12 business days from order confirmation. Production runs 1–5 business days for standard aluminium parts — complex geometry or specialist alloys are confirmed at quoting. Transit via DHL Express or FedEx International Priority adds 5–7 business days from Dongguan. West Coast cities (Los Angeles, San Francisco, Seattle) are at the lower end. East Coast destinations (New York, Boston) are at the upper end. All shipments are DDP — duties, Section 301 tariff, and customs clearance included in the price.

**Q2: Who handles US customs and import duties — including Section 301 tariffs?**
Weldo CNC handles it. DDP — Delivered Duty Paid — means Weldo CNC pays the standard US import duty and the Section 301 tariff (25% on most Chinese-origin CNC machined components) before the shipment departs. Both are included in your quoted price. Your team receives a package, not a customs hold or broker invoice. Confirm DDP is explicitly stated in your quote before placing the order.

**Q3: How do I protect my IP when sending drawings to a supplier in China?**
An NDA is available before any file transfer, on request. Your STEP files and engineering drawings are used for quoting and manufacturing only — not shared with third parties. ISO 9001:2015 certification includes document control procedures governing how customer data is stored and handled at the Dongguan facility.

**Q4: What if the parts don't meet the drawing when they arrive?**
Parts ship with a CMM dimensional inspection report — coordinate measuring machine — verified against your drawing callouts. If a part is non-conforming and the report confirms it, Weldo CNC remakes or credits without dispute. Non-conformances are caught at the inspection stage before parts leave the facility. The CMM report gives you an independent measurement record before you open the box.

**Q5: Can Weldo CNC read ASME Y14.5 GD&T drawings?**
Yes. ASME Y14.5 GD&T callouts — positional tolerances, flatness, runout, perpendicularity, concentricity — are applied directly from your 2D drawing. Inch and metric dimensions are both read natively. UNC and UNF thread callouts are handled alongside M-series. Manufacturability questions are raised at quoting — not after machining starts.

**Q6: Can you work from ASTM or AMS material specifications?**
Yes. Specify ASTM B209 for aluminium, AMS 2770 for heat treatment, or any standard ASTM or AMS alloy designation on your drawing or purchase order. Material certificates reference the specified standard. If your programme requires a specific AMS or MIL specification affecting process (e.g. AMS 2625 for titanium cleaning), confirm at quote stage and the relevant documentation is included in the shipment.

**Q7: Is there a minimum order quantity for US engineers?**
No minimum. Single prototype parts to production quantities — Weldo CNC accepts all. Per-unit cost is higher on single pieces because setup time is fixed regardless of quantity, but there is no floor on order size. Engineers working on 1–10 part prototype builds are a normal part of the workload.

**Q8: What tolerances can Weldo CNC hold on CNC machined parts?**
±0.005 mm is the confirmed standard for CNC milling and turning. Five-axis simultaneous machining holds ±0.003 mm on complex geometry — all critical features cut in one clamping, eliminating repositioning error between setups. All critical-tolerance features are CMM-verified and reported per feature on the dimensional inspection report. Include GD&T callouts on your 2D drawing for any feature where positional, flatness, or runout tolerance matters.

**Q9: What file formats do you accept?**
STEP files are preferred for 3D geometry. Drawings in PDF or DWG. Additional accepted formats: IGES, SolidWorks (.sldprt), Parasolid (.x_t), Pro/E (.prt), and ACIS (.sat). Upload STEP plus 2D PDF drawing for the most accurate quote. Reference images in JPG or PNG are accepted for additional context.

**Q10: How does Weldo CNC compare to a US-based precision machine shop?**
A domestic precision machine shop is the right choice when parts are needed in 24–48 hours, or when the programme requires a locally auditable supplier with AS9100D or ISO 13485 registration. Weldo CNC is typically the stronger choice for tight-tolerance work (±0.005 mm), full CMM documentation, specialist alloys, 5-axis geometry, or quantities of 5–500 parts where domestic pricing and lead times — often 3–6 weeks for documented work — become the constraint. Many US engineering teams use both: a local shop for urgent one-offs, Weldo CNC for production-intent documented builds and repeat production batches.
