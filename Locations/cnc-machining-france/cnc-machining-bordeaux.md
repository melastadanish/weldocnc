Weldo CNC supplies precision CNC machined parts to engineers in Bordeaux — manufactured at its ISO 9001:2015 certified facility in Dongguan, China. Tolerances to ±0.005 mm. Delivered to Bordeaux in 4–10 business days DDP — Delivered Duty Paid — with a CMM-verified dimensional inspection report in every tight-tolerance shipment. Quote within 24 hours.

Bordeaux sits at the northern end of France's southwest aerospace and defence corridor — a region that stretches south to Toulouse. Two major programmes define the local engineering requirement: Dassault Aviation's Rafale production facility in Mérignac (adjacent to Bordeaux) and Thales Bordeaux, which develops defence electronics and targeting systems. Both organisations and their tier-2 supply chains require precision machined components with tight tolerances and complete documentation.

Dassault's Rafale programme is a different machining demand profile from the commercial aerospace work centred on Toulouse. Fighter aircraft components — canopy frames, airframe structural parts, actuator housings, hydraulic manifolds — are machined in aluminium alloy, titanium, and high-strength steel. Tolerances are tight: positional callouts on fastener hole patterns, surface finish requirements on aerodynamic surfaces, and interface tolerances on mating structural parts all push into ISO 2768 fine (f) and tighter. Material traceability is a baseline requirement, not an option.

Thales Bordeaux creates a second requirement: defence electronics. Radar system housings, electronics enclosures, EMC-critical housings, and precision mechanical components for targeting systems are machined in aluminium alloy — often hard anodised or alodine treated — and stainless steel. Tolerances are tight, quantities are typically small (1–50 parts), and the programme classification means IP handling is sensitive.

The Bordeaux-Toulouse corridor is also developing as a tech and smart city cluster. Bordeaux Métropole's digital infrastructure programme has attracted tech companies and startups requiring precision machined parts for sensors, embedded electronics housings, and urban mobility hardware. These are lower-volume, shorter-run requirements where rapid prototyping and DFM feedback matter more than volume pricing.

Wine and food industry machinery adds a more localised requirement: stainless steel machined components for bottling lines, labelling equipment, and processing machinery — standard-tolerance parts where corrosion resistance is the primary specification driver.

Weldo CNC operates 100+ CNC machines in Dongguan with confirmed 1–5 business day manufacturing lead times. For Bordeaux engineers working on development and pre-production quantities — where local aerospace shops are committed to long-term programme supply chains — that available capacity is a practical alternative.

**Design:** Accordion. White background. First item open.
**Heading:** CNC Machining for Bordeaux Engineers — FAQs

**Q1: How long does delivery take from China to Bordeaux?**
3–5 business days from Dongguan via DHL Express or FedEx International Priority — after manufacturing. Total lead time is 4–10 business days combining 1–5 days manufacturing with 3–5 days shipping. All shipments are DDP — duties and customs clearance handled, included in the quoted price. Parts arrive in Bordeaux with no import charges on your end.

**Q2: Can Weldo CNC supply aerospace and defence companies in Bordeaux — Dassault and Thales suppliers?**
Yes — aerospace and defence are confirmed industry verticals. Aluminium and titanium machined parts with CMM-verified inspection reports and full material traceability are standard on every tight-tolerance order. Parts ship with material certificate (heat number, temper, specification), CMM inspection report, and finish process record. For programmes requiring AS9100D-certified suppliers, confirm your documentation requirements at quoting — Weldo CNC holds ISO 9001:2015 and documents against your drawing. Many Bordeaux engineers use Weldo CNC for development and prototype quantities evaluated against the first-article.

**Q3: What tolerances does Weldo CNC hold for Bordeaux aerospace work?**
±0.005 mm is the confirmed standard for CNC milling and turning. For 5-axis simultaneous machining on complex aerospace geometry, ±0.003 mm is achievable. CMM — coordinate measuring machine — inspection verifies every critical-tolerance feature against your drawing. Bordeaux aerospace and defence work typically specifies ISO 2768 fine (f) or tighter positional callouts on fastener hole patterns — both are covered at standard capability.

**Q4: What import duties apply to CNC machined parts shipped to Bordeaux?**
CNC machined parts under CN 8466.93 attract EU import duty of 2.7–3.7%. TVA at 20% applies on import and is reclaimable if your company is VAT-registered in France. Weldo CNC ships DDP — Delivered Duty Paid under Incoterms 2020 — duties and customs clearance included in the quoted price. No surprise charges on arrival in Bordeaux.

**Q5: How does Weldo CNC handle IP and drawing confidentiality for Bordeaux defence customers?**
NDA is available before any file is transferred or drawing reviewed. Part data stays within the quoting and manufacturing team and is not shared or reused. For Bordeaux defence electronics and aerospace customers where programme classification means IP sensitivity is high, NDA-first is the standard working practice.

**Q6: How does Weldo CNC compare to local Bordeaux CNC shops?**
Local Bordeaux shops — particularly those serving Dassault Mérignac — are the right answer for jobs needed in 24–48 hours and for programmes requiring a contractually approved AS9100D supplier. For development and pre-production work — 1–50 part batches, prototype first articles, or geometry under revision — Weldo CNC is typically the stronger choice. Manufacturing lead time of 1–5 days versus the typical Bordeaux aerospace shop queue of 3–6 weeks is the key differentiator on development-phase sourcing.

**Q7: What file formats does Weldo CNC accept for quoting?**
STEP is the preferred format for 3D geometry. Also accepted: IGES, SolidWorks (.sldprt), PDF 2D drawings, DXF, and compressed packages (.zip, .rar). For tight-tolerance features, include a 2D drawing with tolerance callouts alongside the STEP file. Metric drawings using M-thread series and ISO 2768 tolerance standards are the standard format — NF norms referenced where supplied.

**Q8: What is the minimum order quantity for CNC parts delivered to Bordeaux?**
No minimum order quantity. One part to full production quantities — both accepted. Per-unit cost is higher on single pieces due to fixed setup cost, but there is no floor. Bordeaux engineers sourcing Rafale-adjacent prototype parts, defence electronics housings, or smart city technology hardware in small batches are a typical use case.

**Q9: Can Weldo CNC machine hard anodised aluminium housings for defence electronics in Bordeaux?**
Yes — hard anodising (Type III) is a confirmed surface finish on aluminium. It produces a thick, hard oxide layer — typically 25–50 µm — that provides wear resistance, electrical insulation, and corrosion protection. Standard on defence electronics enclosures and radar housings where surface hardness and EMC performance matter. Finish process record ships with every anodised order. Alodine (chromate conversion) is also confirmed — often specified alongside hard anodising on aluminium components requiring both corrosion protection and paint adhesion.

**Q10: Does Weldo CNC provide surface finishing for Bordeaux aerospace parts?**
50+ surface finish options are confirmed. For Bordeaux aerospace and defence work: Type II anodising (soft, corrosion-resistant), Type III hard anodising (wear-resistant), alodine for MIL-spec chromate conversion, passivation for stainless components, and bead blasting for uniform matte texture. Each finish ships with a process record documenting the treatment parameters. Confirm your finish specification at quoting.
