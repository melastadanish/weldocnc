## Section 8: Importing CNC Parts to the UK
**Design:** 2-column — editorial text left (60%), summary table right (40%). Light grey background.
**Heading:** Importing CNC Machined Parts to the UK — What to Expect

Understanding how import and customs work when sourcing CNC parts from China prevents delays and unexpected costs. Here is what to expect when ordering with Weldo CNC.

**DDP means we handle it**
All Weldo CNC shipments to the UK are sent DDP — Delivered Duty Paid. We file the import declaration, pay the customs duty, and handle clearance before the parcel reaches your door. You receive parts with no additional charges and no need to appoint a customs broker.

**UK import duty on CNC machined parts**
Most CNC machined metal parts fall under Chapter 84 or 73 of the UK Global Tariff. Typical duty rates:
- Machined aluminium parts (HS 7616.99): 0–3%
- Machined stainless steel parts (HS 7326.90): 2.7%
- Precision machined components (HS 8466.93): 0–2%
- Titanium parts (HS 8108.99): 0%

Under DDP, these are paid by Weldo CNC and included in your quoted price.

**VAT on import**
20% UK import VAT applies to goods imported from China. Under DDP, this is handled at the border and included in the delivered price. If you are VAT-registered, you can reclaim import VAT through your standard VAT return — confirm with your accountant.

**Documents provided with every UK shipment**
Commercial invoice, packing list, CN22/CN23 or air waybill, country of origin certificate, HS code per line item, material certificates, and CMM dimensional report inside the package.

**UKCA vs CE marking**
CNC machined parts are components — they do not require UKCA or CE marking. If you are incorporating our parts into a product that requires UKCA marking, that obligation sits with you as the product manufacturer.

## Section 11: Engineering in the UK
**Design:** Full-width editorial section. White background. Large heading. 2-column text on desktop.
**Heading:** Engineering in the UK — Where We Deliver

The UK engineering market is one of the most technically demanding in the world. Across aerospace, medtech, defence, automotive, and energy, the parts being designed and specified by UK engineers are operating in environments where the margin for error is measured in microns, not millimetres. That context shapes what a credible CNC machining supplier needs to provide — and it's why documentation, traceability, and tolerance consistency matter as much as unit price.

**South West England — Aerospace**
The aerospace cluster centred on Bristol, Filton, and the West of England is one of the largest outside the USA. Airbus UK, GKN Aerospace, Rolls-Royce Bristol, and their tier 1 and tier 2 supply chains generate sustained demand for precision aluminium and titanium structural components. 7075-T6 wing brackets, titanium fastener-adjacent hardware, aluminium access panels, and actuation system components are representative of the work. Full material traceability — mill cert, heat number, temper designation — is not a request in this market; it is the baseline expectation. AS9100D certification is increasingly required at tier 1, but tier 2 and prototype suppliers operating to ISO 9001:2015 with rigorous CMM inspection are viable for a significant portion of the workload.

**London and the South East — Medtech, Robotics, and Deep Tech**
London's engineering community is split between two distinct clusters. The medtech and life sciences community around King's Cross, the Royal Free Hospital campus, and the satellite institutions of Imperial College and UCL generates consistent demand for ISO 9001:2015 compliant stainless and titanium components — surgical instrument prototypes, diagnostic device housings, implant-adjacent hardware. Full material traceability and CMM reporting are expected from the first enquiry. The deep tech and robotics scene in East London, the Old Street corridor, and the growing cluster around White City produces a different requirement: rapid iteration on aluminium structural parts, actuator housings, sensor mounts, and precision mechanisms where the geometry may change every few weeks but the tolerance requirement remains tight.

**The Midlands — Automotive and Industrial Machinery**
The West Midlands automotive supply chain — with Jaguar Land Rover, the WMG at the University of Warwick, and a dense network of tier 1 and tier 2 suppliers — drives demand for precision powertrain components, suspension and chassis hardware, and increasingly, EV-specific parts: battery module housings, motor end caps, cooling system components in aluminium and stainless. Alongside automotive, the broader Midlands industrial machinery sector produces steady demand for gearbox housings, bearing blocks, shafts, and machine frame components where dimensional accuracy and surface finish are the controlling requirements.

**Scotland — Energy and Defence**
Scotland's engineering demand is dominated by two sectors. The North Sea oil and gas industry — and its expanding offshore renewables supply chain — generates requirements for downhole tooling, valve bodies, manifold assemblies, and subsea components in duplex stainless, super-duplex, and Inconel. High-pressure, high-temperature environments, demanding material specifications, and pressure-rated documentation are typical. Defence procurement centred on Edinburgh, Rosyth, and the broader Scottish defence industrial base produces a second stream of precision machined work in aluminium, stainless, and specialist alloys.

**Cambridge — Deep Tech and Life Sciences**
The Cambridge cluster — ARM, Marshall Aerospace, Cambridge Biomedical Campus, and a dense ecosystem of university spinouts — produces some of the most technically demanding machined parts in the UK. Semiconductor tooling in MIC-6 aluminium, surgical device prototypes in 316L and titanium, precision actuation mechanisms for robotics and automation: tolerances are tight, surface finish requirements are specific, and the engineers specifying the work have a detailed understanding of what they're asking for.

## Section 13: FAQ
**Design:** Accordion. White background. First item open.
**Heading:** Sourcing CNC Machining from China — UK FAQs

**Q1: How long does delivery take from China to the UK?**
3–5 business days via DHL Express or FedEx International Priority. All shipments are DDP — duties and customs clearance included in the price. Parts arrive at your UK address with no additional charges. Northern Ireland may add 1 day; Scottish Highlands 1–2 days depending on the carrier.

**Q2: Who handles customs and import duties?**
We do. DDP means we file the import declaration, pay the applicable duty, and handle clearance. You receive a package — not a customs hold notice. No customs broker required on your end. UK import VAT (20%) is included in the delivered price and reclaimable if your business is VAT registered.

**Q3: How do I protect my IP when sending files to a supplier in China?**
We sign an NDA before any file transfer, on request. Your STEP files and drawings are used for quoting and manufacturing only — they are not shared with third parties. ISO 9001:2015 certification includes document control procedures governing how customer data is handled.

**Q4: What if the parts don't meet the drawing when they arrive?**
Parts ship with a CMM dimensional report checked against your drawing. If a part is non-conforming and the report confirms it, we remake or credit — without dispute. Non-conformances are identified at our inspection stage before parts leave the facility. The report gives you an independent record before you even open the box.

**Q5: Can you communicate during UK business hours?**
All communication is in English. The timezone difference (UTC+8 vs UK UTC+0/+1) means there is a working day overlap of 1–2 hours in the morning — UK afternoon to Dongguan is asynchronous. In practice: send a query in the afternoon UK time, receive a response by morning. 24-hour response is the standard; most queries are answered same-day.

**Q6: Is there a minimum order quantity?**
No minimum. One piece to production quantities. Per-unit cost is higher on single pieces — setup time is fixed regardless of quantity — but there is no floor on order size. UK prototype and pre-production engineers working on 1–10 parts are a normal part of our workload.

**Q7: Do you work with UK universities and research institutions?**
Yes. Academic and research programme requirements — specific material grades, unusual alloy specifications, tight tolerances on one-off or short-run parts — are well within the scope of our normal operation. Purchase order or invoice-based procurement both work.
