## Section 8: Importing CNC Parts to Germany
**Design:** 2-column — editorial text left (60%), summary table right (40%). Light grey background.
**Heading:** Importing CNC Machined Parts to Germany — What to Expect

Understanding how EU import and customs rules work when sourcing CNC parts from China prevents delays and unexpected charges. Here is what to expect when ordering with Weldo CNC.

**DDP means we handle it**
All Weldo CNC shipments to Germany are sent DDP — Delivered Duty Paid. We file the EU customs declaration, pay the applicable import duty, settle the import VAT, and arrange clearance before the shipment reaches your door. You receive parts — not a customs hold notice and not a broker invoice.

**EU import duty on CNC machined parts**
Germany applies the EU Common External Tariff (CET) on goods imported from China. For most CNC machined metal components the applicable rates under CN code 8466.93 are:
- Precision machined components (CN 8466.93): 2.7–3.7% MFN rate
- Machined aluminium parts (CN 7616.99): 2.7%
- Machined stainless steel (CN 7326.90): 2.7%
- Titanium parts (CN 8108.99): 0%

Under DDP, these duties are paid by Weldo CNC and included in your quoted price. No additional charge at delivery.

**Import VAT — Einfuhrumsatzsteuer**
German import VAT (Einfuhrumsatzsteuer) is 19%. Under DDP, this is settled at EU border entry and included in the delivered price. If your business is VAT-registered in Germany — Umsatzsteuer-Identifikationsnummer — you can recover import VAT through your standard VAT return (Vorsteuerabzug). Confirm the process with your Steuerberater.

**EORI number — not required on your end**
Under DDP, Weldo CNC acts as the importer of record. Your EORI number is not required for the customs declaration — the obligation sits with us, not with you.

**CE marking**
CNC machined components do not require CE marking. The CE obligation applies to finished products placed on the EU market, not to machined components used in their manufacture. If you incorporate Weldo CNC parts into a product requiring CE marking under a relevant EU Directive or Regulation, that obligation sits with you as the product manufacturer.

**REACH**
Standard aluminium, stainless steel, and titanium alloys machined at Weldo CNC are not subject to REACH restrictions. Certain surface treatments — chromate conversion coating, for example — may be relevant depending on your application. Request material data sheets for any REACH-sensitive specification at quote stage.

**Transit to Germany**
DHL Express and FedEx International Priority: typically 3–5 business days from Dongguan to Germany. Frankfurt, Munich, and Hamburg are the fastest entry points by air. Combined with 1–5 business days production, total delivery to Germany is 4–10 business days.

## Section 11: Engineering in Germany — Where We Deliver
**Design:** Full-width editorial section. White background. Large heading. 2-column text on desktop.
**Heading:** Engineering in Germany — Where We Deliver

Germany's engineering sector is among the most technically demanding in the world. Automotive, aerospace, precision machinery, and energy supply chains — concentrated in four major industrial regions — generate continuous demand for tight-tolerance, fully documented CNC machined parts. German engineers expect DIN/ISO standard compliance, metric drawings, M-thread series (DIN 13), and dimensional reports as baseline. The Mittelstand — Germany's medium-sized industrial manufacturers — are a major buyer type, sourcing precision components for OEM machinery where delivery reliability and dimensional consistency are contractual requirements, not preferences.

**Baden-Württemberg — Automotive and Mechanical Engineering**
Stuttgart and Karlsruhe sit at the centre of Germany's automotive heartland. Daimler, Porsche, Bosch, Schaeffler, and ZF Friedrichshafen anchor a tier supply chain that generates sustained demand for precision powertrain components, sensor housings, EV battery housings, and actuation system hardware. Aluminium (6061, 7075) and stainless steel are the dominant materials. Engineers in this region specify to DIN EN ISO 2768 for general tolerances, ISO 286 for fits and bore/shaft callouts, and expect dimensional reports that reference feature-by-feature against the 2D drawing. IATF 16949 context is present throughout the tier 1 and tier 2 automotive supply chain — suppliers operating under IATF 16949 quality management systems are common, and the documentation expectations that flow down to component suppliers reflect that rigour.

**Bavaria — Automotive, Aerospace, and Medtech**
Munich is the engineering capital of southern Germany. BMW's procurement network, Airbus Defence and Space in Ottobrunn, MTU Aero Engines in Munich, and MAN Truck & Bus all generate demand for precision machined parts in aluminium, titanium, and Inconel. Tight geometric tolerances — flatness, position, runout — specified to ISO 286 and verified by CMM are standard at aerospace tier 2 and tier 3. The Munich medtech cluster — Siemens Healthineers, and a network of surgical device and diagnostic equipment manufacturers — adds requirements for stainless 316L, titanium Grade 5, and biocompatible surface finishes. Augsburg and Ingolstadt extend the automotive and aerospace workload across the region. AS9100D adjacent documentation is increasingly requested at tier 2 and tier 3 aerospace suppliers — full material traceability, heat number, certificate of conformance, and CMM dimensional report are expected as standard.

**North Rhine-Westphalia — Industrial Machinery and Process Engineering**
Düsseldorf, Cologne, and Dortmund form Germany's largest industrial region by population and output. The engineering demand here is diverse: fluid handling, valve bodies, pump components, hydraulic manifolds, and mechanical assemblies for process industries. Stainless 316L and duplex stainless grades are heavily used in process engineering applications. Engineers in this region are cost-aware — the industrial machinery sector is competitive — but they expect measurable quality evidence: dimensional reports, material certs, and clear traceability. The Mittelstand is strongest in this region, with precision component sourcing driven by OEM machinery build schedules.

**Berlin and Brandenburg — Robotics, Deep Tech, and Defence**
Berlin's engineering ecosystem has expanded significantly. OHB System in Bremen has extended procurement reach into Berlin's aerospace cluster; the broader defence procurement growth across Germany has increased demand for precision machined aluminium and stainless parts for sensor housings, mounting structures, and actuation hardware. The deep tech and robotics community — concentrated in Berlin-Mitte and Adlershof — produces iterative prototype work in aluminium with 5-axis geometry: robot arm links, actuator housings, gripper bodies, and structural members where geometry changes frequently but tolerance requirements (±0.005 mm on critical interfaces) remain constant. Short lead times on revised parts are a common requirement here.

**Hamburg and North Germany — Maritime and Energy**
Hamburg's engineering demand is split between maritime and aerospace. Airbus Germany's Hamburg facility — Finkenwerder — produces structural assembly work that generates demand from the tier supply chain for aluminium structural components, titanium fastener hardware, and precision brackets. The maritime and offshore engineering sector generates requirements for duplex stainless and super-duplex components: valve bodies, subsea fittings, pump housings, and manifold assemblies where corrosion resistance and pressure-rated material traceability are mandatory. Material certificates including heat number, melt analysis, and test results are expected as standard on these applications.

**What German Engineers Specify**
Drawings arrive metric — always. Thread callouts use the M-series (DIN 13): M3, M4, M5, M6, M8, M10, M12, M16, M20. General tolerances reference DIN EN ISO 2768-m (medium) or DIN EN ISO 2768-f (fine). Fits and bore/shaft tolerances reference ISO 286 directly: H7/g6, H8/f7, H6/k5. If your drawing specifies any of these, Weldo CNC machines and inspects to those callouts — CMM reports reference each feature against the specified tolerance class, not against a general house standard.

**Heading:** Sourcing CNC Machining from China — Germany FAQs

**Q1: How long does delivery take from China to Germany?**
4–10 business days total — production 1–5 business days plus transit 3–5 business days via DHL Express or FedEx International Priority. All shipments are DDP — EU customs declaration, import duty, and German import VAT included in the price. Parts arrive at your German address cleared, with no additional charges and no customs administration on your end. Frankfurt, Munich, and Hamburg are the fastest entry points by air from Dongguan.

**Q2: Who handles EU customs and import duties for shipments to Germany?**
Weldo CNC handles it. DDP — Delivered Duty Paid — means we file the EU customs declaration, pay the applicable import duty under the EU Common External Tariff, and settle the Einfuhrumsatzsteuer (German import VAT at 19%) before the shipment is released for delivery. Your EORI number is not required. You receive cleared goods, not a customs hold and not a broker invoice. VAT-registered German businesses can reclaim import VAT through their standard Vorsteuerabzug process.

**Q3: Do you machine to DIN standards and metric drawings?**
Yes — metric drawings are standard. Thread callouts to the M-series (DIN 13): M3 through M20 and beyond are all within standard capability. General tolerances to DIN EN ISO 2768-m or -f as specified on your drawing. Fits and shaft/bore tolerances referenced to ISO 286 — H7/g6, H8/f7, H6/k5 — are inspected and reported per feature on the CMM dimensional report. Submit your 2D drawing with all tolerance callouts and reference standards specified; Weldo CNC machines and inspects to the drawing, not to a generic house standard.

**Q4: What if parts don't meet the drawing when they arrive?**
Every order ships with a CMM dimensional report — coordinate measuring machine — checked against your 2D drawing, feature by feature. If a part falls outside the specified tolerance and the report confirms non-conformance, Weldo CNC remakes or credits without dispute. Non-conformances are identified at the final inspection stage in Dongguan before parts leave the facility. The 97.48% annual pass rate reflects how often parts reach that inspection stage without issue.

**Q5: How do I protect my IP when sending files to a supplier in China?**
Weldo CNC signs an NDA before any file transfer, on request. STEP files and 2D drawings are used for quoting and manufacturing only — not shared with third parties. ISO 9001:2015 certification includes document control procedures governing how customer technical data is stored and handled. File transfer is via secure upload on the quote page — no email attachments required.

**Q6: Is there a minimum order quantity?**
No minimum. One part to full production quantities. Per-unit cost is higher on single pieces because setup time is fixed regardless of quantity — but there is no floor on order size. German prototype engineers and Mittelstand buyers sourcing 1–50 parts for development or pre-production validation are a normal part of the workload.

**Q7: What tolerances can Weldo CNC hold on precision CNC parts?**
±0.005 mm is the confirmed standard for CNC milling and turning — verified by CMM and reported on the dimensional inspection report that ships with every tight-tolerance order. For complex geometry on 5-axis simultaneous machining, ±0.003 mm is achievable on critical features. Include GD&T callouts or ISO 286 fit designations on your 2D drawing for any features with positional, flatness, cylindricity, or runout requirements — these are reported individually on the CMM report.

**Q8: What materials does Weldo CNC machine for German engineers?**
100+ metals and engineering plastics. Metals: aluminium (6061, 7075, 2024), stainless steel (303, 304, 316L, 17-4PH, duplex), titanium (Grade 2, Grade 5 Ti-6Al-4V), brass, copper, carbon steel, alloy steel, tool steel (H13, D2), Inconel. Engineering plastics: PEEK, Delrin (POM), nylon, polycarbonate, ABS, PTFE, HDPE, ULTEM. Confirm your specific grade and condition (e.g. 7075-T6, 316L annealed) at quote stage. Material certificates — mill cert, heat number, chemical composition — are included with every metallic order.

**Q9: Can you communicate during German business hours?**
All communication is in English. The timezone gap — Dongguan UTC+8 vs Germany CET UTC+1 / CEST UTC+2 — leaves a 1–3 hour working-day overlap in the morning. Send a technical query during German afternoon hours and a response arrives by German morning. The 24-hour response standard applies to all queries; most are answered faster. For time-sensitive DFM questions during production, same-day responses are standard.

**Q10: How does Weldo CNC compare to a German precision machining supplier?**
German Präzisionsmechanik shops are the right choice for same-week turnarounds, locally auditable supply chains, or programmes that require IATF 16949 or DIN EN 9100 certification at the component supplier level. Weldo CNC is typically the stronger choice for tight-tolerance work (±0.005 mm), full CMM documentation, specialist alloys, complex 5-axis geometry, or quantities of 5–500 parts where German precision shop lead times (2–4 weeks typical) and pricing become a constraint. Many German engineers use both — a local shop for urgent 48-hour parts, Weldo CNC for the documented pre-production run or ongoing supply of a proven design.
