**H2:** Why Engineers Look for a Protolabs Alternative

**Card 1 — Per-Unit Cost at Volume**
Protolabs pricing is structured for rapid single-unit prototyping at US/EU manufacturing cost levels. At 20, 50, or 100 parts, the per-unit cost remains high — the automated platform model and Western facility cost base don't offer the reduction that comes with volume in a direct CNC facility. Engineers running pre-production or bridge quantities find the economics difficult to justify.

**Card 2 — Geometry and Tolerance Constraints**
Protolabs' instant-quote system is automated. Complex 5-axis geometry, deep pockets, tight positional tolerances, and parts requiring engineering judgement on fixturing often get flagged, rejected, or quoted at a significant premium. The platform works best within a defined geometry and tolerance envelope — outside that envelope, it is not the right tool.

**Card 3 — Documentation Depth**
Protolabs' CNC offering is part of a broader platform — injection molding and 3D printing lead their positioning. For programmes requiring CMM reports, material traceability, and finish process records as a standard rather than an optional add-on, a dedicated CNC machining facility with a manufacturing-focused QMS is a stronger match.

**H2:** Quality Control & Production Inspection

**Pre-Production:**
Material verified against mill certificate before any job starts. Drawing reviewed by an engineer for DFM issues, tolerance feasibility, and finish interactions. Unlike an automated geometry checker, engineer review catches fixturing constraints, thin-wall risks, and tolerance stack-ups that algorithms miss.

**In-Production:**
In-process checks at defined intervals using calibrated hand tools and CMM — Coordinate Measuring Machine. Datum references verified after each setup change. Non-Conformance Reports — NCRs — trigger a production stop before the next operation.

**Final-Production:**
CMM inspection against drawing revision. Measured values recorded per feature — nominal, tolerance, actual, pass/fail. Material cert, dimensional report, and packing list ship with every order. Documentation is included as standard — not as a platform add-on.

**H2:** Weldo CNC vs Protolabs — Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: What tolerances does Weldo CNC hold on CNC machined parts?**
±0.005 mm as standard on milling and turning. ±0.003 mm on 5-axis complex geometry. Protolabs states ±0.025 mm — Weldo CNC is 5× tighter. All critical-tolerance features are CMM-verified and reported per feature on the dimensional inspection report included with every order.

**Q2: What CNC machining services does Weldo CNC offer?**
CNC milling (3-axis to 5-axis), CNC turning, 5-axis CNC machining, precision grinding, wire EDM, and 50+ surface finishing options. All services are in-house at a single facility in Dongguan. No subcontracting.

**Q3: What materials can Weldo CNC machine?**
100+ metals and engineering plastics. Metals include aluminium (6061, 7075, 2024, MIC-6), stainless steel (303, 304, 316L, 17-4PH), titanium (Grade 2, Grade 5), brass, copper, carbon steel, alloy steel, and tool steel. Plastics include PEEK, Delrin (POM), Nylon, Polycarbonate, ABS, PTFE, and Acrylic. Protolabs offers 76 materials on their platform.

**Q4: What are Weldo CNC lead times?**
Prototype parts: 1–5 business days manufacturing. Small batch: 5–15 business days. Production runs: 2–5 weeks. Transit to UK, Europe, and North America adds 3–7 days. Protolabs offers 1–3 day lead times for simple standard-geometry parts — for those, they are faster. For tight-tolerance or complex geometry, lead times are comparable.

**Q5: Is there a minimum order quantity?**
No minimum. One part to production quantities — the same quality process applies at any volume.

**Q6: What file formats does Weldo CNC accept?**
STEP is preferred. Also accepted: IGES, SolidWorks (.sldprt), PDF 2D drawings, DXF, and compressed packages (.zip, .rar). Include a 2D drawing with tolerance callouts alongside the STEP file for precision features.

**Q7: How does Weldo CNC pricing compare to Protolabs at volume?**
At 1–5 parts, Protolabs can be competitive for standard geometry and short lead times. At 20–500 parts, Weldo CNC pricing decreases significantly — setup cost is spread across units and there is no platform margin applied to each order. For pre-production and bridge quantities, the cost difference is substantial.

**Q8: What is the core difference between Weldo CNC and Protolabs?**
Two things: focus and manufacturing model. Protolabs is primarily an injection molding and rapid prototyping company — CNC is part of their service portfolio, not their specialism. Weldo CNC does one thing: CNC machining. Second, Protolabs uses automated quoting and manufacturing systems. Weldo CNC uses engineers — for DFM review, for quoting, for QC sign-off. For complex geometry and documentation-critical work, that difference is significant.

**Q9: Can Weldo CNC machine parts that Protolabs rejects?**
Frequently. Protolabs' automated system flags or rejects complex 5-axis geometry, deep pockets, tight positional tolerances, and parts requiring non-standard fixturing. Weldo CNC uses engineer review — not an automated geometry checker. Send the drawing and we will assess it. If it is machinable, we will quote it.

**Q10: How does international delivery work — do I need a customs broker?**
No. Weldo CNC ships DDP — Delivered Duty Paid — to the UK, USA, Germany, France, and most of Europe and North America. DDP means Weldo CNC files the customs declaration, pays the import duty, and handles clearance before parts reach the destination. No customs broker required, no unexpected charges on arrival.
